Chapter 18
OVERVIEW
In the Bible, God reveals himself to
humanity so that every person can know
and worship him. The Bible is inspired,
trustworthy, and rooted in history.
To understand and apply its message,
we must learn to read Scripture well,
understanding its grand narrative. The
Bibles story unfolds in six acts. Through
the Bible, we learn about who God is;
who humans are; God's plans for us and
creation; and how we are to relate to God,
one another, and creation.
The Christian faith provides answers
to life's biggest questions. It teaches that
God and truth exist. It tells us that we
were created by God in his image and
given the responsibility of ruling the earth.
We each have a God-given purpose. We
can know right from wrong and obtain
forgiveness for our sins through faith in
Christ. Death is not the end. Christians
have the hope of eternal life because Jesus
rose from the dead. We look forward to
the new creation where we will reign with
God.
Before he ascended into heaven,
Jesus commissioned his disciples to
share the good news with others. We are
commanded to share the gospel with
others, encouraging them to enter into
a relationship with God through Christ.
The Holy Spirit empowers us to live in
obedience to God and the Scriptures,
doing good works and demonstrating our
faith by the way we live. We are also called
to share the good news (evangelism) and
to defend the faith (apologetics) as we
have opportunity.

1. How does Christianity address where humans came from and
who they are? [18.3]
The Christian worldview teaches that human beings are not the
product of random chance. We were created by God as a result of
God's love. Each person bears God's image and is endowed with
intrinsic dignity and value. We are deeply loved by God and invited
to claim our identity as his children.
2. What does Christianity teach about the world and its future?
[18.3]
Christianity teaches that the world is purposeful and valuable. It is
the product of God's creation, and though it is corrupted by sin, it
will one day be made new and flourish as part of God's new creation.
3. How do Christians answer the question: Is there a God, and if
so, what is God like? [18.3]
Christians teach that God exists and is the Creator and Sustainer
of all things. He is a personal God characterized by love, goodness,
and justice. He is separate from creation but not distant. He desires
relationship with us.
4. How would you explain what Christians believe to someone
who says that everyone has their own truth and everyone is
equally right? [18.3 and 18.4]
Answers will vary, but should include that the Christian faith teaches
that truth exists and that the truth about God can be known because
it has been revealed to us through the Scriptures that point to Christ
as our Savior. We can use reason and evidence to pursue truth. Only
God is worthy of our ultimate trust because he never fails. Scripture
shows how God has been faithful in the past, giving us confidence
that we can trust him in the future.
5. What do people say about what humans are meant to do and
how they should live? How does the Christian faith address
these topics? [18.4]
Answers will vary concerning what people say. The Christian faith
teaches that our lives have a God-given purpose. We are here because
God loves us, wants us to know him, and wants us to enjoy his good
gifts. He also wants us to participate in his mission, bringing light
into the darkness. We are to imitate Jesus in giving away our lives in
acts of sacrificial love for others.
6. Describe the Christian answer to the question: How can I
know right from wrong? [18.4]
The Christian worldview teaches that God is the standard of
morality. We are to imitate his character in our actions and decision
making. In tricky situations, we must use wisdom, discernment,
prayer, and careful reflection on Scripture to determine what is right.

7. How do some people answer the question: What happens after I die? How
would you describe the Christian answer? [18.4]
Answers will vary regarding what some people would say. The Christian worldview

with him in the new creation where they will be given new bodies and freedom
from sin. Those who reject Christ will spend eternity away from his presence.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is evangelism? What does it involve? [18.5]
Evangelism is the task of sharing the good news of the gospel with
others and inviting them to place their trust in Jesus to receive
forgiveness and new life. It is offering a clear message about Jesus,
his death, burial, and resurrection; his defeat of sin and death; his
offer of salvation; and his kingly rule over the world. Evangelism
may also involve sharing your own testimony about what God has
done for you.
2. What role do good works play in evangelism? [18.5]
Good works are a powerful testimony to the difference Jesus has
made in our lives. They are evidence of an active and living faith.
If people see that our faith in Jesus doesn't make any difference in
the way we live, why would they want to follow Jesus themselves?
Our good works and the way we live point people to the reality that
God's kingdom has broken into the world.
3. What is apologetics? What does it involve? [18.5]
Apologetics is the art of defending, commending, and translating
the Christian faith. Apologetics helps address objections to and
questions about Christianity, as well as roadblocks people have
that keep them from following Jesus. Apologetics helps people
see that Christianity is reasonable, relevant, and desirable. It
involves listening to people's concerns, asking good questions, and
thoughtfully articulating responses to people's questions.

CUMULATIVE STUDY GUIDE
1. Explain what a worldview is and give examples of some
worldviews.
A worldview is a pattern of ideas and beliefs that helps us make sense of
the world and that guides our decisions and actions. Worldviews claim
to explain the world and to help us find direction and purpose for our
lives. Some examples of worldviews are Christianity, Islam, Marxism,
new spirituality, secularism, and postmodernism.
2. Describe what the Bible is and how it is arranged.
The Bible is God's revelation. It is a collection of 66 books and letters
that tells a story in six acts and an interlude. It tells about God, human
beings, and the universe. The first part of the Bible is called the Old
Testament and the second part is called the New Testament. The Old
Testament contains 39 books of history, poetry, and prophecy. The
New Testament contains 27 books, including the Gospels, church
history, letters, and prophecy.
3. Explain how the church recognized which books should be in
the Bible.
The canon is the collection of biblical writings commonly accepted as
genuine and authoritative. The Jews had settled on most of the Old
Testament canon before Jesus was born. The church accepted those
books as Scripture. Then they decided additional books should also be
considered Scripture, based on their apostolicity (was the book written
by an apostle or an associate of an apostle?), orthodoxy (did the book
conform to the teachings of the apostles and the theology of early
church leaders?), and pedigree (was the book quickly accepted by most
churches?). Those additional books form the New Testament.
4. Describe why relativism is a false belief.
Relativism is false because it contradicts itself. It says that there is
no truth, but it provides no way of knowing whether that is a true
statement. Relativism claims everyone can have their own truth, even
when one person's "truth" contradicts another's "truth."
5. Christians believe the Bible is authoritative and that internal
and external evidence supports this claim. Explain the difference
between the two kinds of evidence.
Internal evidence is evidence from Scripture that supports the claims of
the Bible, such as prophets' claims that they are communicating God's
revelation. External evidence is evidence from outside the Bible that
confirms the historical reliability of Scripture, such as archaeological
discoveries that support biblical accounts.

6. In ancient times, scribes had to copy manuscripts because there were
no printing presses. How do we know that what they copied is what the
Scriptures really said?
We know this because the different copies of Bible books mostly agree, even
when they were copied in different regions or hundreds of years later. The parts
that don't agree, called textual variants, are differences between particular words,
phrases, or passages within multiple copies of the same ancient manuscript.
More than 99 percent of the textual variants are minor spelling and word order
differences, unclear readings, or uses of synonyms. Less than 1 percent of the
textual variants are meaningful and viable, and none of them change core
Christian doctrines.
7. Explain how the creation account and Jesus' Incarnation contradict
gnosticism.
Gnosticism taught that the material world was evil. But the creation account
contradicts this because after God created the world, he declared that it was
"very good." Jesus' Incarnation contradicts gnosticism because Jesus chose
to become human. This means that our human bodies are not evil in and of
themselves.
8. List five common mistakes that people make in reading the Bible.
Five common mistakes that people make when reading the Bible are assuming
(1) that the Bible does not need to be interpreted, (2) that the Bible somehow
applies uniquely to them, (3) that they can ignore passages they do not like or
that do not fit their ideas about God and faith, (4) that the context of a verse or
passage doesn't matter, especially if the verse is meaningful to you, and (5) that
you can interpret it based on today's standards.
9. List seven steps to reading the Bible well.
Seven steps to reading the Bible well are (1) choosing a good translation, (2)
understanding the content of a passage and its (3) historical and (4) literary
context, (5) looking for any relationships a passage has to other parts of the
Bible, (6) using Bible helps as needed, and (7) determining how you will
respond to what you read.
10. What does it mean to say the Bible is a metanarrative?
A metanarrative is an overarching narrative that helps us make sense of other
narratives. Even though the Bible is made up of 66 individual books, each of
which has its own narrative, the whole Bible tells a cohesive grand story, or
metanarrative, that offers answers to the big questions of life.
11. What is the grand story that the Bible tells? How many parts does it
have? Which part are we living in now?
The Bible tells the grand story of God. It is a story with six acts and an interlude:
• The first act is Creation, when God created the universe and human beings.
• The second act is The Fall, when humans sinned and their relationships with God,
themselves, others, and creation were broken.
• The third act is Redemption Initiated, when God chose a nation, Israel, through whom the
world would be blessed.
• The interlude, the Second Temple Period, began when Israel was unfaithful to God and
was sent into exile for 70 years. No revelation came from God for 400 years.
• The fourth act is Redemption Accomplished, when God sent Jesus into the world to
redeem us from sin and establish the kingdom of God.
• The fifth act is Restoration Initiated. Jesus' followers were tasked with preaching the good
news of the kingdom of God to everyone on Earth.
• The sixth act is Restoration Accomplished, when Jesus will return to reign and to make all
things new.
12. Describe three ways in which human beings bear God's image.
Human beings, both male and female, are like God in resemblance, relationality, and rule.
This means we are like God in that we share some of his attributes, such as intelligence,
creativity, morality, and the ability to communicate. Like him, we are relational. God made
us to relate to him, ourselves, other people, and creation. The third way that we are like God
is rule. God rules the universe, yet he gave human beings rule over creation.
13. What does it mean to say that God is transcendent and immanent?
God is transcendent, which means he is not a part of his creation but separate from it. He
created it ex nihilo, out of nothing. God is also immanent, which means that he is within
and among what he has created. He is present everywhere and active in all of his creation.
14. What were the consequences of the first sin?
The consequences of the first sin were spiritual and physical death for all human beings and
broken relationships with God, ourselves, others, and creation. Adam and Eve's first sin
corrupted human nature; we all have a tendency to sin.
15. Describe the way of Cain and the way of the Lord.
To follow the way of Cain is to live a life of sin, following Cain's example of rebellion against
God. To follow the way of the Lord is to worship God and obey his commands.
16. Summarize the story of the family that eventually became the kingdom of Israel.
God made a covenant with Abraham, promising that his descendants would become as
numerous as the stars and that the whole world would be blessed through his family.
Abraham's descendants moved to Egypt to escape a famine and then God raised up a
leader to free the Israelites. He made a new covenant with Israel, giving them the Ten
Commandments on Mount Sinai and instructions for offering sacrifices and celebrating
festivals. The Israelites wandered the desert for 40 years and then entered the Promised
Land, where they had to conquer various tribes. They were ruled by judges. Later they told
God they wanted to have a king. Then they became the kingdom of Israel.

17. What is the Passover? How does it foreshadow the story of Jesus?
The Passover is an annual Jewish celebration to commemorate God rescuing
Israel from slavery in Egypt. Because Pharaoh refused to let the Israelites go
free, Moses told him that God would send ten plagues. The last plague was that
the firstborn sons of every family in Egypt would be struck dead by the Lord.
All Israelite families who sacrificed a lamb and put its blood on their doorposts
would be passed over; their firstborn sons would live. The Passover foreshadows
the story of Jesus because Jesus is the Lamb who was slain on the cross. Those
who trust in him are saved from death by his blood.
18. Describe the history of Israel from the time of its first king to the return
from exile.
The first king of Israel was Saul. He began humbly but then became proud
and sinful. God made David king in Saul's place. David ruled Israel with
righteousness, but he also sinned, committing adultery and murder. He
repented. His son Solomon inherited the throne. He began ruling Israel well
and built the temple in Jerusalem, but later he worshipped his many wives idols.
Solomon's son inherited the throne, but then the kingdom split in two. Ten
tribes became the northern Kingdom of Israel. They had many wicked rulers and
eventually were conquered and taken into exile. The other two tribes became the
southern Kingdom of Judah. It had many wicked kings, but also three faithful
kings. This kingdom lasted longer than the northern kingdom, but it was also
conquered and its people were taken into exile. King Cyrus allowed Jews to
return to Israel rebuild the temple. Later, another king gave permission for more
Jews to go back to rebuild the walls of Jerusalem.
19. What is a prophet? What did prophets do for Israel?
The prophets were people who spoke to Israel in the name of the Lord.
Sometimes they predicted the future events, but most of the time they warned
Israel that they would face God's judgment if they did not stop sinning. The
prophets also told the people about God's love and his promises, including the
promises of the coming messiah, the forgiveness of sin, the restoration of the
united kingdom of Israel, and the new heavens and the new earth.
20. Daniel had a vision of four beasts. What do many scholars think the
beasts represented?
Many scholars think the four beasts in Daniel's vision represented various
empires that would rule over the people of Israel. The first beast represented the
Babylonian empire; the second one, the Medo-Persian empire; the third, the
Greek empire; and the fourth, the Roman empire.
21. Was hellenization helpful or harmful to the Jewish faith?
Hellenization was both helpful and harmful. Because Alexander the Great
introduced Greek culture to the lands he conquered, the Jews learned Greek and
translated the Old Testament into that language. The use of Greek would later
make it easier for the church to spread the gospel. However, hellenization also
had harmful effects because the Jews faced pressure to abandon their religious
traditions.
22. Who were the Maccabees? What happened to them?
The Maccabees were rebels who overthrew their Greek king to briefly gain independence for
Israel. They were led by Judah, one of five sons of a priest who refused to make sacrifices to
pagan gods in the temple. The Maccabees and their descendants became tyrants over Israel
until the Roman empire conquered Israel one hundred years later.
23. What factions emerged among the Jews during the second temple period? Describe
them.
There were four factions that emerged in Israel during the second temple period:
• The Pharisees resisted hellenization and promoted strict observance of the Torah and oral
traditions of the Jews.
• The Sadducees cooperated with the rule of Rome, rather than resisting it. They upheld the
Torah but not the oral traditions; they also did not believe in the resurrection.
• The Essenes believed that the priests and the temple were corrupt and argued that people
should seek God in desert communities away from Jerusalem.
• The Zealots were revolutionaries who wanted to overthrow the Romans.
24. Most of the books of the Apocrypha were written during the second temple period.
What purpose did they fulfill for Israel?
The books encouraged the people of Israel to remain faithful to God and to reject idolatry.
25. What did the Israelites expect the messiah to do? Explain whether Jesus fulfilled
their expectations and his three roles as the Messiah.
The Jews expected that the messiah would be a political and military leader who would
liberate Israel from its Roman oppressors and reestablish the kingdom. Jesus did not fulfill
their expectations, though he fulfilled the prophecies in Scripture that related to his first
coming. As the Messiah, Jesus had three roles: Prophet, Priest, and King. As Prophet, Jesus
told the people about God's love and his commands. As Priest, Jesus offered himself as a
sacrifice for our sins. As King, Jesus claimed authority over life, death, and all creation. He
commanded demons to be quiet, performed miracles, and rose from the dead.
26. How and what did Jesus teach about the kingdom of God?
Jesus taught about the kingdom of God using parables. He also demonstrated the kingdom's
power by healing people from sickness and raising the dead. Jesus taught that the kingdom
of God did not come all at once or with irresistible power. Though in the kingdom all that
was broken would be set right in accordance with God's promises to restore creation, the full
revelation of the kingdom, and the final judgment, would happen in the future.
27. What did Jesus do for us by dying on the cross and rising again?
Jesus died on the cross as our substitute, paying the price for our sins. He suffered in our
place and defeated the power of sin because he was sinless. On the cross, Jesus secured our
redemption and enabled us to be restored to a right relationship with God through faith. In
Christ, we are made new. Because Christ rose from the dead, we can have eternal life.

28. Explain the relationship between the Great Commission and Pentecost.
The Great Commission is Jesus' command to the apostles to make disciples,
baptize, and instruct people about all he taught. Pentecost is the day the
promised Holy Spirit filled the apostles and the other disciples of Jesus who were
with them, giving them the power to share about Jesus with signs and wonders
so that the church would grow.
29. Why did the disciples and early church experience persecution? When
did it end?
The Jews who did not believe in Jesus were against those who preached that
he had risen from the dead; they persecuted the early church. The Roman
authorities persecuted people who claimed that Jesus was Lord, not Caesar.
The persecution ended in AD 313 when Constantine, the emperor, became a
Christian and granted Christianity legal status in the Roman empire.
30. How far has the Christian faith spread from the first century until now?
The Christian faith has spread all around the world. Though there are still
unreached people groups and many people do not profess faith in Christ,
Christianity is the largest religion on Earth and the majority of Christians now
live outside the West.
31. Apocalyptic literature conveys meaning through vivid images that are
symbols and metaphors. Explain the following images in the book of
Revelation: the slaughtered Lamb, the city of Babylon, and the city of
New Jerusalem.
The image of the slaughtered Lamb stands for Jesus, who died on the cross to
secure our redemption and rose again in victory over death and sin. The image
of the city of Babylon stands for the Roman empire and all who align themselves
against God. The image of the New Jerusalem is where God will rule and dwell
with his people when Jesus comes again and all things are restored.
32. List attributes of God that humans do not share, even when they are
made new in Christ.
Attributes of God that humans do not share, even when they are made new
in Christ, are self-existence, omniscience, omnipotence, omnipresence, and
immutability.
33. The word Trinity is not found in the Bible. Explain how the doctrine of
the Trinity is based on Scripture.
The doctrine of the Trinity is based on Scripture because the Bible presents God
as one Being in three Persons. Each Person of the Trinity can be seen in both the
Old Testament and the New. Sometimes, all three Persons are seen together at
the same time, such as at Jesus' baptism, when the Holy Spirit came down in the
form of a dove, and the Father spoke from heaven.
34. In what ways are believers supposed to become like Jesus?
Believers are supposed to become like Jesus by imitating him. This means that we seek to
avoid sin, to grow spiritually mature, and to obey and glorify God through serving and
loving others. As the Holy Spirit transforms us, we become the people God created us to be.
35. Why is growing in Christlikeness something that happens in community?
God created us to be in relationship with others. We cannot love or serve our neighbors if
we are isolated. A community of believers around us encourages us and helps us grow.
36. What is the purpose of spiritual disciplines?
The purpose of spiritual disciplines is to help us to be more like Christ. These habits and
practices help us to know God and Scripture better. They encourage us to attend to the work
of the Holy Spirit in and around us so we can be open to what God is doing in our lives.
37. What benefits do Christians receive by participating in a church community?
In church communities, Christians give and receive encouragement, comfort, and support
to follow Jesus more closely.
38. What are the two greatest commandments, and why should we keep them?
The two greatest commandments are to love God and to love our neighbors as ourselves. We
should keep these commandments because Jesus said that they are the most important and
that all of God's moral laws and commands are based on them. When Christians observe
these commandments, they transform the world.
39. What do evangelism and apologetics have to do with the Great Commission?
The Great Commission says to go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them
and teaching them to observe all that Jesus taught. Apologetics is the art of defending,
commending, and translating the Christian faith to clear the way for evangelism, and
evangelism is inviting people to become participants in God's story—to become disciples of
Jesus by trusting in him, so that they can be forgiven of their sins and receive eternal life.
