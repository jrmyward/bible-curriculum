Chapter 9
OVERVIEW
The prophet Daniel had an unusual vision
in which he saw four beasts that represented
four mighty kingdoms. These kingdoms
included the Babylonian Empire, the Medo-
Persian Empire, the Greek Empire, and the
Roman Empire. During Daniel's life, King
Cyrus of Persia facilitated the Jews' return
to Israel in order to rebuild Jerusalem's walls
and the temple. Many Jews never returned,
remaining among the nations as the Jewish
diaspora.
The intertestamental period or second
temple period stretched from the final events
of the Old Testament to the events recorded
in the New Testament. Most apocryphal
literature was written during this period.
Other important Jewish literature, such
as the works of Philo and Josephus, were
all written late in the period. During this
period, Alexander the Great conquered the
Persian Empire and initiated hellenization, a
movement to universalize Greek culture. The
Jewish people divided over hellenization. The
pressure to hellenize climaxed as Antiochus
IV instituted pagan worship in the Jewish
temple. The Maccabees revolted, reclaimed
Jerusalem, cleansed the temple (celebrated at
Hanukkah), and won Jewish independence.
Their descendants, the Hasmoneans, became
tyrants and ruled until the Roman Empire
installed Herod the Great. Caesar Augustus
rose to power and used crucifixion to punish
rebels.
The temple, land, Torah, and Jewish
racial identity became central symbols to
the Jews and differentiated them from other
peoples. The synagogue became an important
supplement to temple worship. Jewish
factions included the Pharisees, Sadducees,
Essenes, and Zealots. The Jewish people
hoped for salvation from their enemies,
establishment of a state of shalom, the arrival
of God's kingdom and reign, forgiveness of
sins, and a messiah who would lead Israel.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Who or what are the four beasts in Daniel's vision? [9.1]
One likely interpretation is as follows:
• First Beast: Babylonian Empire (Daniel 7:4)
• Second Beast: Medo-Persian Empire (Daniel 7:5)
• Third Beast: Greek Empire (Daniel 7:6)
• Fourth Beast: Roman Empire (Daniel 7:7)
2. Why were Ezra and Nehemiah so zealous in their reforms?
[9.1]
Ezra and Nehemiah were deeply committed to God and determined
to lead Israel in following the Lord. They knew that idolatry and
unfaithfulness to God were the reasons God had sent Israel into
exile. When they returned to Jerusalem and saw people being lazy,
marrying foreign wives, and being friendly with Israel's enemies,
they had every reason to be concerned. They may have feared such
practices would once again bring judgment.
3. What is the difference between the intertestamental period and
the second temple period? [9.2]
The intertestamental period covers roughly 400 years between
the last book of the Old Testament and the beginning of the New
Testament. The second temple period covers much of the same
time period, but the second temple period begins earlier (with the
rebuilding of the second temple in Jerusalem in 515 Bc and ends
later (with the destruction of the second temple in 70 AD).
4. What is hellenization? How was it helpful and how was it
harmful? [9.2]
Hellenization refers to the spread of Greek culture throughout the
lands conquered by Alexander the Great-ancient Europe, North
Africa, and the Middle East. Hellenization helpfully contributed
a common language, Greek, which made the spread of the gospel
much easier. However, the push to hellenize the world also crushed
some local cultures. Jews faced pressure to abandon their religious
traditions and values in favor of Greek ones. This caused tension
among Jewish leaders, and sometimes led to violent revolt.
5. How did Jews respond to the pressures of hellenization? [9.2]
The Jewish community divided over the hellenization problem.
Some embraced syncretism between hellenization and Judaism,
choosing to adopt Greek practices to fit in. Some Jews rejected
hellenization and saw it as a threat to a Jewish way of life. They saw
Jews who accepted hellenization as traitors to the Jewish community.
Note: Syncretism is combining two or more belief systems.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How do the Maccabees compare with other Jewish leaders
such as Daniel, Ezra, and Nehemiah? [9.3]
Like previous Jewish heroes, the Maccabees were zealous for Yahweh
and resisted the pressure to worship pagan gods and compromise
their faith. They wanted to make sure that Jewish identity was
preserved and that the people did not fall into idolatry again.
This makes them similar to Daniel, Ezra, and Nehemiah. It is
important to note that each leader faced a different situation and
had different goals. However, the Maccabees resorted to violence
and warfare to achieve their ends, whereas Daniel never did. Both
Ezra and Nehemiah rebuked but did not kill their own people. The
Maccabees made war on compromising Jews as well as their foreign
enemies.
2. According to Matthew 21:1-17, what similarities do you see
between the actions of Judah Maccabee and Jesus? How are
they different? [9.3]
Both Jesus and Judah Maccabee rode into Jerusalem on a donkey
while people waved palm branches and shouted, "Hosanna!" Both
Judah and Jesus cleansed the temple—Judah cleansed it from pagan
idolatry, while Jesus drove money lenders out. However, Judah's
entrance into Jerusalem was the result of a military victory. The
Maccabees and their descendants, the Hasmoneans, later became
tyrants and claimed to be kings, but none lived up to the title of
messiah. Jesus, on the other hand, entered Jerusalem peacefully while
claiming to be the true king. He did not assert his authority and win
the victory by slaying his enemies, but by giving his life.
3. In what ways is Herod similar to the Pharaoh of the Exodus
story (see Exodus 1): [9.4]
Much like Pharaoh, Herod was obsessed with maintaining his own
power and was worried that someone else might take it. Just as
Pharaoh commanded that all newborn Israelite males be drowned
in the Nile River, so Herod commanded that all boys in Bethlehem
under two years of age be slaughtered. Neither Pharaoh nor Herod
had any true regard for the God of Israel.
4. What were the positives and negatives of Roman rule? [9.4]
Roman rule brought a measure of stability and peace to the world.
The Romans built roads and highways, which made travel much
safer and more efficient. However, with Roman rule came a measure
of tyranny. Caesar was considered the true king of kings and lord
of lords. It was dangerous to cross him or show any resistance to
Roman rule. Rome showed no mercy to rebels, using crucifixion as a
means of breaking the spirit of a people group.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Why were the temple and the land so important to Jews? [9.5]
The temple in Jerusalem was the center of all religious and political
life. It was the place where God dwelled among the people. Sacrifices,
worship, and prayer in the temple were a few ways that Jews resisted
the influence of hellenization. God had promised Abraham that he
would give the land of Canaan to the Jewish people. But after the
return from exile, the land was occupied by many foreign peoples
who had their own gods and religious practices. Jews looked forward
to the day when the land would be cleansed of foreign influence and
pagan idolatry.
2. Why were the Torah and racial identity so important to Jews?
[9.5]
Obedience to the Torah was seen as part of the qualification for
receiving God's blessing and staying on the land. Failing to be faithful
to God's instructions in the Torah would result in judgment and
exile. Jewish people greatly valued their identity. They saw themselves
as the true inheritors of God's promises to Abraham. Two distinctive
practices—circumcision and refusing to intermarry with people from
other nations—became especially important markers of identity
during the second temple period.
3. What potential dangers might be associated with these Jewish
symbols: temple, land, Torah, and racial identity? [9.5]
Possible answer: None of these symbols are inherently problematic,
however each of them has a potential danger. For example, there was
a danger that Jews might come to rely on the temple and the rituals
associated with it as a way of guaranteeing God's favor even when
they were living in sin. Strict observance of the Torah could become
a barometer for measuring how good a Jew someone was and for
looking down on others. The promise of land and the importance of
racial identity might lead people to a form of nationalism or racism
that ignored God's desire to bless the nations and saw other people
groups as inferior.
Note: The potential dangers associated with these symbols are not
unique to the Jewish community in the second temple period. Every
culture has its own set of symbols that may have similar pitfalls. You
may wish to discuss with your students what symbols in your own
culture could potentially lead to similar problems if misunderstood.
4. What was the purpose of the synagogue? [9.6]
The synagogue was a meeting place where Jews gathered to study the
Torah, pray, and discuss issues relevant to Jewish life. The synagogue
was not a replacement for the temple, but a supplement to it. There
were numerous synagogues both in Judea and in the diaspora.
5. Who were the Samaritans and why was there such animosity between
them and the Jews? [9.6]
The Samaritans were most likely the descendants of Israelites from the northern
kingdom who had intermarried with the Assyrians. The Samaritans may
have been among those who were first excluded from, and then opposed, the
rebuilding of the second temple in Jerusalem. They also built their own temple
in opposition to the temple in Jerusalem. In one of the most trying times in
Jewish history, the Samaritans turned their backs on the Jews. For these reasons,
the Jews hated Samaritans. The Jews treated them with disdain because of their
mixed background, regarding them as half-breeds.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Compare and contrast the Pharisees with the Sadducees. [9.7]
The Pharisees were the most popular Jewish group in Jesus' day. They
believed that Jews must resist hellenization by strict obedience to the
commands of the Torah. In addition, they also observed numerous
oral laws and traditions. The Sadducees were from the upper classes
and generally cooperated with the ruling powers. They held only
to the Torah and did not believe in the resurrection. In the New
Testament, both the Pharisees and the Sadducees frequently appear
as adversaries of Jesus.
2. Compare and contrast the Essenes with the Zealots. [9.7]
Both the Essenes and the Zealots were fiercely opposed to
hellenization and compromising Jews. The Essenes rejected the
Jerusalem temple and its leadership by withdrawing to desert
communities to devote themselves to greater discipline. They
believed that they alone were the true Israel. The Zealots took
matters into their own hands; they were willing to resort to violence
in order to achieve their ends, including revolting against Rome and
attacking compromising Jews.
3. What role did the books of the Apocrypha (such as 1-2
Maccabees, Susanna, and Tobit) play in the life of Jews? [9.8]
While these books were not considered direct revelation from God
on a par with the Torah, the Writings, and the Prophets (the three
main divisions of the Jewish Scriptures), they did serve to encourage
the Jews to remember their history and remain faithful to Yahweh
in whatever situation they happened to be in. They told stories of
faithful Jews who rejected idolatry and refused to compromise.
4. What were some beliefs that arose or became prominent
during the second temple period? [9.8]
During the second temple period, several ideas gained considerable
traction in Jewish communities:
• Doctrines about angels and demons were extensively developed
from extrabiblical sources.
• Monotheism, though long held by Jews, became an essential
conviction since the worship of other gods had previously led Israel
into exile.
• Many Jews found hope in the idea of a resurrection of God's
people, which would occur when God returned to restore Israel.
5. How might stories encourage Christians today to remain faithful to God?
[9.8]
Possible answers: As in the second temple period, stories today can play a major
role in encouraging Christians by providing relatable examples of faithfulness,
showcasing a biblical worldview implicitly or explicitly, providing a starting
point for communicating biblical truth to nonbelievers, and pointing us back to
the ultimate story in the Bible.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. List five of the main hopes of the Jewish people during the
second temple period. [9.9]
The Jews hoped for salvation from their enemies, the establishment
of a state of shalom, the arrival of God's kingdom and rule over
Israel and the world, the forgiveness of sins, and a messiah who
would lead Israel.
2. What kind of messiah did the Jews expect? How does that
expectation compare with who Jesus was? [9.9]
Not all Jews hoped for a messiah, but those who did hope did not
always agree about what the messiah would be like. The general
expectation seems to have been for a messiah who would overthrow
the Romans in battle and set up an earthly kingdom. By contrast,
Jesus did not come as a military ruler to overthrow the Romans.
He preached a message of forgiveness and taught people to love
their enemies. Rather than humiliating Rome, Jesus was humiliated
on the cross. This kind of messiah would have been profoundly
disappointing for those who were expecting Jesus to bring Rome to
its knees and restore Israel to a place of prominence.
3. What similarities do you see between modern times and the
time of the second temple period? [9.10]
Possible answer: The second temple period was a time of confusion,
persecution, violence, expectation, and disappointment for the
Jewish people. The same problems afflict many people in the
world today. Some feel abandoned. Some are persecuted for their
race or religion; others experience firsthand the effects of war and
oppression. Yet, many still hold out hope for some kind of better
world. Many long for peace, and many have dedicated their lives
to trying to solve the world's problems. However, as in the second
temple period, people are divided about what exactly the problems
are and how we should respond to them.
4. What are some of the messiah-like figures that people today
look to in order to fix what is wrong with the world? Can any
of these ultimately solve the world's problems? Explain. [9.10]
Some possible figures include: presidents or political leaders,
media influencers, or spiritual or religious leaders. From a biblical
perspective, the heart of the world's problem is sin. Since every
person and organization (each of which is made up of people) is
broken and compromised by sin, they cannot ultimately fix what is
wrong with the world. What we need is a true Messiah who can take
care of the sin problem because he is not compromised by sin. This
does not mean that leaders, organizations, or causes seeking to make
the world a better place are all bad or that Christians should never
support any of them. We simply cannot hope in them to ultimately
fix what is wrong with the world.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
• Video 1:
What Happened between the Testaments?
1. How can we know what happened between the Old and New
Testaments?
A number of books of Jewish literature were written during that
time period. Some were history books about that time period. These
books can tell us about what happened and how people thought.
2. What major movement occurred among the Jewish leadership
during the intertestamental period and how did the post-revolt
leadership react to it?
There was a movement to hellenize. The leaders encouraged Jews
to become Greeks, to stop the practice of circumcision, and to eat
unclean foods. The post-revolt leadership forced every Jewish man to
get circumcised or leave the land.
3. What similar temptations do modern Christians face and how
can they be overcome?
Christians can be tempted to forget God's Word and to
compromise. Christians can overcome these temptations by never
turning our eyes away from God, by remembering his words, and
always remembering how easy it is for us to compromise on our
convictions.
4. What is one thing that can help Christians think well and live
well under pressure?
Instead of only trying to have all the right answers to hard questions,
Christians could share God's affections and aversions. They could
desire what God desires and despise what he despises. Christians
should want to be more like God.
Video 2:
Why Do Christians Believe Contradicting Views?
1. What does it mean to hold contradictory views?
To hold contradictory views means to believe two or more things
that cannot be true at the same time.
2. Why do many people, including Christians, hold
contradictory views?
People are being taught what to think, not how to think. Christians
don't know how to examine contradictory claims, so they combine
them. They believe what they feel is true instead of what is
objectively true.

3. What is the solution for helping Christians to think well and reject
contradictory views?
Christians need to learn and teach the basics laws of logic so people can
recognize when they are holding two views that contradict.
Video 3
How Should Christians Interact with Culture?
1. What is a common but poorly constructed definition of "culture"?
For Christians, the word culture often becomes a synonym for all the bad stuff
out in the world, what's happening out "in culture."
2. What are two poor responses to culture?
Christians can't just pull themselves out of culture. God did not create human
beings to stay away from the world. We also can't just go along with culture or
agree with everything that culture says it right.
3. What does the freedom God gave to humans allow for?
Because humans are free to make choices, that includes being free to move
culture away from God and choose unrighteousness.
4. What does God call Christians to do?
God calls Christians to live out of Scripture. This is how Christians can make
sense of our cultural moment. We are called to understand this moment in light
of the whole biblical story, not the other way around.
Video 4
Can We Maintain Hope in a Broken Culture?
1. How should we make sense of the moment we live in?
Instead of using the moment to build an idea of what the overarching story is,
we should use the Bible's grand story to help us understand the moment we are
living in.
2. What is Christian hope, and how can Christians maintain a sense of hope
in this culture?
Christian hope is not a feeling, it's security in the reality that Christ is risen from
the dead. If we Christians can keep the story and the moment straight, then we
can live with hope in this cultural moment.
3. How can Christian hope be a bridge to people who are not Christians?
Christian hope can be attractive to people who are not Christians because they
will see the hope Christians have and be curious about it. Christians should be
ready to give an answer for the hope that we have.
Video 5
How Can the Church Encourage Healthy Grieving?
1. How is the book of Psalms helpful for grieving?
The book of Psalms is a manual for lament and grieving in a healthy way, a great
prayer guide, and also a songbook.
2. What are two ways Christians can lament?
Christians can lament individually and corporately. Individuals can pour out
their hurts and fears out to God in prayer. Corporate lament looks like bearing
each other's burdens and being a safe place for people who are lamenting.
3. How can the church facilitate healthy grieving and lament?
The church can facilitate healthy grieving by not turning people away, trying to
fix them, or patching up pain with a Bible verse. Instead, the church can walk
alongside hurting people, letting God help and heal them in his own timing.

Activity
GUESS WHO I AM
OBJECTIVE:
Students will identify biblical characters from the
second temple period based on nonverbal or verbal
clues.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: TR 9.8A, box, timer, student
textbook, and paper
• Time required: 25-30 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Divide the class into two teams. Have the box with
slips of paper from TR 9.8A Guess Who I Am ready.
Pass the box around and have each student pull one
name from the box.
2. Allow students three minutes to look up their
respective characters in the student textbook.
3. Have one student come to the front of the classroom
and pantomime his or her character for 20 seconds
while other students on his or her team try to guess
who it is. If the team guesses correctly, it receives
three points.
4. If no one guesses correctly after 20 seconds, give the
student 30 seconds to speak "in character" (but he
or she cannot say the name of the character or the
team gets no points) while the team guesses. For
example, a student playing Antiochus IV might say,
" like to call myself Epiphanes, because I'm so great.
But some people call me Epimanes because they
think I'm crazy! I think I'll execute them." If the team
guesses correctly, it will receive one point. If no one
guesses correctly, give the other team a chance to
guess. If they answer correctly, they get one point. If
neither team guess correctly, no points will be given.
Inform students who the correct character was.
5. Have teams alternate taking turns to pantomime
until all characters have been acted out. Add up the
points and announce the winning team.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What are the empires represented in Daniel's vision?
Babylonian Empire, Medo-Persian Empire, Greek Empire, Roman
Empire
2. Give examples of the challenges Jews returning from exile faced.
The Jewish people faced challenges rebuilding the temple and the walls
of Jerusalem. Though they had the support of the king of Persia, some
neighboring groups of people in the Promised Land tried to thwart the
building efforts. Nehemiah and Ezra struggled to overcome the Jewish
peoples indifference and their lapses back into the sins that had sent
them into exile in the first place. Also, around the same time there was
also a plot in Persia against the Jews; that plot was thwarted by Queen
Esther.
3. Describe the rise of Greek culture during the second temple
period.
Alexander the Great conquered most of the Mediterranean world,
creating a huge empire where he promoted Greek culture. The
movement to universalize Greek culture in language, arts, ideas,
religion, and government was called bellenization. After Alexander died,
the process of hellenization lasted for almost 1,000 years. For a while,
the Jewish people were allowed relative freedom to live and worship as
they pleased. The Greek version of the Old Testament, the Septuagint,
was translated then. The Jewish people divided over whether to accept
or reject hellenization.
4. Explain the factors that led to the Maccabean Revolt.
The pressure to hellenize came to a high point under Seleucid king
Antiochus IV, who oppressed the Jews and forced them to participate
in pagan worship. He was so arrogant he gave himself the name
Epiphanes, which means God manifest. He outlawed many aspects of
Jewish culture and worship. Finally, an old priest named Mattathias
refused to participate in pagan sacrifices. He and his sons fled to
the wilderness and started a military resistance. They succeeded in
cleansing the temple and winning Jewish independence.
5. How did the Maccabee family get their name and what were they
like as rulers?
The old priest Mattathias' third son, Judah, earned the nickname
Maccabee, which means the hammer. Judah was the leader of the
Maccabean revolt who rode into Jerusalem with his army and cleansed
the temple after defeating Israel's enemies. The Maccabean leaders
were seen as Moses- or messiah-like figures. After Judah's death, the
Maccabee family and their descendants, the Hasmoneans, became
tyrants. Some of them were almost as bad as Antiochus IV.

6. Explain how the rise of the Roman Empire set the stage for the birth, life,
and death of Christ.
Rome installed a client king, Herod the Great, over Judea. Herod's fear of
challenges to his leadership prompted some of the events surrounding Jesus'
birth. Judea also became a hotbed of rebellion at this time, and the Jewish people
were looking for a messiah. A bit later Rome installed prefects to rule Judea,
including a particularly bad one named Pontius Pilate. The Romans also built
lots of roads and highways, which made traveling and spreading news much
easier. Also, they promoted emperor worship of Caesar and the use of crucifixion
to punish rebels who rose to prominence.
7. What were the four key Jewish symbols during the second temple period?
The four key symbols were the temple, land, Torah, and racial identity.
8. Explain the role of synagogues post-exile.
For the diaspora Jews who did not return to Judea after the exile, synagogues
became an incredibly important place for Torah study, prayer, and discussing
issues relevant to the Jewish community.
9. How did Jews and Samaritans relate to one another?
The Samaritans were quite likely related to Jews from the northern kingdom
who had been left in Samaria and intermarried with Assyrians after the conquest
of the northern kingdom. When the Jews returned from exile, they refused to
let the Samaritans help rebuild the temple, so the Samaritans built their own
on Mount Gerizim. The Jews looked down on them with disdain and believed
the Samaritans were outside of God's true people. The Jews even had racial
prejudices toward Samaritans because of the intermarriage. The Samaritans also
hated the Jews. Some Samaritans may have tried to hinder the rebuilding of the
temple by writing letters to government officials accusing the Jews of treachery.
The Samaritans disowned the Jews at one point and dedicated their Mount
Gerizim temple to Zeus.
10. Describe the Pharisees and Sadducees.
The Pharisees were the largest group and were most popular among the common
people. They resisted hellenization and insisted on strict observance of the
Torah, obedience to the law, and respect for oral traditions. They believed that
God would not come to rescue his people unless they were living purely. The
Sadducees were from the upper classes and gladly cooperated with the ruling
powers. They made up the majority of the Sanhedrin and only upheld the Torah
rather than the oral traditions. They also opposed Jesus and did not believe in
the resurrection.
11. Describe the Essenes and the Zealots.
The Essenes were an extreme group that believed the jerusalem temple was too
corrupt, that the priests were illegitimate, and that the people were failing to
obey God. They withdrew from the culture and formed desert communities.
They likely produced the Dead Sea Scrolls that were found at their Qumran community.
The Zealots were an extreme group of political revolutionaries who wanted to overthrow
the Romans. They were willing to die for their convictions and often had skirmishes with
the Romans.
12. Give examples of Jewish literature from the second temple period.
Literature from this period included 1-2 Maccabees, Judith, Tobit, Bel and the Dragon,
and Wisdom of Solomon. Other literature included works by Philo and Josephus.
13. What were the five hopes of the Jewish people during the second temple period?
The first hope was salvation from their enemies. The Jews hoped God would save them
from their Roman oppressors. The second hope was the establishment of a state of shalom.
The Jews hoped for a state of peace and freedom to go about their business and worship
God without fear of oppression. The third hope was the arrival of God's kingdom and
rule over Israel and the world. The Jews hoped for a true king from the line of David who
would rule over Israel and all nations with justice and faithfulness. The fourth hope was for
the forgiveness of sins. Israel had a sin problem, and the people believed that when God
arrived, he would deal with their sin and cleanse them from wickedness. The fifth hope was
for a messiah who would lead Israel. Many hoped he would be a military leader who would
crush Rome.
