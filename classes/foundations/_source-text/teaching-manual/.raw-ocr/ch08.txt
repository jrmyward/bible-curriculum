OVERVIEW
God chose Abraham to establish a nation
through which the whole world would be
blessed. God promised Abraham and Sarah a
son and that the Offspring who would bruise
the head of the serpent would come from
their family. The covenant blessing was passed
from Abraham to Isaac, and then from Isaac
to Jacob. Jacob had 12 children, through
whom God grew the nation of Israel. God
continued to show his faithfulness during
the Israelites enslavement in Egypt and their
deliverance through Moses.
God gave Israel laws and commandments,
not as a way for the people to earn his favor
but as a way to respond to his grace. God
descended to speak to his people in the
tabernacle, where the priests represented the
people before him. Through the law, God
wanted to shape a kingdom of priests—a
people who relied on him, practiced justice
and mercy, and so demonstrated to the world
that Yahweh is the true God. God warned
Israel that if they did evil like the surrounding
nations did, they would face great judgment.
God sent prophets, who sometimes predicted
the future but mostly highlighted God's
covenant promises and rebuked evil.
Israel demanded a king, so God gave
them Saul and later replaced him with David.
David's son Solomon replaced the tabernacle
with a permanent temple. After Solomon's
death, Israel split into the northern kingdom
(Israel) and southern kingdom (Judah).
The kings of Israel were wicked, and Israel
was exiled in 722 Bc. Judah had good and
evil kings, but eventually went into exile
between 587 and 586 Bc. The challenge of
exile was to remain faithful to God despite
intense pressure to conform to pagan culture.
The stories of Daniel and others provide a
model of diplomatic, nonviolent, and patient
faithfulness to God as well as refusal to
compromise.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What was God's goal in blessing Abraham and his family?
[8.1]
God's blessing was an act of grace to Abraham, but it was not only
for Abraham. God blessed the family of Abraham so they could be a
blessing to all nations and peoples. Abraham's family was to bless the
world by witnessing to God's goodness and faithfulness. Ultimately,
the Offspring that would bruise the head of the serpent and bring
salvation to the world would come from Abraham's descendants.
2. What did Joseph mean when he said, "You meant evil against
me, but God meant it for good"? What does this say about
how God accomplishes his purposes? [8.1]
Joseph's brothers did evil and sold him into slavery. In spite of this
hardship, Joseph saw how God accomplished his purposes and used
Joseph to keep many people alive during the years of famine. God's
purposes cannot ultimately be thwarted by our evil intentions. Our
actions have real consequences, but God will use us anyway. Even
when we sin, God remains faithful and committed to his promises.
3. What does the name I AM mean? In what way would this name
have been significant for Moses and the Israelites? [8.2]
The name I AM signifies God's eternal and timeless nature. God has
always been, is, and will always be. This name would have reminded
Moses and the Israelites to take courage in the fact that God had
never abandoned them, even in slavery. They knew he heard their
cries of distress and was able to deliver them.
4. How is the covenant God made with the people of Israel
similar to the covenant God made with Abraham? [8.2]
In both covenants, God revealed himself as loving and faithful. In
the Mosaic Covenant, He promised to be present with Israel and
to bless them, just as he promised Abraham. He also called Israel
to be a kingdom of priests. Just as with Abraham, God told Israel
that he would bless the whole world through them. He gave them
laws to follow and clearly instructed them in his ways. The Israelites
promised to be faithful to God. Abraham's obedience demonstrated
his faith. Likewise, Israel's obedience was to be a response to God's
grace, rather than a way of earning his favor.
5. Based on what you learned about Moses in the story of
Israel and the exodus, does God only use perfect people to
accomplish his purposes? Explain. [8.2]
Though he grew up in Pharaoh's household, Moses identified
with his people. In revenge for the beating of an Israelite, Moses
murdered the guilty Egyptian and was forced to flee to Midian.
Moses spent years in hiding there before God called him to speak
on God's behalf in front of Pharaoh and to lead the Israelites out
FOUNDATIONS S
of slavery. Moses was a flawed person who did some very wrong things, yet God still
redeemed the situation and used Moses' life for good and for his glory.
6. What was the purpose of the laws and commandments God gave? [8.2]
The Ten Commandments and many other instructions outlined the moral and
ceremonial laws that God wanted Israel to follow. Keeping the laws and commandments
was not a way for the Israelites to earn God's favor. Rather, obedience to the law
was to be a response to God's grace to Israel. Obedience to the law was also a way of
representing God to the nations so that they could trust in him as well.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What was the purpose of the tabernacle? [8.3]
The tabernacle was a sacred meeting place where God's presence
dwelt among the Israelites. The imagery of the tabernacle reminded
the Israelites of the garden of Eden, where God and humans had
perfect fellowship. The tabernacle was a tangible sign of God's desire
to be personally present with his people in spite of their sin.
2. What was the purpose of the laws, sacrifices, and festivals in
the Old Testament? [8.3]
In order for people to dwell in the presence of a holy God, the
problem of sin had to be dealt with. The laws, sacrifices, and festivals
helped the Israelites learn about how to deal with sin and follow
God's ways. The laws helped shape the Israelites into a people who
could live in God's presence and be a light to the nations. The
sacrifices helped Israel express thanksgiving to God and demonstrate
repentance for sin. The festivals helped Israel remember all that God
had done for them and provided physical reminders of God's grace
and forgiveness.
3. Why are most of these laws, sacrifices, and festivals no longer
observed by Christians today? [8.3]
Possible answer: Because of Jesus' once-and-for-all sacrifice on the
cross, the sacrificial system is no longer necessary and feast days are
no longer obligatory. With the coming of Jesus, the way to God's
presence has been opened, so the ceremonial laws associated with
the tabernacle have passed out of necessity. All of these things have
been fulfilled in Christ. Most Christians maintain that the moral
law of the Old Testament remains in force though not the specific
punishments since it is about reflecting God's unchanging character.
4. Why did God command the Israelites to drive out the
Canaanites? [8.4]
God commanded the Israelites to drive out the Canaanites because
of their wickedness. In addition to practicing idolatry, the Canaanite
nations abused women, sacrificed children, and practiced sex slavery.
God knew that if these nations were not brought to account, the
Israelites would fall into the same practices. The conquest of Canaan
was not an arbitrary punishment. In fact, God brought similar
punishments on the Israelites when they fell into wickedness. God
also had patiently given the Canaanites around 400 years to repent
of their sin.
5. What is significant about the story of Ruth? [8.4]
Ruth was a Moabite (a nation known to be Israel's enemy), but she came to trust
in the Lord. The story of Ruth is significant because it shows that God's salvation
is open to the nations and that women are also included and valued in God's
kingdom. Ruth was the ancestor of King David, from whose lineage came the
Messiah, Jesus. Ruth is included in the genealogy of Jesus in Matthew 1.
6. Why did Israel keep repeating their destructive pattern during the time of
the judges? Do we have similar tendencies today? [8.4]
Israel went through a cycle of idolatry and sin, oppression by other nations,
desperation for God, deliverance, and then back to idolatry and sin. Perhaps
the Israelites worshipped false gods because they seemed easier to manipulate,
because the Israelites wanted to shake off the constraints of God's moral law,
or because they wanted to be like everyone else. It can be easy for us to do
something similar today, blending worldly philosophies or the beliefs of other
religions into our own. We might do this because it gives us a sense of control or
comfort, because it allows us to make our beliefs more sin-friendly, or perhaps
because we were deceived or pressured to adopt a false belief or practice.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What did the first three kings of Israel have in common? What
choices created different endings to their personal stories? [8.5]
Saul, David, and Solomon all began ruling with humility and
seemed to desire to follow God. They also all fell into sin. Saul
usurped Samuel's duties, made foolish vows, and stopped short
of fully obeying God's commands. Though Saul was sorry about
the consequences of sin, he was not repentant, so God took away
the kingdom from his family. David took many wives, committed
adultery and murder, and was a poor parent. Because he repented,
God was merciful to David, though he allowed David to suffer some
consequences for his actions. Solomon took hundreds of wives, fell
into idolatry, and abused his power. Because of this, the kingdom was
divided after his death.
2. What was the covenant God made with David? How does it
compare to the covenant God made with Abraham and the
Israelites? [8.5]
God promised to make David's name great and give him an eternal
dynasty, as well as to give the people of Israel rest from their enemies.
David was to lead Israel in faithfulness to God. David's eternal
dynasty also looks forward to the Messiah who would be David's
descendant. The Davidic covenant is similar to the Abrahamic and
Mosaic covenants in that God made promises to bless people so they
might bless the whole world.
3. Describe the history of the northern kingdom. [8.6]
The northern kingdom included ten tribes of Israel that split off to
follow King Jeroboam. Jeroboam became the model for all wicked
kings; every king who came after him followed in his path. These
wicked kings led Israel into idolatry and away from the worship
of Yahweh. Most notorious were King Ahab and his wife, Queen
Jezebel, who persecuted God's prophets and caused Israel to worship
the false god Baal.
4. What role did Elijah and Elisha play in the history of the
northern kingdom? [8.6]
Throughout the history of the northern kingdom, God sent prophets
like Elijah and Elisha to call Israel to repentance and covenant
faithfulness. However, because of Israel's persistent sin, God
eventually punished the nation by allowing them be conquered by
the Assyrians and taken into exile.
5. Why do you think Elijah challenged the prophets of Baal to a
contest, and was this effective? [8.6]
Elijah challenged the prophets of Baal to a public test as a witness
to Israel about who was the one true God. Elijah was confident that
FOUNDATIONS S
Baal couldn't respond, as he was a false god. While the false prophets engaged in extreme
acts, including self-harm, to try to gain Baal's attention and favor, Elijah simply prayed and
God responded. This proved who the true God was and moved the people of Israel to take
action, at least temporarily, against idol worship.
6. How did the healing of Naaman from leprosy relate to Israel's mission? [8.6]
Naaman was a foreigner and a military commander who sought Elisha in desperation to
heal his medical condition. When God healed Naaman miraculously, Naaman recognized
Yahweh as the one true God. In this moment Israel, represented by Elisha, was showing
God's goodness to the nations outside Israel so that they could choose to trust in him, too.
7. Why did God send Israel into exile? [8.6]
The Israelites' persistence in sin kept them from achieving their mission. They had become
like the pagan nations instead of being a light to the nations. God could not let this go on
forever, so he disciplined the Israelites for their unfaithfulness to the covenant. At the same
time, God did not want Israel to suffer forever. God's promises included judgment but also
restoration when his people repented. In fact, God accomplished the redemption of his
people and their return to the land—a remarkable feat for a captive people to reestablish
their homeland and semi-independent rule.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Describe the history of the southern kingdom. [8.7]
When the kingdom was divided at the beginning of Rehoboam's
reign, two tribes remained loyal to David's house-Judah and
Benjamin. The southern kingdom had many wicked kings who
followed the way of Jeroboam, but they also had several kings who
followed the way of David and led Judah in faithfulness to God.
Judah's history was one of revival followed by lapses into sin. On
the whole, the southern kingdom may have been more faithful in
following the Lord than its northern neighbors. Eventually, Judah
also fell deeply into sin and was exiled to Babylon.
2. How were Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, and Josiah different from
the kings of the northern kingdom? [8.7]
The kings of the northern kingdom were evil without exception.
They usually tried to overcome crises through their own strength
and cleverness. They consistently participated in idolatry
and adopted the practices of the pagan nations. Jehoshaphat,
Hezekiah, and Josiah were all righteous kings who followed in the
ways of David and were obedient to God. All three purged the
land of idolatry and tried to get people to follow the Lord and
be faithful to him. In addition, all three men called on God in
moments of crisis.
3. How did Daniel and his friends remain faithful to God in
exile? [8.8]
Daniel and his friends were often pressured to conform to
Babylonian culture. Daniel and his friends refused to defile
themselves with certain Babylonian foods. Shadrach, Meshach,
and Abednego chose to be thrown into a furnace rather than
worship a false god. Daniel was condemned for praying to
Yahweh, slandered, and thrown into a lion's den. Each time,
these men refused to compromise even when they knew it might
cost them their lives. They chose to trust God and accept his will
regardless of what happened. They actively resisted the pressure
to cave to Babylonian idolatry, but they did so in a way that was
tactful, diplomatic, nonviolent, and patient.
4. How do you think Christians today should live in a non-
Christian culture? [8.8]
Answers will vary, In every age, Christians must trust God during
trials and resist pressure to conform to worldly culture. The
stories of Daniel and his friends reveal a strategy of resistance
characterized by tact, nonviolence, and patience. Christians are
not called to use violence to achieve God's purposes; nor are we
to defend the faith in an arrogant, obnoxious manner. Rather, our
stance must be peaceful, uncompromising, patient, and loving.
Even as we actively resist evil, we must trust our well-being to God and recognize
that he is the one who will ultimately set things right.
5. What do Daniel's visions about the future reveal? [8.8]
Daniel's strange visions of the future told of four rulers and kingdoms that would
dominate the world, the last of which would be extremely oppressive to God's
people. The visions also reveal that God is on the throne, the ultimate ruler of the
universe, and that "one like a son of man" would come and be given an everlasting
kingdom and authority over all nations. This vision looks forward to the Messiah,
Jesus, and encouraged Daniel to have hope in God. Though persecution would
come, God would have the final say.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is the primary function of a biblical prophet? [8.9]
A prophet is a person authorized by God to speak on his behalf.
Prophets sometimes predicted future events, but their primary job was
reminding Israel of God's promises, calling the Israelites to covenant
faithfulness, and urging them to repent when they fell into sin.
2. What things did the prophets condemn Israel for? How might
the words of the prophets be relevant in our own time? [8.9]
The prophets condemned Israel for the worship of idols and
injustice, primarily oppression of the poor, sexual immorality,
slaughter of innocent children, and unjustified violence. Many such
sins persist today. Unjust economic practices keep many stuck in
poverty. Thousands of unborn babies are aborted every year. Though
many do not worship physical idols today, people often make gods
out of money or power. The prophets' words are still relevant now
because they warn about coming judgment for those who persist in
evil. They also tell us about God's character, love for the world, and
grace if we turn from sin.
3. What are some of the promises the prophets made to Israel?
How might Christians today be encouraged by them? [8.9]
The prophets promised the day of the Lord-God's judgment on
the wicked, the restoration of Judah and Israel, the coming of the
Messiah, a new covenant, new hearts for God's people, and the
forgiveness of sin. Many of these promises have already been fulfilled
through Jesus, and all Christians benefit from their fulfillment.
God's fulfillment of his promises in the past gives us confidence that
he will fulfill his promises for the future, returning to banish evil and
rule over all creation.
4. How would you summarize the storyline of the Old Testament

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Explain God's covenant with Abraham. Who were the next two
descendants who received the covenant blessing?
God made a covenant with Abraham, promising to make him a great
family and nation, and to bless Abraham's family, who would then be a
blessing to the whole world. Abraham's next two descendants were Isaac
and Jacob. From Abraham would come the Offspring who would bruise
the head of the serpent.
2. Relate the Passover to the exodus from Egypt.
The first Passover took place on the night the firstborn of Egypt were
struck dead. All Israelite families that sacrificed a lamb and put its
blood on their doorposts were passed over by the angel of death. God
instituted the yearly celebration of Passover to commemorate this event
and the deliverance of the Israelites from Egypt.
3. Explain the purpose of the tabernacle.
The tabernacle was the place where God would come down to interact
with humans. The tabernacle, along with the rituals and sacrifices that
were done in it, was God's way of reconnecting with sinful people.
4. Differentiate between the two categories of Old Testament laws,
the two types of sacrifices, and the three major festivals.
God gave the Israelites ceremonial and moral laws:
• Ceremonial laws- dietary restrictions, sacrifices, washing before
entering the tabernacle so that they could live in God's presence
• Moral laws-the Ten Commandments, instructions about sexual
integrity, justice, restitution, to help Israel reflect God's character
Leviticus details five kinds of sacrifices divided into two categories:
• Thanksgiving sacrifices-grain offerings and peace offerings-
celebrated God's goodness and gratefully acknowledged his blessings
• Sacrifices for sin— burnt offerings, sin offerings, guilt offerings—-were
a way of confessing and acknowledging sin and receiving forgiveness
The people of Israel were to celebrate three major feasts or days of
rememberance:
• Feast of Tabernacles a remembrance of God's provision for the
Israelites while they lived in tents after leaving Egypt
• Day of Atonement—an annual holy day when all the sins of Israel
were confessed and atoned
• Passover—an annual Jewish celebration to commemorate God
rescuing Israel from slavery in Egypt
5. Describe the pattern Israel fell into during the period of the
judges.
First Israel falls into idolatry and sin, so God allows them to be
oppressed by another nation. Then Israel cries out to the Lord, so he
raises up a judge to deliver them. Next, Israel turns back to God for a
time. Eventually, they fall back into idolatry and the cycle repeats.

6. Explain why God told the Israelites to remove the Canaanites from the Promised
Land.
The Canaanites did incredibly evil things and did not repent, so they fell under God's
judgment. Another reason God ordered the Israelites to drive them out was because
the Israelites might intermarry with them and learn pagan practices. In fact, that is
exactly what happened after the Israelites failed to obey God fully.
7. Explain the purpose of the temple.
The temple was a permanent place to worship God and fulfill the required rituals of
the law. It was a place for Israel and all nations to pray to God, who came to dwell
with people, although the temple itself could never contain him.
8. Give a brief description of the first three kings of Israel.
At first, Saul was humble and reluctant to lead, but he became proud. God then
rejected him and his descendants from leading Israel. After Saul's death, God made
David king. David ruled Israel with righteousness but did some wicked things. He
repented and cried out to God for mercy, and God forgave him. Solomon, David's
son, was the wisest man who ever lived. He ruled Israel well but became heavy-handed
and worshipped idols after he married pagan wives.
9. Describe the consequences of sin in the northern kingdom and how that sin
interfered with Israel's mission.
The kings of Israel were wicked without exception and actively promoted false gods. This
led them to participate in evil like child sacrifice and sexual immorality, representing
God poorly among the nations. So, God sent them into exile. Israel was to show the
world what Yahweh is like and how to be in relationship with him, but instead they
worshipped idols and acted like the nations around them.
10. Distinguish Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, and Josiah from the other kings of Judah
and Israel by indicating how they served the Lord.
The northern kingdom had many wicked kings who brought in idol worship. Many
kings of Judah were also evil, but Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, and Josiah were righteous
kings. They tried to stop idol worship and turn people toward God. They cried out to
God in times of trouble.
11. Give examples of how Daniel, Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego modeled
faithfulness to God in exile.
Daniel, Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego served in the Babylonian government and
faced intense pressure to conform to pagan culture. Part of their training included
daily food and wine, which Israelites were forbidden to eat, but Daniel and his friends
were faithful to God, and he blessed them with great knowledge.
12. What is a prophet, and what types of messages were they entrusted to deliver?
A prophet is a person authorized to speak on God's behalf. In Scripture, some prophets
spoke messages with predictions for the future and highlighted God's covenant promises.
Whenever God's people became entangled in sin, the prophets challenged them to turn
from evil and worship God, who would forgive them.
