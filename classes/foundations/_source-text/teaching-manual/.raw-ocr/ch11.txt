OVERVIEW
After Jesus died on the cross, rose from
the dead, and ascended into heaven, his
followers had to come to terms with all
that had happened and what it meant.
At Pentecost, the Holy Spirit empowered
believers to fulfill the Great Commission,
or assignment, of proclaiming the good
news to all. While waiting for Jesus to
return and fully institute the kingdom of
God on Earth, the church has grown. God
inspired the writers of the books of the New
Testament to help believers know how to
live out their faith.
The church experienced persecution from
religious and political leaders, and some
were martyred. But in AD 313, Christianity
gained legal status. The good news spread
throughout Europe. Differences led to
major splits: Roman Catholic and Orthodox
churches split in 1054, and Luther set off the
Protestant Reformation in 1517. Religious
revival and missionary work followed. Now
Christianity is the largest religion on Earth.
The message the church proclaims
has implication for our relationships with
God, ourselves, others, and creation.
Through faith in Jesus, we receive
forgiveness and justification. As we live in
obedience, imitating Jesus, we go through
transformation and sanctification. The work
of the Holy Spirit in our lives is evident
also in our relationships with others. We are
called to love and reconciliation. We must
also steward this planet for the glory of God
and the good of all.
God's kingdom is already here but it is
not fully realized. Waiting is not easy, but
it is easier when we are part of a Christian
community. The church can help us proclaim
the gospel and live our faith in the world.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What did Jesus accomplish while he was on Earth? How did he
do this? [11.1]
When Jesus was on Earth, he accomplished our redemption, the
main event of the Bible's grand story. He did this by proclaiming
God's kingdom, dying on the cross for the forgiveness of sins,
initiating a new covenant with his blood, rising again from the dead,
and then ascending to heaven.
2. What does it mean that the kingdom of God is already here,
but not yet? [11.1]
The kingdom of God has already arrived in Jesus. Jesus has
been enthroned as the rightful Ruler of the universe. However,
the kingdom of God is not yet fully realized. Evil still plagues
the world and not everyone acknowledges Jesus as Lord. When
Jesus comes again, the kingdom of God will be fully realized,
sin will be banished, all things will be made new, and all people
will acknowledge that Jesus is Lord. We now live between the
Incarnation and the second coming of Jesus. Because of Jesus, the
world has been changed forever, but it is not yet where it needs to
be. We still await the final consummation of all things.
3. What is the church? What is the Great Commission? What
is the relationship between the church and the Great
Commission? [11.2]
The church consists of the followers of Christ in all places and
times. The Great Commission is Jesus' command to the apostles to
make disciples, baptize, and instruct people about all he taught. The
apostles began the work, and the church continues to carry it out.
The church is to be a faithful witness to the transforming power of
the gospel here and now.
Note: The term church is capitalized when referring to a specific
congregation or denomination and not capitalized when referring to
the body of Christ, all believers, the historical church, or the early
church.
4. What was Pentecost? What happened on that day that
mattered to the followers of Jesus? [11.2]
Pentecost was a Jewish feast that was celebrated 50 days after
Passover. It was a day that mattered to Jesus' followers because on
that day, the promised Holy Spirit came into the room where they
were meeting with a great sound of rushing wind and appeared as
flames of fire on each person. The Holy Spirit filled the disciples
and empowered them to fulfill the Great Commission and grow the
church.
5. How did early Christian communities testify to the transformative power
of the gospel? [11.2]
The early Christian communities showed the power of the gospel by the way
they lived. They met together regularly for fellowship, food, and prayer. They
shared with one another and had a good reputation among their neighbors.
Many sold their possessions in order to give to those in need.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Why do you think Jesus' disciples experienced persecution
from Jewish leaders? [11.3]
Answers will vary. Christianity arose out of the Jewish faith, so most
of the early followers of Jesus were Jews. Yet the apostles taught that
Gentile followers of Jesus did not need to be circumcised or observe
other Jewish customs. This would have deeply offended the Jews.
Jewish leaders were jealous of Jesus, and they were jealous of his
followers as well.
Note: Another possible reason for persecution is that the Romans
had made an exception for the Jews, allowing them to worship
largely as they pleased. Meanwhile, the apostles were claiming that
Jesus was Lord, not Caesar. The Jews may have been afraid that
Jewish Christians' claims that "Jesus is Lord" would put all Jews in
danger of incurring Rome's wrath. Sadly, animosity between Jews
and Christians persisted for hundreds of years, and eventually, some
Christians would persecute Jews.
2. Where did Jesus' followers preach the gospel? [11.3]
The apostles began by preaching in Jerusalem, but soon they and
other believers began preaching in other areas. Philip preached in
Samaria, Paul led missionary journeys throughout Asia Minor, and
others are said to have shared the gospel in places as far away as
India, Russia, and North Africa.
3. What features of the Roman empire helped the gospel spread?
[11.3]
The Roman empire had good roads and Greek was a common
language. These features helped the gospel spread through the
empire.
4. What did Paul teach was the mystery, hidden before Christ's
coming, that was revealed in the church? [11.4]
Paul said the mystery revealed in the church was that God's plan had
always been that all people might experience blessing through the
family of Abraham. Paul made a point of emphasizing that in the
body of Christ, there need be no division between Jews and Gentiles
since all were united in Jesus.
5. How did the apostles address the conflict between Jews and
Gentiles? [11.4]
Both Paul and Peter (eventually) rejected the view that Gentile
believers were bound by Jewish laws and customs or needed to be
circumcised. However, Paul warned Gentile believers not to be
arrogant, thinking that God had abandoned Israel. Gentiles, Paul
said, have been grafted into Israel, not the other way around.
6. The apostles taught that the gospel makes a difference in all of our
relationships. Think about your relationship with your parents or your
teachers. How might the gospel make a difference in how you behave in
those relationships? [11.4]
Answers will vary. The gospel encourages us to submit to the various authorities
in our lives and to serve one another in love. We are to relate to one another
with generosity, kindness, respect, and forgiveness. This means honoring our
parents and being open to learning from our teachers. This doesn't mean that
parents and teachers will always be right, but it does mean that we treat them as
people for whom Jesus died and respect their positions of authority.
7. What behaviors should characterize a Christian's life? [11.4]
A Christian's life should be characterized by love, forgiveness, mercy,
compassion, care for others, sexual integrity, trusting God for justice, blessing
enemies, living at peace, and patiently waiting for Christ's return. It is
important to note that behaving in these ways is the work of the Holy Spirit,
who empowers us to live godly lives. Though we continue to struggle with sin
and often fail, God is working on us. He has compassion and is always ready to
forgive and restore us.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. List three major events in Christian history. Why are they
significant? [11.5]
Answers will vary. Students may choose three of the following:
• AD 80-100: Revelation was written. This is significant because it
would become part of the New Testament in the Bible.
• 313: The Emperor Constantine ended the persecution of Christians
and granted Christianity legal status in the empire. This event is
significant because it allowed Christians to worship God freely.
• 1054: The Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox churches split
due to doctrinal differences and tension from the Crusades against
Islam. This event is significant because it divided the body of
Christ.
• 1517: Martin Luther unintentionally kicked off the Reformation.
The Reformation helped recover important biblical doctrines like
justification, stimulated a renewed emphasis on Scripture, and led
to the founding of many new denominations.
• 18th and 19th centuries: Protestant missionaries from the US and
the United Kingdom traveled around the world sharing the good
news. This was significant because it brought the gospel to many
new areas around the world.
• 20th and 21st centuries: Pentecostal and evangelical revivals led
to a renewed emphasis on global missions. These movements led
to a shift in the makeup of the global Christian population-the
majority of Christians now live outside the West.
2. Why is it important to study church history? [11.5]
If you are a Christian, church history is a part of your family story.
In studying church history, we learn about those who have walked
the path of faith before us, drawing encouragement from their
faithfulness and warning from their failures. Studying church history
helps us to consider how we might more faithfully follow Jesus in our
own time.
3. How does sin affect humans' relationship with God? [11.6]
Because of sin, humans do not have a good relationship with God.
Instead, they have a warped view of who God is. Some people hide
from God and are afraid of him. Others disobey and disrespect him.
Still others deny him or put idols in place of God.
4. What is justification, and how does it restore our relationship
with God? [11.6]
Justification is the state of being declared righteous before God
through the work of Jesus Christ. We are incapable of fixing the sin
problem that damages our relationship with God. Justification is a
gift of grace purchased with Jesus' blood on the cross whereby our
sins are forgiven and we are set on the path to full restoration. Reconciled to God, we need
not fear him. We can be confident in his love, grace, and forgiveness, and rejoice in his
presence.
5. What benefits do we receive when we become children of God? [11.6]
Answers will vary but may include that when we are adopted as sons, we receive the Holy
Spirit and are fellow heirs with Christ. The Spirit within us bears witness to the fact that
we are children of God.
6. How might seeing yourself as a beloved child of God change the way that you think
about him? [11.6]
Answers will vary. When we see ourselves as beloved children and God as a good and
loving Father, we can come to him without fear or shame. Even in our failure, we can be
confident that we are still loved unconditionally.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Paul wrote: "I do not understand my own actions. ... I have
the desire to do what is right, but not the ability to carry it
out. For I do not do the good I want, but the evil I do not
want is what I keep on doing" (Romans 7:15, 18-19). Can you
relate? Why is it often hard to do what is good, even when we
want to do it? [11.7]
Answers will vary. Before trusting Christ, we were slaves to sin.
Through Jesus, sin's power over us is broken and we are no longer
enslaved. However, justification does not mean that we immediately
stop sinning. We need the Holy Spirit's help. Just as breaking a bad
habit or building a good habit can be a long and difficult process,
so too, choosing to do right is a daily battle. Thankfully, we are not
alone in the fight!
2. When does Scripture say that salvation happens? [11.7]
The Bible says that we are saved when we confess Christ as Lord
(Romans 10:9), but it also says that we are currently being saved
(1 Corinthians 1:18, Philippians 2:12-13) and that we look forward
to salvation (1 Peter 1:3-5).
3. What is sanctification, and how does it occur? [11.7]
Sanctification is the process of becoming like Jesus. The key word
here is process. Sanctification is neither quick nor easy. It is a lifelong
journey of being transformed into the image of Christ. We do not
achieve sanctification by working harder at being better people;
rather, the Holy Spirit works in us, and we cooperate with him.
There will be many growing pains along the way, but the Holy Spirit
is always at work.
4. What does it mean to forgive someone? Why was forgiveness
important to Jesus? [11.8]
Answers will vary. Forgiveness is acknowledging a wrong that
someone has committed, but refusing to hold it against them.
Forgiveness does not mean saying that they did not do wrong or that
it did not matter or that appropriate boundaries are not necessary.
But forgiveness is offering grace instead of condemnation. This is
not always easy, but it opens up the possibility of reconciliation.
Forgiveness was important to Jesus because it is necessary for us to
truly love others. It is inevitable that we will wrong and hurt one
another, whether intentionally or unintentionally. Jesus showed how
important forgiveness was to him by going to the cross and forgiving
those who put him there.
5. Think about a strained or difficult relationship in your life. How might the
good news of reconciliation with God help you reconcile with that person?
[11.8]
Answers will vary. When we are in right relationship with God, he can help us to
seek reconciliation. When we are forgiven, we are empowered to offer forgiveness
to others. This will not always be easy-sometimes it may take years—but God can
give us the strength to offer forgiveness or to receive it from those we have wronged.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Is caring for creation a distraction from the gospel? Why or
why not? [11.9]
Possible answer: The gospel is good news for the whole world,
for individual people as well as nature. The material world is not
secondary in God's plan. God's creation is good, he loves it, and
he will one day restore it. Therefore, caring for creation is not a
distraction. Caring for the creation is one way in which we show
that the gospel has implications for all of life, not just our eternal
destinies. While we await the new creation, we can extend God's
redemptive work through wise stewardship of the planet.
2. What are some things you can do to be more aware of and
grateful for the natural world? [11.9]
Possible answers on how to increase awareness and enjoyment of
creation include:
• Sitting outside and listening to the sound of birds or the rustling of
the leaves.
• Hiking or exercising outside instead of at the gym.
• Planting a small garden or growing a single plant.
• Noticing the fragrance of flowers, trees, and rain.
• Visiting a park.
• Planning a camping trip with friends and family.
• Helping with lawn care in the neighborhood.
3. "Redemption saves us for life on Earth, not from it." What do
you think this means? [11.9]
Possible answer: When God saves us, he offers us abundant life right
now. That life starts with knowing God personally, loving others,
and respecting the gift of the natural world. It also means that we are
agents of reconciliation, bringing the hope and healing of the gospel
to people and creation. Though we are ultimately headed toward a
new creation that will no longer be stained by sin, God still loves the
world and so should we.
4. Recognizing that there is no perfect church, what has been
your experience in the church? [11.10)
Answers will vary. This is a good place to talk about students
backgrounds and experiences with church. Church community will
be a major topic in Chapter 16.
5. Why is church community important for believers? [11.10]
Answers will vary. Church is important for believers. Healthy
churches provide a place for us to grow in our relationship with
God, find healing, practice reconciliation with others, and think
creatively about how to bring the gospel to bear on all creation.
Christians are generally called to participate in church even when it is hard to do
so. Individual Christians acting in isolation from the church lack accountability
and have no one to journey with them through difficulties and sins. Also,
bringing the good news of reconciliation to others is difficult if we are not
practicing reconciliation with other believers.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
Video 1:
How Does Christianity Truly Fulfill Our Humanity?
1. The speaker says the gospel goes further than just redemption.
What else does he say the gospel does?
The gospel redeems us and seeks to restore us to what we were
originally intended to be and do in God's creation.
2. How does the speaker describe the internal compass he says all
human beings have?
The compass refers to God's image in us and Ecclesiastes 3:11 that
says God has put eternity into the human heart.
3. What longings does the speaker believe are addressed by the
gospel?
The longings we have are for significance, intimacy, love, security,
beauty, truth, goodness, resolution, acceptance, and shalom.
4. The speaker quotes C. S. Lewis, who said our Lord finds our
desires are not too strong, but too weak. What did Lewis mean?
Lewis meant people are half-hearted in their pursuit of God, and
concerned with drink, sex, and ambition, when infinite joy is
available to them.

What Is Discipleship?
1. What is a disciple?
A disciple is a learner-someone who sits at the feet of a teacher and
seeks to learn from him or her.
2. When someone disciples us, what are they doing?
They are teaching us what Jesus taught his disciples by telling us the
stories and parables he gave and what he taught.
3. How is sharing the gospel different from discipleship?
Sharing the gospel can mean giving a message one time, but
discipleship takes time and effort. It's a commitment to learn so that
you can, in turn, teach someone else.

• Video 3
Is Discipleship More than Mentoring?
1. How are discipling and mentoring similar? What is one difference?
Both involve teaching, learning, and personal relationship. But mentoring can
take place with any topic, whereas discipling is Jesus-centered. To be a disciple
means to conform to the image of the Master and Teacher, Jesus.
2. What is a second difference between mentoring and discipling?
You can mentor someone by relying on your own experience, but discipling
requires you to rely on God's power and trust the Holy Spirit to guide you.
3. What is a third difference between mentoring and discipling?
Mentoring can be focused on a very specific topic or skill, but discipling involves
your whole life because Jesus' teaching applies to all aspects of life.
Video 4
What Is a Christian's Role in Evangelism?
1. According to many Christians, who is involved in evangelism?
Most Christians think evangelism is done by superstar Christians who are great
speakers and really smart (the kind of Christian they wish they were) or by weird
ones (the kind of Christians they are glad they are not).
2. Why does the speaker believe normal people should evangelize?
He believes this because Jesus commanded his followers to share the good news
and make disciples. That command extends to everyone who follows Jesus.
3. What styles of evangelism did the speaker describe?
The speaker said you can be direct and forthright like Peter, have intellectual
discussions like Paul, give your testimony like the blind man, or give a party.
4. What does this variety of styles imply about how God made you?
It implies that God knew what he was doing when he made me. It doesn't
matter what my personality is; God can use me to reach others.
• Video 5
Why Is Church Important?
1. Why is the church important?
Faith is not just about me. Jesus came to redeem me and the people around me.
He established the church so others can hear about Jesus.
2. Ephesians 4:11-12 talks about apostles, prophets, pastors, teachers, and
evangelists. What do these church office holders do for God's people?
The offices are supposed to keep Christians from being led astray by false
teaching so that we can continue to follow in God's ways.
3. The speaker said that the church is valuable in a corporate sense and in an
individual sense. What did he mean?
All believers are part of a body, and Christ is the head. The church is also
valuable because it keeps people from being led astray.

HOW CHRISTIANITY GREW
OBJECTIVE:
Students will research how Christianity grew and
spread around the world.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: computer, paper, and pen
• Time required: 20-30 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Inform students that they will work in six groups to
represent each continent (except for Antarctica).
Have each group use computers to research the
story of how Christianity spread within its assigned
country or state and how the name they were
provided relates to that story. Groups will have up to
15 minutes for this task.
2. Assign groups one of the following:
• Group 1: Asia-Adoniram Judson in Myanmar
• Group 2: Africa-David Livingstone in Zambia
• Group 3: Australia-Richard Johnson
• Group 4: North America-Juvenaly in Alaska
• Group 5: South America-Huguenots in Brazil
• Group 6: Europe-Olaf Tryggvason, King Olaf I of
Norway in Iceland
3. Have groups present their findings to the class.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What did Jesus mean when he said, "The kingdom of God is at
hand" (Mark 1:15)?
He was referring to the work that he would accomplish by securing our
redemption. By dying on the cross, Jesus fulfilled the covenants in the
Old Testament and began a new covenant with his blood. He made it
possible for human beings to be forgiven of their sins and be saved.
2. How is the kingdom of God something that is already here on
Earth, but not yet fully realized? Explain.
The kingdom of God is already here because Jesus died for our sins
and rose again from the dead. By doing so, he showed that those who
believe in him will receive eternal life. Though Jesus is the rightful King
of the world, he is not on Earth. He ascended into heaven and believers
are waiting for his return, which will lead to the full consummation of
the kingdom of God. Then, everyone will acknowledge Jesus is Lord,
sin and death will be banished, and all things will be made new.
3. Explain what the church is and what it has to do with the Great
Commission.
The church is the followers of Christ in all places and all times. Jesus
told the apostles to carry out the Great Commission, his command
for them to make disciples, baptize, and instruct people about all he
taught. He also told the apostles to wait for the Holy Spirit, who would
empower them for this mission.
4. What is Pentecost, and what happened on Pentecost?
Pentecost is the day the promised Holy Spirit filled the apostles
and followers of Jesus so that they could accomplish the Great
Commission. When they were all together, the Holy Spirit came with
the sound of wind and flames of fire appeared on each person. The
Spirit filled them and they spoke in tongues. Peter proclaimed the
gospel and 3,000 people accepted Christ that day.
5. How does the Bible describe the early church?
In the book of Acts, the church is described as believers who met in
homes to hear the apostles preach and break bread together. They
shared their possessions with people who were in need. In the rest of
the Bible, the early church is described with word pictures, such as a
bride, a temple, a nation, a royal priesthood, and more.
6. What did the leaders of the Jews do when they saw the church
grow? Who were Stephen and Saul?
The book of Acts says that the priests and Sadducees were annoyed
that Jesus' followers were preaching about Jesus. They thought his
death would silence his followers, but this was not so. They had
Peter and John arrested and put on trial, but then they released them
with a warning not to preach. The believers did not stop preaching.

Stephen was a believer who was stoned to death. Saul, later known as Paul, was a
Pharisee who arrested and put believers in jail. He sought to arrest Christians in
Damascus, but he saw a vision of Jesus and became a follower of Jesus.
7. How did the church grow beyond Jerusalem?
First, Philip preached in Samaria. Then, Paul and other church leaders spread
the gospel in Asia Minor. This was possible, in part, because Greek was spoken
in much of the Roman empire and there were good roads to travel from place
to place. Church traditions say that the apostles traveled all over the world
preaching about Jesus: Thomas went to India, Matthew preached in North
Africa, and Andrew went to Russia.
8. What challenges did the early church have because its members came
from both Jewish and Gentile cultures?
The Jewish believers in the church were asking whether Gentile believers needed
to be circumcised and follow other Jewish customs. God had promised to bless
everyone through Abraham's family, not just Israel. God poured out the Spirit on
Gentile believers and he accepts everyone into his family. The Gentile believers
had to remember they were grafted into Israel and not the other way around.
9. What additional challenges were addressed in the epistles?
The epistles discuss social relationships and Christian ethics. They talk about
how Christians should behave in family relationships, master/slave relationships,
and in relationship to the government. The epistles also teach that Christians are
to live out their faith by loving others and forgiving them, by caring for those in
need by blessing their enemies, and by remaining faithful.
10. What difference does it make that the epistles were not written directly
to us, even though as Scripture they are for us?
Because the epistles were not written directly to us, we have to interpret how
they apply to us today.
11. Summarize the history of the church from the first century until today.
At the beginning of the first century, the church was being persecuted, which
continued until AD 313, when the Roman emperor Constantine granted
Christianity legal status. Christianity flourished for the next 500 years. Church
leaders met and discussed Christian doctrine, and the Scriptures were translated
into many languages. When the Roman empire ended, Christian monks and
nuns preserved the Scriptures. Later, the Crusades began in order to fight Islamic
conquerors. In 1054, doctrinal differences between Roman Catholic and Eastern
Orthodox churches led to a split. The church experienced corruption. In 1517,
Martin Luther began the Protestant Reformation and more denominations
began. Some Protestants came to the Americas in search of religious freedom.
In the 18th, 19th, and 20th centuries, Protestants spread the gospel message all
over the world with missionary movements and revivals. Now Christianity is the
largest religion on Earth, with followers on every continent.
12. How do humans relate to God because of sin?
Sin has distorted the way people relate to God. Some are afraid of God because they know
that they have sinned and are ashamed. Other people are angry or disrespectful toward God.
Some people wish God were different and put idols in his place. Other people deny that
God exists. But all people can have a restored relationship with God through faith in Jesus.
13. What is justification, and what happens to us when we are justified?
Justification is the state of being declared righteous before God through the work of Jesus
Christ. It is what happens when we make a profession of faith in Christ and are saved. When
we are justified by faith through God's grace, we are restored to the original relationship God
had in mind. We are adopted as God's children and heirs. We know that he loves us and that
we do not have to be afraid but can come to him and that he will forgive us even if we sin.
14. In what ways does God restore our relationship with ourselves?
God restores our relationship with ourselves by setting us free from sin's power and by
transforming us so we want to and can live godly lives for him. Sanctification is the process
of becoming like Jesus. It is a continual process of growth where we let go of destructive
attitudes and habits and become who God designed us to be. Because sanctification takes
time, the Bible says we were not only saved when we believed in Jesus, but we are being
saved every day as the Spirit works within us.
15. In what ways does God restore our relationships with other people?
God restores our relationships with others by enabling us to love them, to forgive them, and
to ask them to forgive us. He also commands us to avoid hurting others. The Bible has many
lists of sin, and most of them are sins against other people that we should not do. God wants
our relationships to be full of love and the fruit of the Spirit. He made us ambassadors to
share the good news that people can be reconciled to God. God also requires us to pursue
reconciliation with others.
16. In what ways does God restore our relationship with creation?
The Bible says that in Christ, all things will be reconciled and made new. This includes
creation. While we wait for Jesus to return and for God's kingdom to be fully realized, God
expects us to live holy lives. This includes being good stewards of the planet, modeling how
to enjoy creation as God's gift, and not exploiting or abusing it.
17. Why do individual Christians need the church?
The Bible says that we are all part of Christ's body. We are supposed to serve God as
individuals but also in community with other believers. Every church community is
composed of broken people who are awaiting full restoration. Through the church, we can
practice relationship and learn how to be God's ambassadors by demonstrating love for one
another. In church, we learn from the Scriptures and receive support and encouragement to
live the Christian life.
