OVERVIEW
Most cultures in the ancient world had
creation myths to explain how everything
came into being. Creation myths were
attempts to convey truths about the nature
of reality and our place within it. While
there are some similarities between the
Genesis creation account and these myths,
the distinctions of the Genesis creation far
outweigh the similarities. Christians believe
that Genesis is truth revealed through
God's Word. The first two chapters of
Genesis explain the true nature of reality
and our place in the world.
Genesis 1 begins with two fundamental
claims about God-that he exists and that
he is the Creator of the universe. God
speaks creation into existence ex nihilo.
Everything he did was purposeful and
orderly. God formed and filled the universe
in six days, culminating with the creation
of humans in his image, which is the focus
of Genesis 2. There we learn that God
designed humans as relational beings with
a purpose. We also learn that God is both
immanent and transcendent.
Three general ways that human beings
bear God's image are rule, resemblance,
and relationship. First, God gave us
rule-authority and stewardship-over
his creation. Second, we resemble God in
that we have certain attributes in common,
such as intelligence and the ability to
communicate. Third, God designed people
to be in relationship with him, ourselves,
other people, and nature.
The biblical creation story confronts
and refutes the ideas and beliefs of many
worldviews, both ancient and modern. It is
important that Christians not only know
the truth about creation and who God is,
but also how to defend those truths. This
chapter will equip students with knowledge
of both.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is a creation myth? What are some examples of creation
myths? [6.1]
A creation myth is a story that explains the nature of reality and
how the universe and human beings came about. Not all creation
myths claim to be historically true. However, these stories helped
people explain the nature of reality and humans place within it.
Some examples of creation myths are the Egyptian myth of Ra, the
Tahitian myth of Ta aroa, and the Sumerian myth of Enlil.
2. What similarities are there between the creation in Genesis
and in the Enuma Elish? Do you notice any differences? [6.1]
In the Enuma Elish, Marduk slays Tiamat, then uses her body to
create an orderly cosmos. In Genesis, God also creates an orderly
world, but he does not have to fight any other god, as there are no
other gods. Some similarities between the two accounts include:
darkness at the beginning; the creation of light, firmament, dry land,
stars, and humans; and the depiction of the deity resting.
Note: Some scholars argue that Genesis' similarities to other creation
myths show the biblical author borrowed from them. However, the
similarities may be explained another way. The author of Genesis
was likely familiar with some creation myths, living around people
who believed them. It is possible that the author of Genesis used
language and imagery from these myths, to claim that it was God,
not the pagan deities, who created the cosmos. The author of
Genesis thus argued that pagan creation stories were false.
3. In what ways do we see evidence of the Trinity in Genesis 1?
[6.2]
God the Father is assumed to be the agent in Genesis 1:1, and
Genesis 1:2 speaks of the "Spirit of God... hovering over the face
of the waters." Jesus is not mentioned, but Colossians 1:15-16
identifies Jesus as the Creator. In Genesis 1:26, God speaks about
"us" and "our,"
possibly addressing other members of the Trinity.
Although the author of Genesis may not have been making an
explicit claim about the Trinity, Christians can see the Trinity was
active in creation.
4. What is significant about being made in God's image? [6.2]
In all creation, only humans, both male and female, are made in
God's image. After God created humans, he saw everyting he had
made and said that it was very good. It is significant that both men
and women bear God's image. Men are not superior to women,
nor women superior to men. They are equal in God's eyes. As God's
image-bearers, humans are given responsibility and authority in God's
world. This means we are not bystanders—we have a job to do!
FOUNDATIONS S
5. The creation account in Genesis 1 is shockingly brief. It leaves many things out
and many questions unanswered. Why do you think this is so? What might this tell
you about what kind of story Genesis 1 is trying to tell? [6.2]
Answers will vary.
Note: Genesis 1 has always been a difficult passage to interpret. It is written in poetic
style, but it is not only poetry. It also seems to provide historical details. However,
the brevity of the account should cause us to stop and ponder. What else might the
author be trying to communicate besides the fact that God is the creator?
Genesis 1 may not be interested in addressing all of the questions that we want
answers to. For example, no one was debating evolution when Genesis was written,
so the author of Genesis wasn't trying to address that. We shouldn't force Genesis
to answer all of our questions about how exactly God created the world. That said,
Genesis 1 definitely confronts and rejects any origin story that leaves God out of the
picture and devalues the goodness of creation or human life.

1. What does it mean that God rested on the seventh day? [6.3]
God did not rest because he was worn out. Isaiah 40:28 reminds us
that God neither faints nor grows weary. In the Bible, the number
seven represents perfection or completion. God rested on the
seventh day because he had completed his creation work and was
satisfied with the goodness of all that he had made.
2. Why is work a good thing? What kinds of work have you
found especially meaningful or fulfilling? [6.3]
Work is not a result of the fall. It is good because God designed
humans to work. There is often great satisfaction and joy that comes
from a job well-done. Answers will vary.
3. What is significant about the creation of the woman in
Genesis 2: [6.3]
It is not good for a person to exist in isolation because humans
were created for relationship. God made the woman as a "helper"
and companion to the man. However, this does not mean that she
was second-class or a servant to the man. (God is often described
as Israel's helper). The man and the women are both image-bearers,
equals in God's world. They are both given the task of working for
the good of God's world. They experienced perfect harmony and
intimacy—neither dominated over the other.
4. What implications do you think this has for the way women
are treated in society today? [6.3]
Possible answer: Since men and women are equal image-bearers,
both tasked with ruling over God's creation, women should
experience full dignity and equality. Any system that belittles,
demeans, devalues, exploits, abuses, or patronizes women is sinful.
5. How does the universe reflect the glory of God? What aspect of
God's amazing world do you enjoy the most? [6.4]
God's glory is displayed in the beauty of creation. God could have
created a universe that is barren, colorless, or tasteless. But this is
not the kind of God we have. God's abundant, creative, generous
personality is reflected in his amazing world of mountains, colors,
and flavors. In this way, creation proclaims God's majesty, power,
and beauty. Answers will vary.
6. Why did God create humans and the world? [G.4]
God did not create the world because he needed company. In
the Trinity, God already had relationship. He created the world
out of love so that he might invite us into the life-giving, joyous
relationship of the Trinity.

7. What does it mean to say that God is transcendent and immanent? [6.4]
To say that God is transcendent is to say that God is not a part of his creation.
He is separate and outside of it. However, this does not mean that God is not
involved in the world. God is also immanent, which means that he is within
and among what he has created. He is active in the world and holds all things
together. Without him, everything would cease to exist.

1. Describe three common views of what it means to be made in
God's image. [6.5]
• Rule-humans were given authority and responsibility to take care
of God's world and bring it to abundance.
• Resemblance-humans are like God in that they have certain
God-like attributes such as creativity and intelligence (though not
to the same degree as God).
• Relationship—humans were created to be in relationship with
God, themselves, others, and creation.
2. How are the ideas of image-bearing and stewardship
connected? [6.5]
In the ancient world, kings set up images of themselves to let
everyone know who was in charge. In the Bible, people are
forbidden from making images of God because humans are made
in God's image. God is the ultimate Ruler of the universe, but he
chooses to give humans authority and responsibility in his domain.
Stewardship, which is caring for and managing what belongs to
another, is one way we bear God's image.
3. What does it mean to be a sub-creator? What are some
examples of sub-creation? [6.5]
God does not need any pre-existing material to create. He creates
ex nihilo (out of nothing). Humans also have the ability to create,
but we need pre-existing materials. To be a sub-creator means to
use what God has already made to make new things. It is to make
culture. Possible examples of sub-creation:
• God creates trees. Humans use wood from trees to create chairs.
• God creates sound. Humans use pre-existing materials in order to
create instruments that, when played, produce music.
• God gives humans imagination. Humans use their imaginations to
create fictional worlds and tell moving stories.
4. How does the Bible describe our relationship with God?
Which description do you find most comforting? Which is
most challenging to you? [6.6]
The Bible describes God as Creator, Parent, Protector, Provider,
Savior, Friend, and Lord. We find our identity in relationship with
God as his beloved children. Answers will vary.
5. What is the purpose of our sexuality? [6.6]
God says humans are male and female, and they are made in God's
image. Our sexuality is profoundly important, but sexuality is not
just about sex. Sexuality is a gift from God that reveals our need
for intimacy and relationship with others. We were created to seek
relationship with other people. Since no human can ever fully satisfy
our need for intimacy, sexuality also points us back to God.
FOUNDATIONS S
6. What is a marriage according to Genesis 1-2? [6.6]
In Genesis, marriage is profoundly good, though it is not the ultimate goal
of life. Those who are single bear God's image just as much as those who are
married. According to the Bible, marriage is the only proper context for sex.
Through marital sex, the man and the woman begin to fulfill the creation
mandate "be fruitful and multiply" (Genesis 1:28). Marriage is meant to be a
loving union between a man and a woman, characterized by emotional, spiritual,
and physical intimacy. It is a relationship of commitment and self-giving.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Does Genesis promote the exploitation of the natural world?
Why or why not? [6.7]
Some people think that the instruction to subdue the earth (Genesis
1:28) is a license for humans to exploit the natural world for their
own purposes. Sadly, some Christians have used this verse to justify
actions with a detrimental effect on the natural world. God placed
Adam in the garden to work and to keep it (Genesis 2:15). These
words can refer to serving and preserving creation. The world exists
for God, but he has given it to us as a gift to wisely steward.
2. What are some practical ways that we can steward the natural
world? [6.7]
Possible answer: Being a good steward of creation means considering
the impact our actions have on the natural world. Simple things like
taking shorter showers, recycling, and picking up trash can have a
positive impact on the natural world. On a larger scale, Christians
can encourage more ethical treatment of animals and more careful
use of soil. Christians can be involved in efforts to conserve forests
and wildlife habitats. We should also consider how best to provide
for the flourishing of human beings.
3. How does Genesis 1-2 confront ancient pagan creation myths?
What makes Genesis 1-2 different from these myths? [6.8]
Genesis 1-2 confronts ancient pagan creation myths by telling a
different story about the origin of the world that rejects many of the
common practices and ways of thinking supported by these myths.
For example, in Genesis:
• There is only one God, not many.
• The universe has a divine origin.
• God is sovereign; he has no challenger.
• Creation is purposeful.
• Creation is not the result of sex.
• God is good and loving, not petty and vindictive.
• Humans are valued in God's creation; they are not slaves.
4. Compared to Egyptian culture as described in the Old
Testament, how does Genesis 1-2 uphold human dignity?
[6.8]
In Egyptian culture, the pharaoh alone was the image of god.
Everyone else existed to serve him. This idea implied the pharaoh
had the right to mistreat people in whatever way he pleased. Pharaoh
exercised this god-like power by brutally enslaving the Israelites and
ordering the destruction of their male babies (Exodus 1:16, 22). By
contrast, Genesis 1-2 told the Israelites that all people were made
in God's image, not just the pharaoh. Because of this, we cannot
simply do whatever we like to one another. Each person has inherent
dignity and should be treated with respect.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How does Genesis 1-2 confront alternative worldviews? What

Genesis 1-2 confronts alternative worldviews by telling the true
story of reality, which undermines many harmful ideas. For example,
Genesis 1-2 affirms:
• direct involvement of a personal God in creation (unlike atheism)
• distinction between creation and Creator (unlike pantheism)
• the universe is good, purposeful (unlike gnosticism and nihilism)
• the special creation of humans (unlike atheistic theories of
evolution teaching humans are just highly evolved animals)
• goodness of the body, sexuality (unlike modern views of sexuality)
• distinction and equality between male and female (unlike modern
views of sexuality
• the dignity and value of all human beings (unlike racism, sexism,
abortion, euthanasia)
2. How might seeing people as made in God's image make a
difference in racism, sexism, abortion, and euthanasia? [6.9]
In a secular society, people can assign value based on some arbitrar
criteria like skin color, gender, age, weight, intelligence, or physica
attractiveness. Because we are made in God's image, we have
inherent dignity and value, which combats the following problems:
• Racism: Since no one is more or less valuable based on their race or
skin color, prejudice against those who are different is unacceptable.
Instead, we should work for dignity and justice for all people.
• Sexism: Since both male and female are made in God's image, men
and women are to be treated with dignity and respect. Any form of
degradation or abuse of one gender is wrong.
• Abortion: Since all people are valuable regardless of their size, age,
or degree of dependency, babies in the womb are to be honored as
valuable members of society.
• Euthanasia: Since the image of God in us does not diminish with
age, we cannot cut short the lives of those who are elderly. Only
God has the right to decide when a person's life is over.
3. How does Christianity uphold the goodness of the body and
sexuality compared with secular views of sexuality? [6.9]
Secular society glorifies the body and places few boundaries around
sex. Secular views encourage us to express ourselves sexually, using
our bodies and the bodies of others in whatever way gives us most
pleasure. This leads to the degradation of our bodies, sex, and our
relationships with others. Christianity teaches that the body and
sex are profoundly good. Our gender is a gift, as are marriage and
singleness. The Bible places sex within the confines of a committed,
loving marriage between a man and a woman, which opens up the
possibility for true human flourishing. Because our bodies are sacred,
we are to live in sexual integrity, celebrating God's good gift of sex.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is a creation myth? Explain the purpose of creation myths.
A creation myth explains the nature of reality and origins of the
universe and human being. These myths answer the questions "Who
am I?" "What is my place in the world?" "What is my relationship to
nature, other people, and the divine?" "How should I live?"
2. Summarize the creation narrative from Genesis 1.
The biblical creation story begins with the words, In the beginning,
God created the heavens and the earth" (Genesis 1:1). This tells us
God exists, and God is the Creator of everything. God completed
his creation in six days. The first three days God formed his creation
with light and darkness, the sky and atmosphere, and then land
and vegetation. The last three days God filled his creation with the
heavenly bodies, birds and water creatures, and finally animals and
humans. Humans are unique in that God made them in his image.
3. What does Elohim mean? What does Yahweh mean?
Elohim is the name for the Creator God used in the Old Testament.
Yahweh is the personal, covenantal Old Testament name for God.
4. What does imago Dei refer to?
Imago Dei means that human beings were created in God's likeness.
5. Describe the creation of humans found in Genesis 2.
In Genesis 2, God created everything and rested on the seventh day.
Then, Genesis 2 describes the unique creation of humans. God
formed man from the ground and breathed life into him. God set
him in the garden of Eden to work and care for it. God instructed
him not to eat from the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil. God
saw that it wasn't good for the man to be alone, so he created a
helper for him. When the man saw her, he named her woman.
6. List nine attributes of God.
God is transcendent, immanent, active, powerful, creative, generous,
involved, personal, and overflowing with love for all of creation.
7. Explain what transcendence and immanence mean.
Transcendence is an attribute of God that describes him as being both
above and outside of his creation. Immanence is an attribute of God
that describes him as being both within and among his creation.
8. What are the three ways in which human beings bear God's
image? Briefly explain each one.
First, God gave humans rule over creation with authority and the
responsibility of caring for and cultivating to prosper. Second,
we share some of God's attributes such as intelligence, creativity,
FOUNDATIONS G
morality, and the ability to communicate. Third, we reflect God's image by
existing in loving relationship with God, ourselves, other people, and nature.
9. What is stewardship? Give an example.
Stewardship is the process of managing something that has been entrusted to
one's care. Examples will vary but should reflect a type of stewardship, such as
preventing pollution, growing a garden, or cleaning up litter.
10. Describe God's plan for people as relational and sexual beings.
God is relational. Because humans bear God's image, we are relational too.
We are in relationship with God as our Creator, Parent, Protector, Provider,
Savior, Friend, and Lord. God wants us to be in relationship with others for
friendship, community, and marriage. God created humans as male and female.
Our sexuality is a gift from God. The act of sex should only be in its proper
context—a loving marriage relationship.
11. How is the biblical mandate of stewardship connected to our
relationship with nature?
People are to bear God's image by ruling as wise stewards over his creation. God
put the man in the garden to work and keep it. We are stewards in God's world.
12. Briefly discuss seven truths in which the biblical creation story is distinct
from other worldviews.
• God is personal and is directly involved in creation. There is only one God.
This refutes the atheistic belief that there is no God.
• Creation is distinct from God, the Creator. The universe has a divine origin.
This contradicts pantheism, which claims the universe is god.
• The universe is good and has purpose. God is sovereign and good. This is
in opposition to both the gnostic idea that the material world is evil, and
nihilism, which asserts that there is no meaning or purpose in the universe.
• Humans are a special creation. Creation is purposeful. This disputes
evolutionary theory that claims humans are highly evolved animals.
• The human body and sexuality are good. Creation is not the result of a sexual
act. This rejects the idea that our bodies are inherently evil.
• There is a distinction and equality between male and female. This refutes the
idea that men and women are interchangeable or superior one over the other.
• All human beings have value and dignity in God's creation. This is in contrast
to any view that devalues people for any reason.
13. What is the main belief of each of the following worldviews?
Atheism: God does not exist.
Pantheism: The universe is god.
Nihilism: There is no meaning or purpose in the universe.
Gnosticism: The material world is evil.
