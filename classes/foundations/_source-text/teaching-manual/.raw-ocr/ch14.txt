OVERVIEW
To live well in this world, we need to know
who we are-humans made in God's image.
Sadly, due to the fall, humans inherited a
sinful nature and are subject to death. Yet
by faith in Christ, we can be made new and
receive eternal life.
Jesus is our example of what it means to
be human and not sin. In the early church
some rejected Jesus' humanity and embraced
Docetism, the heretical teaching that Jesus
was completely divine and only appeared
to be human. Others embraced Arianism,
the heretical teaching that Jesus was not
God, but only a divine being created by the
Father. But Jesus was both fully human and
fully divine. Jesus glorified the Father by
living a sinless life.
We are called to imitate Jesus by
becoming people who glorify God. By
faith we can have union with Christ and
participate in the divine life, which is about
love, generosity, goodness, and much more.
Our sanctification is a gradual process.
The doctrine of glorification teaches that
all believers will finally be freed from sin,
restored, and given new, eternal bodies.
Christians must adopt Jesus attitude
of being a servant to others. We must
also take up our cross daily, choosing love
despite trouble and persecution. Jesus never
abandons us, and we can have hope because
nothing can separate us from God's love.
Without denying the pain, suffering, and
evil in our world, we can exercise Christian
hope, trusting in what Jesus has done and
will do. We can also look at the examples of
those whose stories in Scripture show how
they grew in Christlikeness and in hope.
Though the church is imperfect, it provides
us companionship, encouragement, and
protection. God transforms us so that we
can be the people he made us to be and
bring transformation and healing into the
world.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. According to the Bible, what are some of the defining
characteristics of human beings? [14.1]
Human beings are made in God's image, meaning that we have
inherent dignity and value. We are also incredibly loved by God
and invited to be his children. We were made for relationship with
God, ourselves, others, and creation. We have the capacity to make
choices and to love. Humans have a purpose and responsibility in
God's world. Humans are limited and finite. We are also broken
and corrupted by sin.
2. Why do you think God created us with limitations? [14.1]
Answers will vary. Our limitations are not meant to keep us under
God's thumb, make us puny, or crush our dignity. Instead, our
limitations allow us to realize our dependence on God and our need
for others. Our limitations lead us to look outside of ourselves to
seek relationship with others.
3. Is being human a problem? Explain. [14.1]
According to the Bible, being human is not a problem. Jesus was
human, so we cannot say that being human is bad. Our problem is
not that we are human, but that our humanity has been broken and
hijacked by sin.
4. Do you tend to think about Jesus more in terms of his
divinity or his humanity? How do you respond to the idea
that Jesus was a human being with real needs, limitations, and
temptations? [14.2]
Answers will vary.
5. Why might Gentiles in Jesus' day consider the doctrine of the
Incarnation foolishness? [14.2]
A common philosophy in Jesus' day was gnosticism, which taught
that the physical and material world was evil, or a shadow or copy
of the true spiritual world. Gnostics thought it was ridiculous to
imagine that God would humiliate himself by becoming a human
being with a physical body. Over time, this kind of thinking
developed into the false doctrine of Docetism, which taught that
Jesus wasn't really human after all but only appeared to be human.
6. What does it mean to follow Jesus in the here and now? (14.2)
Answers will vary. Since Jesus did his ministry in the flesh and
engaged with real human problems, we must follow his example.
We do not follow Jesus by withdrawing from the world to
contemplate spiritual knowledge, but by taking action to love and
minister to others and by worshipping God in everything we do in
our daily lives.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What does it mean to become like God? What doesn't it mean?
[14.3]
To become like God is to become the individuals we were created
to be through the transforming power of the Holy Spirit. It is to
become spiritually mature people who love others and glorify God
in everything. Becoming like God does not mean that we become
gods, are absorbed into God, or become indistinguishable or
miniature copies of God.
2. In Matthew 5:48, Jesus said to his disciples: "You therefore
must be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect." What
does this mean? [14.3]
Jesus' command to be "perfect" means to be complete or spiritually
mature. It is to pattern our lives after God's character and follow
Jesus in all we do. Becoming perfect does not mean that we must
eradicate all sin from our lives through our own effort. That only
happens through the power of the Holy Spirit at work in our hearts.
3. What does it mean to glorify God? How did Jesus glorify God?
[14.3]
Something that has "glory" has weight or value. To glorify God is to
give him the honor of which he is worthy. It is to make God famous
by worshipping him and attesting to his goodness through holiness
and acts of love. It is mirroring God in such a way that God can be
seen in us. Jesus glorified God by emptying himself of his own glory
to highlight the Father's glory instead. Jesus' choice to submit to the
Father led him to die on the cross, the ultimate sacrifice to glorify
God and bring us to salvation.
4. How might we glorify God in the classroom? At home? On the
sports field? What other everyday scenarios can you think of
in which we can bring glory to God? [14.3]
Possible answers:
• We glorify God in the classroom by using our God-given intellect
to seek wisdom and understanding so that we might learn more
about God's world and how to live in it. We also glorify God by
respecting our classmates and teachers and listening to what they
have to say.
• We glorify God at home by honoring our parents and siblings and
putting their needs before our own. We practice integrity when we
are alone and and when we are with others by looking for ways to
serve them.
• We glorify God in sports by practicing athletic skills and working
together with others. We treat our fellow teammates and rivals
with respect.
5. What does it mean to participate in the divine life and be united to Christ?
[14.4]
The divine life is all about living like Jesus by embodying love, generosity, and other
aspects of his character. We can participate in this life by trusting Christ so that we
can be freed from slavery to sin and empowered by God's Spirit to become like God.
According to Romans 6, for the Christian, the old self has died, and the new self has
been raised with Christ. We are united with him. This means we are dead to sin, but
alive to God.
6. What is glorification? [14.4]
Glorification is the doctrine that all believers will be finally freed from sin, restored,
and given new, eternal bodies. Then, they will be able to be everything God intended
them to be. Through unity with Christ, the process of sanctification has already
begun, but it will not be completed until Jesus returns. Only then will we be glorified.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How did Jesus model a life of service? [14.5]
Jesus modeled a life of service by emptying himself and becoming
human. He spent time with sinners and social outcasts, washed the
disciples' feet, and gave his life for the world to save those who were
hopelessly lost.
2. What does true humility look like? [14.5]
True humility is not thinking poorly of yourself, denying your gifts,
or feeling worthless. If you are humble, you can recognize your
identity as God's beloved child, acknowledge the ways that God
has gifted you, and then use those gifts to benefit and bless others.
Rather than seeking to boost their own egos, the truly humble forget
themselves in the service of others.
3. Can a servant also be a leader? Explain. [14.5]
Answers will vary. Serving and leading are not necessarily opposed.
Being a servant does not mean that we can't hold positions of
leadership. (Remember that Jesus was the ultimate Servant, yet he
was also Lord and King.) Leading as a servant means that we dont
use our authority to lord it over others. Servant leadership is not
mainly about being in charge or telling others what to do. It means
leading by being the first to serve, the first to get down on hands
and knees to do the jobs that no one wants to do, the first to pick up
another's burden. A servant leader is defined by love and puts others
first.
4. What role does suffering play in the Christian life? [14.6]
According to Jesus, following him does not lead to material
prosperity, ease, or comfort. Jesus promised that we would have
trouble in the world, and he called us to pick up the cross and
follow him. As his disciples, we will experience pain and suffering
in one form or another. Yet we should never seek out persecution,
adopt a martyr complex, or inflict pain on ourselves. Following Jesus
into suffering is not about seeking out suffering but being willing
to go with him wherever he goes, even if that leads us into pain or
discomfort. To pick up the cross, we must first be willing to lose our
lives in the cause of Jesus.
5. What might it look like to suffer for Christ today? [14.6]
Answers will vary.
Note: Christians in many parts of the world experience suffering
or persecution for their belief in Christ. Some face death, prison,
physical abuse, rape and sexual violence, abductions, harassment,
fines, forced conversion or forced marriage, exile, expulsion from
their community or family, or shunning. They may also have limited
opportunities to meet, buy property, or obtain work or education.

Such consequences are less common in the West; however, that does not mean that Western
Christians don't suffer. In addition to physical and legal mistreatment, believers who suffer for
Christ's sake may experience verbal abuse, criticism, or ridicule.
6. What should encourage Christians in suffering? [14.6]
Even in suffering, Christians can have confidence that God has not abandoned or forgotten
them. They can know that he is always with them, even in the darkest places. They can take
comfort knowing that Jesus also suffered and can sympathize with their suffering. They can also
be confident that their suffering will not compare to the future glory that will be revealed. God
will bring justice and work all things for good. Nothing can ever separate us from God's love.

PROCEDURE:

b. Explain that the person falling should cross his or her arms in front and not bend at the
knees as this can make it more difficult for others to catch him or her. When the faller is
ready to fall, he or she should say, "Falling."
3. If desired, choose a group to come to the front of the class to demonstrate the activity.
4. Allow time for students to take turns doing trust falls in their respective groups.
5. After the activity, have students relate how they felt falling or catching and how this
activity relates to their readiness to serve or to suffer for Christ.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is the difference between optimism and pessimism?
What is Christian hope, and how does it differ from optimism
and pessimism? [14.71
Optimists often look on the bright side. Asserting that things will
get better, they tend to minimize negative feelings and experiences
and may fall short of addressing real pain. Pessimists tend to see
circumstances in the worst light possible. Because they think
circumstances may not ever change or get better, they despair and
believe it is not worthwhile to get involved or try to help. Christian
hope is the quality of looking honestly at the world, and trusting in
what Jesus has done and will do. In contrast to optimism, it honestly
acknowledges the brokenness of the world. In contrast to pessimism,
it refuses to despair. Christian hope motivates us to trust God in all
circumstances and work to make the world better while we await the
day when he will restore all things.
2. Explain what it means for a Christian to live in hope. [14.7]
The Christian who hopes recognizes that it is impossible for us
to ultimately fix the brokenness of the world. We await the day
when Jesus will put an end to evil and make all things new. In the
meantime, however, we trust he is always at work for our good and
his glory, and patiently await his return by anticipating his kingdom
through acts of love and service. Hope motivates us to work for
change, generously give of ourselves, live in joy, and bear up under
suffering.
3. Explain how Peter, Martha, and Paul grew in Christlikeness.
[14.8]
Peter made many mistakes while following Jesus. After denying Jesus
publicly, Peter was restored by Jesus himself. He grew in courage and
humility, boldly preaching Jesus and accepting Paul's rebuke.
Eventually, he gave his life for the cause of Christ. Though Martha
was too busy to sit at Jesus feet when he taught at her house, she
later demonstrated her belief and confidence in Jesus as the Messiah.
Paul went from being a persecutor of Christians to a humble
preacher of the gospel and a spiritual guide for many believers,
including Timothy.
4. As you reflect on your own spiritual growth, do you relate
most to Peter, Martha, Paul, or Timothy? Why? [14.8]
Answers will vary.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Is it possible to be a Christian alone? Why or why not? [14.9]
God created us to be in relationship with other people. To be most
truly who God made us to be, we need to be in community. A
community of Christians can provide vital encouragement and
support for our spiritual lives. The New Testament warns us not to
neglect fellowship with other believers. Furthermore, much of what
the New Testament teaches (like loving your neighbor) is impossible
to live out unless you are in community.
2. What problems did the early church face? [14.9]
There was no ideal or perfect church in the early days of Christianity.
The New Testament shows that the early churches were just as
broken as our own. They faced problems with ethnic prejudice,
popularity contests, sexual misconduct, and legalism. They also
struggled with doubt, practicing what they preached, compromising
the faith, and enduring persecution.
Note: The letters to the seven churches in Revelation 2-3 detail
additional problems. These include decreasing love and work for
God, holding on to or tolerating idolatrous teaching, relying on
one's past reputation, leaving tasks incomplete, indifference, and
boasting (Revelation 2:4-5, 14-15, 20; 3:1-2, 15-17).
3. How do the New Testament letters encourage you as you think
about the problems in your own church? [14.9]
Answers will vary.
4. Why do we try to be people we are not? [14.10]
Answers will vary. We may try to be someone were not to fit in
with others, to be liked, or to be respected. We may feel pressure to
conform to others expectations, sacrificing our own desires to be the
people they want us to be.
5. Who does God want us to be? [14.10]
God longs for us to become most truly ourselves—that is, people
like Jesus who love God and others and reach their God-given
human potential. And he stops at nothing to help us reach that goal,
even giving his own life for us.
6. What does it mean to be the bride of Christ? [14.10]
In Revelation, the church is called the bride of Christ. To be the bride
of Christ is to be the recipient of Jesus' abundant and unfailing
love. In Ephesians, Jesus is pictured as the ideal husband who self-
sacrificially lays down his life for the church. As Christ's bride, we are
intimately united to him, and when we are finally glorified, we will
rejoice with him at the great marriage supper of the Lamb.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. According to Scripture, what does being human mean?
Scripture teaches that we are made in God's image. We were not made
by mistake. God planned to make us, and he values us. In fact, we are
so valuable to God that Christ died so we could become God's children
by faith. The Bible also teaches that we are made for relationships with
God, ourselves, others, and creation. God made us so that we can make
choices, love others, and have responsibility for ruling the earth.
2. Why do humans have a need for God?
We need God to live every day, to forgive us of our sins, and to have
eternal life. The Bible teaches that we are limited. We are not gods but
instead are dependent on God himself. Human beings are also sinful.
We inherited a sinful nature from Adam and Eve. Because of this, we
are also mortal. Our bodies decay. Through faith in Christ, we can
obtain eternal life and be transformed inside and out, receiving new
bodies that will be immortal.
3. Why is it important that Jesus was fully human in addition to
being fully God?
Jesus' being fully human, in addition to being fully God, is important
because it shows us that being human, in itself, is not the problem.
Jesus is our model for how to be fully submitted to the Father and to
live holy lives. Jesus did not withdraw from sociery in order to minister.
He gave us an example for how to follow him while dealing with the
demands we face in ordinary life.
4. What did Jews and Gentiles think of the Incarnation, and how
does this relate to Docetism?
Jews thought that the idea of God becoming human was blasphemous.
Greeks thought that idea was foolish. Both groups were appalled at
the idea that the divine God would choose to reveal himself as a lowly
human. As a result, some embraced Docetism, teaching that Jesus was
completely divine and only appeared to be human. But the church
rejected Docetism as a heresy.
5. What did Jesus mean when he commanded his disciples to "be
perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect" (Matthew 5:48)?
The word translated as perfect comes from the Greek telios, which
means that something or someone is complete, perfect, or spiritually
mature. By commanding his disciples to be perfect, Jesus did not
demand something impossible or expect that his disciples would never
sin or make a mistake. Rather, what Jesus meant is that his disciples
should aim for holiness. They should try to become spiritually mature
and complete.

6. Explain what happens to our character as we follow Jesus. What are some
ways we can glorify God?
As we follow Jesus, our character is transformed to become more holy. God
works in our hearts to help us change and grow more like Jesus. As we mature,
we try to follow God's standards rather than the standards of the culture we
live in. Our behavior, also, is changed as we more frequently act out of love. To
glorify God is to make God famous by worshipping him and attesting to his
goodness through holiness and acts of love. We glorify God by worshipping him,
living holy lives, and loving and telling them about him.
7. What is glorification, and when will it happen?
Glorification is the doctrine that all believers will be finally freed from sin,
restored, and given new, eternal bodies. Our glorification will be completed
when Jesus returns and God's kingdom is fully realized.
8. What is Arianism?
Arianism is a heresy that arose because people found it difficult to accept that
Jesus was not only fully human, but also fully God. Arianism is the heretical
teaching that Jesus was only a divine being created by the Father.
9. Why is it necessary to serve others when you follow Jesus? If you are a
servant, can you be a leader?
To follow Jesus, you have to imitate his example. Jesus gave us the supreme
example of a servant when he chose to become a human being, obeying the
Father and dying on the cross for our sake. Jesus described himself as a servant
who came to seek and save the lost. He told his disciples to follow his example
of servanthood. He did this after washing the apostles feet, a job reserved for the
lowest slaves. In fact, the Greek word translated servant in the New Testament
means slave. Being a servant does not mean you can't be a leader: it means that
when you lead, you seek to serve others and exercise authority for their benefit.
10. What did Jesus mean when he said his followers should pick up their
cross daily?
By saying that those who follow him should pick up their cross daily, Jesus was
saying that they should be committed to follow him despite the difficulties,
trouble, suffering, or persecution they might experience. For followers of Jesus,
suffering is not so much a matter of if, but when.
11. What advice does Scripture give to believers who suffer?
The Bible says that if we experience persecution as a result of following Jesus, we
will be blessed and inherit the kingdom of God. It says that Jesus promises to
be with us, will never abandon us, and will ultimately bring justice and reward
those who are faithful to him. The suffering we experience on earth cannot
be compared to the future glory that will be revealed for the children of God.
Suffering is preparing us for future glory. In the meantime, God works in all
situations for our good, and nothing can separate us from his love.
12. What is Christian hope? How does it compare to optimism or pessimism?
Christians have hope because that hope is based on Jesus. Christian hope is the quality
of looking honestly at the world and trusting in what Jesus has done and will do. Unlike
optimism, Christian hope does not ignore or minimize the evil in the world. It says that
pain and suffering are real, but Jesus will make things right in the end. Death, sin, suffering,
and evil will end. Unlike pessimism, Christian hope does not ignore or minimize the good
in the world. Rather, it sees the value of what is good. It waits for the ultimate fulfillment of
God's promises when his kingdom is established and all things are made new.
13. How did Peter, Martha, and Paul grow in Christlikeness?
Peter, Martha, and Paul grew in Christlikeness as they followed Jesus. Their lives showed
their transformation. At first, Peter said things without thinking, He denied Christ three
times. Later, he was a hypocrite because he wanted to please others. But Peter also accepted
correction and learned to be humble. Eventually, Peter died as a martyr. At first, Martha
complained that Mary sat at Jesus' feet instead of helping her. But Martha changed as
she learned to listen to Jesus instead of focusing on her own concerns. Paul changed from
persecuting Christians to being willing to suffer persecution for Christ's sake. He told others
to follow him in the same way he followed Christ. He taught many people, including
Timothy, whom he mentored. Like Peter, Paul died as a martyr.
14. Why is growing in Christlikeness something that happens in community:
Many of the things that Christians are called to do, such as loving and serving others,
cannot happen if a Christian is isolated and cut off from relationship with others. Being
in community gives us the opportunity to fully obey Christ. It also provides us with
companionship, encouragement, and protection.
15. Were the New Testament churches perfect? Explain.
No, the New Testament churches experienced many problems. These early churches were
the first recipients of the epistles that are part of the New Testament. The epistles addressed
these problems that included disunity, popularity contests, sexual misconduct, false teaching,
persecution, doubt, hypocrisy, and the temptation to compromise their faith.
16. Why does the Bible give us examples of disciples and churches that were not perfect?
The Bible's examples of imperfect disciples and churches show us that even though in Christ
we are new creations, becoming like Christ is a lifelong journey. Problems still arise among
Christians because we are all sinners. Following Jesus is not always easy. We still have a sin
nature. We face pain, trouble, and sometimes even persecution. Spiritual growth means we
must persevere, seek to overcome sin, obey God's commands, walk with the Spirit, and try
to encourage our brothers and sisters in the faith as we all seek to become more like Christ.
17. Who does God want us to become?
God wants us to become the people he made us to be. He wants us to reach our full
potential. Because we are broken and sinful, Jesus died so that, through faith in him, we
could be forgiven and restored. As the Holy Spirit works in us, God will ultimately make
us to be like his Son and give us glorious new bodies so we can live eternally, glorifying and
praising him.
