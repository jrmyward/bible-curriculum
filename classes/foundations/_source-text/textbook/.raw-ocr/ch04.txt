Academically, postmodernism developed as
scholars responded to the failure of modern science
and technology to produce a utopian existence. As
a response, postmodernists turned their attention
away from "capital T Truth" to small, localized truths that were constructed through
people's individual and cultural experiences. In other words, postmodernism says there
are no universal truths that apply to all people. Instead, all you can know is your own
"truth," which is determined by your experience. Postmodernism assumes the concept of
relativism.
Culturally, postmodernism affects how we think about everything, including the Bible.
Postmodernists say that we can only really know our descriptions of the world, not the
world itself. Our talk about the world, they say, isn't really about the world at all, but about
us. In 1968, Roland Barthes wrote "The Death of the Author," a short essay in which he
argued that we should not value the origin of a text (the author's intentions) as much as
its destination (the reader's experience). Readers should free themselves from the author's
single intended meaning and invent new meanings.
If we cannot really know the world, then we certainly cannot learn anything about the
world from a written book. According to postmodernism, there is no truth in the Bible to
be discovered, only our own interpretations. Postmodernism assumes that what you think
is true, may be true for you, but that doesn't mean it is true for anyone else.
If we accept the assumptions of postmodernism, we can basically make the Bible say
anything we want it to say; we don't have to concern ourselves with what God actually
wants to communicate to us. According to postmodernism, we can invent our own
meanings and use the Bible in any way we want.
Let's consider a few examples of this self-centered approach to the Bible: Ignoring
passages that speak about suffering, some people have taught that the Bible is all about
how to gain health and wealth and live a prosperous life. Dangerous cult leaders have used
portions of Scripture to lead people to support evil causes. Slave traders used the Bible to
justify slavery.
These are all tragic examples of people taking Scripture out of context to suit their
own personal desires. Though not all fauly interpretations reflect postmodern thinking,
postmodernism provides no grounds for us to say an interpretation is wrong; it holds that
every single interpretation is equally valid.
All of us inevitably read the Bible through our own cultural lens, experience, or
sometimes self-centered motives. However, we must try to be aware of these things and
not allow them to determine what the Bible means. If we don't, we not only miss what
God wants to communicate to us, but we are also in danger of misapplying Scripture. To
guard against this danger, we must learn how to read Scripture correctly. To get started,
let's look at some common mistakes and see how we might avoid them.

Good hermeneutics begins with another term theologians

and therefore to more intelligent reading of the Bible, is to learn to read the text carefully
and to ask the right questions of the text."'I Attention must be paid to both the details
and the big picture. To make interpreting Scripture easier, let's take a look at a seven-step
process.
Step 1: Choose a good translation. Since most of us cannot read in Hebrew, Aramaic, or
Greek (the original languages the Bible was written in), we will need a solid translation to
study God's Word. There are three types of Bible translations according to Fee and Stuart:
1. Formal equivalence translations: These translations "attempt to keep as close to the
'form' of the Hebrew or Greek, both words and grammar, as can be conveniently put
into understandable English." Examples are the King James Version (KJV), the New
King James Version (NK)V), the New American Standard Bible (NASB), and the
English Standard Version (ESV).
2. Functional (dynamic) equivalence translations: These translations "attempt to keep
the meaning of the Hebrew or Greek but to put their words and idioms into what
would be the normal way of saying the same thing in English." Examples are the
New American Bible (NAB), the New Living Translation (NLT), and the New
International Version (NIV).
3. Free translations or paraphrases: These translations "attempt to translate the ideas
from one language to another, with less concern about using the exact words of the
original. A free translation ... tries to eliminate as much of the historical distance as
possible and still be faithful to the text." Examples are the New English Bible (NEB),
the Living Bible (LB), and The Message (MSG).2

Step 5: Look for relationships. If you look closely, you will see many interesting parallels
in Scripture. For example, Matthew describes Jesus being led into the wilderness, where
he fasted for 40 days and nights. He was then tempted by the devil to turn stones into
bread. Jesus answered, "It is written, Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word

passage Jesus quoted, we find this is where Moses gave instructions to Israel. By comparing
and contrasting Jesus' 40 days with the Israelites 40 years in the wilderness, we see
stunning parallels. God's Word is full of recurring themes and symbols. Identifying such
connections helps us have a fuller understanding of what God is communicating to us.
Step 6: Use Bible helps. Bible commentaries are helpful tools to understand both the
context and the content of Scripture. According to Fee and Stuart, a good commentary
should supply three things: "(1) helps on sources and information about the historical
context, (2) answers to [many] content questions, and (3) thorough discussions of difficult
texts as to the possibilities of meaning, along with the supporting arguments."5
A good commentary, or set of commentaries, digs into the theological issues rather than
merely sharing the author's experiences. It offers balanced discussions of possible meanings
of the text rather than proposing only what the author prefers. The best commentaries also
give insights into the culture and historical background of each biblical book, as well as
points of interest regarding the Hebrew and Greek words used.
Step 7: Courageously pursue a response. We are to be "doers" of the word, not hearers
only (James 1:22). That said, not every passage is meant as a command or point we must
act upon. Most of the Bible is descriptive in nature. It tells of past events, explores the lives
of our ancestors, and provides a record of God's plan of redemprion. A descriptive passage
describes what happened and teaches us through inference and example. It gives us a sense
of where the people of God have been so that we might discern how to live in our present
situation more faithfully.
A prescriptive passage, on the other hand, provides specific hallmarks of how we ought
to live. There are many prescriptive passages in the Bible. They reflect something of
God's nature and the created order and thus give commands to be obeyed or values to be
adopted. Examples include the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:2-17), the Sermon on
the Mount (Matthew 5:1-16), and the Great Commission (Matthew 28:16-20).

Scripture. A good way to avoid this problem is to talk to others about what you are
reading in the Bible. Try finding a trusted friend or mentor, or a Bible study where you
can regularly share what you learn and hear what others are learning.
3. Live what you're learning. God's Word is powerful and often God meets us with
specific promptings about how we should live differently. Pray for courage and commit
to putting God's Word into practice!
10. Conclusion
Some people read Scripture and steadily increase in faith. Others read and grow doubtful
because of difficult passages or perhaps because they encounter a God in Scripture they
didn't quite expect. If you experience doubt, you're not alone. Very godly people have had
doubts. John the Baptist experienced doubt that Jesus was
actually the Messiah (Matthew 11:2-3). Peter doubted
when he was walking on the water to Jesus (Matthew
14:22-33). Thomas wouldn't believe Jesus had risen until
he placed his hands in Jesus wounds (John 20:24-29).
One of the most celebrated figures of the Protestant
Reformation, Martin Luther, also experienced grave
doubts. Ten years after posting his 95 theses on the
church door at Wittenberg, Luther wrote of an agonizing
experience of doubt: "For more than a week I was close to
the gates of death and hell. I trembled in all my members.
Christ was wholly lost. I was shaken by desperation and
blasphemy of God."16
Reflecting on the experience, Luther emphasized the
importance of being anchored in truth in the middle of
such experiences: "All this is written for our comfort that we should see how deeply God
hides his face and how we must not go by our feeling but only by his Word."
In the moment, doubt can feel paralyzing, causing worry and fear; but the truth is
that doubt is actually a healthy part of spiritual growth. If we handle it properly, we can
grow in greater understanding and confidence by asking good questions and courageously
pursuing answers. Ultimately, like Luther, we can find confidence if we are anchored in
Scripture—Scripture which we have carefully studied and explored, as we are guided by
the Holy Spirit.
When you experience doubts about the Bible, or have questions, don't bury them. Look
for answers in commentaries and other resources. Share your thoughts with a trusted
FOUNDATIONS S
pastor, teacher, or mentor. Find a trained theologian, take him or her out for coffee, and
share what's on your heart. God never intended for us to try and grow spiritually all by
ourselves. Spiritual growth takes place in community. The church is a team. An army. A
body. A family. A hospital. No church is perfect, and many people have been involved
with one for a long time and yet not grasped what the Bible is all abour. Maybe part of
your role is to help them grow too.
In the end, remember this: God is shaping every believer into the image of his Son:
"Now the Lord is the Spirit, and where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is freedom. And we
all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the
same image from one degree of glory to another" (2 Corinthians 3:17-18). And one of
the ways this happens is through "the word of God, which is at work in you believers"
(1 Thessalonians 2:13). God's Word will transform us if we will read and study it carefully.
Pick up the Bible. Read it! As you do, you'll learn that it carries many significant
implications for understanding God and ourselves.
