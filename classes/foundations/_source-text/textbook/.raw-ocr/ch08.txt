10. Conclusion

God created a perfect world and made humans in his image to rule and reign with him
over all creation. However, humans rebelled against God. This brought sin into the world
and sent people on a downward spiral of chaos and ruin. Determined not to give up on
his creation, God chose one family to bring blessing to all the families of the earth. The
descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob formed a great nation called Israel. Through
Israel, God desired to set up a kingdom of priests that would show God's lovingkindness
to all nations. He rescued Israel from slavery, made a covenant with them, and made it
possible for them to live in his presence. But over and over, Israel failed to live up to the
covenant. They quickly fell into idolatry and wickedness.
God raised up judges and kings to lead his people, but most fell prey to the destructive
power of sin. The prophets warned of God's judgment and urged the leaders and the
people to return to God, but they refused. Eventually, the people of God were captured by
their enemies and taken into exile. Even then, God did not abandon them. He was with
those who were faithful, and he revealed himself as sovereign over all nations. As Daniel's
time was drawing to a close, a new king came
to power who would allow the Israelites to
return to their homeland.
The metanarrative of the Bible reveals that
God is absolutely committed to the world and
the people he created. He is patient, kind,
generous, forgiving, and merciful, but also
just, and unwilling to let sin run on forever
without judgment. The characters in this
grand story-Abraham, Moses, David, and
many more—reveal God's persistence in
working through broken and corrupt people
to bring redemption and blessing to all. And
just as God did not abandon Israel when they
failed, so God does not abandon us even in our worst moments. The Bible reveals a God
who is trustworthy and faithful, even when we are not (2 Timothy 2:13).
You might expect that the next chapter will tell the story of Jesus. But between the end
of the Old Testament and the beginning of the New, some 400 years passed when the
faith of God's people was put to the ultimate test. In the next chapter, we'll look at what
happened in the time between Malachi and Matthew.
