6. How We Got the Bible
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to determine how the canon of Scripture was
selected.
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to describe the three criteria used to select the
books of the New Testament.
As previously noted, the Bible is a collection of 66 books and letters written over 1,500
years by many different writers. But many other works were written during this time
period. How do we know which books are God's revelation? Why aren't some of those
other works included in our Bible? How did Christians decide upon these 66 books?
In the first centuries of church history, certain books stood out to believers as being
divinely inspired. These books make up what scholars call the canon of Scripture, the
collection of biblical writings commonly acknowledged as genuine and authoritative. Canon
comes from the Greek kanón, meaning a rule or standard for testing straightness. Likewise,
the term came to be applied to the catalog of sacred books held to be God's inspired Word
since it is the authoritative standard for morality and guide for our lives.
The Old Testament canon had mostly been established by the time of Jesus. It
contained the majority of books that are in our Old Testament today. The New Testament
canon of 27 books was ratified at the Third Council of Carthage (AD 397). However, the
books of the Bible weren't made canonical by any council. They were simply recognized
as such, meaning the Council recognized which books were being used and considered
authoritative by most Christians in the early church.

belief that truth, knowledge, or morality is relative to the

10. Conclusion
Let's review. Christianity says the Bible is the inspired Word of God in both the Old and
New Testaments. Through it, the almighty God, the Creator of the universe, speaks to
humankind. The Bible is God's revelation, not a rule book or checklist of instructions. As
one theologian says:
The Bible is not a book like any other. It makes a claim that God spoke and speaks
through its message. It argues that as his creatures, we are accountable to him for
what he has revealed. The trustworthiness of Scripture points to its authority as well.

It is a book that calls us to examine our lives and relationship to God. Beyond the
fascinating history, it contains vital and life-transforming truths about God and us?
