## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 8 days** — by *merging*, not cutting.
The calendar shapes the block hard: **two short Wednesdays** (Day 1 Apr 21 and Day 5 Apr 28) and
**two sub days** (Day 3 Mon Apr 26 and Day 8 Mon May 3 — the teacher is out for golf both days).
Day 3 is a real content day, so it takes the publisher's fully self-contained **Videos** lesson,
where a sub only presses play; Day 8 is the **test**, which is naturally sub-friendly. The two short
Wednesdays carry the chapter opener (trimmed to fit) and the **Live, Love, Collage** activity, which
is built to run in 45–50 minutes and adds no new tested content.

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 17.10A` (**/55**) is **multiple choice (14 × 1 = 14 pts) · true/false (8 × 1 = 8 pts) ·
fill-in-the-blank (7 × 2 = 14 pts) · short answer (3 × 3 = 9 pts) · two essays (2 × 5 = 10 pts).** Its
weight lands on the **four Greek loves** — *agape, eros, philia, storge* — their definitions and why
*eros, philia,* and *storge* go corrupt without *agape* (multiple-choice, fill-in, and a short answer
all target this); the **key verses** (John 15:12, Romans 13:8–10, Matthew 5:43–45, Leviticus 19:13–18,
Psalm 135:15–18); **idolatry** and how idols make us like them; **why humans can't build a perfect
society**; **loving ourselves** rightly; **Jesus's many demonstrations of agape** (a five-point essay);
the **Good Samaritan / who is my neighbor** (the other essay, with Leviticus 19); **dual citizenship**
and Christian responsibilities; **shalom, the three institutions** (family, church, government) and
**intermediary institutions**; **basar / one human family**; the **four questions for building strong
communities**; the **four "loves, loyalties, longings, liturgies" questions**; and the **five
principles for loving enemies**. Day 1 (the four loves), Day 4 (Jesus's love + the Good Samaritan),
and Day 6 (community, citizenship, enemies) carry the most weight; Day 7 review pulls it together.

## Day 1 — Wed Apr 21 · The two greatest commandments & the four loves (17.1–17.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) discuss why keeping the two greatest commandments matters and why we
can't build a perfect society on willpower alone; (b) distinguish the four Greek words for love —
*agape, eros, philia, storge.* `[17.1, 17.2]` *(High test weight — the four loves run through the
multiple-choice, fill-in, and short-answer sections.)*

**Materials.** The graded Ch 16 tests (to return); student textbooks; class Bibles, paper, and pen for
Hunting for Love.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:08 · Return & review the Chapter 16 test.** Hand back Ch 16 tests. Walk the two or three
  items most students missed. Keep it brief — close last chapter's gaps before they harden, then move
  on. **Announce the collage:** everyone brings a poster board, scissors, and magazines / printed
  images for next Wednesday (Day 5).
- **0:08–0:26 · Content & discuss 17.1–17.2 (manual p.356–357).** The **two greatest commandments**:
  love the Lord with all your heart, soul, and mind, and love your neighbor as yourself — *"on these
  two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets"* (Matt 22:37–40). Every OT law rested on
  *love God, love neighbor.* Then the hard truth: even if everyone agreed to keep them, we couldn't —
  **sin** means we all fail to love as we ought, so we all contribute to the mess. We don't need more
  willpower; we need **new hearts**, which Jesus gives. Then the **four Greek loves** — write them on
  the board and keep them there all block: **agape** (selfless, self-giving love — the love God has
  for us), **eros** (romantic love and desire), **philia** (love of friends, family, community), and
  **storge** (natural affection). Land the C. S. Lewis point: *eros, philia,* and *storge* are all
  good in their place, but they **go corrupt without agape.** Use manual Qs 1–4 (p.356–357).
- **0:26–0:44 · Activity: Hunting for Love (four groups; manual p.357).** Divide into four groups.
  Each group searches their Bibles for a verse or story that shows each of the four loves — *agape,
  eros, phileo, storge.* First group to match all four correctly wins; check answers against the
  manual's examples (e.g., agape → the "love chapter," 1 Cor 13; eros → Isaac and Rebekah, Gen 24;
  phileo → David and Jonathan, 1 Sam 20; storge → Moses' mother hiding him, Exod 2). Groups share
  their approved verses. *Purpose:* the four loves are the test's most-hit topic — this makes students
  find each one in the actual text.
- **0:44–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **17.3–17.4** (ST 236–239) — loving God and receiving his love — for tomorrow.
**Assessments.** Hunting for Love (participation — correct matches).
**Notes.** Culture-and-content opener on a 45-minute clock. If the mini-lecture runs long, Hunting for
Love is the flex item — it can shrink to 12 minutes and still land the four loves. Leave the four
loves on the board through the block.

## Day 2 — Thu Apr 22 · Loving God & receiving his love (17.3–17.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain how to love God — by seeking and enjoying him, obeying him,
and rejecting idolatry; (b) relate receiving God's love for themselves to their ability to love
others. `[17.3, 17.4]`

**Materials.** `TR 17.2A Six Views on Love` (1 per student) + the answer key; class Bibles and pens.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up.** *"Can you love someone you don't always feel warm toward?"* Surface a few
  answers; name the point of today — loving God is more than feelings.
- **0:06–0:30 · Read & discuss 17.3–17.4 (manual p.358–359).** **Loving God**: we love him because he
  first loved us (1 John 4:19); we love him by seeking him, enjoying his presence, obeying his
  commands, and loving other people — John says if we don't love others we can't claim to love God
  (1 John 4:20). Loving God means putting him first, which means **rejecting idolatry** — the sin of
  worshipping anything other than God. An idol can be a good thing valued above God or a bad thing we
  get addicted to (money, sex, status, fame), and **we become like what we worship** — shallow,
  unstable, uncaring (Ps 135:15–18). Then **receiving God's love**: before we can love others we must
  accept his love for ourselves, resting in our identity as God's beloved children — not thinking of
  ourselves *more* highly than we ought (Rom 12:3), but not *less* than God has made us either. Close
  with Stonestreet's four self-examining questions — *loves, loyalties, longings, liturgies* — that
  connect love for God to the habits that shape us. Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:30–0:56 · Activity: Six Views on Love (`TR 17.2A`, individual; manual p.358).** Hand out
  `TR 17.2A`. Students read the instructions and complete the worksheet, contrasting the **biblical
  view of love** with the views of other worldviews. Then discuss their answers as a class using the
  answer key. *Purpose:* students see that "love" means very different things depending on the
  worldview behind it — and why the biblical *agape* is the one that holds.
- **0:56–0:60 · Close & homework.** *"Which of the four questions — loves, loyalties, longings,
  liturgies — is hardest to answer honestly?"*

**Homework.** Read **17.5–17.6** (ST 239–241) — how Jesus loved and who our neighbor is — for
**Tuesday**. *(Monday is a sub day — a video set that counts like any class day; no reading due
Monday.)*
**Assessments.** Six Views on Love worksheet (completion).
**Notes.** Tell students Monday is a sub day and walk them through the routine so they arrive ready.
The *loves / loyalties / longings / liturgies* set is a near-certain test item (multiple choice) —
say the four words out loud more than once.

## Day 3 — Mon Apr 26 · **SUB DAY** · Love, citizenship & clichés video set (Lesson 7)

## Day 4 — Tue Apr 27 · How Jesus loved & who is my neighbor (17.5–17.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) give specific examples of the ways Jesus demonstrated *agape* love;
(b) summarize what loving your neighbor means, using Leviticus 19 and the parable of the Good
Samaritan. `[17.5, 17.6]` *(High test weight — Jesus's love is a five-point essay, and the Good
Samaritan is the other essay plus a true/false item.)*

**Materials.** Student textbooks; class Bibles. *(Ditch the Clique needs no materials.)*

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Read & discuss 17.5–17.6 (manual p.360–361).** **How Jesus loved:** walk the textbook's
  list of the many ways Jesus showed *agape* — he spent time with people, listened, served (washed
  feet), grieved with the hurting, told the truth, honored women and children, advocated for the poor,
  welcomed outsiders, gave his life, forgave his enemies, empowered others, and prayed for others. His
  command back to us: *"love one another as I have loved you"* (John 15:12). Then **who is my
  neighbor:** in **Leviticus 19:13–18** God spelled out how Israel was to treat one another (don't
  oppress, rob, slander, hate, or take vengeance; judge justly; love your neighbor as yourself); and
  in the **parable of the Good Samaritan** (Luke 10) Jesus answered *"who is my neighbor?"* — my
  neighbor is anyone who needs mercy, even an enemy, and being a neighbor means sacrificial action.
  Use manual Qs 1–5.
- **0:24–0:44 · Activity: Ditch the Clique (three groups; manual p.361).** Three groups each run a
  short demonstration of an "outsider" trying to join a group of "insiders": Group 1 forms a tight,
  resistant circle; Group 2 a loose circle that eventually welcomes; Group 3 a moving line that
  actively goes and invites the outsider in. Debrief: how do prejudices and cliques keep Christians
  from reaching out, how do they hurt the local church, and how can we intentionally invite outsiders
  in? *Purpose:* students *feel* what exclusion does, then connect it to the Good Samaritan's
  boundary-crossing love.
- **0:44–0:58 · Circle back to the neighbor.** Tie the demonstration to the parable: the priest and
  Levite kept their distance; the Samaritan crossed the line. *Who is the "outsider" you're tempted to
  pass by?* Keep it honest, not preachy.
- **0:58–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **17.7–17.10** (ST 241–244) — community, citizenship, loving enemies, and the
conclusion — for **Thursday**. *(Tomorrow's short Wednesday is the collage — bring your materials; no
new reading due for it.)*
**Assessments.** Ditch the Clique (participation) + neighbor discussion.
**Notes.** The "five ways Jesus modeled agape" essay and the Leviticus-19-plus-Good-Samaritan essay
both come straight from today. If a class is discussion-heavy, this is a good day to let it run — both
essays reward students who can *name specifics.*

## Day 5 — Wed Apr 28 · Live, Love, Collage (Lesson 6)

*Short ~45-min period. This activity asks students to illustrate their answers to a set of four
textbook questions, making the chapter's ideas personal and visual. It carries no new tested content,
so the shortened Wednesday costs nothing on the test — and the two question sets it draws on are both
test targets.*

**Objective.** Students illustrate their answers to four questions about either the life habits that
help Christians flourish (17.4) or how to build strong communities (17.7).

**Materials.** Glue sticks or transparent tape (one per student); students' own poster board,
scissors, and magazines / newspapers / photos / printed images.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:08 · Set up (manual p.366).** Students get their materials ready. Each student picks **one**
  of two question sets for their collage: the **17.4 set** — *What are my loves? loyalties? longings?
  liturgies?* (we covered these on Day 2) — or the **17.7 set** — *What's good we can protect? broken
  we can fix? evil we can stop? missing we can contribute?* (from the reading due Thursday). Point
  students who haven't gotten to 17.7 yet toward the 17.4 set, which they already know cold.
- **0:08–0:36 · Build.** Students write their four questions on the poster board and arrange
  cut-out images around each question that represent their answers, then glue or tape them down.
  Circulate — push them past the first obvious picture toward one that actually says something true
  about *their* loves or *their* community.
- **0:36–0:44 · Present.** As many students as time allows hold up their collage and explain one
  question and its images. *(If the room is large, do a gallery walk instead — posters on the wall,
  students circulate.)*
- **0:44–0:45 · Homework reminder.**

**Homework.** Finish **17.7–17.10** (ST 241–244) for tomorrow if you haven't; begin light review of
the four loves.
**Assessments.** Collage (participation / synthesis).
**Notes.** Both question sets are on the test — the *loves / loyalties / longings / liturgies* set is
a multiple-choice item, and the *good / broken / evil / missing* set is a short-answer item. Keep a
few finished collages up through Day 7 as free review.

## Day 6 — Thu Apr 29 · Community, citizenship, loving enemies & the conclusion (17.7–17.10, merged)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) express how Christians build healthy communities by pursuing
*shalom*; (b) explain Christian **dual citizenship** and our responsibilities as citizens of God's
kingdom; (c) apply the **five principles for loving enemies**; (d) name the defining marks of a
Christian. `[17.7, 17.8, 17.9, 17.10]` *(Merges publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the four closing
subsections. High test weight: dual citizenship, the three institutions, basar, and the five
principles run through every section.)*

**Materials.** `TR 17.4A Three Scenarios` (cut into three strips); computers for the Good Samaritans
research; `TR 17.5A Overcoming Evil with Good Answers` (for you); class Bibles and pens.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:22 · Read & discuss 17.7–17.10 (manual p.362–365).** Move through the four sections.
  **Community (17.7):** we love neighbors by pursuing **shalom** — peace and wholeness — in the three
  institutions Luther named (**family, church, government**) and in the **intermediary institutions**
  (schools, hospitals, service organizations). Note **basar** — the Hebrew for "flesh" that also means
  "clan": Adam and Eve became **one human family**, so everyone is our neighbor. Stonestreet's four
  building questions: *what's good / broken / evil / missing?* **Citizenship (17.8):** Christians have
  **dual citizenship** — first of God's kingdom, then of an earthly nation; we live peaceably, obey
  laws, pay taxes, pray for and respect authorities, and seek justice, while giving **ultimate
  allegiance to God alone** and resisting commands to do evil. **Loving enemies (17.9):** Dr. King's
  five principles — *start by looking at yourself, look for the good in others, choose love rather
  than hate, guard your heart, persevere.* **Conclusion (17.10):** love is the **primary defining
  mark of a Christian.** Use manual Qs from p.362–365 — favor the five principles and dual citizenship.
- **0:22–0:44 · Activity: Good Samaritans (three groups; manual p.363).** Hand each group one strip
  from `TR 17.4A Three Scenarios` — (1) Good Samaritan laws, (2) the bystander effect, (3) the
  consequences of sharing the gospel in a Communist country. Groups research their concept, read Mark
  12:17; Acts 5:25–29; James 1:5 and 4:17, and analyze how Christian principles apply. Each group
  presents its conclusion. *Purpose:* students take dual citizenship out of the abstract and into
  actual civic dilemmas.
- **0:44–0:58 · Activity: Overcoming Evil with Good (four groups; manual p.364).** Quick version:
  assign each group one pair — David and Saul, Joseph and his brothers, Rahab and the spies, or Sarah
  and Hagar — to evaluate how the biblical character responded to an enemy. Groups skim their passages
  and answer the student-manual questions; two or three groups share. Grade against `TR 17.5A`.
- **0:58–0:60 · Close & homework.** *"Which of King's five principles would be hardest for you right
  now — and why?"*

**Homework.** Begin studying for the Chapter 17 Test (Study Guide, SM 352; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Good Samaritans presentations (participation) + Overcoming Evil with Good (completion).
**Notes.** This is the block's fullest day — four sections and two activities. **Overcoming Evil with
Good is the flex item:** if the class runs slow, shorten it to one shared pair or assign the
student-manual questions as homework rather than cut the Good Samaritans research, which maps directly
onto the dual-citizenship test items.

- **0:56–0:60 · Test preview.** Monday: 55 points — multiple choice, true/false, fill-in, short answer,

**Notes.** If Day 6 (community/citizenship/enemies) felt rushed, this is the place to reteach — it is

## Day 8 — Mon May 3 · **SUB DAY** · Chapter 17 Test (Lesson 10)

*Teacher absence (golf). A test is naturally sub-friendly — the sub distributes, monitors, and

**Objective.** Demonstrate mastery of Chapter 17.

**Materials.** `TR 17.10A Chapter 17 Test` — 1 per student (from [portal/ch17](../_source-text/portal/ch17/));

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:04 · Settle & distribute.** Sub takes attendance, clears desks, and hands out the test
  (55 pts).
- **0:04–0:52 · Test.** Students complete the test independently. Early finishers may begin reading
  **18.1–18.2** (Chapter 18).
- **0:52–0:60 · Collect.** Sub collects every test and any scratch paper.

**Sub instructions (verbatim-ready).** *"Take attendance and have students clear their desks. Hand
each student one copy of the Chapter 17 Test. They work silently and on their own — no notes, no
phones, no talking. Anyone who finishes early may read the start of Chapter 18 (page 245) quietly.
Collect every test at the end of the period, even from students who didn't finish, and leave them on

**Homework.** Read **18.1–18.2** (ST 245–248) — start of Chapter 18, the conclusion on Jesus.
**Assessments.** **Chapter 17 Test — collected and graded** (`TR 17.10A`, /55). Trimester 3 grade.
**Notes.** Return and review the test early in the Chapter 18 block so misconceptions (especially the
four loves and dual citizenship) don't harden — and because Chapter 18 is the final chapter, it's the
