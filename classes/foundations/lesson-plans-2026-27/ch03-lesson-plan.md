## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 8 days** — by *merging*, not cutting.
The calendar shapes the block: **Day 2 (Mon Sep 21) is a sub day** (teacher absent), and **Day 4
(Wed Sep 23) is a short ~45-min period**. So Day 1 opens the chapter with the teacher in the room,
the sub day carries the publisher's fully self-contained Videos lesson, and the short Wednesday
carries the one lesson that adds no new tested content — the *Recognize, Evaluate, Decide* game,
which is built to run in 40–45 minutes.

| 2 | **Mon Sep 21 · SUB** | Lesson 7 (Videos) | — | Independent video + questions |

**Two merges make the compression:** the four closing subsections **3.7–3.10** combine on Day 6
(publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the objections, the reach of God's authority, why we submit, and the
conclusion), and the publisher's separate Review (L8) and Study-Guide (L9) days combine on Day 7 —
the same review/study merge used in Chapters 1 and 2. Two lessons are also *resequenced* onto the
awkward calendar days rather than dropped: the **Videos** lesson (L7) moves to the front sub day
(Day 2), where a sub only has to press play, and the **Recognize, Evaluate, Decide** game (L6)
moves up to the short Wednesday (Day 4). Neither carries new test material, so the sub day and the
short period cost nothing on the test.

**Lean-layer note:** Chapter 3 needs **no newly generated handouts** — the publisher supplies every
activity (`PS 3.6A`, `PS 3.8A`, `TR 3.5A`, `TR 3.8A–B`) plus the discussion questions with answers,
the video questions with answers, and the Study Guide. This plan sequences and times them; it does
not reproduce them. Discussion questions are cited by their teacher-manual location; open

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 3.10A` (/60) is **multiple choice (11 pts) · true/false (7 pts) · fill-in-the-blank (9 items ×
2 = 18 pts) · short answer (3 × 3 = 9 pts) · two-plus essays (3 × 5 = 15 pts).** Its weight lands on
**external evidence** — the ancient non-Christian writers (Josephus, Tacitus, Pliny the Younger, the
Talmud), church fathers and eyewitness testimony, archaeological confirmations (Solomon's building
projects, Hezekiah's tunnel), fulfilled prophecy, the **Dead Sea Scrolls**, and the **25,000 NT
manuscripts / 99.5% agreement**; on the **three objections and their answers** (human authorship,
corruption / **textual variants**, and the **lost gospels** — with **gnosticism** and the **three
canon criteria** of apostolicity, orthodoxy, pedigree); on **internal evidence** (how Jesus affirmed
the Old Testament; what the writers claimed they were doing); on the **five areas of God's
authority** and **why God wants obedience** (flourishing, not control); and on **radical
individualism** and the airplane/blind-faith analogy. Day 5 (external NT evidence + preservation)
and Day 6 (objections, gnosticism, God's authority) carry the most weight, with Day 1 (Jesus and the
OT) and Day 3 (external OT evidence) close behind. Day 7 review pulls it together.

## Day 1 — Thu Sep 17 · Authority, "Jesus is Lord," and internal evidence from the OT (3.1–3.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) articulate what *authority* is and what it means to submit to God's
authority; (b) use internal evidence from the Old Testament to defend its authority; (c) give
specific examples of how Jesus confirmed his trust in the Old Testament. `[3.1, 3.2]`

**Materials.** The graded Ch 2 tests (to return); student textbooks; class Bibles, paper, and pen
for the pairs activity.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:10 · Return & review the Chapter 2 test.** Hand back Ch 2 tests. Walk the two or three
  items most students missed — expect **general vs. special revelation** to need reteaching. Keep it
  brief; the point is to close last chapter's gaps before they harden.
- **0:10–0:22 · Hook — "Who's in charge?" (ST 31–32).** Read or tell the opening: the poem
  *Invictus* ("I am the master of my fate, I am the captain of my soul") was the favorite of both
  Nelson Mandela and mass murderer Timothy McVeigh. *Authority* comes from a Latin word for
  influence or command; it answers **"Who's in charge?"** Land the chapter's thesis: **"question
  authority" usually means radical individualism, but everyone serves somebody — the question is not
  *whether* we'll submit to an authority but *which* one.** To confess **"Jesus is Lord"** is to
  place yourself under his authority (the NT word for *obey* means "to hear under").
- **0:22–0:40 · Read & discuss 3.1–3.2 (manual p.48–49).** God conveys his authority most clearly in
  Scripture. Internal evidence: across the Old Testament, phrases like **"Thus says the LORD"** and
  **"The Word of the LORD came to me"** occur **more than 3,000 times** — the writers claimed to be
  delivering God's authoritative revelation, not advice or good stories. Then Jesus' witness: he
  called Scripture unbreakable, treated OT stories and miracles as real history, leaned on it in his
  anguish, and said it pointed to him. Use manual Qs 1–4 (p.48–49): *Is it ever okay to question
  authority? What does it mean to confess "Jesus is Lord"? How did OT authors mark their writings as
  authoritative? Why does Jesus' affirmation matter?*
- **0:40–0:58 · Activity: Jesus and the Old Testament (pairs; manual p.49).** Assign each pair one
  question + its verse(s) — e.g., *How does Jesus confirm the divine origin of Scripture?* (Mark
  7:13); *…show Scripture is the final authority?* (Matt 15:3; 19:4–5); *…rely on the OT in
  distress?* (Matt 4:3–4; Mark 15:34). Pairs read, discuss, and write their answer, then present.
  Check against the bulleted list in 3.2 of the textbook. *Purpose:* students see, verse by verse,
  that Jesus treated the OT as God's Word.
- **0:58–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **3.3–3.4** (ST 35–37) — internal evidence from the New Testament and external
evidence for the Old Testament. *(Reminder for Monday: it's a sub day — a video set that counts like
any class day.)*
**Assessments.** Jesus-and-the-OT responses (participation).
**Notes.** Tell students Monday is a sub day and briefly explain the routine so they walk in ready.
Leave "Who's in charge? — everyone serves somebody" on the board through the block.

## Day 3 — Tue Sep 22 · Internal evidence from the NT; external evidence for the OT (3.3–3.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) describe the apostles' intent and authority for writing the New
Testament; (b) identify specific examples of external evidence — non-biblical sources,
archaeological discoveries, and fulfilled prophecy — that support the Old Testament's reliability.
`[3.3, 3.4]`

**Materials.** One poster board and markers per group of 3–4 (`Recognize the Evidence`).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:05 · Welcome back & connect the videos.** One-minute link: Friday you saw *how* the OT
  claims God's authority; the sub-day videos previewed the New Testament and its evidence — today we
  make it solid.
- **0:05–0:26 · Read & discuss 3.3–3.4 (manual p.50–51).** *Internal evidence, NT:* Jesus gave the
  apostles authority to bear witness (Matt 28:16–20); the Spirit inspired their writings; **John**
  reiterated old commands and gave new ones, **Peter** called Paul's letters "Scripture," and
  **Paul** claimed to write "a command of the Lord." Then the key limit: internal evidence alone
  doesn't *prove* the Bible — anyone can claim to write for God — but it shows the writers *believed*
  they spoke God's Word, and their consistent message across centuries makes that believable.
  *External evidence, OT:* historical events confirmed in non-biblical sources (Sennacherib's
  campaign, the fall of Nineveh, Jerusalem's fall to Nebuchadnezzar); **50+ biblical figures**
  confirmed archaeologically; **fulfilled prophecy** (Babylon's 70 years; the return from exile;
  Edom toppled; **300+ Messianic prophecies**). Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:26–0:52 · Activity: Recognize the Evidence (groups of 3–4; manual p.51).** Assign each group
  one category — (a) historical events confirmed by extra-biblical sources, (b) archaeological
  discoveries, or (c) fulfilled prophecies. On poster board, groups design **symbols, acronyms,
  rhymes, or visual cues** to help the class memorize two to four examples from their category (they
  needn't include every example). Groups present their memory aids. *Purpose:* the test asks
  students to *name* external evidence — this builds the recall hooks.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Which memory aid do you think you'll actually remember on the
  test — and why?*

**Homework.** Finish reading **3.5–3.6** (ST 38–40) if you haven't, for Thursday. *(Tomorrow's short
Wednesday is a thinking-skills game — no new reading due.)*
**Assessments.** Recognize the Evidence posters (participation/synthesis).
**Notes.** Keep the posters up on the wall through Day 7 — they're free review for the memory-heavy
external-evidence items.

## Day 4 — Wed Sep 23 · Recognize, Evaluate, Decide (Lesson 6)

*Short ~45-min period. This game teaches the analytical habit the whole chapter is after — recognize
a claim, evaluate the evidence, then decide — and its "Bible Telephone" round previews Thursday's
preservation lesson and Friday's "the Bible has been corrupted" objection. It carries no new tested
content, so the shortened Wednesday costs nothing on the test.*

**Objective.** Students apply analytical tools that help them evaluate and respond to the Bible's
claims to authority.

**Materials.** `PS 3.6A Recognize, Evaluate, Decide` (preview beforehand); index cards marked by
group color per slide 20; a pen per student; a stopwatch.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Set up (`PS 3.6A`, slides 1–2; manual p.58).** Introduce the acronym **Recognize →
  Evaluate → Decide** and read/discuss the prompt on slide 2: we constantly receive claims; the
  question is how we test them.
- **0:06–0:16 · Recognize — "Misconstrued" (slides 3–13).** Play the game on slides 4–11, then
  discuss slide 12 (answers on slide 13). Students practice *recognizing* what is actually being
  claimed before reacting to it.
- **0:16–0:36 · Evaluate — "Bible Telephone" (slides 14–24).** Run the two Telephone variants: first
  one line with a silly sentence (e.g., *"Three old miners commenced a claim-jumping calamity"*),
  then three separate lines, then the color-card copying rounds (Group 1 → 2 → 3, slides 20–23).
  Tally the variants on slide 24. *The point students should reach:* a message garbles fast through
  one careless channel, but with many independent copies to compare, the original is recoverable —
  exactly how scholars handle biblical manuscripts.
- **0:36–0:44 · Decide (slides 25–29).** Summarize the *Evaluate* takeaway, then read and discuss
  the *Decide* portion (slide 27) and the acronym recap (slide 29). *Given the evidence, what will
  you do with the Bible's claim to authority?*
- **0:44–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **3.7–3.8** (ST 41–43) — answering objections, and how far God's authority
extends — for Friday.
**Assessments.** Game participation (informal).
**Notes.** If the two Telephone variants run long, the color-card copying round (slides 20–24) is
the flex item — you can shorten it and still land the manuscript point. Tie it forward: *"Hold that
Telephone result — Thursday and Friday we'll ask whether the Bible got 'telephoned' into something
unreliable."*

## Day 5 — Thu Sep 24 · External evidence for the NT; accurate preservation (3.5–3.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) list ancient non-biblical writings that confirm the historicity of
Jesus and the early Christians; (b) describe the role of eyewitness testimony and the church fathers
in affirming the Gospels; (c) explain how the Dead Sea Scrolls and the early NT manuscripts confirm
the Bible's accurate preservation. `[3.5, 3.6]` *(High test weight — this material is all over the
true/false, fill-in, and essay sections.)*

**Materials.** Calculators and rulers (computers optional); paper and pencil per pair.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:22 · Read & discuss 3.5–3.6 (manual p.52–53).** *External evidence, NT:* four
  non-Christian writers — **Josephus, Tacitus, Pliny the Younger, and the Talmud** — confirm Jesus
  existed, taught, was crucified under Pilate, and had followers who believed he was alive. The book
  of **Acts** is corroborated archaeologically (Paul's journeys). **Eyewitness testimony** grounds
  the Gospels (John 21:24; 1 John 1:1), and the **church fathers** — Ignatius, Polycarp, Clement,
  who sat under the apostles — repeat the same teaching, confirming the NT. *Preservation:* the
  **Dead Sea Scrolls** (300–400 BC) held a copy of Isaiah nearly **1,000 years older** than the next
  oldest, yet virtually identical; the medieval **Masoretes** counted letters to guarantee accuracy;
  and we have **over 25,000 early NT manuscripts** from five document families with **99.5%
  agreement**, none of the differences affecting doctrine. Use manual Qs 1–5. *Careful nuance
  (manual note, p.53):* external evidence doesn't *prove* every claim — much of the ancient world is
  unexcavated — but it shows trust in Scripture is not blind.
- **0:22–0:52 · Activity: How High Is the Evidence? (pairs; manual p.53).** Take a quick class height
  tally (thumbs-up over 5'5", "V" under), add the total, then have pairs build a **bar graph**
  comparing: the class's stacked height, the stacked NT manuscripts (**~24,000 early manuscripts —
  over a mile high**), and the average stack of other ancient documents (**about four feet**). With a
  computer, pairs can add bars (Homer's *Iliad*, the Empire State Building) for scale. *Purpose:* the
  manuscript count is an abstract number until you see it tower over everything else on the graph.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Does a mile-high manuscript stack prove the Bible is true?
  What does it prove, and what does it not?* (It establishes reliable transmission, not the truth of
  the claims themselves.)

**Homework.** Read **3.9–3.10** (ST 44–45) — why we should submit to God's authority, and the
conclusion — for tomorrow. *(Tomorrow merges two sections, so it's a fuller reading day.)*
**Assessments.** How High Is the Evidence? bar graphs (completion).
**Notes.** The Dead Sea Scrolls, the 25,000 manuscripts, and 99.5% agreement are near-certain test
items (true/false 12, 16, 17). If the discussion runs long, trim the computer-research extension of
the graph, not the manuscript comparison.

## Day 6 — Fri Sep 25 · Answering objections; the reach of God's authority; why submit (3.7–3.10, merged)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) appraise and answer the three common objections to the Bible's
authority (human authorship; corruption / textual variants; the lost gospels); (b) summarize the
five areas of life under God's authority; (c) explain the importance of evaluating truth claims and
why God wants obedience; (d) explain why submitting to authority requires faith, but not *blind*
faith. `[3.7, 3.8, 3.9, 3.10]` *(Merges publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the four closing subsections.
High test weight: the objections, gnosticism, and God's authority run through every section.)*

**Materials.** Prewritten objection cards (a third each: *The Bible was written by humans* / *The
Bible has been corrupted* / *Important books have been left out of the Bible*) + a container
(`Answering the Skeptic`); one copy of `TR 3.5A Idolatry Bible Study` per student + the answer key;
Bibles.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Read & discuss 3.7–3.10 (manual p.54–57).** Move through the four sections, favoring
  the objections. **Three objections:** (1) *Written by humans* — ask whether humans can write
  anything true, and whether God could use them to; Scripture's origin is both human and divine
  (2 Peter 1:20–21). (2) *Corrupted over time* — introduce **textual variants** (differences among
  copies); of 400,000+ variants, **fewer than 4,000** touch meaning and **none** affect core
  doctrine. (3) *Books left out* — the **lost gospels** are **gnostic** texts (AD 200–400) that fail
  all **three canon criteria** (apostolicity, orthodoxy, pedigree); note that **gnosticism** taught
  the material world is evil and Jesus only seemed human. Then **five areas of God's authority**
  (culture, government, knowledge, emotions, entertainment); why we resist authority and how to
  question it *with humility*; and the closing **airplane analogy** — trusting God's authority is
  like trusting the airline system: it requires faith, but not *blind* faith, because there's good
  evidence behind it. Use manual Qs from p.54–57 — favor the three objections and the five areas.
- **0:24–0:42 · Activity: Answering the Skeptic (pairs; manual p.55).** One partner draws an
  objection card and plays the **skeptic** (in their own words); the other responds as the
  **Christian**, asking clarifying questions first (*"What do you mean by…? How did you come to
  that?"*) before answering from the lesson. Swap roles with a new card. *Purpose:* students
  rehearse the exact three answers the short-answer and essay questions ask for.
- **0:42–0:56 · Activity: Idolatry Bible Study (`TR 3.5A`, individual; manual p.57).** Students work
  the worksheet — reading Exodus 20:1–6 and Romans 1:18–20 and applying **Recognize, Evaluate,
  Decide** to modern idols (including making an idol of *yourself*). Share a few answers. Grade
  against the `TR 3.5A` answer key. *This is where the chapter turns personal:* God's authority isn't
  abstract once you ask what you've put in his place.
- **0:56–0:60 · Close & homework.** *One area of life — culture, government, knowledge, emotions,
  entertainment — where you find God's authority hardest to accept, and why?*

**Homework.** Begin studying for the Chapter 3 Test (Study Guide, SM 64–67; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Answering the Skeptic (participation); Idolatry Bible Study worksheet (completion).
**Notes.** This is the block's fullest day — two sections and two activities. If a class runs slow,
the **Idolatry Bible Study** is the flex item: assign the second half as homework rather than cut
the skeptic role-play, which maps directly to the test's short-answer and essay questions.

## Day 7 — Mon Sep 28 · Evidence Tag + review + Study Guide (Lessons 8 + 9, merged)

**Objectives.** Students sort **internal vs. external evidence**, consolidate the chapter, and
self-assess against the Study Guide; lock in the **Key Terms** (church fathers, Dead Sea Scrolls,
textual variants, lost gospels, gnosticism) and **Key Verses** (Prov 30:5–6; 2 Peter 3:15–16;
1 John 1:1; 2 Peter 1:20–21). *(High test weight.)*

**Materials.** `TR 3.8A–B Internal / External Evidence Tags` (cut apart), masking tape, and a soft
object to toss per circle of six; display `PS 3.8A Chapter 3 Review`; student manuals (Study Guide,
SM 64–67); textbooks.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:22 · Activity: Evidence Tag (`TR 3.8A–B`; manual p.62–63).** Each student tapes an
  "evidence tag" to their shirt (e.g., *"Thus says the LORD…"*, *Jesus leaned on Scripture in
  anguish*, *Hezekiah's tunnel found*, *Babylon ruled Judah 70 years*). Form circles of six (at
  least one tag from each of the six categories per circle). Students first decide whether their tag
  is **internal** or **external** evidence; then you call out one or the other and they toss the ball
  to someone wearing that type — a wrong toss sits the student down (still playing, seated). Last one
  standing wins. *Purpose:* the internal/external distinction is a fill-in and short-answer target,
  and this makes students *categorize* real examples, not just define the words.
- **0:22–0:40 · Chapter Review (`PS 3.8A`).** Walk the review slides as a class; let students read
  them aloud. Hit the heavy-weight items hard: **external evidence** (the four non-Christian writers,
  Dead Sea Scrolls, 25,000 manuscripts, 99.5%), the **three objections + three canon criteria**,
  **gnosticism**, and the **five areas of God's authority**.
- **0:40–0:55 · Study Guide + Key Terms/Verses (manual p.64–67).** Students work the Study Guide;
  confirm the five Key Terms and four Key Verses. The manual's 21 study-guide questions with answers
  are the answer key.
- **0:55–0:60 · Test preview.** Tomorrow: 60 points — multiple choice, true/false, fill-in, short
  answer, and essays. *"If you can name the external evidence, answer the three objections, and
  explain why trusting God's authority isn't blind faith, you're most of the way there."*

**Homework.** Study for the test.
**Assessments.** Evidence Tag (informal — internal vs. external) + Study Guide completion.
**Notes.** If Day 5 (external evidence / preservation) or Day 6 (objections) felt rushed, this is the
place to reteach — they are the test's center of gravity.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:04 · Settle & distribute.** Clear desks; hand out the test (60 pts).
- **0:04–0:52 · Test.** Students complete independently. Early finishers begin reading **4.1–4.2**
  (Chapter 4).
- **0:52–0:60 · Collect & bridge.** Collect tests. Preview Ch 4: *"How to Read the Bible" — we've

**Homework.** Read **4.1–4.2** (ST 48–51) — start of Chapter 4.
