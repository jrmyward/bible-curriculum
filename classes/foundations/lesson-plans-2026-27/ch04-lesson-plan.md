## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 9 days** — by *merging*, not cutting.
A 9-day block buys room: every content lesson keeps its own day and its own activity, the Videos
lesson stands on its own, and only the tail-end Review and Study-Guide lessons combine.

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 4.10A` (/72) is matching + multiple choice + true/false + fill-in-the-blank + short answer +
four essays. Its weight lands on: the **four context/relevance terms** — *historical context,
literary context, eternal relevance, historical particularity* — and the **four Key Verses**
matched to references (matching, 8 pts); **hermeneutics, context, and exegesis** defined (MC); the
**five common mistakes** and the **descriptive/prescriptive** distinction (true/false, where a false
statement's underlined word must be corrected, 8 pts); the **three translation types** (formal,
functional/dynamic, free), **eternal relevance**, and **Tolle lege** (fill-in, 18 pts); **benefits
of Bible reading** and the **three ways to apply** Scripture (short answer, 12 pts); and four
5-point essays — **postmodernism**, **self-centeredness in the hermeneutical mistakes**, the **kinds
of context**, and **dealing with doubt** (20 pts). The essay and fill-in sections are the biggest
point blocks. **Day 3 (mistakes + context) and Day 4 (steps + translations) carry the most test
weight, with Day 2 (postmodernism) close behind;** Day 8 review pulls it together.

## Day 1 — Wed Sep 30 · A Bible study gone wrong; why we read (4.1–4.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) name what goes wrong when people read the Bible carelessly and
describe how *not* to do a Bible study; (b) explain why personal Bible reading is necessary.
`[4.1, 4.2]`

**Materials.** Graded `TR 3.10A Chapter 3 Test` to return; class Bibles; paper and pen; the opening
question on the board: *"Can a verse mean one thing to me and the opposite to you — and both be
right?"*

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:08 · Return & review the Ch 3 test.** Hand back `TR 3.10A`; walk the two or three items
  most students missed so the misconceptions don't harden. Keep it brief — full item-by-item review
  isn't needed.
- **0:08–0:22 · Read & discuss 4.1–4.2 (manual p.72–73).** Read "A Bible Study Gone Wrong" (ST 49):
  Sophia claims Jeremiah 29:11 as a personal promise; Chloe pulls Matthew 18:20 out of context;
  several students assume a verse can mean anything to anyone. Name what's wrong — they're **ignoring
  context** and reading the Bible as if it's mainly about them. Then 4.2: for most of history few
  Christians owned Bibles (illiteracy, cost); the **printing press** and the Reformation changed
  that; and **Augustine** heard *"Tolle lege"* — *"pick it up and read it"* — and it turned his life.
  Land the point: an unread Bible is *"kindling without a match."* Use manual Qs 1–6.
- **0:22–0:40 · Activity: According to God's Word (manual p.73).** Assign each student a section of
  **Psalm 119** (it has 22, one per Hebrew letter; pairs may share a section). Each reads their
  section and lists the **benefits of studying God's Word** it names. Collect the benefits on the
  board — students will see how much one psalm promises to the reader.
- **0:40–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **4.3–4.4** (ST 51–53) — the lifelong journey and self-centered reading.
**Assessments.** According to God's Word (participation).
**Notes.** *Tolle lege* is a near-certain fill-in item — say the phrase, write it, and tie it to
Augustine so it sticks. Leave the Psalm 119 benefits list up; it feeds the short-answer question on
Day 3–8 review.

## Day 2 — Thu Oct 1 · A lifelong journey & self-centered reading (4.3–4.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) name several benefits of studying God's Word and explain why no one
ever "arrives"; (b) define **postmodernism** and give examples of how it has affected the study of
the Bible. `[4.3, 4.4]` *(Postmodernism is a 5-point essay — spend real time on 4.4.)*

**Materials.** Class Bibles (for the assigned passages); the board for a running definition.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up.** *"You'll never finish learning the Bible."* — is that discouraging or good
  news? Surface a few takes; set up 4.3.
- **0:06–0:26 · Read & discuss 4.3–4.4 (manual p.74–75).** Studying Scripture is a **lifelong
  journey** — even Paul kept studying to his dying day (2 Tim 4:13), and the Bible promises the
  reader blessing, insight, freedom, direction, and the ability to grasp truth. Then the turn to
  4.4: it's fine to ask *"what does this mean to me?"* — but **postmodernism** (write the Key Term:
  *a worldview that teaches there is no defining source for truth and reality*) pushes that question
  into narcissism. Roland Barthes' "Death of the Author" said the reader's experience outranks the
  author's intent; applied to the Bible, that means *no truth to discover, only your interpretation*.
  Name the danger — health-and-wealth teaching, cult leaders, slave traders all bent Scripture to
  their own ends. Use manual Qs 1–5, favoring Qs 4–5 (postmodernism).
- **0:26–0:52 · Activity: Self-Centered or God-Centered? (three groups; manual p.75).** Model it with
  Daniel in the lions' den (*self-centered:* Daniel was rewarded for being good; *God-centered:*
  Darius decreed the whole kingdom fear God, Dan 6:25–27). Then Group 1 takes David and Goliath
  (1 Sam 17), Group 2 Solomon's prayer for wisdom (1 Kings 3; 10:23–24), Group 3 the fiery furnace
  (Dan 3). Half of each group tells the story **self-centered**, half **God-centered**; groups
  present both. *Purpose:* students feel the difference between reading Scripture as about them vs.
  about God.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Which version was easier to tell? Which is truer to what the
  passage is doing?*

**Homework.** Read **4.5–4.6** (ST 53–55) — the five common mistakes.
**Assessments.** Self-Centered or God-Centered? (participation).
**Notes.** Freshmen conflate "the Bible is relevant to me" with "the Bible is about me." Draw that
line explicitly — it's the heart of the postmodernism essay.

## Day 3 — Mon Oct 5 · Common mistakes in Bible reading (4.5–4.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) identify three common mistakes people make reading the Bible —
assuming it needs no interpretation, that it applies uniquely to them, that verses that don't fit
their theology can be ignored; (b) explain two more — taking the Bible out of **context**, and
interpreting it by **contemporary standards**. `[4.5, 4.6]` *(High test weight — the five mistakes,
`context`, and eternal relevance/historical particularity are all over the test.)*

**Materials.** `TR 4.3A Common Mistakes in Bible Reading` — 1 per pair or group (+ Answer Key);
class Bibles.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:22 · Content: the five mistakes (mini-lecture, guided notes; manual p.76–77).** Open with
  the Key Term **hermeneutics** (*the process of devising the best methods for understanding and
  interpreting a text*). Then walk the five, writing each on the board:
  1. Assuming the Bible **doesn't need to be interpreted** — Fee & Stuart: *"every reader is at the
     same time an interpreter."*
  2. Assuming it **applies uniquely to us** — Jeremiah 29:11 was a promise to Israel, not a personal
     one to Sophia.
  3. **Ignoring passages that don't fit our theology** — but 2 Tim 3:16 says *all* Scripture is
     profitable; approach it in **humility**, making ourselves fit it.
  4. **Taking the Bible out of context** — Matthew 18:19–20 is about resolving conflict with
     witnesses, not a blank check on group prayer. Key Term **context** (*the historical background
     and literary setting of a text that helps clarify its meaning*).
  5. **Interpreting by contemporary standards** — Scripture is **eternally relevant** yet
     **historically particular** (the Keller "sons/heirs" example in Galatians 4).
- **0:22–0:30 · Quick check.** Fast cold-call: *Sophia claiming Jer 29:11 — which mistake? Reading
  "man" out of an old text and rewording it — which? Skipping a hard verse — which?* Lock the five.
- **0:30–0:55 · Activity: Common Mistakes in Bible Reading (`TR 4.3A`, pairs; manual p.77).** Pairs
  work the handout — analyzing sample passages against the five mistakes, Bibles in hand. Circulate;
  share out with the Answer Key. *Purpose:* students practice spotting the mistake before they can
  avoid it.
- **0:55–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **4.7–4.8** (ST 56–59) — the seven steps to reading the Bible well.
**Assessments.** Common Mistakes handout (completion).
**Notes.** *Eternal relevance* vs. *historical particularity* is both a matching pair and a fill-in
— nail the two terms and the "in tension, like exercise" picture. If the mini-lecture runs long, the
quick-check shrinks; don't cut the `TR 4.3A` practice.

## Day 4 — Tue Oct 6 · Steps to reading the Bible well (4.7–4.8)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain the first three of seven steps — choose a good translation,
understand the content, understand the **historical context**; (b) describe the remaining four —
**literary context**, look for relationships, use Bible helps, pursue a response. `[4.7, 4.8]`
*(High test weight — translation types, `exegesis`, and the context terms run through fill-in and
matching.)*

**Materials.** Student textbooks (the seven steps are drawn from 4.7–4.8 for the skits).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Content: the seven steps (mini-lecture, guided notes; manual p.78–79).** Start with
  the Key Term **exegesis** (*the exposition or explanation of a text based on careful study and
  analysis* — reading *out of* the text, the opposite of *eisegesis*, reading *into* it). Then the
  seven steps:
  1. **Choose a good translation** — the three types: **formal equivalence** (keeps words/grammar:
     KJV, NASB, ESV), **functional/dynamic equivalence** (keeps meaning in natural English: NIV,
     NLT), and **free translation/paraphrase** (renders the ideas: The Message). *These three are a
     fill-in near-certainty.*
  2. **Understand the content** — what does it say, who, to whom, when, why?
  3. **Understand the historical context** — Key Term: *time, place, culture, audience.*
  4. **Understand the literary context** — Key Term: *genre, structure, grammar* (a psalm is poetry,
     a proverb isn't a promise).
  5. **Look for relationships** — Jesus' 40 days echo Israel's 40 years (Matt 4 / Deut 8).
  6. **Use Bible helps** — good commentaries do three things (historical info, content answers,
     discussion of difficult texts).
  7. **Pursue a response** — but note the **descriptive** vs. **prescriptive** distinction (most of
     the Bible describes; some passages command). Use manual Qs 1–6.
- **0:24–0:30 · Quick check.** Cold-call the translation types with an example each, and *"the Ten
  Commandments — descriptive or prescriptive? The book of Acts?"*
- **0:30–0:55 · Activity: Steps to Reading the Bible Well (seven groups; manual p.79).** Assign each
  group one of the seven steps; each builds a **two-minute skit** to illustrate it and performs it.
  *Purpose:* students teach the class one step apiece — the whole method gets dramatized in 25
  minutes.
- **0:55–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **4.9–4.10** (ST 59–61) — applying Scripture and facing doubt.
**Assessments.** Steps skits (participation).
**Notes.** The descriptive/prescriptive distinction shows up in both true/false and matching-adjacent
items — if skits run over, trim to five groups and double up, but keep the translation-types
mini-lecture intact.

## Day 5 — Wed Oct 7 · Practicing what we learn & facing doubt (4.9–4.10)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) list three ways to apply what they learn — **journal it, share it,
live it**; (b) explain that believers do experience doubt and describe how to handle it well.
`[4.9, 4.10]`

**Materials.** `TR 4.5A Historic Journals` — 1 per student; pencils.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:16 · Read & discuss 4.9–4.10 (manual p.80–81).** The Holy Spirit guides us into truth
  (John 16:13; Rom 8:14) and prompts us to act — so be **"doers of the word,"** not hearers only
  (James 1:22). Three ways to become a doer: **journal, share, live** what you learn. Then 4.10:
  doubt is normal — John the Baptist, Peter, Thomas, and **Martin Luther** all doubted; not all
  doubt leads to unbelief, and handled well it can deepen faith. Don't bury doubts — take them to a
  pastor, teacher, or mentor. Use manual Qs 1–5.
- **0:16–0:38 · Activity: Historic Journals (`TR 4.5A`; manual p.81).** Students read the
  autobiographical excerpts on the handout and identify **at least four benefits** of keeping a
  record of one's spiritual life (notes on the back of the TR). Share findings with the class.
  *Purpose:* students see, in real lives, why "journal what you're learning" is worth doing.
- **0:38–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** No new reading — Day 6 is the in-class study lab. Optional: skim 4.7–4.8 again before
the lab.
**Assessments.** Historic Journals (completion — four benefits identified).
**Notes.** The **four Key Verses** now all have a home — 2 Tim 2:15 (4.3), 2 Tim 3:16–17 (4.5),
James 1:22 (4.9), 1 Thess 2:13 (4.10). Point students to the matching section: they'll pair each
verse to its reference. Dealing with doubt is one of the four essays — leave students with the
"don't handle doubt alone" line.

## Day 6 — Thu Oct 8 · Bible Reading and Study lab (Lesson 6)

**Objective.** Students discuss reasons to read and study the Bible, then **analyze a real passage
(Ephesians 6:10–20) using the chapter's methods** — the seven steps, in their own hands.

**Materials.** `PS 4.6A Bible Reading and Study` (preview beforehand); `TR 4.6A Armor of God` — 1
per student; the Bible study resources you collected (study Bibles, commentaries, concordances); a
timer; Bibles, paper, pencil.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:10 · Set up & first brainstorm (`PS 4.6A`, slides 1–2; manual p.82).** Three groups; each
  names a **secretary** and a **spokesperson**. Slide 1, two-minute timer: brainstorm reasons/
  benefits of Bible reading. Slide 2: reveal the benefits; secretaries cross off matches;
  spokespersons add any the slide missed.
- **0:10–0:20 · Second brainstorm (slides 3–4).** Three-minute timer on the second exercise, then
  reveal and discuss; emphasize the Key Terms as they come up.
- **0:20–0:48 · Study lab (`TR 4.6A Armor of God`, slide 5).** Hand out `TR 4.6A`; point out the
  commentaries and concordances and, if needed, demo how to use them. Groups study **Ephesians
  6:10–20** with the chapter's method — content, historical context, literary context, relationships,
  Bible helps — keeping their own notes.
- **0:48–0:58 · Report out (slides 6–12).** Display the question slides; call on students to answer
  from their study. This is where the seven steps stop being a list and become a skill.
- **0:58–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** None required. Optional: begin skimming the Study Guide (SM 86).
**Assessments.** Armor of God study notes (participation/completion).
**Notes.** This is the chapter's synthesis checkpoint — listen for whether students can name *which*
step they're using. The manual budgets 45–50 min for the lab, so the report-out is the flex item if
groups need more study time.
