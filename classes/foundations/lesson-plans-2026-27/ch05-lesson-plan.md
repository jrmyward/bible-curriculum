This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 9 days** — by *merging*, not cutting.
A 9-day block only needs one merge, so nearly every publisher lesson keeps its own day: the five
content lessons, the full-period metanarrative skit activity, and the Videos lesson all stand
alone. Only the Review and Study-Guide lessons combine.

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 5.10A` (/57) is multiple choice (12) + true/false (8) + fill-in-the-blank (9 × 2 pts) + short
answer (3 × 3 pts) + two essays (2 × 5 pts). Its weight lands on: the **six acts of the biblical
metanarrative** (list them in order; which are acts and which are decoys — "Perfection Restored,"
"Church Repaired"; Act 1 = Creation, Act 4 = Redemption Accomplished); the **five non-Christian
worldviews** and how each contrasts with Christianity (secularism, new spirituality, postmodernism,
Marxism, Islam — the essay asks students to name one and contrast it); **narrative and metanarrative**
definitions plus the **elements of a well-written story**; **comprehensive, coherent, and normative**
(which one points to divine inspiration); **historical narrative** ("about half" of the Bible, not
two-thirds); and the **Key Verses** (Mark 1:14–15, Rom 1:1–5, Gal 4:4, Rev 21:5). Day 2 (worldviews),
Day 3 (comprehensive/coherent/normative), and Day 4 (the six acts) carry the most test weight, and
Day 8 review pulls it together.

## Day 1 — Wed Oct 14 · Stories, story elements & Ch 4 return (5.1–5.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain why stories matter and how they shape us; (b) list the
components of a well-formed narrative and describe the three-act structure. `[5.1, 5.2]`

**Materials.** Graded Ch 4 tests to return; Bibles, paper, and pen (Anatomy of a Bible Story); the
opening question on the board: *"What's a story that changed how you see something?"*

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:08 · Return & review the Ch 4 test.** Hand back Ch 4 tests; walk the two or three items
  most students missed so misconceptions don't harden into the new chapter. Keep it brief — this
  is the short day.
- **0:08–0:26 · Read & discuss 5.1–5.2 (manual p.94–95).** Stories are how we make sense of reality —
  they teach us good and evil, sacrifice, loyalty, justice; the characters we admire shape who we
  become. Land the term **narrative** (an account of related events told to listeners or readers),
  then the **elements of a well-written story**: setting, characters (protagonist / antagonist),
  conflict, theme, plot, point of view. Add the **three-act structure** — setup, confrontation,
  climax/resolution. Close on the hook: *each of our lives is a story with a plot we can't yet see
  the end of.* Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:26–0:42 · Activity: Anatomy of a Bible Story (pairs; manual p.95).** Pairs pick one Bible story
  from the manual's list (Tower of Babel, Birth of Moses, Moses Strikes the Rock, Jesus and the
  Sabbath, Jesus Appears to His Disciples, Healing of Aeneas and Dorcas) and identify its setting,
  characters, conflict, theme, plot, and point of view, plus how tone and style shape it. A few pairs
  share. *Purpose:* students prove the story elements are real by finding them in Scripture.
- **0:42–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **5.3–5.4** (ST 68–70) — metanarratives and worldviews.
**Assessments.** Anatomy of a Bible Story (participation/completion).
**Notes.** Short day — if the Ch 4 review or the reading runs long, the Anatomy activity is the flex
item; pairs can finish it as they leave and hand in tomorrow.

## Day 2 — Thu Oct 15 · Metanarratives & worldviews (5.3–5.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) define **metanarrative** and give examples; (b) describe and critique
the **postmodern** view of metanarratives; (c) compare and contrast the common non-Christian
worldviews. `[5.3, 5.4]` *(High test weight — the worldviews run through the fill-in, short-answer,
and essay sections.)*

**Materials.** `TR 5.2A Worldview Bingo` (1 per five students) + answer key; several small prizes; pens.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up.** *"What movie or series has a 'story behind all the stories'?"* — surface a
  few (MCU, Star Wars). Name what they're describing: a **metanarrative**.
- **0:06–0:28 · Read & discuss 5.3–5.4 (manual p.96–97).** A **metanarrative** is the story beyond the
  story — an overarching narrative that makes sense of the world (*meta* = beyond). Examples: the MCU,
  Star Wars, the immigration story of the United States. Then the pivot: **postmodernism** rejects
  metanarratives as untrustworthy power plays — yet *"there is no big story"* is itself a big story,
  so it's **self-defeating**. Tie metanarrative to **worldview** (the Ch 1 definition: a pattern of
  ideas and beliefs that helps us make sense of the world and guides our decisions and actions). Then
  the five popular non-Christian worldviews — write them on the board and keep them there:
  1. **Secularism** — atheistic/agnostic; faith in human ability and progress
  2. **New spirituality** — everything is god; peace through divine consciousness
  3. **Postmodernism** — skeptical of all truth claims; rejects metanarratives
  4. **Marxism** — history as class warfare; a future utopian atheistic state
  5. **Islam** — theistic; centered on Muhammad; Quran, Hadith, Sunnah
  Close on the point: not all worldviews can be true, so we need one **rooted in reality**. Use manual Qs 1–5.
- **0:28–0:52 · Activity: Worldview Bingo (`TR 5.2A`; manual p.98–99).** Each student gets a bingo
  sheet. Call out a worldview; students mark the squares that match it with that worldview's symbol
  (secularism = stick figure, new spirituality = three circles, postmodernism = arrow, Marxism = star,
  Islam = half-moon, Christianity = cross). First correct row calls "bingo"; check against the answer
  key (p.99). Small prizes to the first correct bingo and the highest number of correct bingos.
  *Purpose:* students learn to recognize each worldview's pattern so they can spot it in culture.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Which of these worldviews have you already heard, and where?*

**Homework.** Read **5.5–5.6** (ST 70–72) — the Bible as a metanarrative.
**Assessments.** Worldview Bingo (informal — can they match beliefs to worldviews).
**Notes.** Freshmen will want to say "all worldviews are equally valid." Draw the line explicitly —
that's exactly what the essay tests. Keep the five worldviews on the board through the block.

## Day 3 — Mon Oct 19 · The Bible as a metanarrative: comprehensive, coherent, normative (5.5–5.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) summarize the metanarrative of the Bible; (b) explain and defend the
Bible as a **historical narrative** that is **comprehensive, coherent, and normative**. `[5.5, 5.6]`
*(High test weight — these three attributes appear in the true/false, fill-in, and short-answer sections.)*

**Materials.** One poster board per group of 3–4; markers.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:22 · Read & discuss 5.5–5.6 (manual p.100–101).** The Bible is neither random moral tales
  nor a dry list of facts — it's one **historical narrative** (a historical account told in story form),
  about **half** the Bible, with the other genres (wisdom, poetry, prophecy, letters) written *within*
  that narrative. Its climax is **Jesus' life, death, resurrection, and ascension**; its ending is the
  **restoration of all things**. Then the three literary attributes — write them on the board:
  1. **Comprehensive** — complete; it tells us everything we *need* to know (not everything we'd *like* to)
  2. **Coherent** — orderly, logical, consistent; many authors over 1,500 years, one unified story
     (this coherence points to **divine inspiration** — a near-certain test item)
  3. **Normative** — it reveals a God-given standard for how we should live; it calls for a response
  Use manual Qs 1–6.
- **0:22–0:30 · Quick check.** *Comprehensive, coherent, or normative — which one means "it fits
  together as one story"? Which one means "it calls for a response"? Which one points to divine
  inspiration?* Fast cold-call to lock the three apart.
- **0:30–0:54 · Activity: Literary Attributes (groups of 3–4; manual p.101).** Each group designs a
  memory aid — a symbol, acronym, rhyme, or visual — to lock in **comprehensive, coherent, normative**.
  Groups present their aids. *Purpose:* the three attributes are a test lock; students build their own
  hook to remember them.
- **0:54–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** Vote on the memory aid the class will actually keep.

**Homework.** Read **5.7–5.8** (ST 72–75) — caught up in a bigger story, and the six acts.
**Assessments.** Literary Attributes memory aid (participation/synthesis).
**Notes.** "Coherent → divine inspiration" is the trap on the true/false section (Q17 flips it to
"human effort"). Say it out loud more than once.

## Day 4 — Tue Oct 20 · Caught up in the bigger story & the six acts (5.7–5.8)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) identify biblical characters who knew they were part of God's larger
story; (b) describe the **six main acts** of the biblical metanarrative and their corresponding
Scriptures. `[5.7, 5.8]` *(Highest test weight — the six-acts listing and matching key off this day.)*

**Materials.** Bibles; paper and pen or markers (Stories within a Story); the six acts written on the board.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up.** *"When have you realized, partway through, that you were part of something
  bigger than you first thought?"* Bridge to the biblical authors, who sensed the same.
- **0:06–0:20 · Discuss 5.7 (manual p.102).** The biblical authors didn't see the whole story, but they
  knew they were inside one. *"Thus says the LORD"* appears 400+ times in the OT. Name the people who
  sensed their part: **Mary** (Luke 1:46–55), **Zechariah** (Luke 1:67–79), **Simeon and Anna**
  (Luke 2:29–38), **Mark** ("the time is fulfilled," Mark 1:14–15), **Paul** (Rom 1:1–5), **Peter**
  (1 Peter 1:3–13). Also name why three popular summaries of the Bible fall short (Creation-Fall-
  Redemption drops Israel and restoration; "sinners → Jesus died → we go to heaven" drops good creation;
  "cruel OT God, kind Jesus" is just wrong). Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:20–0:34 · The six acts (guided notes; manual p.102–103, ST fig. 5.a).** This is the spine of
  Chapters 6–12 — the next seven chapters walk these acts one at a time. Write each on the board with
  its primary text:
  1. **Creation — God's Good World** (Genesis 1–2)
  2. **The Fall — Sin and Its Consequences** (Genesis 3–11)
  3. **Israel — Redemption Initiated** (Torah, Prophets, Writings)
  · *Interlude: The Second Temple Period* (Ezra, Nehemiah, Esther, Daniel) ·
  4. **Redemption Accomplished — Jesus** (Gospels)
  5. **Restoration Initiated — The Church** (Acts, Epistles)
  6. **Restoration Accomplished — New Creation** (Revelation)
  *In which act do we live?* (Act 5 — the church, awaiting the full restoration.)
- **0:34–0:56 · Activity: Stories within a Story (manual p.103).** Each student draws a horizontal
  timeline, marks the acts in order, and places nine Bible narratives in the right act (Joseph sold /
  Jesus heals a blind man / Jonah / Esther / Jesus returns on a white horse / Adam and Eve expelled /
  David and Goliath / Paul and Silas jailed / Israel receives the Law). Check against the manual's key.
  A few present. *Purpose:* students prove they can locate any Bible story inside the six acts.
- **0:56–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **5.9–5.10** (ST 73–75) — meeting the Author, and the conclusion.
**Assessments.** Stories within a Story timeline (completion — correct act for each story).
**Notes.** Leave the six acts on the board for the rest of the block. These are the same acts from the
Ch 1 six-act story, now with Scripture attached — and they anchor every chapter from here to Ch 12.
This is the day to spend your extra minutes; the test's short-answer "list the six acts in order" is
worth 3 points and the matching items lean on it.

## Day 5 — Wed Oct 21 · Meeting the Author & conclusion (5.9–5.10)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) express God's active role in the biblical metanarrative; (b) explain
why knowing the Bible is a metanarrative helps us interpret difficult passages. `[5.9, 5.10]`

**Materials.** `TR 5.5A God's Story Bible Study` — 1 per student + answer key; Bibles; pens.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:16 · Read & discuss 5.9–5.10 (manual p.104–105).** God is not a distant observer — he's
  active in his own story, "like a director who is also acting in a movie he wrote." This reframes the
  hard passages (*"Why was slavery permitted in the OT?"*): isolated from the big story a text can make
  God look petty, but read inside the whole story — with careful study and the Spirit's help — we see
  God is good, wise, and committed to redeeming his creation. Land the C. S. Lewis line: his love is
  *"quite relentless in its determination that we shall be cured of [our] sins."* Close on the
  conclusion — we're not spectators; we have a part to play. Use manual Qs 1–5.
- **0:16–0:41 · Activity: God's Story Bible Study (`TR 5.5A`; manual p.105).** Students walk the steps
  on the handout, then read the story of **Jonah** in their Bibles and apply the metanarrative to it —
  reading a difficult story inside the big story rather than in isolation. Share a few if time allows.
  *Note:* this sets up the deeper Jonah study in Chapter 18.
- **0:41–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** None required (tomorrow is a hands-on activity). Optional: skim the Study Guide (SM 102–105).
**Assessments.** God's Story Bible Study (completion).
**Notes.** Short day — if discussion runs long, the Bible study can drop to Jonah chapters 1 and 3
plus the handout's summary steps. This is the "how to read hard passages" idea the essay (Q34) rewards.

## Day 6 — Thu Oct 22 · Building a Metanarrative (Lesson 6)

**Objective.** Students create their own individual narratives that combine into one larger
metanarrative — feeling from the inside what the biblical authors experienced. *(Reinforces the
chapter's core idea: separate stories fit inside one grand story.)*

**Materials.** Four student backpacks (one per group).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:08 · Set up (manual p.106).** Four groups; each gets a backpack as a prop symbolizing
  **hope**. Assign each group its part in a shared metanarrative: Group 1 — conflict introduced, hope
  lost (sad ending); Group 2 — the consequences of lost hope; Group 3 — an action that restores hope
  (one character refuses it); Group 4 — hope fully restored (a divided beginning, a resolved, positive end).
- **0:08–0:28 · Plan (15–20 min).** Groups plan a five-minute skit for their assigned part. Circulate;
  keep each group inside its piece of the arc.
- **0:28–0:50 · Present in order.** Each group performs (≤5 min). Watch how four separate skits build one
  story none of them could see at the start.
- **0:50–0:60 · Debrief.** *Was it easy or hard to see how the skits fit one bigger story? Where did the
  backpack (hope) show up in each? Did any parts connect unintentionally?* Land it: **this is how the
  biblical authors worked — each writing a part of a story most of them couldn't see whole.**

**Homework.** None required (tomorrow is the Videos lesson). Optional: begin skimming the Study Guide.
**Assessments.** Skit participation (informal).
**Notes.** The debrief is the lesson — don't let the skits eat the closing discussion. The payoff is
the analogy back to Scripture, so protect the last ten minutes.
