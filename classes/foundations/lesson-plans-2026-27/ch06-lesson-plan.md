## How this block works

This chapter is the first act of the six-act story students met in Chapter 1 — **Creation.**
It compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 9 days** by *merging*, not cutting. Chapter 6 is
activity-heavy (a writing activity, three group activities, a full-period game, a five-video set,
and an index-card activity), so with nine days almost every lesson keeps its own slot:

**One merge makes the compression:** the publisher's separate Review (L8) and Study-Guide (L9)

(L7) keeps its own day (Day 7), and the Creation Feud Game (L6) keeps its own full period (Day 6),
because both are built to fill a class and shouldn't be crowded.

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 6.10A` (/56) is multiple choice + fill-in-the-blank + matching + true/false + short answer + two
essays: **MC 9 pts · fill-in 8 pts · matching 10 pts · true/false 10 pts · short answer 9 pts ·
essay 10 pts.** Its weight lands on: the **Key Terms** (creation myth, Elohim, Yahweh, imago Dei,
transcendence, immanence, stewardship) and the **four worldviews** (atheism, pantheism, nihilism,
gnosticism) — the whole 10-point matching section keys off these; the **three ways humans bear God's
image** (rule, resemblance, relationship) and **stewardship**; **Genesis 1's** forming/filling
pattern, *ex nihilo*, and purposeful creation; the **creation of woman** and **marriage as one man
and one woman**; the **seven ways Genesis is distinct** from other worldviews; and the **Key Verses**
(Gen 1:1, 1:26–28, 1:31, 2:18; Ps 8:4–6). The two essays ask students to (1) explain our relationship
with nature using *stewardship*, and (2) explain the Christian view of creation to someone who
believes in evolution. **Day 5** (worldview distinctives) and **Day 4** (stewardship/nature) carry
the heaviest essay weight; **Days 1–3** carry the terms, verses, and image-of-God items; **Day 8**
review pulls it together.

## Day 1 — Wed Oct 28 · Creation myths & Genesis 1 (6.1–6.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain the purpose of creation myths and name a few examples;
(b) summarize the creation narrative of Genesis 1 — God exists, God is Creator, creation is orderly
and *ex nihilo*, humans made in his image. `[6.1, 6.2]`

**Materials.** Graded Chapter 5 tests to return; display `PS 6.1A Two Creation Myths`; your copy of
`TR 6.1A`; paper and pen; student manuals (Creating a Creation Myth, SM 110–111).

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Return & review the Chapter 5 test.** Hand back graded tests; walk the two or three
  items most students missed. Keep it brief — this block has a lot to carry.
- **0:06–0:09 · Open Chapter 6.** *"We're starting the story itself. Chapter 1 gave you the six
  acts; today we open Act 1 — Creation."* Read the objectives (ST 79). Note this chapter's test
  closes Trimester 1.
- **0:09–0:24 · Content & discuss 6.1–6.2 (manual p.118–119).** What a **creation myth** is (a story
  explaining the nature of reality and where the universe and humans came from) and examples (Ra —
  Egyptian; Ta'aroa — Tahitian; Enlil — Sumerian; and the **Enuma Elish**, where Marduk kills Tiamat
  to build the cosmos). Then Genesis 1: *"In the beginning, God created…"* — two claims: **God
  exists** and **God is Creator.** Creation is orderly — **forming** (days 1–3) then **filling**
  (days 4–6) — spoken into being ***ex nihilo*** (out of nothing). The high point is v. 26: humans,
  male and female, made in **God's image** (*imago Dei*); the "us" hints at the **Trinity**
  (Col 1:15–16; the Spirit in v. 2). Use manual Qs 1–5.
- **0:24–0:42 · Activity: Creating a Creation Myth (`PS 6.1A` / `TR 6.1A`; manual p.119; SM 110–111).**
  Display slide 1 and read the Ancestral Puebloan Emergence myth from `TR 6.1A`; slide 2, read the
  Canaanite myth. Students then write their **own** creation myth (allow ~12 min) covering: who/what
  did the creating, what was created, how, and the similarities/differences between creator and
  creation. Share two or three aloud; display slide 3 (eight details of the biblical account) and
  ask how the myths differ from Genesis.
- **0:42–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **6.3–6.4** (ST 81–84) — Genesis 2 and the attributes of God.
**Assessments.** Creation myth (participation/completion).
**Notes.** Short day — the writing time is the flex item; if you're tight, have students finish the
myth for homework and share on Day 2. Leave the **forming/filling** table on the board; it reappears
on the test's fill-in section.

## Day 2 — Thu Oct 29 · Genesis 2 & the God who creates (6.3–6.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) discuss the creation of humans in Genesis 2 — the seventh-day rest,
work as good, the woman as *ezer* (helper of equal worth); (b) articulate attributes of God,
especially his **transcendence** and **immanence.** `[6.3, 6.4]`

**Materials.** Class Bibles; concordances or computers (one per group); student manuals (Praising
the Creator, SM 112–113).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up.** *"God 'rested' on the seventh day — was he tired?"* Surface answers, then
  land it: **seven** signals completion; God rested in the satisfaction of finished, good work
  (Isa 40:28 — he doesn't grow weary).
- **0:06–0:30 · Read & discuss 6.3–6.4 (manual p.120–121).** Genesis 2 zooms in on humans and names
  God **Yahweh** ("I AM"). God forms the man, sets him in Eden **to work and keep it** — so **work
  is good, not a result of the fall.** The woman is made because *"it is not good that the man should
  be alone"* — the Hebrew *ezer* ("helper") carries no second-class meaning (it's used of God
  himself); man and woman are **equal image-bearers.** Then God's attributes: he is **transcendent**
  (above and outside creation) *and* **immanent** (within and among it, holding all things together —
  Acts 17:28, Col 1:17). Why did God create? Not from need — the Trinity was already in love — but to
  **share** that love. Use manual Qs 1–7.
- **0:30–0:54 · Activity: Praising the Creator (groups of 4–5; manual p.121; SM 112–113).** Each
  student finds **one New Testament passage** that refers to creation (concordance or computer);
  groups read theirs aloud, then discuss *why the Bible keeps returning to creation, not just in
  Genesis.* If time allows, groups turn their verses into a short psalm of praise and share it.
- **0:54–0:60 · Debrief & homework.**

**Homework.** Read **6.5–6.6** (ST 84–87) — the image of God and our relational nature.
**Assessments.** Praising the Creator (participation).
**Notes.** **Transcendence** and **immanence** are both Key Terms and both appear in the matching
section — make sure the distinction lands cleanly before students leave.

## Day 3 — Mon Nov 2 · The image of God; relational & sexed beings (6.5–6.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) analyze the **three** ways humans bear God's image — **rule,
resemblance, relationship**; (b) describe God's design for people as relational and sexed beings,
including **stewardship**, **sub-creation**, and **marriage.** `[6.5, 6.6]`

**Materials.** Pen per student; student manuals (Community Stewardship Plan form, SM 115).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Content & discuss 6.5–6.6 (manual p.122–123).** The **three views of the image of
  God** — write them on the board and keep them there:
  1. **Rule** — God gave humans authority and responsibility to care for and cultivate creation
     (**stewardship**: managing what's been entrusted to your care).
  2. **Resemblance** — we share God-like attributes: intelligence, creativity, morality,
     communication. We are **sub-creators** — we can't make from nothing, but we take what God made
     and make culture (grain → bread; trees → a chair; sound → music).
  3. **Relationship** — we're made for loving relationship with God, ourselves, others, and nature.
  Then 6.6: humans are **relational** (God is Creator, Parent, Protector, Provider, Savior, Friend,
  Lord) and **sexed** beings — sexuality is a gift pointing us toward intimacy and ultimately toward
  God; **marriage** is a loving union of **one man and one woman**, the proper context for sex, and
  singleness bears God's image just as fully. Use manual Qs 1–6.
- **0:24–0:50 · Activity: Community Stewardship Plan (groups of 4–5; manual p.123; SM 115).** Groups
  pick a real geographical area or environmental issue in the community that needs care and build a
  stewardship plan together — but **each student fills out the form individually** (area/issue,
  action plan, who helps, supplies needed, how to spread the word). Groups share plans with the
  class.
- **0:50–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *How is a stewardship plan an example of bearing God's image
  through rule?*

**Homework.** Read **6.7–6.8** (ST 87–90) — nature as gift; how Genesis confronts ancient myths.
**Assessments.** Community Stewardship Plan form (completion — collect the individual forms).
**Notes.** **Rule / resemblance / relationship** and **stewardship** are near-certain test items
(short answer, matching, and essay 37). The **image-of-God = equal worth of male and female** point
also feeds the true/false section — reinforce it.

## Day 4 — Tue Nov 3 · Nature as God's gift; Genesis vs. the ancient myths (6.7–6.8)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) correlate the biblical mandate for **stewardship** to our
relationship with nature — Genesis does *not* license exploitation; (b) distinguish the false ideas
in ancient creation myths from the truth of Genesis. `[6.7, 6.8]`

**Materials.** Computers/tablets (individual, pair, or group); textbooks; student manuals (Reasons
to Believe in God, SM 117).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Read & discuss 6.7–6.8 (manual p.124–125).** Does Genesis let us exploit the world?
  **No.** *"Subdue"* (Gen 1:28) doesn't mean plunder; God placed the man in the garden **to work it
  and keep it** (Gen 2:15) — to *serve* and *preserve*. We are **stewards**, not owners: pollution,
  soil destruction, and cruelty to animals are failures of stewardship, but so is neglecting human
  flourishing. Then 6.8 — how Genesis **confronts pagan myths**: one God (not many), a divine origin,
  a sovereign God with no rival, purposeful creation, creation not from a sexual act, a good and
  loving God, and **humans valued** (not made as slaves — unlike Egypt, where only Pharaoh bore the
  divine image). Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:24–0:52 · Activity: Reasons to Believe in God (individual or pairs; manual p.125; SM 117).**
  Students compile **10 reasons to believe in God**, grounded in forensic and historical evidence,
  drawing on Chapters 1–6 and careful research. As they share, tally reasons on the board (mark
  repeats). Discuss which kinds of evidence came up most. If time allows, students write a short
  paragraph to a skeptic that includes their reasons — the point is to be ready to *give an answer*
  (1 Peter 3:15).
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Which of your 10 reasons would actually move a skeptic — and
  why?*

**Homework.** Read **6.9–6.10** (ST 88–90) — how Genesis confronts modern worldviews.
**Assessments.** Reasons to Believe list (completion).
**Notes.** Essay 37 asks students to explain our relationship with nature and **use the term
*stewardship* with an example** — make sure everyone leaves with a concrete example (recycling,
reducing waste, caring for land and animals). If devices are short, run this in small groups.

## Day 5 — Wed Nov 4 · Genesis confronts modern worldviews; gender & marriage (6.9–6.10)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) determine the **seven ways** the biblical creation story is distinct
from other worldviews (against atheism, pantheism, gnosticism, nihilism, evolutionary reductionism,
and modern views of the body/sexuality); (b) explain why there are two sexes and defend biblical
marriage. `[6.9, 6.10]`

**Materials.** The two videos queued — *Why Did God Only Create Two Genders?* (Stonestreet, 5:45) and
*How To: Defend Marriage* (Stanton, 12:18); student manuals (Gender and Marriage, SM 119–120).

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:03 · Warm-up.** On the board: *"What gives a person value?"* Take two or three answers —
  you'll return to this.
- **0:03–0:17 · Content & discuss 6.9–6.10 (manual p.126–127).** The **seven distinctives** — Genesis
  affirms: a personal God (vs. **atheism**); creation distinct from Creator (vs. **pantheism**); a
  good, purposeful universe (vs. **gnosticism** and **nihilism**); the special creation of humans
  (vs. evolutionary reductionism); the goodness of the body and sexuality; the distinction and
  equality of male and female; and the dignity of **all** people. Land the payoff: because you bear
  God's image, your value is **inherent** — which is the ground for opposing racism, sexism,
  abortion, and euthanasia. Use manual Qs 1–3.
- **0:17–0:42 · Activity: Gender and Marriage (videos; manual p.126–127; SM 119–120).** Play *Why
  Did God Only Create Two Genders?* (5:45) in full; discuss the manual's questions (how many
  sexes; why Eve was the fitting helper; why "inner sense over body" is false). Then, given the
  short period, play the **"three things marriage does as a social institution"** segment of *How To:
  Defend Marriage* (regulates sexuality · gives every child a mother and father · protects and
  provides for women) and discuss how the marriage video connects to the gender video. *(The full
  Stanton video runs 12:18 — assign the remainder as optional viewing or show it in full on a
  regular-length day if your period allows.)*
- **0:42–0:45 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Begin reviewing 6.1–6.10 for the test; skim the Study Guide (SM 128). *(Next two days
are the game and the video set — no new reading.)*
**Assessments.** Gender and Marriage discussion (participation).
**Notes.** The **four worldviews** (atheism, pantheism, nihilism, gnosticism) and their main beliefs
own the matching section, and essay 38 asks students to explain creation to someone who believes in
evolution — this is the day that essay is built on. If content runs long, the marriage-video segment
is the flex item.

## Day 6 — Thu Nov 5 · Creation Feud Game (Lesson 6)

**Objective.** Students review the creation account and contrast it with pagan myths and alternative
worldviews through a whole-class game.

**Materials.** `PS 6.6A Creation Feud Game`; two Bibles; two desk bells; a timer.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:08 · Set up (manual p.128–129).** Two teams facing the board; each picks a representative
  and gets a desk bell (no bells? reps stand instead). Explain the rules: read the question; teams
  have 5 seconds to decide to answer or pass; ring in; both teams get 60 seconds in the textbook or
  Bible; the ringing team picks a student to answer. Regular questions **20 pts**, bonus **40 pts**;
  a wrong or timed-out answer opens a **steal** for the other team.
- **0:08–0:52 · Play (`PS 6.6A`).** Work through the questions — 30 seconds to answer Qs 1–10, 60
  seconds for Qs 11–12, 90 seconds for bonus. A **different student** answers each new question, so
  everyone participates. Guide the discussion that surfaces — the review value is in the back-and-forth,
  not just the score.
- **0:52–0:60 · Score & close.** Tally, recognize the winning team, and clear up any question the
  class stumbled on.

**Homework.** None required (light before the last two days).
**Assessments.** Game participation (informal — listen for which concepts still wobble).
**Notes.** Question 1 (*"Genesis 1–2 teaches core truths about what four important topics?"* — God,
humans, nature, the universe) is the chapter's frame; make sure it lands. Note weak spots to reteach
in the Day 8 review.

## Day 8 — Mon Nov 9 · Attributes activity + review + Study Guide (Lessons 8 + 9, merged)

**Key Terms** (creation myth, Elohim, Yahweh, imago Dei, transcendence, immanence, stewardship) and
