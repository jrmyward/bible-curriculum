## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 8 days** — by *merging*, not cutting. This
is the pivot of the whole six-act story: the Incarnation, the kingdom, the cross, and the empty tomb.
The block has **two short Wednesdays** (Day 2 and Day 7) and no sub day, so the sequencing puts the
self-contained Videos lesson on the first short day and the review on the second, and keeps the four
content-and-activity days (Days 1, 3, 4, 5) at full length where the heaviest teaching lands.

**Two merges make the compression:** the four closing subsections **10.7–10.10** combine on Day 5
(publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the crucifixion, the resurrection, the ascension, and the conclusion —
one narrative sweep to the climax), and the publisher's separate Review (L8) and Study-Guide (L9)
days combine on Day 7, the same review/study merge used all year. Two lessons are also *resequenced*
onto the block's shape rather than dropped: the **Videos** lesson (L7) moves up to the first short
Wednesday (Day 2), where it previews the chapter's big questions and needs only a press of Play; the
**First-Century Sleuth** game (L6) moves to Day 6, right after the resurrection is taught on Day 5,
so students investigate the evidence for material they've just read.

**Lean-layer note:** Chapter 10 needs **no newly generated handouts** — the publisher supplies every
activity (`TR 10.3A`, `TR 10.5A`, `PS 10.6A`, `TR 10.6A–B`, `PS 10.8A`, `PS 10.8B`) plus the

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 10.10A` (/57) is **multiple choice (12 items × 1 = 12 pts; several are select-all) · true/false
(8 × 1 = 8 pts; correct the underlined word if false) · fill-in-the-blank (9 × 2 = 18 pts) · short
answer (3 × 3 = 9 pts) · two essays (2 × 5 = 10 pts).** Its weight lands on the **Incarnation** (the
eternally divine Son of God becoming human; the three ways it's significant; fully God and fully
human; essential for salvation); **Jesus' identity** (the Messiah, not merely a teacher or reformer;
the roles of **prophet, priest, and king**; *the Christ* = "anointed"; the **I AM** and **Son of
Man** claims to deity); the **kingdom of God** (the just and peaceful rule of God over all creation;
the **four parable lessons**; the **vineyard** parable and why it enraged the leaders); the **Sermon
on the Mount** (the eight **beatitudes**, the **Golden Rule**, "judge not," heart vs. outward
actions); **miracles as signs of the kingdom**; **redemption** and the cross; the **resurrection**
objections and the disciples' transformation; the **ascension** (Jesus is accessible); the **gospel**;
and **1 Corinthians 15:3–4**. Days 1 (Incarnation + kingdom), 3 (identity + parables), 4 (Sermon on
the Mount + miracles), and 5 (cross + resurrection) all carry heavy weight — this chapter tests almost
everywhere — and Day 7 review pulls it together.

## Day 1 — Tue Jan 19 · The Incarnation and the kingdom of God (10.1–10.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) conclude three ways the Incarnation is significant; (b) relate Jesus'
ministry to the gospel and the kingdom of God. `[10.1, 10.2]`

**Materials.** The graded Ch 9 tests (to return); student textbooks; class Bibles, paper, and pen for
the Symbolic Numbers groups.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:10 · Return & review the Chapter 9 test.** Hand back Ch 9 tests. Walk the two or three
  items most students missed; keep it brief — the point is to close last chapter's gaps before they
  harden. Then set the stage: *the Second Temple history of Chapter 9 was the runway; today Act 4 —
  redemption accomplished — takes off.*
- **0:10–0:22 · Hook — Gabriel's announcement (ST 135–136).** Read or tell the opening: after roughly
  400 years of apparent silence, Gabriel announces two births, and to Mary he promises a son who *"will
  reign… and of his kingdom there will be no end"* (Luke 1:30–33). Land the definition on the board and
  keep it there: the **Incarnation** — *the action of the eternally divine Son of God becoming a human
  being.*
- **0:22–0:40 · Read & discuss 10.1–10.2 (manual p.204–205).** The **three ways the Incarnation is
  significant:** it is **essential for salvation** (only a fully human, fully divine representative can
  stand in our place); it **affirms that creation is good** (matter is not evil — the problem is sin,
  not being human); and it means **Jesus suffers with us** (*"the Word became flesh and dwelt among
  us,"* John 1:14 — he got tired, hungry, grieved). Then the **kingdom of God** — not a place in the
  clouds but *the just and peaceful rule of God over all creation* — and the **gospel** (*euangelion*),
  the good news that Jesus is Lord and that through him salvation and forgiveness are available to all.
  Use manual Qs 1–4 (p.204–205), including the discussion question on *"not human enough."*
- **0:40–0:56 · Activity: Symbolic Numbers (six groups; manual p.205).** Divide the class into six
  groups; give each a passage (Group 1 Matt 2:7–12 · 2 Matt 4:1–11 · 3 Matt 5:2–11 · 4 Matt 9:18–26 ·
  5 Matt 10:1–8 · 6 Matt 12:38–42) and the biblical-numbers key (1 = God's unity / Christ's single
  sacrifice; 3 = the Trinity and completeness; 4 = creation and universal truth; 7 = completion and
  rest; 8 = new life and resurrection; 12 = God's government; 40 = testing and trial). Groups find the
  symbolic numbers in their passage and explain how the number reinforces the message, then a rep
  reports out. *Purpose:* students read the Gospels closely and see how the details carry meaning.
- **0:56–0:60 · Homework + week preview.** Tell students tomorrow is a **short Wednesday video day**.

**Homework.** Read **10.3–10.4** (ST 137–140) — Jesus' identity and his parables — for Thursday.
**Assessments.** Symbolic Numbers reports (participation).
**Notes.** The Incarnation is one of the test's heaviest topics (MC 1, 11, 12; T/F 15, 18; essay 34).
Leave the definition of the Incarnation and of the kingdom of God on the board through the block.

## Day 3 — Thu Jan 21 · Jesus' identity and his parables (10.3–10.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) contrast popular views of Jesus' identity with Jesus' own claim to be
the Messiah; (b) explain four lessons Jesus taught about the kingdom of God through parables and
interpret the parable of the tenants. `[10.3, 10.4]` *(High test weight — identity and the parables
run through multiple choice, fill-in, and short answer.)*

**Materials.** Class Bibles, paper, and pen per pair (`Pen a Parable`).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up.** On the board: *"Who do people say Jesus was?"* Collect the common answers
  (teacher, healer, prophet, good example) — you'll test each against Jesus' own claim.
- **0:06–0:30 · Read & discuss 10.3–10.4 (manual p.206–207).** **Popular views** — philosopher, faith
  healer, one more prophet, social reformer — each true in part but none big enough: Jesus identified
  himself as **the Messiah** and as **God incarnate**, so (C. S. Lewis's point) he can't be filed under
  "great moral teacher." As Messiah he filled three roles — **prophet** (announced God's covenant and
  called for repentance), **priest** (offered himself as the sacrifice, now advocates for us), and
  **king** (brought the kingdom and will complete it). He claimed deity by forgiving sins, calling
  himself **"I AM"** (Exod 3 / John 8:58), and taking the title **"Son of Man"** (Dan 7); *the Christ*
  means **"anointed"** in Greek, another word for Messiah. Then the **four parable lessons** about the
  kingdom (Mark 4, Matt 13): it does **not come all at once**; it does **not come with irresistible
  power** in the present; the **final judgment is reserved for the future**; and its **full revelation
  is postponed** so many can enter it now. Close with the **parable of the tenants/vineyard** (Mark
  12:1–12) — owner = God, farmers = Israel's leaders, servants = the prophets, son = Jesus — and why
  the leaders wanted to kill him once they understood it. Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:30–0:52 · Activity: Pen a Parable (pairs; manual p.207).** Pairs read Galatians 1:6–10, then
  write their own parable — a story of everyday characters carrying a biblical truth — showing the
  foolishness of trading the true gospel for a false one. A few pairs read theirs aloud. *Purpose:*
  writing a parable teaches how a parable works, and why Jesus chose the form.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Whose parable made the point clearest — and what made it land?*

**Homework.** Read **10.5–10.6** (ST 140–143) — Jesus' miracles and his teaching — for Friday.
**Assessments.** Pen a Parable responses (participation).
**Notes.** Near-certain test items live here: *the Christ* = anointed (MC 3), the three Messianic roles
(MC 4), the ways Jesus claimed deity (MC 5), the four parable lessons (MC 8), and the vineyard parable
(fill-in 22, short answer 30). If the reading runs long, the vineyard parable is the piece not to cut.

## Day 4 — Fri Jan 22 · Jesus' miracles and the Sermon on the Mount (10.5–10.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) discuss two miracles Jesus accomplished and what they were a sign of;
(b) name Jesus' most famous ethical teaching; (c) list and describe the eight categories of people
Jesus called blessed in the beatitudes. `[10.5, 10.6]` *(High test weight — the beatitudes, the Golden
Rule, and "judge not" are all over the test.)*

**Materials.** `TR 10.3A Who Do You Say I Am?` — 1 per student (+ answer key); class Bibles; pens.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:26 · Read & discuss 10.5–10.6 (manual p.208–209).** **Miracles as signs:** Jesus healed the
  sick and raised the dead as **signs that the kingdom had come** and confirmation that he was the
  Messiah. Work the two the textbook develops — the **feeding of the 5,000** (prefiguring his own body
  and blood given for us) and the **raising of Lazarus** (where Jesus **wept** — God does not stand
  aloof from our grief). Then the **Sermon on the Mount** (Matthew 5–7), Jesus' most famous **ethical
  teaching** (instruction about right and wrong — thoughts, words, and actions). Walk the **eight
  beatitudes** and write a one-line gloss for each (poor in spirit = humble before God; those who mourn
  = grieving; the meek = gentle, strength under control; hunger and thirst for righteousness = long for
  God to set things right; the merciful = generous, no revenge; pure in heart = pure inwardly;
  peacemakers = reconcile others; persecuted for righteousness = suffer for following God). Jesus came
  to **fulfill** the law, not abolish it — he **intensifies** the commands to reach the **heart**
  (hating is a kind of murder). Close with the **Golden Rule** (treat others as you want to be treated,
  Matt 7:12) and **"judge not"** (Matt 7:1 — not "never call anything wrong," but judge with grace, and
  clear the log from your own eye first). Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:26–0:52 · Activity: Identifying Jesus (`TR 10.3A`; manual p.209).** Hand out `TR 10.3A Who Do You
  Say I Am?` Students match each false belief about Jesus to the counter-evidence that refutes it (more
  than one answer is possible); go over it together. Then **role-play:** you play the skeptic or the
  member of another religion listed on `TR 10.3A`; student volunteers play the Christian, opening with
  *"Who do you think Jesus is?"* and asking clarifying questions (*"What do you mean by…? How did you
  come to believe that?"*) before answering from the evidence and their Bible. *Purpose:* students
  rehearse defending Jesus' identity — the exact work the identity questions on the test ask for.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Which false view of Jesus do you hear most often, and what's the
  one sentence you'd say back?*

**Homework.** Read **10.7–10.10** (ST 143–147) — the crucifixion, resurrection, ascension, and
conclusion — for Monday. *(This is the weekend's bigger read; it feeds the block's fullest day.)*
**Assessments.** Identifying Jesus worksheet (completion) + role-play (participation).
**Notes.** The beatitudes (MC 10, short answer 32), the Golden Rule (fill-in 23-adjacent, MC 6),
"ethical teaching" (MC 7), "Sermon on the Mount" (fill-in 24), heart vs. actions (fill-in 27), and
"judge not" (essay 33) are all near-certain items. If time is tight, the beatitudes walk-through is
the piece to protect; the role-play can shrink to two rounds.

## Day 5 — Mon Jan 25 · The cross, the empty tomb, the ascension & the call to faith (10.7–10.10, merged)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain two aspects of Jesus' redemptive work on the cross; (b)
respond to skeptical objections to the resurrection and give the most plausible explanation for the
disciples' transformation; (c) analyze two major implications of Jesus' ascension; (d) explain what it
means to have faith in Jesus. `[10.7, 10.8, 10.9, 10.10]` *(Merges publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the four
closing subsections. This is the pivot's climax; high test weight on the cross and the resurrection.)*

**Materials.** Paper and pen (`Teach Others Also`); `TR 10.5A The Incarnation Bible Study` + answer key
if you run it here (otherwise it is the day's flex item — see Notes); class Bibles.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:28 · Read & discuss 10.7–10.10 (manual p.210–213).** Move fast but land the two heavy
  sections. **The cross (10.7):** the Roman sign *"This is Jesus, the King of the Jews"* was meant as
  mockery but told the truth. Define **redemption** — *to be rescued from the consequences of sin
  through the sacrificial death of Jesus* — and its **two aspects:** Jesus died as our **substitute**,
  taking the punishment we deserved, and Jesus **defeated the enemies** (sin and death) that held us in
  bondage. **The resurrection (10.8):** the disciples went from hiding to fearless within a few years —
  why? Work the **three skeptical objections** and their answers: *the disciples stole the body*
  (they'd fled, and would have had to overpower the guard), *Jesus only swooned* (the Romans verified
  death with a spear; a mangled Jesus wouldn't inspire worship), and *the disciples hallucinated* (over
  **500** saw him at once — hallucinations aren't shared). Paul's stakes: if Jesus didn't rise, our
  faith is worthless (1 Cor 15:17). **The ascension (10.9):** its **two implications** — Jesus now
  reigns with **all authority** at the Father's right hand (so he commissions us to spread the gospel),
  and Jesus is **available and accessible** everywhere (no temple needed; our great High Priest
  intercedes for us). **The conclusion (10.10):** faith in Jesus is not blind belief but **trust and
  allegiance** — to stop trusting yourself, repent, and give him your loyalty. Use manual Qs from
  p.210–213, favoring the cross and the resurrection objections.
- **0:28–0:50 · Activity: Teach Others Also (manual p.211).** Each student builds a teaching aid — an
  illustration, acronym, or acrostic — to explain the gospel to someone who's never heard it: *what's
  wrong with the world, how Jesus overcame it, why we need him, how we get right with God, and why it
  matters* — in plain words, no Christian jargon. A few students share or role-play using theirs.
  *Purpose:* students put the whole act into one clear, shareable message.
- **0:50–0:60 · Close & homework.** *If a friend asked why the empty tomb matters, what would you say
  in thirty seconds?* Tie it back to redemption.

**Homework.** Begin studying for the Chapter 10 Test (Study Guide, SM 204–205; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Teach Others Also (participation); `TR 10.5A` (completion) if used.
**Notes.** The block's fullest day — four subsections and an activity. The **Incarnation Bible Study**
(`TR 10.5A`, Lesson 5's assigned activity, on the goodness of the body) is the **flex item:** assign
it as homework or hold it for Day 7 rather than cut the cross-and-resurrection discussion, which maps
straight onto the test (MC 9; T/F 13, 17, 19, 20; fill-in 26, 29). Redemption (MC 9) and the
resurrection objections are near-certain.

## Day 6 — Tue Jan 26 · First-Century Sleuth (Lesson 6)

*Full-period stations game (the manual budgets 45–50 minutes). It lands the day after the resurrection
is taught, so students investigate the evidence for the death and resurrection of Jesus they've just
read — and decode a first-century Christian creed, 1 Corinthians 15:3–4, which is a fill-in item on
the test.*

**Objective.** Students examine the evidence for the death and resurrection of Jesus and identify a
first-century Christian creed.

**Materials.** `PS 10.6A First-Century Sleuth` (preview beforehand); `TR 10.6A` clue sheets and code
sheets placed at stations around the room; `TR 10.6B` answer key (for you); pens and paper.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Set up (`PS 10.6A`, slides 1–2; manual p.214).** Divide the class into four or five
  teams; give each paper and pens. Read the storyline on slides 1–2. Tell teams they have **35 minutes**
  to work every station.
- **0:06–0:41 · Investigate the stations.** Teams move among the clue and code stations, gathering
  evidence and filling in the scroll. Circulate and keep the pace; don't let a team camp at one station.
- **0:41–0:52 · Report & decode (`PS 10.6A`, slides 3–6).** Display slide 3; teams give their answers
  for each blank on the scroll (check against `TR 10.6B`). Read the possible explanations on slide 3,
  then slide 4 — each team gives its conclusion. Read slides 5–6: did teams decode the italicized
  excerpt of **1 Corinthians 15:3–4**? Have the class recite it together.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief.** *A few skeptics admit they can't offer a better explanation but still won't
  accept the resurrection — why? What is a bias against the supernatural, and is it fair?* Tie back to
  Day 5's three objections.

**Homework.** Continue studying (Study Guide, SM 204–205; Key Terms & Verses). *(Tomorrow is a short
Wednesday review, then the test Thursday.)*
**Assessments.** First-Century Sleuth (participation — evidence gathered, creed decoded).
**Notes.** 1 Corinthians 15:3–4 is a Key Verse and a fill-in item (26); the decode round makes it
stick. If the stations run long, trim the debrief, not the decode.

  confirm the five Key Terms and four Key Verses. The manual's 22 study-guide questions with answers are
  the answer key.
- **0:42–0:45 · Test preview.** Tomorrow: 57 points — multiple choice, true/false, fill-in, short
  answer, and two essays. *"If you can give the three ways the Incarnation matters, say who Jesus
  claimed to be, and explain why the resurrection is the best explanation for the empty tomb, you're
  most of the way there."*

**Homework.** Study for the test.
**Assessments.** Mythical or Factual? (participation) + Study Guide completion.
**Notes.** If Day 3 (identity/parables) or Day 5 (cross/resurrection) felt rushed, this is the place to
reteach — with Day 1's Incarnation, they are the test's center of gravity. Fold in the Incarnation
Bible Study here if it was held over from Day 5.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:04 · Settle & distribute.** Clear desks; hand out the test (57 pts).
- **0:04–0:52 · Test.** Students complete independently. Early finishers begin reading **11.1–11.2**
  (Chapter 11).
- **0:52–0:60 · Collect & bridge.** Collect tests. Preview Ch 11: *"Act 5: Restoration Initiated — The
  Church" — Jesus has ascended and reigns; next we ask what it looks like to live for him, in his
