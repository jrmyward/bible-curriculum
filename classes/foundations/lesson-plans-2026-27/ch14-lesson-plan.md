## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 7 days** — by *merging*, not cutting.
There is **no sub day** this block, but the calendar hands us **two short Wednesdays** (Day 1
Mar 10 and Day 5 Mar 17). We steer the two lowest-new-content lessons onto them: the publisher's
**Videos** lesson opens the chapter on Day 1 (it primes the exact questions the chapter answers —
*What does it mean to be human? How can we change to be more like Christ?*), and the **Castle
Squares** review game lands on Day 5. Neither adds new tested material, so the short periods cost
nothing on the test.

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 14.10A` (/58) is **matching (4 pts) · multiple choice (9 pts) · true/false (5 pts) ·
fill-in-the-blank (8 × 2 = 16 pts) · short answer (3 × 3 = 9 pts) · essay (3 × 5 = 15 pts).** Its
weight lands on the **four Key-Term definitions** in the matching section — **Docetism, Arianism,
glorify God, glorification**; on **Jesus as fully human and fully God** and why Jews called the
incarnation blasphemy; on **glorifying God** (examples) and the **doctrine of glorification**; on
**Jesus as servant** and what it means to **pick up your cross daily**; on **Christian hope** (vs.
optimism and pessimism); on the **problems of the New Testament churches**; and on the fill-in
cluster about **suffering and God's love** — suffering is "not a matter of *if* but *when*,"
earthly suffering "cannot be compared" to future glory, and "nothing" can separate us from God's
love. The three essays ask students to (30) reconcile service, the cross, and persecution with
hope; (31) explain why Scripture shows the NT churches were imperfect; and (32) say who God wants
us to be and how we become that. **Day 2** (the four Key Terms + the human/divine doctrine) and
**Day 4** (hope, Christlikeness, community, conclusion) carry the most weight, with **Day 3**
(servant/cross) feeding the fill-ins and the first essay. The **16-point fill-in section is the
single largest** — the Day 6 review should drill exact wording, not just gist.

## Day 2 — Thu Mar 11 · Being human, Jesus' humanity, becoming like God & union with Christ (14.1–14.4, merged)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain what being human means and why we have a need for God;
(b) express that because Jesus was fully human, he is a model for holy living; (c) infer that as we
follow Jesus, our character is gradually transformed so we can glorify God; (d) describe the
benefits of being in union with Christ. `[14.1, 14.2, 14.3, 14.4]` *(Merges publisher Lessons 1
and 2 — the front doctrinal half. Highest test weight: the four Key Terms, the human/divine
doctrine, and glorification all key off these sections.)*

**Materials.** `PS 14.1A Being Human` (display); one copy of `TR 14.2A Glorification Study Answer
Key`; student manuals (Glorification Study); Bibles and pens.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:10 · Warm-up: Being Human (`PS 14.1A`; manual p.293).** Display the slides; students read
  the first two, then compare the quotations on the following slides to the Christian view of human
  nature. Name where each falls short: **Harari** denies purposeful creation and says human life has
  no meaning; **Nhat Hanh** says the self doesn't exist; **Xunzi** says human nature is evil;
  **Tesla** compares humans to robots without free will. *Land it:* the Bible says we are made in
  God's image — dignity and worth "right from the get-go," made for relationship, yet limited and
  broken by sin. *(This warm-up is the flex item — if a class runs slow, shorten it and protect the
  Glorification Study.)*
- **0:10–0:38 · Read & discuss 14.1–14.4 (manual p.292–295).** Move through the four sections:
  - **14.1 Being human** — made in God's image (Gen 1:26–27); made for relationship with God, self,
    others, creation; *limited* (which drives us to depend on God), and *broken* by the sin nature
    inherited from Adam. Being human is not the problem — sin is.
  - **14.2 Living in the here and now** — Jesus was *fully human* (John 1:14), with a real body,
    real needs, real temptations, yet without sin. Name the heresy: **Docetism**, the teaching that
    Jesus was completely divine and only *appeared* human — rejected by the church. To follow Jesus
    is to love people and worship God in the "mud and muck" of ordinary life, not to withdraw from it.
  - **14.3 Becoming like God & glorifying him** — we don't become God; we become like him through
    the Holy Spirit. "Be perfect" (Matt 5:48) uses *telios* — complete, mature — not sinless. To
    **glorify God** is to make God famous by worshipping him and attesting to his goodness through
    holiness and acts of love.
  - **14.4 Participating in the divine life** — union with Christ (Rom 6): the old self dies, the
    new self is raised; we "partake of the divine nature" (2 Pet 1:4). Name the second heresy:
    **Arianism**, the teaching that Jesus was only a divine being *created* by the Father — also
    rejected. Then **glorification**: the doctrine that all believers will finally be freed from sin,
    restored, and given new eternal bodies. Use manual Qs from p.292–295 — favor the two heresies,
    glorify/glorification, and Q4 on Jesus' humanity.
- **0:38–0:56 · Activity: Glorification Study (`TR 14.2A`; manual p.294).** Students work the
  Glorification Study in their student manual on their own (Bible + pen), then share; check
  responses against `TR 14.2A`. *Purpose:* the doctrine of glorification is a matching item and a
  fill-in — this puts students in the actual verses behind it.
- **0:56–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **14.5–14.6** (ST 193–195) — becoming a servant and picking up the cross.
**Assessments.** Glorification Study (completion/participation).
**Notes.** Keep the four Key Terms on the board through the block — **Docetism · Arianism · glorify
God · glorification** — with a one-line definition each. The matching section tests exactly these,
and students confuse Docetism and Arianism unless the wording stays visible.

## Day 3 — Mon Mar 15 · Becoming a servant; picking up the cross (14.5–14.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) conclude that following Jesus requires serving others and that a
servant can still be a leader; (b) discuss how following Jesus means picking up our cross daily and
being willing to face suffering and persecution for his sake. `[14.5, 14.6]`

**Materials.** One exercise mat per group of five (optional, for Trust Falls); clear floor space.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:05 · Warm-up.** On the board: *"What's the lowest job you'd be embarrassed to be caught
  doing?"* Surface a few, then set up the section — Jesus did the lowest job on purpose (washed
  feet).
- **0:05–0:30 · Read & discuss 14.5–14.6 (manual p.296–297).** *Becoming a servant:* the Greek
  *doulos* (servant/slave); Jesus "emptied himself, taking the form of a servant" (Phil 2:7–8),
  washed the disciples' feet, came "to seek and save the lost." True humility isn't thinking poorly
  of yourself — it's recognizing God's gifts and using them for others. A servant *can* lead: he
  leads by serving first, not by "lording it over" others (Mark 10:42–45). *Picking up the cross:*
  Jesus rebuked Peter and called disciples to "deny himself and take up his cross" (Matt 16:24–25).
  Following Jesus doesn't promise ease — it promises trouble (John 16:33) and sometimes persecution.
  But suffering *for* Christ (not for being rude or foolish) is blessed; God never abandons us
  (Heb 13:5), sympathizes with us (Heb 4:15), and "nothing can separate us from God's love"
  (Rom 8:31–39). Use manual Qs 1–6 (p.296–297) — favor Q3 (servant-leader), Q4 (suffering), Q6
  (what encourages us in suffering).
- **0:30–0:50 · Activity: Trust Falls (manual p.297).** Volunteers form groups of five — one faller,
  four catchers — on an exercise mat. Catchers pair off, grasp wrists (not hands), and say "Ready";
  the faller crosses arms, keeps knees straight, and says "Falling." Be sensitive to students who'd
  rather observe and encourage. Afterward, connect it: *falling backward is trusting people you
  can't see — how does that relate to relying on God and his people when you serve or suffer?*
  *(Purely voluntary — no student should feel pressured to fall or catch.)*
- **0:50–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Where in real life does following Jesus already cost you
  something — time, comfort, reputation?*

**Homework.** Read **14.7–14.10** (ST 195–200) — hope, growing in Christlikeness, community, and
the conclusion. *(Tomorrow merges four sections, so it's a fuller reading day.)*
**Assessments.** Trust Falls (informal — participation and the debrief connection).
**Notes.** Two Key Verses live here — **Matt 16:24–25** ("take up his cross") and **Phil 2:3–4**
(count others more significant) — and both surface as fill-ins ("*save* his life," "not look *only*
to our own interests"). Say them out loud and leave them on the board. If a class is uneasy about
physical Trust Falls, the discussion of Q6 is the safe substitute — it lands the same point.

## Day 4 — Tue Mar 16 · Living in hope, growing in Christlikeness, community & conclusion (14.7–14.10, merged)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) differentiate Christian hope from optimism and pessimism and
explain why believers can have such hope; (b) give biblical examples of growing in Christlikeness
(Peter, Martha, Paul); (c) summarize why believers must be in community; (d) state who God wants us
to be. `[14.7, 14.8, 14.9, 14.10]` *(Merges publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the closing arc. High test
weight: Christian hope, the Christlikeness examples, the NT churches, and the conclusion all feed
short-answer and essay questions.)*

**Materials.** Paper and pen per student (Hope Role Play, groups of four); student manuals (Growing
in Community questionnaire).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:28 · Read & discuss 14.7–14.10 (manual p.298–301).** Move through the four sections,
  favoring hope and the examples:
  - **14.7 Living in hope** — **Christian hope** is neither *optimism* (which minimizes real pain)
    nor *pessimism* (which despairs); it looks honestly at the world and trusts *what Jesus has done
    and will do*. Its object is Jesus — backward to the cross, forward to his return. Hope isn't
    passive; it "gets busy" serving God.
  - **14.8 Growing in Christlikeness** — becoming like Christ is a *lifelong journey*. Walk the three
    examples: **Peter** (hotheaded, denied Jesus → restored, bold, martyred), **Martha** (too busy
    to listen → boldly confessed Jesus as Messiah), **Paul** (persecutor → mentor of Timothy,
    martyred).
  - **14.9 Growing together** — there are no solo Christians; much of what Jesus commands (loving,
    serving) is impossible alone. The NT churches were *not* ideal — Rome (Jew/Gentile disunity),
    Corinth (popularity contests, sexual sin), Galatia (legalism), Thessalonica (persecution) — yet
    God worked through them.
  - **14.10 Conclusion** — God isn't trying to make you someone you're not; he made you "fearfully
    and wonderfully" and is restoring you to who you were made to be — like Jesus. The church is the
    bride of Christ. Use manual Qs from p.298–301 — favor 14.7 Q1 (hope vs. optimism/pessimism),
    14.8 Q3 (Peter/Martha/Paul), and 14.9 Q1–2 (why community; the early churches' problems).
- **0:28–0:50 · Activity: Hope Role Play (groups of four; manual p.298).** Each group assigns four
  roles — an *optimist*, a *pessimist*, a *defender of Christian hope*, and a *critic*. The
  pessimist speaks two minutes on the state of the church today; the optimist offers two minutes of
  counterpoint; the defender responds to both for two minutes; the critic takes notes on each
  argument's strengths and weaknesses, then shares. Groups discuss and report what they learned
  about presenting Christian hope. *Purpose:* students rehearse the exact distinction the multiple
  choice and essay ask for.
- **0:50–0:58 · Growing in Community (manual p.300–301).** *(Flex — the day's compressible piece.)*
  If time holds, students complete the Growing in Community questionnaire in their student manual and
  share one answer. If the room ran slow, assign it as part of tonight's study instead.
- **0:58–0:60 · Close & homework.** *Who is one person in your church community who has helped you
  grow — and how?*

**Homework.** Begin studying for the Chapter 14 Test — reread any thin spots in 14.1–14.10 and start
the Study Guide (SM 283–285). *(Tomorrow is a review game — no new reading due.)*
**Assessments.** Hope Role Play (participation); Growing in Community questionnaire (completion, if
run in class).
**Notes.** This is the block's fullest day — four sections and up to two activities. The **Growing
in Community** questionnaire is the flex item; protect the **Hope Role Play**, which maps straight
onto the "Christian hope" multiple choice and the first essay. Essay 32 ("who does God want us to
be?") draws on the 14.10 conclusion — make sure it doesn't get rushed at the buzzer.

## Day 5 — Wed Mar 17 · Castle Squares (Lesson 6) · **short**

*Short ~45-min period. This full-period review game is built to run in 45–50 minutes and adds no new
tested content, so the shortened Wednesday costs nothing. Its questions map to the chapter's
sections, so it doubles as a first pass at test review.*

**Objective.** Students collaborate to respond to scenarios drawn from every topic in the chapter,
finding the correct answer and the supporting Scripture.

**Materials.** `PS 14.6A Castle Squares` (preview beforehand); two copies of `TR 14.6A Castle
Squares` (one per team) + the answer key for you; the nine-square "castle" prepared (eight papers
numbered 1–8 plus one marked **K**, taped to chairs/floor); a foam ball; Bibles; paper.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Set up (`PS 14.6A`, slide 1; manual p.302–303).** Build or reveal the nine-square
  castle. Divide the class into two teams; give each team one copy of `TR 14.6A` (12 possible
  answers — two of them false — matching 10 questions). Seat the student with the nearest birthday
  at the **King (K)** square; each team sends four players to the numbered squares, the rest rotating
  in. Remind students they may use their textbooks to find answers.
- **0:06–0:40 · Play (manual p.303).** The King tosses the ball; a surrounding player catches it (no
  jumping, leaving a square, or fighting for it). The catcher exits to collaborate with their team
  (max two minutes) to pick the correct answer from `TR 14.6A` and find its Scripture in the Bible;
  meanwhile the others rotate up a square. The team presents by naming the letter, reading the
  answer, and reading the Scripture aloud; confirm with the slide. A wrong or incomplete answer
  passes to the other team for one minute. Repeat through all 10 questions or until time is up.
- **0:40–0:45 · Wrap & homework.** Recognize the leading team; name the two or three questions the
  class struggled with most — those are the review targets for tomorrow.

**Homework.** Study for the test (Study Guide, SM 283–285; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Game participation (informal — note which sections tripped students up).
**Notes.** If the castle setup or rotations eat time, cut the number of rounds rather than the
collaboration step — the point is that students *find and defend* answers with Scripture, which is
exactly the recall the test rewards.

conclusion) felt rushed, this is the place to reteach — they are the test's center of gravity.
