## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 9 days** — by *merging*, not cutting. It
is the **final act**: the climax and resolution of the whole six-act story we started in Chapter 1.
Creation → Fall → Israel → Jesus → the Church → **New Creation** lands, by design, on the last day
of Trimester 2. Two short Wednesdays (Days 3 and 8) shape the block, so the two lessons that add no
new tested content — the **Videos** and the whole-story synthesis game **God's Big Story** — move
onto those short periods, which keeps the heavy Hell/Heaven lesson on a full day.

**One merge makes the compression:** the publisher's separate Review (L8) and Study-Guide (L9) days
combine on Day 7 — the same review/study merge used in every prior chapter. Two lessons are also
*resequenced* onto the awkward short Wednesdays rather than dropped: the **Videos** lesson (L7) moves
up to Day 3, where six short clips plus questions fit a 45-minute period cleanly and preview the back
half of the chapter, and **God's Big Story** (L6) moves to Day 8 — a single focused synthesis

thing students do before the test that completes the story. Neither carries new tested content, so
the two short periods cost nothing on the test. Keeping them off the full days lets the
heavily-tested **Hell/Heaven** lesson (Day 4) run a full hour.

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 12.10A` (/60) is **multiple choice (14 pts) · true/false (8 pts) · fill-in-the-blank (7 items ×
2 = 14 pts) · short answer (3 × 3 = 9 pts) · essay (3 × 5 = 15 pts).** Its weight lands on **hell**
(what it is, why the imagery may be symbolic without being less awful, and that people in a sense
*choose* it — multi-select 13, fill-in 23, and essay 33: *"If God is loving, why does he send people
to hell?"*); on **judgment** (that many judgments are the natural consequences of evil and that
judgment is God's justice against evil, not petty revenge — multi-select 12, fill-in 28, essay 34
tying judgment to the slaughtered Lamb); on the **new creation / restoration** (a renewal, not a
replacement, of creation — 2, 14, 22, 24, short answer 30 on the New Jerusalem); on **Revelation as
apocalyptic literature and its context** (apocalypse = unveiling; symbol and metaphor; prophecy;
letter to the church — 3, 4, 5, 6, 7); on the **Lamb and the way of suffering** (the Lion who
conquers as a slain Lamb; suffering is normative — 8, 9, 11, 29); on **heaven vs. the new creation
as the Christian's destiny** (16, 25, short answer 31); on the **second coming** (18, short answer
32); and on **why the biblical metanarrative gives hope** (essay 35). Day 4 (hell/heaven) and Day 2
(the Lamb, suffering, judgment) carry the most weight, with Day 5 (second coming + the Genesis↔
Revelation chart) and Day 6 (New Jerusalem + the metanarrative) close behind. Day 7 review and the
Day 8 six-act synthesis pull it together.

## Day 1 — Thu Feb 11 · Longing for restoration; the context of Revelation (12.1–12.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) relate redemption through Jesus Christ to the Christian hope of
future restoration; (b) explain the literary and cultural context of the book of Revelation.
`[12.1, 12.2]`

**Materials.** The graded Ch 11 tests (to return); student textbooks; class Bibles for the Restoring
Hope groups.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:10 · Return & review the Chapter 11 test.** Hand back Ch 11 tests. Walk the two or three
  items most students missed — expect the church's *mission* (Act 5) to need a quick reteach, since
  this chapter picks up right where it left off. Keep it brief; close last chapter's gaps before they
  harden.
- **0:10–0:20 · Hook — restoration, not the trash heap (ST 162–163).** The word *restoration* comes
  from *restore* — to repair something or return it to its original state. **God doesn't discard
  broken things or people; he repairs them.** That's why the Bible's story ends not with the
  destruction of this world but its **restoration**. Read the string of restoration verses on ST 162
  (Ps 23:3; Amos 9:14; Zech 9:12; Acts 3:21; **1 Pet 5:10** — a Key Verse). Land the hope: because
  God has kept his promises in the past and his Spirit is with us now, we can trust him for the day
  the new creation arrives.
- **0:20–0:40 · Read & discuss 12.1–12.2 (manual p.248–249).** Then the context of Revelation:
  **apocalypse** means *revelation, unveiling, or uncovering*; **apocalyptic literature** conveys
  meaning through symbol and metaphor, reveals the true nature of reality, and encourages God's
  people in crisis. Revelation is an apocalypse, written as a **prophecy** (its most common job, like
  the OT prophets, is to call God's people to faithfulness), and sent as a **letter** to seven
  churches in Asia Minor. Historical context: John lived under Rome, where **emperor worship** was
  tied to economic life; Christians who refused to worship Caesar were treated as outsiders and faced
  sporadic persecution. Use manual Qs 1–4 (p.248–249), including *what similarities do you see between
  Roman culture and ours?* (money, self, power, sexuality as demanding "gods").
- **0:40–0:58 · Activity: Restoring Hope (groups; manual p.249).** Four groups; assign each two of the
  eight Bible references (2 Cor 1:3–4; 2 Cor 10:3–5; Phil 2:3–4; Col 3:12–13; Col 3:23–24; James
  1:27; 1 Pet 1:14–17; 1 Pet 2:11–12). Groups brainstorm how to *apply* their verses to restoring
  hope in their families or communities while we wait for God to restore all things. Groups share.
- **0:58–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **12.3–12.4** (ST 165–167) — the Lamb, suffering, and judgment.
**Assessments.** Restoring Hope (participation).
**Notes.** This is the last chapter of the story arc — say so out loud. Leave the six acts on the
board (or point to them if they're already up) so students feel Act 6 close the loop this week.

## Day 2 — Tue Feb 16 · Worshipping God, following the Lamb; judgment (12.3–12.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) describe the relevance of the Lamb in the book of Revelation;
(b) articulate the role of suffering in the Christian life; (c) defend the Christian view of God's
final judgment. `[12.3, 12.4]` *(High test weight — the Lamb, the way of suffering, and judgment run
through the multiple choice, fill-in, and essay 34.)*

**Materials.** `TR 12.2A Biblical Symbolism` (1 per student) + answer key; a pen per student.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up.** Put on the board Revelation's key question: **"Whom do we worship?"** Rome
  said *Caesar is lord*. Ask: who or what quietly asks for your ultimate allegiance today?
- **0:06–0:30 · Read & discuss 12.3–12.4 (manual p.250–251).** The two central images of Revelation:
  **God on the throne** as the only true Ruler, and the **slain Lamb**. John is told to look for the
  **Lion of Judah** but sees a **slaughtered Lamb** — Jesus, who conquers sin and death *not by
  violence but by sacrifice*, his righteous life, death, and resurrection. The Lion says he
  conquers; the Lamb shows how. Followers "follow the Lamb wherever he goes," even to death:
  **suffering is a normal part of the Christian life** (the way of the Lamb is the way of the cross,
  Luke 9:23). Then **judgment**: some judgments in Revelation are people and empires reaping the
  **consequences** of their own evil; judgment is God's **justice against evil**, not petty revenge —
  a God of love will not let evil run rampant forever. **Babylon** stands for Rome and every evil
  empire that sets itself against God. Use manual Qs 1–6, especially Q6 (*can God judge and still be
  loving?* — yes; justice is a function of love).
- **0:30–0:52 · Activity: Biblical Symbolism (`TR 12.2A`; manual p.251).** Students match the letters
  for each symbol to the correct OT and Revelation imagery on the worksheet, then discuss *why* God
  sometimes teaches through symbols and numbers (to help limited human minds grasp and remember hard
  truths). Check against the answer key.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Where have you seen the "way of the Lamb" — winning by
  sacrifice rather than force?*

**Homework.** Read **12.5–12.6** (ST 168–170) — hell and heaven. *(Wednesday is a video day, so this
reading is due Thursday.)*
**Assessments.** Biblical Symbolism worksheet (completion).
**Notes.** Freshmen hear "God judges" and picture a temper. Land Q6 hard — *because* God is love, he
will not let evil win forever. That's the exact move essay 34 asks for.

## Day 4 — Thu Feb 18 · Hell and heaven (12.5–12.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) clarify the truth about hell; (b) discuss the eternal destiny of
Christians. `[12.5, 12.6]` *(Highest test weight — hell drives multi-select 13, fill-in 23, and essay
33; heaven vs. the new creation drives 16, 25, and short answer 31.)*

**Materials.** `TR 12.3A God's Character Bible Study` (1 per student) + answer key; Bibles; a pen per
student.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Read & discuss 12.5–12.6 (manual p.252–253).** **Hell** is a place where all who
  reject Christ spend eternity **excluded from God's presence** — prepared for Satan and for those
  who reject God. The fire images may be literal or symbolic, but symbolic is *not* less awful:
  spiritual death and the loss of God, the source of all joy, is worse than any physical fire.
  Crucially, God does not gleefully cast people down so much as he **finally lets people have the
  eternity apart from him that they wanted** — as C. S. Lewis put it, there are only two kinds of
  people: those who say to God "Thy will be done," and those to whom God says "Thy will be done." God
  is patient, holding off judgment so many can repent (2 Pet 3:9). Then **heaven**: not clouds
  "up there," and not the *final* destination. Heaven is where God dwells and is worshipped, and
  where believers who have died are now present with Jesus — but that is an **interim** state. The
  Christian's final home is the **new creation**, when God comes down to dwell with us and we're
  given new bodies. "Kingdom of God / kingdom of heaven" means **God's righteous rule**, not the
  place. Use manual Qs 1–5, especially Q3 (*does God send people to hell?*) and Q5 (*what's wrong
  with treating heaven as the only goal?*).
- **0:24–0:52 · Activity: God's Character Bible Study (`TR 12.3A`; manual p.253).** Skeptics — and
  some Christians — think God's love and justice contradict. Individually, students work the
  three-step worksheet through Scripture on God's love and justice, so they can **articulate what the
  Bible says about God's character and defend the biblical view of God's justice.** If time allows,
  discuss answers. Grade against the `TR 12.3A` answer key.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *In your own words: is it more accurate to say God sends people
  to hell, or that he lets them go? What does each phrase get right?*

**Homework.** Read **12.9–12.10** (ST 172–175) — the New Jerusalem and the conclusion.
**Assessments.** God's Character Bible Study worksheet (completion — love and justice).
**Notes.** This is the block's heaviest test day. Essay 33 asks students to answer *"If God is
loving, why does he send people to hell?"* — the note on the answer key says to watch the wording,
because the biblical answer reframes *sending* as *allowing*. Rehearse that phrasing here.

## Day 5 — Fri Feb 19 · The second coming; from the garden to the city (12.7–12.8)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain the significance of the second coming and what Christians
should do while awaiting Christ's return; (b) compare and contrast the new creation described in
Revelation with the first creation in Genesis. `[12.7, 12.8]`

**Materials.** `TR 12.4A Detecting Deception` — cut into its four sections; computers for the
research.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:26 · Read & discuss 12.7–12.8 (manual p.254–255).** The **second coming** is Jesus'
  *personal and visible* return from heaven to Earth — the event that triggers the final restoration.
  Frame it with the wedding picture: **Act 5 (the Church) is the engagement; Act 6 (New Creation) is
  the wedding.** The church is the bride, Christ the Groom, and the second coming is *joy* — but bad
  news for those who persist in evil. How to wait well: **not** by quitting jobs, **not** by
  date-setting, **not** by neglecting our other callings — but by living faithfully, proclaiming the
  gospel, and working for restoration now. Then **garden to city**: the new creation is a **renewal**
  of God's creation, not a discarding of it, and not merely a return to Eden — heaven comes to Earth
  as a **city** with the tree of life. Walk the **Genesis 1–3 ↔ Revelation 20–22 chart** (figure
  12.a, ST 172): night/no night, death/no death, serpent triumphs/Lamb triumphs, tree of life cut
  off/reopened, driven out/"they will see his face." Use manual Qs 1–4.
- **0:26–0:52 · Activity: Detecting Deception (`TR 12.4A`, four groups; manual p.255).** Give each
  group one of the four passage-and-prompt sections. Groups read, research, and discuss deceptive or
  false claims about Jesus' second coming (date-setting, escapism, and the like), then share findings.
  *Purpose:* the "how to wait" material becomes a skill — spotting bad end-times thinking and
  answering it.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Which of the wrong ways to "wait" for Jesus have you actually
  heard? What does faithful waiting look like instead?*

**Homework.** Begin studying for the Chapter 12 Test (Study Guide, SM 239–241; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Detecting Deception (participation/research).
**Notes.** The Genesis↔Revelation chart is a near-certain test item (study-guide Q9; multi-select 14;
fill-in 24). If discussion runs long, the flex item is trimming the number of chart rows you walk
aloud — but leave the chart on display and point students to it in the textbook.

## Day 6 — Mon Feb 22 · All things new — the New Jerusalem; why the story is unique (12.9–12.10)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) articulate the details of the New Jerusalem; (b) explain what makes
the biblical metanarrative unique among the world's stories. `[12.9, 12.10]` *(High test weight —
the New Jerusalem is short answer 30; the metanarrative's hope is essay 35.)*

**Materials.** `TR 12.5A Face-to-Face` — strips cut apart, G statements kept separate from R
statements.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Read & discuss 12.9–12.10 (manual p.256–257).** The **New Jerusalem**: God fully
  present with his people, **no temple and no barrier** — the temple *is* "the Lord God the Almighty
  and the Lamb" (Rev 21:22); the sin that came between God and humans in Genesis 3 is gone. It is
  **open to all followers of the Lamb**, from every tribe and tongue, with no prejudice, division,
  pain, or death; Satan is banished; the city is beautiful (streets of gold, a river, the tree of
  life whose leaves are "for the healing of the nations"); and there we **worship God and flourish**,
  doing meaningful work with new, un-decaying bodies. Then the **conclusion** (12.10): what makes the
  Bible's story unique? Newbigin's point — other worldviews either invite you to build a future
  utopia *you'll never live to see*, or say there's no big story at all, only *me and my story*.
  Christianity calls us out of selfish individualism into **God's story**, where our work for his
  kingdom is not in vain and *all* believers share in the new creation. Use manual Qs 1–4, including
  Q4: *how would you summarize the great biblical story?*
- **0:24–0:48 · Activity: Face-to-Face (`TR 12.5A`, two lines; manual p.257).** Split into two
  groups; hand out the **G** (Genesis) strips to one line and the matching **R** (Revelation) strips
  to the other, in random order. Facing lines, students ask up to three yes/no questions to find the
  partner whose strip matches theirs (e.g., *"Does yours mention Satan? … walking with God?"*).
  Matched pairs step aside; the rest shift left and repeat until everyone is paired. Then pairs line
  up from the earliest Genesis verse to the latest and read their **G/R verse pairs aloud**, starting
  with Genesis 1:1. *Purpose:* the students *become* the Genesis↔Revelation chart — the story's first
  three chapters answered by its last three.
- **0:48–0:58 · Close — the end of the story.** This is the last new content of the six-act arc.
  *"We started in a garden in Chapter 1 and we've ended in a city where God dwells with us. The story
  is finished."* Confirm study plan for the test.
- **0:58–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Study for the test (Study Guide, SM 239–241; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Face-to-Face (participation — Genesis/Revelation parallels).
**Notes.** Let the "we've reached the end of the story" moment land — it's the emotional payoff of a
whole trimester. Note who can and can't summarize the six acts when you cold-call in the close; that
tells you where to aim tomorrow's review.

## Day 8 — Wed Feb 24 · God's Big Story — the whole six-act synthesis (Lesson 6)

*Short ~45-min period. The capstone of the entire biblical metanarrative and the last thing students
do before the test that completes it. A single focused synthesis activity, ideal for a short day.*

**Objective.** Students define the key terms of each act and relate them to the biblical
metanarrative — telling the whole six-act story from Creation to New Creation.

**Materials.** `PS 12.6A God's Big Story` (preview beforehand); Bibles and student textbooks per
group.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Set up (`PS 12.6A`, slides 1–2; manual p.258).** Three groups, each with a Bible and
  textbook. Display slide 1, read the instructions, then slide 2 and let students briefly review the
  **six acts** before starting. (The red blanks are the two key terms for each act.)
- **0:06–0:30 · Term rounds (slides 3–15).** For each act in turn, show the two definitions; groups
  have three minutes to find the correct terms **and which act** they belong to, writing answers on
  paper. Reveal the answers, discuss the question at the bottom of each slide, and continue through
  all six acts. Review the full set on slide 15.
- **0:30–0:42 · Verse pick (slide 16).** Split the three groups into six; assign one act to each.
  Each group chooses the **one Bible verse** from that act's textbook chapter that best captures the
  act's main theme, then presents it and explains the connection.
- **0:42–0:45 · Close & send-off.** *"You just told the whole story — Creation to New Creation.
  Tomorrow you get to show it."*

**Homework.** Final study for the test.
**Assessments.** God's Big Story (informal — can students hold and connect the six acts).
**Notes.** This is the narrative climax of the whole course. If time runs short, the flex item is the
optional draw-a-picture extension of the verse-pick — keep the term rounds and the six presentations,
since those *are* the six-act review. Save a couple of minutes at the end to answer any last test
questions.
