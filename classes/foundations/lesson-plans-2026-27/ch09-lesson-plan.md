## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 8 days** — by *merging*, not cutting.
Chapter 9 is the story's **interlude**, not one of the six acts: it fills the roughly 400 silent
years between Malachi and John the Baptist, the gap between Act 3 (Israel) and Act 4 (Jesus). It is
the first chapter back from Christmas break, so Day 1 re-grounds students in where the six-act story
stands before adding the new material. The two short Wednesdays carry the lessons with the least new
tested content — the videos early, the review right before the test:

| Day | Date | Publisher lesson(s) | Textbook | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Tue Jan 5 | Lesson 1 (9.1–9.2) + **six-act re-grounding** + Ch 8 test return | ST 123–125 | Content + discussion + **Rise of Kingdoms** (pairs) |
| 2 | **Wed Jan 6 · short** | Lesson 7 (Videos) | — | Six expert videos + questions |
| 3 | Thu Jan 7 | Lesson 2 (9.3–9.4) | ST 125–128 | Content + **Clash of Worldviews** (`TR 9.2A`) |
| 4 | Fri Jan 8 | Lesson 3 (9.5–9.6) | ST 128–130 | Content + **God-Tinted Glasses** (`TR 9.3A`) |
| 5 | Mon Jan 11 | Lesson 4 (9.7–9.8) | ST 130–134 | Content + **What to Do?** (Dilemmas) |
| 6 | Tue Jan 12 | Lessons 5 + 6 (9.9–9.10 + Debate) *merged* | ST 131–133 | Content + **Debating Factions** prep & debate |
| 7 | **Wed Jan 13 · short** | Lessons 8 + 9 (Review + Study) *merged* | SM 180–181 | **Guess Who I Am** + review + Study Guide |
| 8 | Thu Jan 14 | Lesson 10 | — | **Chapter 9 Test** |

**Two merges make the compression:** the **Debating Factions Prep** (publisher Lesson 5, run
alongside 9.9–9.10) and the **Debate itself** (Lesson 6) combine on Day 6 — they are two halves of
one activity the publisher split across two lessons, and 9.9–9.10 is a short, reflective read, so the
day holds all three. And the publisher's separate Review (L8) and Study-Guide (L9) days combine on
Day 7, the same review/study merge used in every prior chapter. The **Videos** lesson (L7) is also
*resequenced* — pulled up to the short Wednesday on Day 2, where it consolidates the opening while
the teacher only has to press play. Neither short Wednesday carries much unique tested material, so
the shortened periods cost little on the test.

**Lean-layer note:** Chapter 9 needs **no newly generated handouts** — the publisher supplies every
activity (`PS 9.3A`, `PS 9.5A`, `PS 9.8A`, `TR 9.2A`, `TR 9.3A`, `TR 9.4A`, `TR 9.6A`, `TR 9.8A`)

`TR 9.10A` (/51) is **multiple choice (11 pts — some items have more than one answer) · true/false

(20), the **Dead Sea Scrolls / Qumran / Essenes** (22), **second-temple literature and the

(the five hopes / the essay) close behind and Day 1 (empires + hellenization) not far off. Day 7
review pulls it together.

## Day 1 — Tue Jan 5 · Back from break: where the story stands, Daniel's vision & hellenization (9.1–9.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) place this chapter as the *interlude* between Act 3 (Israel) and
Act 4 (Jesus); (b) name the four empires represented in Daniel's vision; (c) give examples of the
challenges Jews faced returning from exile; (d) recount the rise of Greek culture during the
intertestamental period. `[9.1, 9.2]`

**Materials.** The graded Ch 8 tests (to return); student textbooks; one sheet of white paper per
pair; class Bibles; Bible commentaries or computers (optional); the six acts on the board.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:08 · Welcome back & re-ground the story.** First day back from Christmas break. Rebuild
  the map on the board: **Creation → Fall → Redemption Initiated: Israel** — and now the *interlude*
  before **Redemption Accomplished: Jesus.** Land where we are: Israel has gone into exile (end of
  Ch 8); this chapter fills the roughly **400 years of silence** between the last OT prophet
  (Malachi) and John the Baptist — the bridge between the testaments. *"This isn't one of the six
  acts. It's the intermission — and it's where the world Jesus stepped into got made."*
- **0:08–0:14 · Return the Chapter 8 test.** Hand back the graded Ch 8 (Israel) tests from before
  break. Walk the one or two items most students missed; keep it brief — the point is to close last
  chapter's gaps, not re-teach it.
- **0:14–0:34 · Read & discuss 9.1–9.2 (manual p.182–183).** Daniel's vision of **four beasts** =
  four empires: **Babylonian, Medo-Persian, Greek, Roman** (Dan 7:4–7). Then the return: **Cyrus**
  of Persia lets the exiles go home (538 BC); **Zerubbabel** rebuilds the temple; **Ezra** reforms;
  **Esther** rescues the Jews still in Persia; **Nehemiah** rebuilds the walls — but the glorious
  restored kingdom the prophets promised does not materialize. Then **Alexander the Great** conquers
  Persia (~333 BC) and launches **hellenization** — the spread of Greek language and culture. Name
  the good (a common Greek tongue that later speeds the gospel; the **Septuagint**, the OT in Greek)
  and the harm (pressure on Jews to abandon their faith; the community splits over whether to accept
  it). Use manual Qs 1–5: *the four beasts · why Ezra and Nehemiah were so zealous · intertestamental
  vs. second temple period · what hellenization is and how it helped and harmed · how Jews responded.*
- **0:34–0:56 · Activity: Rise of Kingdoms (pairs; manual p.182–183).** Pairs draw the four beasts of
  Daniel 7 on white paper, **label each with the empire it represents**, and list the biblical
  figures from 9.1–9.2 who lived under that kingdom. If time and devices allow, they research the
  beasts' attributes (lion with eagle's wings, bear with three ribs, four-headed leopard, ten-horned
  iron beast) and add notes. A few pairs show their sketches. *Purpose:* the four empires are a
  test item (MC 1), and drawing them makes the sequence stick.
- **0:56–0:60 · Homework & week preview.** Tell students tomorrow is a **video day** (six short
  clips) — no new reading due for it.

**Homework.** Read **9.3–9.4** (ST 125–128) — the Maccabees and Roman rule — for Thursday. *(No
reading due tomorrow.)*
**Assessments.** Rise of Kingdoms sketches (participation).
**Notes.** Leave the six acts on the board through the block, with the interlude marked between Acts
3 and 4 — students should feel the whole chapter sitting in that gap. If the Ch 8 return runs long,
trim the optional beast-research extension on Rise of Kingdoms.

## Day 3 — Thu Jan 7 · Antiochus IV, the Maccabees & Roman rule (9.3–9.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain the factors that led to the Maccabean Revolt; (b) paraphrase
how the rise of the Roman Empire set the stage for the birth, life, and death of Christ. `[9.3, 9.4]`
*(High test weight — Antiochus, Mattathias, Hanukkah, Herod, Pilate, Augustus, and crucifixion are
all over the multiple-choice, T/F, and fill-in sections.)*

**Materials.** `TR 9.2A Clash of Worldviews` — 1 per student; Bibles and pens.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:26 · Read & discuss 9.3–9.4 (manual p.184–185).** The pressure to hellenize peaks under
  the Seleucid king **Antiochus IV**, who took the name *Epiphanes* ("God manifest"), outlawed
  Jewish practice, and desecrated the temple (a pig on the altar, renamed for Zeus). An old priest,
  **Mattathias**, refuses to sacrifice to idols and sparks a revolt; his son **Judah "Maccabee"**
  (the *hammer*) cleanses the temple in 164 BC — the event **Hanukkah** celebrates — and by 142 BC
  the Jews win independence. But the Maccabees' heirs, the **Hasmoneans**, turn into tyrants, and in
  63 BC Rome takes over. Rome rules the Jews through **Herod the Great**, who — like Pharaoh in
  Exodus — slaughters the boys of Bethlehem out of fear of a rival king; later Roman prefects
  (including the brutal **Pontius Pilate**) govern Judea. **Caesar Augustus** ushers in the
  **Pax Romana** and emperor worship, and Rome uses **crucifixion** to crush rebels. Use manual Qs
  1–4: *the Maccabees vs. Daniel/Ezra/Nehemiah · Judah Maccabee compared with Jesus (Matt 21) ·
  Herod vs. Pharaoh · the positives and negatives of Roman rule.*
- **0:26–0:52 · Activity: Clash of Worldviews (`TR 9.2A`; manual p.184–185).** Distribute one copy
  per student. Students read the background on the collision of **hellenism and Judaism** and
  complete the exercises, then map it onto a present-day worldview conflict of their own. Discuss a
  few answers as a class. *Purpose:* students see that "conform or resist the dominant culture" is
  not just an ancient Jewish problem — it frames the factions coming on Day 5.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Judah Maccabee and Jesus both entered Jerusalem to shouts of
  "Hosanna" and both cleansed the temple — so why wasn't Judah the Messiah?* (He won by killing his
  enemies; Jesus won by giving his life.)

**Homework.** Read **9.5–9.6** (ST 128–130) — the four Jewish symbols, the synagogue, and the
Samaritans — for tomorrow.
**Assessments.** Clash of Worldviews worksheet (completion).
**Notes.** *Maccabee = hammer* and *Antiochus/Epiphanes = "God manifest"* are near-certain items
(MC 5, T/F 15, fill-in 28). Say them out loud and put them on the board.

## Day 4 — Fri Jan 8 · The four symbols, the synagogue & the Samaritans (9.5–9.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) describe the **four key Jewish symbols** of the second temple
period; (b) explain the role of the synagogue post-exile; (c) explain who the Samaritans were and
why Jews and Samaritans despised one another. `[9.5, 9.6]` *(Highest test weight — the four symbols
are the 3-point short answer, and the Samaritans alone account for four separate items.)*

**Materials.** `PS 9.3A Map of Palestine` (display); `TR 9.3A God-Tinted Glasses` — 1 per student
(+ the answer key for you); Bibles.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Read & discuss 9.5–9.6 (manual p.186–187).** Write the **four symbols** on the board
  and keep them there — they are the short-answer question: **Temple** (the center of religious and
  political life, where God dwells among his people); **Land** (God's promise to Abraham, meant to
  be a new Eden of *shalom*); **Torah** (the instructions that keep Israel faithful and set apart);
  **Racial identity** (descent from Abraham, marked by circumcision and by not intermarrying). Then
  the **synagogue** — a meeting place for Torah study, prayer, and discussion that arose for the
  **diaspora** Jews with no access to the temple, a *supplement* to the temple, not a replacement.
  Then the **Samaritans** — likely northern Israelites who intermarried with Assyrians; when the
  returning Jews refused their help rebuilding the temple, they built a rival one on **Mount
  Gerizim**, and the two peoples came to hate each other. Use manual Qs 1–5: *why temple and land
  mattered · why Torah and racial identity mattered · the potential dangers of each symbol · the
  purpose of the synagogue · who the Samaritans were and the source of the animosity.*
- **0:24–0:32 · Map orientation (`PS 9.3A`).** Display the Map of Palestine; show **Galilee, Judea,
  and Samaria.** Explain that Jews traveling from Galilee to Jerusalem for festivals would detour
  *around* Samaria rather than pass through it (Luke 9:51–56) — geography made the prejudice a daily
  choice.
- **0:32–0:56 · Activity: God-Tinted Glasses (`TR 9.3A`; manual p.186–187).** First have students
  privately reflect: *Is there a person or group you tend to avoid or treat differently because of a
  hurt, a conflict, or a prejudice?* Then distribute the worksheet — students research biblical
  models for resolving conflict and overcoming prejudice (Jesus with the Samaritan woman, John 4;
  the good Samaritan, Luke 10) and complete the activity. Share a few answers. *Purpose:* the
  Samaritan material is heavily tested *and* this is where it turns personal.
- **0:56–0:60 · Homework.**

**Homework.** Read **9.7–9.8** (ST 130–134) — the four factions and second-temple literature — for
Monday.
**Assessments.** God-Tinted Glasses worksheet (completion; grade against the answer key).
**Notes.** This is a heavy content day. If the discussion runs long, the map orientation can fold
into the start of the activity rather than stand alone — but do not shortchange the four symbols,
which is the surest 3 points on the test.

## Day 5 — Mon Jan 11 · The four factions; literature & theology (9.7–9.8)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) list and describe the **four Jewish factions** — Pharisees,
Sadducees, Essenes, Zealots — and how each responded to hellenization; (b) give examples of
literature from the second temple period. `[9.7, 9.8]` *(High test weight — the factions are a T/F
item and the second short-answer question; the literature and the Dead Sea Scrolls run through
multiple choice and fill-in.)*

**Materials.** `TR 9.4A Dilemmas` — cut into the seven strips; Bibles; paper.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:24 · Read & discuss 9.7–9.8 (manual p.188–189).** Put the **four factions** on the board
  with a one-line response to culture each: **Pharisees** — the largest, popular group; resist
  hellenization by strict obedience to the Torah *and* the oral traditions (Jesus' frequent
  adversaries). **Sadducees** — upper-class priests who cooperate with the ruling powers, held the
  majority on the Sanhedrin, kept only the Torah, and denied the resurrection. **Essenes** — an
  extreme group who judged the temple corrupt and withdrew to desert communities (likely the ones
  who produced the **Dead Sea Scrolls** at **Qumran**). **Zealots** — violent revolutionaries,
  "no king but God," who fought Rome. Then the **literature**: the books that became the
  **Apocrypha** — *1–2 Maccabees, Judith, Tobit, Bel and the Dragon, Susanna, Wisdom of Solomon* —
  which encouraged faithfulness (not new revelation on par with Scripture), plus **Philo** and
  **Josephus**, and the theology that developed then (angels/demons, hardened **monotheism**, and a
  growing hope in **resurrection**). Use manual Qs 1–5: *Pharisees vs. Sadducees · Essenes vs.
  Zealots · the role of the Apocrypha · beliefs that arose in the period · how stories still
  encourage faithfulness today.*
- **0:24–0:52 · Activity: What to Do? (seven groups; manual p.188–189).** Divide the class into
  seven groups; give each a dilemma from `TR 9.4A Dilemmas` — a hard choice a Jew in the second
  temple period might face (compromise vs. faithfulness). Groups discuss and solve it, drawing on OT
  principles or second-temple literature, then present their dilemma and solution to the class.
  *Purpose:* students feel the pressure the factions were responding to — obedience was "easier said
  than done."
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.** *Which faction's strategy — strict obedience, cooperation,
  withdrawal, or violence — is most tempting today, and where does each go wrong?*

**Homework.** Read **9.9–9.10** (ST 131–133) — the hopes of Israel and the conclusion — for tomorrow.
**Assessments.** What to Do? presentations (participation/synthesis).
**Notes.** Naming the **four factions** and their responses is the second short-answer question — if
you run short, the *What to Do?* presentations can go to four or five groups instead of seven, but
keep all four factions on the board. The Essenes → Dead Sea Scrolls → Qumran chain is a fill-in
target (22).

## Day 6 — Tue Jan 12 · The five hopes of Israel + Debating Factions (9.9–9.10 + Lesson 6, merged)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) restate the **five hopes** of the Jewish people during the second
temple period and describe the kind of messiah most Jews expected; (b) draw the parallel between
that hope and the way people today look for a "messiah" to fix the world; (c) argue a real
first-century issue from the perspective of an assigned faction. `[9.9, 9.10]` *(Merges publisher
Lessons 5 and 6 — the Debate Prep and the Debate are two halves of one activity. High test weight:
the five hopes and the expected messiah are the 5-point essay.)*

**Materials.** Display `PS 9.5A Debate Topics`; Bible reference books or computers; paper; `TR 9.6A
Debating Factions Ballot` — 1 per student; a timer.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:16 · Read & discuss 9.9–9.10 (manual p.190–191).** The **five hopes**, on the board —
  they are the essay: **(1) salvation from their enemies** (rescue from Rome), **(2) a state of
  shalom** (peace and freedom to worship), **(3) the arrival of God's kingdom and rule** over Israel
  and the world, **(4) the forgiveness of sins**, **(5) a messiah to lead Israel.** Then the twist:
  most who wanted a messiah expected a **military** one to crush Rome — not a man who would preach
  forgiveness and be crucified. Land the conclusion's parallel: a chaotic, discouraged world still
  looks for a "messiah" — a political leader, a movement — to set everything right, but only a
  sinless one can fix a sin problem. Use manual Qs 1–4: *the five hopes · what messiah the Jews
  expected vs. who Jesus was · similarities between then and now · the messiah-figures people look to
  today.*
- **0:16–0:38 · Debating Factions Prep (`PS 9.5A`; manual p.190–191).** Groups of four; display the
  debate topics and assign each group one. Within each group, assign each student a different
  **faction** — Essene, Sadducee, Pharisee, Zealot. Students use Bibles, reference books, or
  computers to build their faction's case: *how would this group answer the assigned issue?* Circulate
  and help them list their points.
- **0:38–0:56 · Debating Factions (`TR 9.6A`; manual p.192).** Hand each student a ballot. Take the
  topics one at a time: each of the four faction-representatives gets ~2 minutes to present, then ~30
  seconds to rebut; the rest of the class scores the ballot for that round. Tally at the end to see
  which faction argued best on each topic. *Purpose:* students inhabit the factions rather than just
  memorize them — the exact recall the short-answer question rewards.
- **0:56–0:60 · Close & homework.** *If the Jews had gotten the military messiah they wanted, what
  would they have missed?*

**Homework.** Begin studying for the Chapter 9 Test (Study Guide, SM 180–181; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Debate ballots + faction presentations (participation).
**Notes.** This is the block's fullest day — one section and a two-part activity. The **five hopes**
are the essay, so protect that opening block. If a class runs slow, shorten the prep to 15 minutes
and run the debate on fewer topics rather than cut it — the debate is where the factions lock in.

## Day 7 — Wed Jan 13 · Guess Who I Am + review + Study Guide (Lessons 8 + 9, merged)

**Objectives.** Students identify the chapter's key figures, consolidate the material, and
self-assess against the Study Guide; lock in the **Key Terms** (hellenization, Hanukkah, synagogue,
Samaritans, Pharisees, Sadducees) and **Key Verses** (Daniel 7:13–14, Acts 17:2–3, Galatians 4:4–5).
`[Review]` *(High test weight — this is the last pass before the test.)*

**Materials.** `TR 9.8A Guess Who I Am` — cut into the character strips, in a box; a timer; student
textbooks; display `PS 9.8A Chapter 9 Review`; student manuals (Study Guide, SM 180–181).

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:18 · Activity: Guess Who I Am (`TR 9.8A`; manual p.196–197).** Two teams; each student
  draws a character from the box (Zerubbabel, Ezra, Esther, Malachi, Herod the Great, Nehemiah,
  Haman, Xerxes, Antiochus IV, Mattathias, Judah Maccabee, Pontius Pilate, Caesar Augustus, and the
  faction groups) and gets three minutes to look the figure up in the textbook. In turn, a student
  pantomimes the character for 20 seconds (3 pts if the team guesses); if not, 30 seconds speaking
  "in character" without naming themselves (1 pt). Alternate teams until all are acted; high score
  wins. *Purpose:* a fast, whole-chapter recall of who's who.
- **0:18–0:32 · Chapter Review (`PS 9.8A`).** Walk the review slides; let students read them aloud.
  Hit the heavy-weight items hard: the **four symbols**, the **four factions**, the **five hopes**,
  **hellenization/Septuagint**, and the **Samaritans**.
- **0:32–0:42 · Study Guide + Key Terms/Verses (manual p.197–199).** Students work the Study Guide
  (SM 180–181); confirm the six Key Terms and three Key Verses. The manual's study-guide questions
  with answers are the answer key.
- **0:42–0:45 · Test preview.** Tomorrow: 51 points — multiple choice, true/false, fill-in, short
  answer, and one essay. *"If you can name the four symbols, the four factions, and the five hopes —
  and explain why Jesus wasn't the messiah the Jews were expecting — you're most of the way there."*

**Homework.** Study for the test.
**Assessments.** Guess Who I Am (informal) + Study Guide completion.
**Notes.** Short day — keep Guess Who I Am moving so the review and Study Guide both get their time.
If Day 4 (symbols/Samaritans) or Day 5 (factions) felt rushed, spend the review minutes there;
they are the test's center of gravity.
