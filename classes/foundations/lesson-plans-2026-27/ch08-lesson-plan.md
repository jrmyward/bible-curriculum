## How this block works

This chapter compresses the publisher's **10 lessons into 8 days** — by *merging*, not cutting.
The calendar shapes the block: there is **no sub day**, but **two short ~45-min Wednesdays** fall on
Day 1 (Dec 2) and Day 6 (Dec 9). So the two lightest, most self-contained publisher lessons go on
those short days — the **Videos** lesson opens the block on Day 1 (a sub only has to press play, and
here the teacher can pair it with returning the Chapter 7 test), and the **What's My Name?** recall
game lands on Day 6, once students have finally met every character it asks about.

| Day | Date | Publisher lesson(s) | Textbook | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Wed Dec 2 · short** | Lesson 7 (Videos) + **Ch 7 test return** | — | Videos + questions + test return |
| 2 | Thu Dec 3 | Lesson 1 (8.1–8.2) | ST 108–110 | Content + **Family Lines** (pairs) |
| 3 | Fri Dec 4 | Lesson 2 (8.3–8.4) | ST 110–113 | Content + **Law Codes** (`TR 8.2A`) |
| 4 | Mon Dec 7 | Lesson 3 (8.5–8.6) | ST 113–115 | Content + **Actions Have Consequences** |
| 5 | Tue Dec 8 | Lessons 4 + 5 (8.7–8.10) *merged* | ST 115–120 | Content + **Faith Under Pressure** |
| 6 | **Wed Dec 9 · short** | Lesson 6 | — | **What's My Name?** (full-period game) |
| 7 | Thu Dec 10 | Lessons 8 + 9 (Review + Study) *merged* | SM 166–167 | Festivals activity + review + Study Guide |
| 8 | Fri Dec 11 | Lesson 10 | — | **Chapter 8 Test** |

**Two merges make the compression:** the four closing subsections **8.7–8.10** combine on Day 5
(publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the southern kingdom, the exile, the prophets, and the conclusion), and
the publisher's separate Review (L8) and Study-Guide (L9) days combine on Day 7 — the same
review/study merge used all block. Two lessons are also *resequenced* onto the short Wednesdays
rather than dropped: the **Videos** lesson (L7) opens the block on Day 1, and the **What's My Name?**
character game (L6) moves to Day 6, where students can finally play it. Neither carries new tested
content, so the two shortened Wednesdays cost nothing on the test.

**Lean-layer note:** Chapter 8 needs **no newly generated handouts** — the publisher supplies every
activity (`TR 8.2A–B`, `TR 8.3A`, `PS 8.8A`, `PS 8.8B`) plus the discussion questions with answers,
the video questions with answers, and the Study Guide. This plan sequences and times them; it does
not reproduce them. Discussion questions are cited by their teacher-manual location; open
[teaching-manual/ch08.md](../_source-text/teaching-manual/ch08.md) for the full list with model
answers.

### What the test asks (so the review aims true)

`TR 8.10A` (/51) is **multiple choice (11 pts) · true/false (8 pts — correct the underlined word if
false) · fill-in-the-blank (8 items × 2 = 18 pts, with question 22 worth 4) · short answer (3 × 3 =
9 pts) · one essay (5 pts).** Its weight lands on **God's covenant with Abraham** and the promised
descendants and Offspring (Q1, Q20, Q21, Q27); the **Passover** and how it differs from the **Feast
of Tabernacles** (Q8, Q12, Q22 — blood on the doorposts); the **kingdom-of-priests / holy-nation
mission** (Q5, Q24, and the Q31 essay); **ceremonial vs. moral law** and the **two kinds of
sacrifice** (Q6, Q7, Q14); the **tabernacle vs. the temple** — the tabernacle in the desert, the
temple that Solomon built (Q11, Q13); the **first three kings** Saul/David/Solomon (Q15, Q18, Q28)
and the **judges cycle** (Q29); the **conquest of Canaan** (Q30); the split into **northern (Israel)
and southern (Judah)** kingdoms and why both went into **exile** (Q2, Q9, Q17); the **prophets** —
what a prophet is and what they condemned (Q3, Q10); the **three righteous kings** Jehoshaphat,
Hezekiah, Josiah (Q23); and the Key-Verse anchors **Naaman / 2 Kings 5:15** (Q25) and **Jeremiah
29:7** (Q26). Day 3 (laws, sacrifices, festivals, the judges) and Day 5 (kingdoms, exile, prophets,
mission) carry the most weight, with Day 2 (Abraham, Passover) close behind. Day 7 review pulls it
together.

## Day 2 — Thu Dec 3 · The family of Abraham & the exodus (8.1–8.2)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain God's covenant with Abraham and name the two descendants who
received the covenant blessing (Isaac, then Jacob); (b) relate the Passover to the exodus from Egypt.
`[8.1, 8.2]`

**Materials.** Class Bibles; white paper and a pen per pair (`Family Lines`); textbooks.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:06 · Warm-up / connect the videos.** Yesterday's video asked *"were Old Testament people
  saved?"* — this chapter starts the answer with the one family God chose to bless the world.
- **0:06–0:30 · Read & discuss 8.1–8.2 (manual p.162–163).** God called Abram/Abraham (Gen 12:1–3)
  and promised descendants as numerous as the stars, the land of Canaan, and — the ultimate promise —
  the **Offspring who would crush the serpent's head.** The covenant passed to **Isaac**, then
  **Jacob** (renamed *Israel*), then to Jacob's 12 sons and to Joseph, who told his brothers *"you
  meant evil against me, but God meant it for good"* (Gen 50:20 — Key Verse). Then the exodus: Israel
  enslaved in Egypt; Moses; God as the great **I AM** (Exodus 3:14); the plagues; the **Passover**
  lamb and its blood on the doorposts, so the angel of death would *pass over* Israel's homes; the Red
  Sea; and the Sinai covenant calling Israel to be a **kingdom of priests.** Use manual Qs 1–6.
- **0:30–0:52 · Activity: Family Lines (pairs; manual p.163).** Pairs diagram Abraham's family tree,
  marking an **X** by those *not* carried in the covenant line (Ishmael, Esau), placing **Jesus**
  connected to the tribe of Judah, and labeling *Promise given* by Abraham and *Promise fulfilled* by
  Jesus. Bibles are for looking up the names of Jacob's sons. A few pairs share.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.**

**Homework.** Read **8.3–8.4** (ST 110–113) — the tabernacle, the law, and the judges.
**Assessments.** Family Lines diagram (participation).
**Notes.** The covenant with Abraham (Q20 descendants, Q21 the sacrifice of Isaac, Q27 Joseph) and
the **Passover** (Q8, Q12, Q22) are heavy test items. Make the Passover land clearly now — the test
pits it against the **Feast of Tabernacles**, and students who blur the two lose easy points.

## Day 3 — Fri Dec 4 · The tabernacle, the law & the judges (8.3–8.4)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) explain the purpose of the tabernacle; (b) differentiate the two
categories of Old Testament law, the two types of sacrifice, and the three major festivals; (c)
describe the pattern Israel fell into during the period of the judges. `[8.3, 8.4]`
*(The test's densest day — laws, sacrifices, festivals, and the judges cycle all key off this.)*

**Materials.** `TR 8.2A Law Codes` (1 per student) + `TR 8.2B Law Codes Answer Key` (for you); a
pencil per student.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:26 · Read & discuss 8.3–8.4 (manual p.164–165).** The **tabernacle** — a sacred meeting
  place, a miniature Eden where God's presence dwelt among the Israelites; the **priests** represented
  the people before God. Two categories of **law**: **ceremonial** (dietary rules, washing,
  sacrifices — keeping Israel ceremonially pure to live in God's presence) and **moral** (the Ten
  Commandments, justice, sexual integrity — reflecting God's character to the nations). Two kinds of
  **sacrifice**: **thanksgiving** (grain and peace offerings, celebrating God's goodness) and
  **sacrifices for sin** (burnt, sin, and guilt offerings, confessing sin and receiving forgiveness).
  Three major **festivals** here: **Passover**, the **Feast of Tabernacles** (remembering God's
  provision in the wilderness), and the **Day of Atonement** (*atonement* = restitution for
  wrongdoing). Then the conquest of Canaan — *why* God commanded it (the Canaanites' extreme
  wickedness, and to keep Israel from adopting it) — and the **judges cycle**: idolatry → oppression
  → crying out → deliverance → back to idolatry; plus the redemptive story of **Ruth**, ancestor of
  David. Use manual Qs 1–6.
- **0:26–0:52 · Activity: Law Codes (`TR 8.2A`; manual p.165).** Students decode the terms for the
  laws, sacrifices, and festivals and draw lines matching each decoded term to its description; check
  against `TR 8.2B`. Then they answer the closing question and share responses.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.**

**Homework.** Read **8.5–8.6** (ST 113–115) — the first kings and the northern kingdom.
**Assessments.** Law Codes worksheet (completion — checked against `TR 8.2B`).
**Notes.** Ceremonial vs. moral law (Q6, Q14), the sacrifice types (Q7), the festivals (Q8, Q12), the
tabernacle-vs-temple line (Q13), *atonement* (Q16), and the judges cycle (Q29 short answer) all come
from here. If the discussion runs long, Law Codes is self-checking and can shrink to 15 minutes.

## Day 4 — Mon Dec 7 · The first kings & the northern kingdom (8.5–8.6)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) summarize the first three kings of Israel and the choices that set
their stories apart; (b) explain the purpose of the temple; (c) describe the consequences of sin in
the northern kingdom and how that sin interfered with Israel's mission. `[8.5, 8.6]`

**Materials.** `TR 8.3A Actions Have Consequences` (cut apart into cards).

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:28 · Read & discuss 8.5–8.6 (manual p.166–167).** Israel demands a king. **Saul** — humble,
  then proud; he usurped Samuel's duties and stopped short of full obedience, so God rejected him and
  his line. **David** — *"a man after [God's] heart"* (Acts 13:22 — Key Verse); the Davidic covenant
  promised an eternal dynasty pointing to the Messiah, and though David sinned gravely he repented.
  **Solomon** — the wisest man alive; he replaced the tabernacle with a permanent **temple**, then
  drifted into idolatry through pagan wives, and the kingdom split after his death. The split:
  Rehoboam's folly sends ten tribes to **Jeroboam** — the **northern kingdom (Israel)** — whose kings
  were wicked *without exception* (Ahab and Jezebel and the worship of Baal). Elijah's showdown with
  the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel; Elisha and **Naaman** (*"there is no God in all the earth but
  in Israel,"* 2 Kings 5:15 — Key Verse); and exile to **Assyria in 722 BC.** Use manual Qs 1–7.
- **0:28–0:52 · Activity: Actions Have Consequences (`TR 8.3A`; manual p.167).** Each student gets one
  action card and one consequence card. A student reads an action aloud; whoever holds the matching
  consequence says *"There will be consequences!"* and links up by the pinky. Continue until the whole
  class is connected. Reshuffle and replay if time permits.
- **0:52–0:60 · Debrief & homework.**

**Homework.** Read **8.7–8.10** (ST 115–120) — the southern kingdom, the exile, the prophets, and the
conclusion. *(Tomorrow merges four sections, so it's a fuller reading day.)*
**Assessments.** Actions Have Consequences (informal).
**Notes.** David as *"a man after God's heart"* (Q18) and Solomon building the temple (Q11) are direct
test items, and the first-three-kings short answer (Q28) comes straight from here. Keep the
Saul → David → Solomon contrast crisp: all three started well, all three sinned, and repentance is
what separated their endings.

## Day 5 — Tue Dec 8 · The southern kingdom, the exile, the prophets & the conclusion (8.7–8.10, merged)

**Objectives.** Students can (a) distinguish Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, and Josiah from the other kings by
how they served the Lord; (b) give examples of how Daniel, Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego modeled
faithfulness in exile; (c) define *prophet* and describe the kinds of messages prophets delivered;
(d) summarize the Old Testament storyline so far and what it reveals about God. `[8.7, 8.8, 8.9, 8.10]`
*(Merges publisher Lessons 4 and 5 — the block's fullest day. High test weight: the mission, the
prophets, and the righteous kings run through every section.)*

**Materials.** Class Bibles; paper and a pen per student (`Faith Under Pressure`); computers or
concordances if you run the optional `God Keeps His Promises` research.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:34 · Read & discuss 8.7–8.10 (manual p.168–171).** Move through the four sections, favoring
  the mission and the prophets. **Southern kingdom (Judah):** mostly wicked kings, but three righteous
  ones — **Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, Josiah** — purged idolatry and called on God in crisis (Hezekiah
  against Sennacherib; Josiah and the rediscovered Book of the Law); Judah was exiled to **Babylon
  ~586 BC.** **The exile:** Daniel and his friends refuse to defile themselves and survive the furnace
  and the lions' den — a faithfulness that is *tactful, nonviolent, and patient* (Jeremiah 29:7 — Key
  Verse: *seek the welfare of the city*); Daniel's visions point to *"one like a son of man"* given an
  everlasting kingdom. **The prophets:** a **prophet** is a person authorized to speak on God's behalf
  — mostly recalling the covenant and calling Israel to repent, sometimes predicting the future; they
  condemned idolatry and injustice (oppression of the poor, sexual immorality, the slaughter of the
  innocent, violence) and promised the coming king, the day of the Lord, restoration, and a new
  covenant. **Conclusion (8.10):** recap the whole arc — God chose Israel to be a light to the
  nations; over and over they failed, yet God stayed faithful *even when we are not* (2 Timothy 2:13).
  Use manual Qs 1–6.
- **0:34–0:56 · Activity: Faith Under Pressure (manual p.169).** Each student picks one exile-era
  figure (Daniel; Shadrach, Meshach, or Abednego; Esther; Nehemiah) and writes a reflection paragraph:
  *What challenge did they face? How did they respond? How did God show up? What pressure to conform
  do I face, and how can I relate to God the way they did?* Share a few aloud.
- **0:56–0:60 · Close & homework.**

**Homework.** Begin studying for the Chapter 8 Test (Study Guide, SM 166–167; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Faith Under Pressure paragraph (completion).
**Notes.** This is the fullest day of the block — four sections. The righteous kings (Q23), what a
prophet is and what the prophets condemned (Q3, Q10), the mission of Israel (Q24 and the Q31 essay),
Naaman (Q25), and Jeremiah 29:7 (Q26) all come from here, so protect the discussion. The
`God Keeps His Promises` prophecy-fulfillment research (manual p.170–171) is the **flex item** —
assign it as optional homework rather than cut the Faith Under Pressure reflection, which feeds the
essay.

## Day 6 — Wed Dec 9 · What's My Name? (Lesson 6)

*Short ~45-min period. A whole-chapter character game — now that students have met every figure from
Abraham to Daniel, they can finally play it. It carries no new tested content, so the shortened
Wednesday costs nothing on the test, and it doubles as recall practice for the names the test leans
on.*

**Objective.** Students identify important Old Testament characters and their role in Israel's history.

**Materials.** Four blindfolds.

**Schedule (45 min · Wednesday — short day).**

- **0:00–0:05 · Set up (manual p.172–173).** Four students sit at the front as a blindfolded panel;
  choose one student from the class and secretly assign them a biblical character to answer *as.*
- **0:05–0:40 · Play.** Panel members take turns asking **yes/no** questions (*"Are you a king?" "Did
  you marry Jezebel?"*), starting general and getting specific. A panelist who guesses the name
  incorrectly is out; the round ends on a correct guess or after 12 questions. Rotate the mystery
  character and swap the panel so everyone participates. Draw from the chapter's cast — **Abraham,
  Isaac, Ishmael, Jacob, Joseph, Moses, Pharaoh, Ruth, Samuel, Saul, David, Solomon, Ahab, Jezebel,
  Elijah, Elisha, Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, Josiah, Daniel.**
- **0:40–0:45 · Close & homework.** As you play, keep a running list on the board of each character
  and their one-line role — free review for tomorrow.

**Homework.** Study for the test (Study Guide, SM 166–167; Key Terms & Verses).
**Assessments.** Game participation (informal).
**Notes.** The blindfolds are the point — they keep the panel from reading the room's reactions. If a
character stumps the class, that's a name to hit hard in tomorrow's review.

## Day 7 — Thu Dec 10 · Finding Jesus in the Festivals + review + Study Guide (Lessons 8 + 9, merged)

**Objectives.** Students trace how Israel's festivals point to Christ, consolidate the chapter, and
self-assess against the Study Guide; lock in the **Key Terms** (Passover, tabernacle, Day of
Atonement, temple, prophet) and **Key Verses** (Genesis 50:20; Acts 13:22; 2 Kings 5:15; Jeremiah
29:7). *(High test weight.)*

**Materials.** `PS 8.8A Finding Jesus in the Festivals` (display) + Bibles; `PS 8.8B Chapter 8 Review`
(display); student manuals (Study Guide, SM 166–167); textbooks.

**Schedule (60 min).**

- **0:00–0:22 · Activity: Finding Jesus in the Festivals (`PS 8.8A`; manual p.174–175).** Walk the
  slides festival by festival (the Feast of Tabernacles first, then the others). Students take turns
  reading the facts and Scripture passages aloud, then discuss the connections they see between the
  Old Testament festivals and New Testament events. Bibles open to examine the passages. *Purpose:*
  the festivals stop being a memory list and become signposts to Christ.
- **0:22–0:40 · Chapter Review (`PS 8.8B`).** Walk the review slides as a class. Hit the heavy-weight
  items hard: the **covenant with Abraham**, **Passover vs. Feast of Tabernacles**, **ceremonial vs.
  moral law** and the two sacrifices, **tabernacle vs. temple**, the **first three kings**, the
  **judges cycle**, the **northern/southern split** and the two exiles, and the **prophets.**
- **0:40–0:55 · Study Guide + Key Terms/Verses (manual p.176–177).** Students work the Study Guide;
  confirm the five Key Terms and four Key Verses. The manual's study-guide questions with answers are
  the answer key.
- **0:55–0:60 · Test preview.** Tomorrow: 51 points — multiple choice, true/false, fill-in, short
  answer, and one essay. *"If you can tell Israel's story from Abraham to the exile, explain the
  kingdom-of-priests mission, and sort a ceremonial law from a moral one, you're most of the way
  there."*

**Homework.** Study for the test.
**Assessments.** Festivals discussion + Study Guide completion.
**Notes.** If Day 3 (laws, sacrifices, festivals) or Day 5 (kingdoms, exile, prophets) felt rushed,
this is the place to reteach — they are the test's center of gravity.
