## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 1 introduces the course and the idea of a worldview. Students start with a plain
question: how do you find your way when you're lost? From there they learn that everyone
carries a worldview, a set of beliefs that makes sense of the world and shapes how a person
acts. The chapter then covers what a Christian actually is (and clears up three common
misunderstandings), why Christians treat the Bible as their authority and how to read it
carefully, and the overall shape of the Bible's story, which the book tells in six acts and an
interlude from Creation to New Creation. Because it comes first, this chapter touches most of
the year's major topics and sets up the Christian worldview as the framework the rest of the
course builds on. It also asks students to place themselves in that story instead of studying
it from a distance.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What is a worldview, and how does mine shape the way I live?
- What does it mean to be a Christian, and what does it not mean?
- Why should Christians trust the Bible and live under its authority?
- What story is the Bible telling, and where do I fit in it?
- How do our beliefs shape our actions, and our actions our beliefs?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Define a worldview and give examples of worldviews people hold today.
- Explain what it means to be a Christian and name three common misconceptions: that a
  Christian is someone born into a Christian family, someone who keeps a set of rules, or
  someone concerned only with heaven.
- Explain why Christians treat the Bible as their authority, even though it is an ancient book.
- Name and explain two common mistakes people make when reading the Bible: pulling verses out
  of context, and treating the Bible itself, rather than God, as the goal.
- Summarize the six acts and the interlude of the Bible's story, from Creation to New Creation.
- Explain why knowing God and knowing ourselves both matter, and why becoming like Christ takes
  a lifetime.
- Explain why love is a Christian's primary calling.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit sets up the Christian worldview as the framework for the whole course. Instead of
treating faith as one topic among many, Foundations approaches questions about truth, identity,
purpose, and history as things Christians work out through what God has revealed. The teaching
is meant to form students, not only inform them: they put their own beliefs into words, raise
honest doubts, and weigh other viewpoints, including skeptics', fairly. The goal is for students
to understand the Christian worldview and to see where they stand in it. The chapter's
discussion questions are built for that; as the teacher manual puts it, "the goal is not for
students to get the correct answers, but to get them thinking through what they actually
believe."

---

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The Bible is the main text of this unit, not just a source of examples.

- Luke 18:22 (key verse). Jesus says, "Come, follow me." This defines a Christian as someone who
  follows Christ, rather than someone who was born into a Christian family, keeps a set of rules,
  or is only concerned with heaven.
- Proverbs 9:10 (key verse). "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom." Knowing God is
  where understanding starts.
- The six-act story. Students trace the Bible's storyline across the whole canon: Creation
  (Genesis 1–2), the Fall (Genesis 3), Redemption Initiated in Israel, the Second Temple
  interlude, Redemption Accomplished in Jesus (the Gospels), Restoration Initiated in the Church
  (Acts and the Epistles), and Restoration Accomplished in the New Creation (Revelation 21–22).
- The Bible as God's revelation. God reveals himself in Scripture so people can know and follow
  him, and its authority comes from God (see 2 Timothy 3:16). Students practice reading it in
  context instead of pulling single verses out of context.
