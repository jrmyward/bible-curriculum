## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 18 is the conclusion of Foundations, and it is built as a review of the whole year rather
than a chapter of new content. Students re-walk the ground they have covered: how to read the
Bible well and the six-act story it tells, who God is and how a person grows to be more like
Christ, the ten big questions the Christian faith answers, and how believers share and defend that
faith through evangelism and apologetics. The back half turns the year into a personal project.
Each student writes and presents a Statement of Faith, a 350 to 500 word summary of what the Bible
is, who Jesus and God are, who we are, how they will grow in Christ, and how this course has
changed them. The unit ends in a cumulative final that sweeps all eighteen chapters, from the
first-day question of what a worldview is to the six-act metanarrative. Coming last, it asks
students to put the whole Christian worldview into their own words and claim it as their own.

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What is the whole story the Bible tells, and where do I stand in it?
- Who is God, and how does a person grow to be more like Christ?
- What are the biggest questions people ask about life, and how does the Christian faith answer
  them?
- What is the difference between evangelism and apologetics, and why does each one matter?
- If I had to say what I believe in my own words, what would I say and why?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- List the seven steps to reading the Bible well and summarize the six-act metanarrative from
  Creation to New Creation.
- Name at least 15 attributes of God and explain how a person grows in relationship with him and
  becomes more like Christ.
- Articulate how the Christian faith answers ten of life's biggest questions, from where we came
  from to what happens after we die.
- Distinguish evangelism from apologetics and explain how each is part of living out the faith.
- Write a 350 to 500 word personal Statement of Faith covering what the Bible is, sin and
  redemption and who Jesus is, who God is, who we are, how they will grow in Christ, and how the
  course has affected them.
- Present that statement clearly to the class within a set time limit.
- Match the five Key Verses to their references and place people and events into the six acts and
  the interlude.
- Define the course's Key Terms and demonstrate cumulative mastery of the whole Foundations course
  on the final.

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit is where the year comes together. Instead of adding a new topic, it asks students to
hold the whole Christian worldview in view at once and then say it back in their own voice. The
Statement of Faith project is the heart of that work: for many freshmen it is the first time they
put what they believe into their own words and say it out loud in front of others. The class
treats it as a capstone, not a test to survive, so the coaching is about honesty and clarity
rather than getting the right answer. The videos and the closing reflection ask students which
ideas from the whole year mattered most to them and why. The aim is not only that students can
recite the worldview but that they can own it, and the final week is built to send them off having
done exactly that.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The Cumulative Study Guide lists five Key Verses, and each names a piece of the story the course
has told.

- Matthew 3:16–17 (key verse). At Jesus's baptism the Spirit descends and the Father speaks, one
  of the clearest pictures of the Trinity, one God in three Persons.
- Matthew 28:18–20 (key verse). The Great Commission, Jesus's command to make disciples of all
  nations, baptizing and teaching them, which grounds the unit's work on evangelism.
- John 13:34–35 (key verse). "Love one another." Jesus says love is how the world will know his
  disciples, the mark of a Christian the course keeps returning to.
- 2 Timothy 3:16–17 (key verse). All Scripture is God-breathed and useful, which anchors the
  unit's review of how to read the Bible well.
- 1 Peter 2:9 (key verse). Believers are "a royal priesthood, a holy nation," God's own people,
  called to declare his praises.
- The six-act story. Students re-walk the Bible's metanarrative from Creation (Genesis 1–2)
  through the Fall, Redemption Initiated in Israel, the Second Temple interlude, Redemption
  Accomplished in Jesus, Restoration Initiated in the Church, and Restoration Accomplished in the
  New Creation (Revelation 21–22), the framework the whole course has built.
