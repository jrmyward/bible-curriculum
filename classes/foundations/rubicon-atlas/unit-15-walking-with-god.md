## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 15 turns from who God is making us to be to the everyday practices that help form us, the
spiritual disciplines. These are habits that put us where God can work: they do not measure how good
a Christian we are, and they cannot earn salvation or God's favor. The chapter walks through them in
families. It begins with the quiet disciplines of solitude, silence, and self-examination, and draws
a clear line between Christian silence, which fills the mind with God, and Eastern practices that try
to empty or transcend the self. From there it covers prayer, with the Lord's Prayer as the model,
and worship as a response to who God is and what he has done. It teaches careful reading, study,
memorization, and meditation on Scripture, along with the value of reading widely, even authors we
disagree with. It closes with fasting, simplicity, stewardship, giving, and the examples of faithful
believers across history. Throughout, the disciplines are an invitation into deeper relationship
with God, not a checklist to complete.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What is a spiritual discipline for, if it cannot earn God's favor or salvation?
- How is Christian silence and meditation different from Eastern practices that empty or transcend
  the self?
- What does the Lord's Prayer teach us about how to pray?
- Why should Christians read widely, including authors they disagree with?
- What is the purpose of fasting, and how is it different from dieting or earning God's favor?
- How can practices like simplicity, stewardship, and giving shape us into people who love God and
  others?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain the purpose of spiritual disciplines and why they do not earn salvation or God's favor.
- Differentiate the Christian disciplines of solitude and silence from Eastern-religion
  transcendence practices.
- Explain why regular self-examination and confession matter in the Christian life.
- Give examples of various approaches to prayer and explain the Lord's Prayer as a model.
- Describe various forms of Christian worship and explain why our whole lives can be worship.
- Incorporate Christian meditation and contemplation into Bible study and name the four steps of
  Lectio Divina.
- Evaluate the merits of reading from a variety of authors, including those they disagree with.
- Distinguish the three types of fasts, describe appropriate fasting, and define asceticism.
- Articulate the biblical view of simplicity and stewardship, and explain how the stories of
  faithful believers encourage spiritual growth.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit is practice as much as doctrine. Rather than only describing the spiritual disciplines,
the chapter has students do them: they pray through a psalm of confession, build a personal plan for
prayer and Bible reading, work Lectio Divina on a real passage, and study how believers across
history walked with God. The framing guards against two errors at once. It refuses to treat the
disciplines as a way to earn God's approval or to measure spiritual status, and it refuses the
Eastern idea that the goal is to empty the self or transcend it. Instead the disciplines are the
ordinary means by which the Spirit forms honest, dependent people who love God and others. Students
are also taught to read and think widely while measuring everything against Scripture, so their
formation is neither anti-intellectual nor uncritical.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

Scripture is both the subject and the tool of this chapter, and its four Key Verses anchor the
review.

- Genesis 2:15 (key verse). God places the man in the garden to work and keep it, showing that work
  itself, done for God, can be an act of worship.
- 1 Thessalonians 5:17 (key verse). "Pray without ceasing" describes a posture of ongoing dependence
  on God, not nonstop talking.
- Ephesians 4:28 (key verse). The believer is to work so that he may have something to share with
  anyone in need, tying honest labor to generosity.
- Hebrews 12:1 (key verse). Surrounded by a great cloud of witnesses, we run our race with
  endurance, the picture behind learning from the saints.
- Prayer, Scripture, and repentance. The Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9–13) is the model Jesus gave for
  prayer; Psalm 51 is David's prayer of self-examination and repentance, worked through in class; and
  the disciplines aim at what Romans 12:2 calls being transformed by the renewing of the mind.
