## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 7 is the second act of the story. Creation was very good, so this unit asks what went
wrong. Students learn that the world was made in shalom, a Hebrew word for peace, wholeness, and
order, and that this harmony was broken by sin, an intruder in God's good world rather than a part
of it. They study the three words the Bible uses for sin (sin, transgression, and iniquity) and
walk through Genesis 3: how the serpent, later identified as Satan, tempted Adam and Eve, and how
the first sin was more than eating fruit. The fall broke four relationships, with God, with self,
with others, and with nature, and its consequences, both physical and spiritual, reach us still.
But Genesis 3 is not only about loss. God responds with grace and gives the protoevangelium in
Genesis 3:15, the first promise of a Redeemer. The chapter follows the spread of sin through Cain,
the flood, the covenant with Noah, and Babel, and sets up the redemption to come.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- If creation was very good, what went wrong, and what does the Bible say the real problem is?
- What is sin, and how is the biblical picture different from common ideas about it?
- What did the fall break, and where do we still see those broken relationships today?
- Who is to blame for evil in the world, and how do Christians answer those who blame God or
  religion?
- How does God respond to human sin, and what does that reveal about his character?
- Why is there still hope after the fall?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Compare the harmony of the original creation (shalom) with the state of the world today.
- Explain the three words the Bible uses for sin: sin, transgression, and iniquity.
- Describe what the fall is and how it happened, including the serpent's tactics and the nature of
  the first sin.
- Identify the physical and spiritual consequences of the fall for our relationships with God,
  self, others, and nature.
- Discuss the doctrine of original sin and respond to the claim that God, society, or religion is
  to blame for evil.
- Explain the protoevangelium of Genesis 3:15 and the ways God showed grace to Adam and Eve.
- Give examples of sin in the lives of Adam and Eve's descendants, and contrast the way of Cain
  with those who walked with God.
- Define covenant and distinguish it from a contract, and describe God's covenant with Noah and
  what happened at Babel.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit gives students a truthful account of what is wrong with the world, and it does so in a
way meant to be honest rather than gloomy. Foundations treats the fall not as an abstract doctrine
but as the explanation for brokenness students already see, in themselves, in their relationships,
and in the news, while insisting that being human is not the problem, being fallen is. The chapter
works hard on the who-is-to-blame question, teaching students to answer secularist and atheist
objections without pretending the difficulty away. And it refuses to end in despair: the same
chapter that describes the fall shows God pursuing his people with grace and promising a Redeemer,
so students learn to read even the hardest part of the story in light of where it is going.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

Genesis 3 is the center of this unit, and the Key Verses trace both the fall and God's response to
it.

- Genesis 3:6 (key verse). Eve took the fruit and ate, and gave some to Adam, who ate as well. The
  first sin, disobedience that was also a failure to trust and love God.
- Genesis 3:14–19 (key verse). God pronounces the consequences of the fall on the serpent, the
  woman, and the man, and curses the ground, yet verse 15 holds the first promise of redemption.
- Genesis 9:7–13 (key verse). God's covenant with Noah, signed by the rainbow, promising never
  again to destroy the earth by flood.
- Romans 3:23 (key verse). "All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." The universal
  reach of sin.
- Romans 5:12 (key verse). Sin entered the world through one man, and death through sin, so death
  spread to all. This is the ground of the doctrine of original sin.
- The protoevangelium. Genesis 3:15, "he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel,"
  is the Bible's first announcement of the gospel, pointing ahead to Jesus crushing Satan at the
  cross. Supporting texts on original sin include Psalm 51:5 and Ephesians 2:1–3.
