## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 11 is Act 5 of the Bible's story, and it is the act Christians live in now: Restoration
Initiated in the Church. After Jesus died, rose, and ascended, his followers had to work out what it
all meant. At Pentecost the Holy Spirit filled them and empowered them to carry out the Great
Commission, and the church has been growing ever since. This is the time of already but not yet:
Jesus reigns as the world's rightful King, but his kingdom is not fully realized, so the church
keeps waiting and bearing witness as it grows. The chapter traces the church from Pentecost through
persecution, its legal status under Constantine, the splits of 1054 and 1517, and worldwide missions,
up to Christianity as the largest religion on Earth today. It also shows what the gospel restores:
our relationship with God through justification, with ourselves through sanctification, and with
others and with creation. Because the kingdom is not yet complete, believers wait together, in a
Christian community.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What does it mean to say the kingdom of God is already here but not yet fully realized?
- What is the church, and what is it here to do?
- How did the gospel spread across the world despite persecution and division?
- What does the gospel restore in our relationships with God, ourselves, others, and creation?
- What is the difference between justification and sanctification, and why does it matter?
- Why do Christians need each other while they wait for Christ's return?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain that the kingdom of God is already here but not yet fully realized, and place the church in
  that in-between time.
- Define the church and the Great Commission, describe how they relate, and explain what happened at
  Pentecost.
- Describe how the early church grew despite persecution, naming key figures and what they did
  (Stephen stoned, Saul converted into Paul, Philip in Samaria, Peter and John arrested and warned,
  Thomas to India).
- Explain what a New Testament epistle is and how the letters addressed the concerns of the early
  church, including the union of Jews and Gentiles in Christ.
- Summarize major events in church history from the first century to today: Constantine's grant of
  legal status in 313, the 1054 split, Luther and the 1517 Reformation, and worldwide missions.
- Explain how sin distorts our relationship with God and how justification restores it.
- Distinguish justification, a state declared at once, from sanctification, the lifelong process of
  becoming like Jesus.
- Describe how Christ's redemption restores our relationships with ourselves, with others through
  forgiveness and reconciliation, and with creation through stewardship.
- Explain why believers need the church community while they wait for Christ's return.

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit places students inside the church's own story rather than asking them to study it from a
distance. As the teacher manual notes, if you are a Christian, church history is part of your family
story, and the aim is for students to draw encouragement from the faithful who went before and honest
warning from their failures. The chapter also keeps turning doctrine toward life: justification and
sanctification are not just terms to memorize but the shape of a restored relationship with God and a
real change the Holy Spirit works in a person over a lifetime. Discussion questions invite students
to name an actual strained relationship and ask how the gospel of reconciliation bears on it, and to
weigh their own experiences of church honestly. Faith here is meant to form students, not only inform
them.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter teaches what the church is directly from Scripture, and five Key Verses hold it together.

- Matthew 28:18–20 (key verse). The Great Commission. Jesus sends the apostles to make disciples,
  baptize, and teach obedience, the mission the church still carries out.
- Acts 2:1–4 (key verse). Pentecost. The promised Holy Spirit fills the disciples with the sound of
  wind and flames of fire, empowering them to proclaim the gospel; Peter preaches and 3,000 believe.
- 1 Corinthians 12:12–14 (key verse). The church as one body with many members, a picture of
  believers united in Christ.
- 1 Peter 2:9 (key verse). The church as a chosen people, a royal priesthood, and a holy nation, one
  of the biblical metaphors this chapter studies.
- John 13:34–35 (key verse). Jesus' command to love one another, by which the world will know his
  disciples, the chapter's picture of the church's clearest witness.
- Justification and sanctification. Students trace how sin warps our relationship with God, how
  justification declares us righteous by grace through faith (Ephesians 2:8–9), and how
  sanctification, the lifelong work of becoming like Jesus, transforms us even as we still struggle
  with sin (Romans 7:15–19).
- The six-act story. Chapter 11 is Act 5, Restoration Initiated in the Church (Acts and the Epistles),
  the act between Jesus' redemption in the Gospels and the New Creation of Revelation 21–22.
