## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 3 asks whether the Bible has authority and what it means to live under it. Students learn
that authority answers the question "Who's in charge?" and that everyone serves some authority; to
confess "Jesus is Lord" is to place yourself under his. The chapter builds its case from evidence.
Internal evidence shows that the writers of both testaments claimed to deliver God's authoritative
revelation, and that Jesus trusted and affirmed the Old Testament. External evidence from
non-Christian writers, archaeology, and fulfilled prophecy confirms the Bible's historical
accuracy, while the Dead Sea Scrolls and more than 25,000 New Testament manuscripts show it was
preserved carefully. Students then answer three common objections, that the Bible is merely human,
that it has been corrupted, and that books were left out, and learn the three criteria that
identify true Scripture. The chapter closes with the five areas of life under God's authority and
why God asks for obedience: it leads to human flourishing, not control.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What is authority, and why does everyone answer to some authority?
- What does it mean to confess that Jesus is Lord?
- What evidence supports trusting the Bible, and what can evidence prove and not prove?
- How should a Christian answer someone who says the Bible is merely human, corrupted, or incomplete?
- Over what parts of life does God's authority reach, and why does he want our obedience?
- Is trusting God's authority blind faith, or something else?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain what authority is and what it means to submit to God's authority.
- Use internal evidence from the Old and New Testaments to defend the Bible's authority, including
  how Jesus confirmed his trust in the Old Testament.
- Name specific external evidence for the Old Testament: non-biblical sources, archaeological
  discoveries, and fulfilled prophecy.
- List ancient non-Christian writers who confirm the historicity of Jesus and the early church:
  Josephus, Tacitus, Pliny the Younger, and the Talmud.
- Explain how the Dead Sea Scrolls and the early New Testament manuscripts show the Bible was
  preserved accurately.
- Answer the three common objections to the Bible's authority: human authorship, corruption and
  textual variants, and the lost gospels.
- Explain what gnosticism taught and how the three canon criteria, apostolicity, orthodoxy, and
  pedigree, exclude the lost gospels.
- Summarize the five areas of life under God's authority and explain why submitting to authority
  requires faith but not blind faith.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit treats faith as reasonable trust, not a leap in the dark. Foundations holds that the
Bible carries God's authority, so the chapter has students examine the actual evidence for that
claim and practice answering honest objections in their own words. Students role-play the skeptic
and the Christian, sort internal evidence from external, and work a Bible study that turns the
question of authority personal by asking what they have put in God's place. The goal is for
students to see that submitting to God is like trusting an airline system, a real act of faith
backed by good reasons, and to weigh for themselves whether Jesus is Lord over every area of their
lives.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter's Key Verses ground its case that the Bible carries God's authority.

- Proverbs 30:5–6 (key verse). Every word of God proves true; do not add to his words. Scripture
  presents itself as God's flawless word.
- 2 Peter 3:15–16 (key verse). Peter calls Paul's letters "Scripture." This is internal evidence
  that the New Testament writers recognized one another's writings as God's Word.
- 1 John 1:1 (key verse). What we have heard, seen, and touched, we proclaim. The Gospel rests on
  eyewitness testimony.
- 2 Peter 1:20–21 (key verse). No prophecy came by human will; people spoke from God as they were
  carried by the Holy Spirit. This answers the objection that the Bible is merely human: its origin
  is both human and divine.
- Internal evidence in the Old Testament. Phrases like "Thus says the LORD" occur more than 3,000
  times, and Jesus treated the Old Testament as unbreakable and as pointing to himself.
