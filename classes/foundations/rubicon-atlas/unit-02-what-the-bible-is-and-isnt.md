## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 2 asks what the Bible actually is, and what it isn't. Students learn that the Bible has
shaped world literature, our nation, and Western civilization, and that people come to it in
different ways. They see how it is arranged, 66 books in two testaments written by more than 40
authors over about 1,500 years, and why some Bibles include the Apocrypha. The chapter corrects
the idea that the Bible is just a book of rules; it is closer to an owner's manual, or a compass
for wise living in a fallen world. Students learn the two kinds of revelation, general and
special, how the canon was formed, and the three criteria used to recognize the New Testament
books. They learn what inspiration and inerrancy mean, then close with why truth matters in a
culture shaped by relativism. The chapter makes the case that the Bible is God's Word and is true
for all people, which sets up the question of its authority in the next unit.

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What is the Bible, and what is it not?
- How does God make himself known, and how much of him can we know?
- Why do Christians trust the Bible as God's Word and not just a human book?
- What makes something true, and can truth be different for each person?
- How does the way a person reads the Bible shape what they find in it?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Name four ways the Bible has influenced the world, and describe how it is arranged, 66 books in
  39 Old Testament and 27 New Testament.
- Identify and explain the four ways people read the Bible: skeptic, scholar, seeker, and saint.
- Explain the map and compass analogies for the Bible.
- Distinguish general revelation from special revelation and give examples of each.
- Explain how the canon was formed and name the three criteria used to recognize the New Testament
  books: apostolicity, orthodoxy, and pedigree.
- Explain why the Bible is God's Word rather than a book that merely contains God's words, and
  define inspiration and inerrancy.
- State the correspondence theory of truth and give two Old Testament and two New Testament
  historical facts that have been corroborated.
- Define relativism, explain why "there is no truth" is self-refuting, and say why truth matters.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit forms how students think about truth itself, not only what the Bible says. Foundations
treats the Bible as God's own Word, so the chapter asks students to weigh their own view of
Scripture honestly and to test the popular claim that truth is relative to each person. Students
practice defending the Bible as God's true Word, examine general and special revelation, and
consider how they personally come to Scripture, whether as a skeptic, scholar, seeker, or saint.
The aim is for students to see that the Christian view of truth holds together and can be lived,
and to decide where they stand, rather than to recite answers they do not hold. As the teacher
manual puts it, the goal is to get them thinking through what they actually believe.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The Bible is the main text of this unit, and its Key Verses ground the chapter's claims about
revelation and truth.

- Psalm 19:1–4 (key verse). The heavens declare the glory of God. This is general revelation:
  creation itself tells everyone something true about God.
- Romans 1:19–20 (key verse). What can be known about God is plain, shown in what he has made, so
  that people are without excuse. Another form of general revelation.
- Romans 2:14–15 (key verse). The requirements of the law are written on human hearts, and
  conscience bears witness. Conscience is a second channel of general revelation.
- 2 Timothy 3:16 (key verse). All Scripture is God-breathed and useful for teaching. This is
  special revelation and the ground for the Bible's inspiration and authority.
- The canon. Students learn how the 66 books were recognized, the Old Testament settled by Jesus'
  day and the New Testament by the second century, using apostolicity, orthodoxy, and pedigree.
