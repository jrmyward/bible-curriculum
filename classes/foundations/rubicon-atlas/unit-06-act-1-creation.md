## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 6 opens the story itself. After Chapter 5 mapped the whole six-act metanarrative, this
unit walks into Act 1, Creation, and asks what it means that God made a good world and made us in
his image. Students read Genesis 1 and 2 and find two claims at the root of everything: God
exists, and God is the Creator, who spoke the world into being out of nothing, forming it and
then filling it with order and purpose. The high point is the making of humans, male and female,
in God's image. Students learn what it means to bear that image in three ways (rule, resemblance,
and relationship), what stewardship of creation requires, and how God is both transcendent and
immanent. The chapter also equips students to see how Genesis confronts both ancient creation
myths and modern worldviews such as atheism, pantheism, nihilism, and gnosticism, and to defend
the goodness of the body, of marriage, and of every human life.

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What does Genesis teach about who God is and who we are?
- What does it mean to be made in God's image, and how should that shape how we treat people?
- What does God's call to stewardship require of us in the way we treat creation?
- How does the biblical account of creation answer the stories and worldviews that compete with it?
- If every person bears God's image, what follows for questions of dignity, gender, and marriage?
- Why is it worth being able to explain and defend what Christians believe about creation?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain the purpose of creation myths and contrast them with the Genesis account.
- Summarize the creation narrative of Genesis 1: God exists, God is Creator, creation is orderly
  and made out of nothing, and humans are made in his image.
- Discuss the creation of humans in Genesis 2, including work as good and the woman as an equal
  image-bearer.
- Articulate attributes of God, especially his transcendence and immanence, and explain why God
  created.
- Analyze the three ways humans bear God's image (rule, resemblance, and relationship) and define
  stewardship and sub-creation.
- Describe God's design for people as relational and sexed beings, including marriage as the union
  of one man and one woman.
- Correlate the biblical mandate for stewardship to our care of nature, showing that Genesis does
  not license exploitation.
- Determine the seven ways the biblical creation story is distinct from other worldviews, and
  defend the goodness of the body and biblical marriage.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit is where the course moves from mapping the biblical story to living inside it. Creation
is not treated as one theory among others but as the ground for how Christians understand God,
human dignity, work, gender, and the natural world. Foundations asks students not only to know
what Genesis teaches but to be able to defend it, so the chapter pairs careful reading of Genesis
1 and 2 with honest comparison to the ancient myths and modern worldviews that tell a different
story. The aim is formation. Students who grasp that their value is inherent, because they bear
God's image, have a reason to oppose racism, sexism, and every other way people are devalued, and
a reason to steward creation rather than exploit it.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

Genesis 1 and 2 are the primary text of this unit, and the Key Verses anchor its main claims.

- Genesis 1:1 (key verse). "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth." The two
  foundational claims: God exists and God is the Creator.
- Genesis 1:26–28 (key verse). God makes humans, male and female, in his image and gives them rule
  over creation, the basis for both stewardship and human dignity.
- Genesis 1:31 (key verse). God saw everything he had made, and it was very good. Creation,
  including the body, is good.
- Genesis 2:18 (key verse). "It is not good that the man should be alone." God makes the woman as
  an equal helper, the ground for marriage and human relationship.
- Psalm 8:4–6 (key verse). "What is man that you are mindful of him?" The psalm marvels that God
  crowns humans with glory and honor and sets them over the works of his hands.
- Supporting texts. Colossians 1:15–17 names Christ as Creator and the one who holds all things
  together (transcendence and immanence), and Acts 17:28 speaks of the God in whom we live and
  move and have our being.
