## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 8 is Act 3 of the Bible's six-act story, Redemption Initiated, and it follows the whole
history of Israel. After the Fall in Act 2, God begins his rescue by choosing one family,
Abraham's, to bless the whole world. The chapter traces that promise across the Old Testament: the
covenant passed to Isaac and then Jacob, the exodus from Egypt and the first Passover, the law and
the tabernacle, the conquest of Canaan and the cycle of the judges, and the kings, from Saul and
David to Solomon and the temple. It then follows the split into the northern and southern
kingdoms, the exile of both, and the prophets God sent to call his people back. Through it all
Israel keeps failing its mission to be a kingdom of priests, yet God stays faithful. The chapter
sets up the problem Act 4 answers: Israel could not save itself, so God would come to do it.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What does it mean that God chose one family and one nation to bless the whole world?
- Why did God give Israel laws, sacrifices, and festivals, and what were they meant to teach?
- What was Israel's mission as a kingdom of priests, and why did they keep failing it?
- How does God respond when his people are unfaithful?
- What does Israel's story reveal about God's character?
- How do the exile stories model faithfulness when the surrounding culture pressures you to conform?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain God's covenant with Abraham and name the two descendants who received the covenant
  blessing, Isaac and then Jacob.
- Relate the Passover to the exodus from Egypt.
- Explain the purpose of the tabernacle and, later, the temple.
- Differentiate the two categories of Old Testament law (ceremonial and moral), the two kinds of
  sacrifice (thanksgiving and sacrifice for sin), and the three major festivals.
- Describe the cycle Israel fell into during the period of the judges.
- Summarize the first three kings, Saul, David, and Solomon, and the choices that set their stories
  apart.
- Describe how the northern and southern kingdoms split, why both went into exile, and how their
  sin interfered with Israel's mission.
- Distinguish Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, and Josiah from the other kings by how they served the Lord,
  and give examples of how Daniel and his friends stayed faithful in exile.
- Define prophet, describe the messages the prophets delivered, and summarize what Israel's story
  reveals about God.

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit teaches Israel's history as the Christian's own family story, not as distant events.
Students learn that God relates to his people through covenant, that obedience is a response to
grace rather than a way to earn it, and that God stays faithful even when his people are not. The
activities keep it personal: students trace the covenant line to Jesus, sort the laws and
festivals that point ahead to Christ, and write about faithfulness under pressure by putting
themselves next to Daniel and his friends. The goal is for students to see where they stand in the
story and to weigh how they respond when the surrounding culture pressures them to conform.

---

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The Old Testament is the text of this unit, and students read long stretches of its story rather
than isolated verses.

- Genesis 50:20 (key verse). Joseph tells his brothers, "You meant evil against me, but God meant
  it for good." God accomplishes his purposes even through human evil and stays committed to his
  promises.
- Acts 13:22 (key verse). God calls David "a man after my heart." David sinned gravely yet
  repented, and the Davidic covenant promised an eternal dynasty pointing to the Messiah.
- 2 Kings 5:15 (key verse). After his healing, the foreign commander Naaman says, "there is no God
  in all the earth but in Israel." Even outsiders came to trust Yahweh, a sign of Israel's mission
  to be a light to the nations.
- Jeremiah 29:7 (key verse). God tells the exiles to "seek the welfare of the city" where they
  live. Faithfulness in exile is tactful, nonviolent, and patient, the pattern Daniel and his
  friends modeled.
- The covenant with Abraham (Genesis 12:1–3). God promised Abraham descendants, the land of Canaan,
  and the Offspring who would crush the serpent's head, and the blessing passed to Isaac and Jacob.
- The six-act story. This chapter is Act 3, Redemption Initiated in Israel, the stretch between the
  Fall (Act 2) and Redemption Accomplished in Jesus (Act 4).
