## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 17 turns the course toward the Christian life and asks what love actually requires. It
starts with the two greatest commandments, love God and love your neighbor, and shows that every
one of God's moral laws rests on them. Because sin means we all fail to love as we should, we
can't build a near-perfect society by trying harder; we need the new hearts Jesus gives. Students
learn the four Greek words for love, agape, eros, philia, and storge, and why the other three go
corrupt without agape. From there the chapter covers loving God and rejecting idolatry, receiving
God's love for ourselves, the many ways Jesus modeled agape, and who our neighbor is. It closes on
building community and pursuing shalom, Christian dual citizenship, and loving even our enemies.
Coming near the end of the year, this chapter puts the whole worldview into practice: love is the
primary defining mark of a Christian.

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What does it mean to love God, and how is that more than a feeling?
- Why do eros, philia, and storge go wrong without agape?
- Who is my neighbor, and what does loving that neighbor actually require of me?
- How do Christians live as citizens of both God's kingdom and an earthly nation?
- Can we love our enemies, and what would that take?
- Why is love the defining mark of a Christian?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain why keeping the two greatest commandments matters and why we can't build a near-perfect
  society by willpower alone.
- Define and distinguish the four Greek words for love, agape, eros, philia, and storge, and
  explain why eros, philia, and storge go corrupt without agape.
- Explain how to love God by seeking and enjoying him, obeying him, and rejecting idolatry, and
  describe how idolatry shapes us into the likeness of what we worship.
- Relate receiving God's love for themselves to their ability to love others.
- Give specific examples of the many ways Jesus demonstrated agape love.
- Summarize what loving your neighbor means, using Leviticus 19 and the parable of the Good
  Samaritan.
- Describe how Christians build healthy communities by pursuing shalom through the family, the
  church, the government, and intermediary institutions.
- Explain Christian dual citizenship and the responsibilities that come with it.
- Apply the five principles for loving enemies and name the primary defining mark of a Christian.

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit moves the Christian worldview from belief into practice. After a year of learning what
Christians hold to be true, students ask what that truth asks of them day to day, and the answer
the chapter keeps returning to is love, love for God and love for neighbor. The teaching is meant
to form students, not only inform them: they examine their own loves, loyalties, longings, and
liturgies, name the people they are tempted to pass by, and weigh honestly how they treat those
they dislike. The class works this out through Scripture and discussion rather than a list of
rules, since the chapter's own point is that we cannot love as we ought by trying harder; we need
the new hearts God gives. The goal is for students to see love as the center of the Christian life
and to take an honest look at their own.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter's Key Verses anchor its teaching on love, and each one grounds a piece of the
Christian life.

- John 15:12 (key verse). Jesus says, "love one another as I have loved you." Loving others is a
  direct command, and Jesus's own life is the model.
- Romans 13:8–10 (key verse). Love is the fulfilling of the law; the commands against harming a
  neighbor are summed up in "love your neighbor as yourself."
- Matthew 5:43–45 (key verse). From the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus tells his followers to love
  their enemies and pray for those who persecute them.
- Leviticus 19:13–18 (key verse). God spells out how Israel was to treat one another, do not
  oppress, rob, slander, hate, or take vengeance, and ends with "love your neighbor as yourself."
  Jesus later answers "who is my neighbor?" in the parable of the Good Samaritan (Luke 10).
- Psalm 135:15–18 (key verse). Idols are lifeless, and those who make them become like them, which
  is why loving God means rejecting idolatry.
- The two greatest commandments. Jesus teaches that all the Law and the Prophets depend on loving
  God and loving neighbor (Matthew 22:37–40), the foundation the whole chapter builds on.
