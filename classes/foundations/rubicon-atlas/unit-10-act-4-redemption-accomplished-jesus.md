## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 10 is Act 4 of the Bible's six-act story, Redemption Accomplished, and it is the pivot the
whole story turns on. After the long interlude, God becomes human in the Incarnation of Jesus.
Students learn why the Incarnation matters in three ways: it is essential for salvation, it affirms
that creation is good, and it means Jesus suffers with us. The chapter follows Jesus' ministry: he
announces the kingdom of God, the just and peaceful rule of God over all creation, and preaches the
gospel; he claims to be the Messiah and God himself, filling the roles of prophet, priest, and
king; he teaches the kingdom through parables and gives his most famous ethical teaching, the
Sermon on the Mount, with its eight beatitudes. It ends at the climax: the cross, where Jesus dies
as our substitute and defeats sin and death, the resurrection, and the ascension, where he reigns
and is available to all who call on his name.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- Why did God have to become human, and what does the Incarnation mean for how we see our bodies
  and our suffering?
- Who did Jesus claim to be, and why can he not be filed away as merely a great teacher?
- What is the kingdom of God, and how is it different from the kingdom people expected?
- What did Jesus accomplish on the cross, and why does the resurrection change everything?
- How do we answer the skeptic who says Jesus never rose, or that he is just a recycled myth?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Conclude three ways the Incarnation is significant, and relate Jesus' ministry to the gospel and
  the kingdom of God.
- Define the kingdom of God as the just and peaceful rule of God over all creation, not a place in
  the clouds.
- Contrast popular views of Jesus' identity with his own claim to be the Messiah, and explain the
  three roles of prophet, priest, and king.
- Explain the ways Jesus claimed deity, including the "I AM" and "Son of Man" titles and the
  meaning of the Christ.
- Explain four lessons Jesus taught about the kingdom through parables, and interpret the parable
  of the tenants.
- Discuss two miracles Jesus performed and what they were a sign of, and list and describe the
  eight beatitudes.
- Explain two aspects of Jesus' redemptive work on the cross, and define redemption.
- Respond to three skeptical objections to the resurrection and give the most plausible explanation
  for the disciples' transformation.
- Analyze two implications of Jesus' ascension, and explain what it means to have faith in Jesus.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit is the center of the whole course, the act every other act points toward. Students do not
study Jesus from a distance; they wrestle with who he claimed to be and decide what to do with the
claim. The activities push toward defense and witness: students role-play answering false views of
Jesus, build a plain-language way to explain the gospel to someone who has never heard it,
investigate the evidence for the resurrection, and test the claim that the Gospels are borrowed
myth. The goal is for students to understand the Incarnation, the cross, and the empty tomb well
enough to trust Jesus themselves and to give an honest answer to a skeptic.

---

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The Gospels are the main text of this unit, and students read them closely.

- Luke 1:26–33 (key verse). Gabriel tells Mary her son "will reign ... and of his kingdom there
  will be no end," announcing the Incarnation and the king the prophets promised.
- Luke 4:17–19 (key verse). Jesus reads Isaiah in the synagogue and announces that the Scripture is
  fulfilled in him, opening his kingdom ministry.
- John 1:14 (key verse). "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us." God took on humanity, which is
  why Jesus can suffer with us and stand in our place.
- 1 Corinthians 15:3–4 (key verse). An early Christian creed: Christ died for our sins, was buried,
  and rose on the third day. Paul says that if Jesus did not rise, our faith is worthless (1
  Corinthians 15:17).
- The kingdom parables (Mark 4; Matthew 13). Jesus taught that the kingdom does not come all at once
  or with irresistible power now, that final judgment is reserved for the future, and that its full
  arrival is postponed so many can enter it during the present age.
- Act 4 in the six-act story. This chapter is Redemption Accomplished in Jesus, the pivot between
  the interlude and Act 5, Restoration Initiated in the Church.
