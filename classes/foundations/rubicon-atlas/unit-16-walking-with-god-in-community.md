## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 16 completes the pair begun in Chapter 15: having practiced the spiritual disciplines on our
own, we now learn to walk with God together. The Christian life is meant to be lived in community,
and the chapter opens by showing why a spirituality that permanently withdraws from others, like
Simeon Stylites on his pillar, goes wrong. The church is imperfect and full of sinners, yet it is
the training ground where God forms us to be like Christ. Students study worship in community,
praying and studying Scripture together, the appropriate use of spiritual gifts, and the sacraments
of baptism and communion. They learn the place of confession, accountability partners, and spiritual
mentors, and how creeds, confessions, and catechisms help the church read Scripture well. The
chapter then looks outward to giving, serving, and sharing the gospel in word and deed, and it
surveys how the church has cared for the sick, protected women and children, promoted education, and
pursued racial unity across history.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- Why can't a Christian grow the way Simeon Stylites tried to, alone and withdrawn from others?
- What makes the local church, even an imperfect one, essential to following Jesus?
- What are spiritual gifts for, and how should they be used?
- Why do baptism and communion matter to the whole community, not just to the individual?
- How should Christians share the gospel in both word and deed?
- How has the church changed the world, and why has it made that kind of impact?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Critique Simeon Stylites' model of spiritual solitude against the Christian's need for community.
- Describe the elements of Christian worship in community.
- Discuss the value of worship services and small groups, and explain why creeds, confessions, and
  catechisms matter.
- Explain the importance of prayer in community and the four guidelines for the appropriate use of
  spiritual gifts.
- Relate the significance of confession, accountability partners, and spiritual mentors.
- Explain the purpose of baptism and communion.
- Express how giving and serving meet the needs of the community.
- Give examples of how to share the gospel in word and deed.
- Identify four specific ways the church has positively impacted society, and explain what the
  universal church is.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit moves the Christian life from private practice into the life of the local church, and it is
honest that the church is imperfect. Students are not told the church is easy or clean; they are
shown that it is filled with sinners and is still the training ground where God forms us to be like
Christ. The chapter is both inward and outward. It teaches the practices that build up believers
(worship, prayer, Scripture study, the sacraments, confession, and mentoring) and then sends students
out to give, serve, and share the gospel in word and deed. A recurring activity has students
summarize one facet of community for their peers, so the class learns the chapter partly from one
another, which enacts the very point the chapter makes. The survey of the church's impact across
history invites students to see their own place in a people God has used to heal and serve the world.

---

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter grounds the church's life in Scripture, and its four Key Verses anchor the review.

- Hebrews 10:25 (key verse). Believers are told not to neglect meeting together but to encourage one
  another, the clearest call to gather as a church.
- 2 Corinthians 5:17 (key verse). Anyone in Christ is a new creation; baptism pictures this dying to
  sin and rising to new life.
- 2 Corinthians 4:16 (key verse). Though the outer self wastes away, the inner self is renewed day by
  day, which is why confession and accountability matter.
- Matthew 5:44 (key verse). "Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you" shapes how the
  church pursues reconciliation and forgiveness.
- Gifts, sacraments, and mission. Spiritual gifts are given to build up the body (Romans 12;
  1 Corinthians 12) in an appropriate, loving, and orderly way; communion remembers Christ's body and
  blood given for us (1 Corinthians 11); and the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19–20) sends the church
  to make disciples in word and deed.
