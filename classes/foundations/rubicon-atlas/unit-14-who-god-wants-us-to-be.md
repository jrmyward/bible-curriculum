## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 14 asks who God made us to be and how he shapes us into that person. It opens with what it
means to be human: made in God's image, given dignity and purpose, yet limited and broken by the
sin we inherited. Jesus, fully human and fully God, shows that being human was never the problem,
and by faith we are united with him and gradually transformed. The chapter names two early heresies
the church rejected, Docetism and Arianism, and teaches what it means to glorify God now and to be
glorified with new, eternal bodies when Christ returns. It then turns practical: following Jesus
means serving others, taking up the cross daily even when that brings suffering, and living in
Christian hope rather than empty optimism or despair. Because becoming like Christ is a lifelong
journey that happens in community, the chapter closes by asking each student who God wants them to
become, and what first step they can take toward it.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What does it mean to be human, and why is being human not our real problem?
- If Jesus was fully human and fully God, what does his life show us about who we are meant to be?
- What does it mean to glorify God now, and what will it mean to be glorified?
- Why does following Jesus involve serving others and taking up the cross?
- How is Christian hope different from optimism and pessimism?
- Who does God want me to become, and how does that change actually happen?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain what being human means and why we have a need for God.
- Explain that because Jesus was fully human, he is a model for holy living in this world.
- Define Docetism and Arianism and explain why the church rejected both as heresies.
- Explain what it means to glorify God, and describe the doctrine of glorification.
- Describe the benefits of being in union with Christ.
- Conclude that following Jesus requires serving others, and explain how a servant can still be a
  leader.
- Explain what it means to pick up the cross daily and why believers can face suffering with hope.
- Differentiate Christian hope from optimism and pessimism.
- Give biblical examples of growing in Christlikeness (Peter, Martha, Paul) and explain why growth
  happens in community.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit treats the Christian life as formation, not just information. Its aim is that students
would see who God made them to be and cooperate with the Spirit who is remaking them into the
likeness of Jesus. The chapter is honest about brokenness, sin, suffering, and imperfect churches,
so students are not sold an easy or triumphant faith; instead they learn that becoming like Christ
takes a lifetime and happens among other believers. Activities ask students to picture their own
future self, to reflect on where following Jesus already costs them something, and to weigh Christian
hope against optimism and despair. The goal is that each student would answer the chapter's title
question personally, naming who God wants them to be and taking a first step toward it.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter's doctrine is built directly on Scripture, and its five Key Verses anchor the review.

- Matthew 5:48 (key verse). "Be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect." The word behind
  perfect means complete or mature, so Jesus calls us toward spiritual maturity, not flawless
  self-effort.
- Matthew 16:24–25 (key verse). Jesus tells his followers to deny themselves and take up their cross
  daily; whoever loses his life for Christ's sake will find it.
- Philippians 1:6 (key verse). God who began a good work in us will carry it on to completion, the
  promise behind sanctification and glorification.
- Philippians 2:3–4 (key verse). Count others more significant than yourselves and look to their
  interests, the pattern of the servant life.
- 2 Peter 1:4 (key verse). Through God's promises we come to share in the divine nature, the heart
  of union with Christ.
- Being human and being remade. Genesis 1:26–27 grounds human dignity in the image of God; Romans 6
  describes the old self dying and the new self raised with Christ; and Romans 8:31–39 assures
  believers that nothing can separate them from God's love, the ground of Christian hope.
