## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 12 is Act 6, the last act of the Bible's story: Restoration Accomplished in the New Creation.
It completes the six-act arc the course began in Chapter 1, and it closes Trimester 2. The good news
has been promised and proclaimed but not yet fully realized, so this chapter looks ahead to the day
God makes all things new. Students read the book of Revelation as apocalyptic literature and
prophecy, a letter written to encourage churches under Roman pressure to worship the emperor. Its two
central images are God on the throne and the slain Lamb: Jesus conquers sin and death not by force
but by sacrifice, and his followers are called to be faithful in suffering. The chapter also teaches
God's judgment as his justice against evil rather than petty revenge, the truth about hell and heaven,
the second coming, and the new creation itself, a renewal of God's world where he dwells with his
people and there is no more sin, death, or barrier between them. This is the climax and resolution
the whole story has been moving toward.

## 2 · Essential Questions

- If Jesus has already won, why do Christians still wait, and what are they waiting for?
- How does Jesus conquer evil as a slain Lamb rather than by force, and what does that ask of his
  followers?
- Can God bring judgment and still be loving?
- What are hell and heaven really, and how is the new creation different from simply going to heaven?
- How does the ending of the Bible's story give a hope that other worldviews cannot?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Relate redemption through Jesus Christ to the Christian hope of future restoration, and explain what
  the word restoration means.
- Explain the literary and cultural context of Revelation as apocalyptic literature, prophecy, and a
  letter to churches living under Roman emperor worship.
- Describe the two central images of Revelation, God on the throne and the slain Lamb, and explain how
  Jesus conquers by sacrifice rather than force.
- Articulate the role of suffering in the Christian life and why believers can suffer with hope.
- Defend the Christian view of God's final judgment as his justice against evil rather than petty
  revenge.
- Clarify the biblical truth about hell, including why its symbolic imagery is no less serious and why
  people in a sense choose it.
- Discuss the eternal destiny of Christians, distinguishing heaven as an interim state from the new
  creation as the final home.
- Explain the significance of the second coming, and compare and contrast the new creation with the
  first creation in Genesis, including the New Jerusalem.
- Explain what makes the biblical metanarrative unique among the world's stories.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit hands students the church's hope and its honesty about suffering at the same time.
Revelation was written to Christians under real pressure, and the chapter does not pretend the
Christian life avoids hardship; it teaches students to be faithful in suffering, as Jesus was. The
class studies persecuted believers by name so the claim that suffering is normal for a Christian
wears a real face. The hard doctrines here, judgment and hell, are taught not to frighten but as
God's justice and as the dignity of a real human choice, and students are asked to defend the
biblical view rather than merely accept it. Because this chapter closes the six-act story, it also
asks students to see themselves inside that story, to find meaning and purpose in God's mission
rather than in building a utopia they will never live to see or in living only for themselves.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter reads the story's ending from Revelation itself, with five Key Verses carrying it.

- Revelation 21:5 (key verse). "Behold, I am making all things new." God's promise that the story
  ends in restoration, not destruction.
- Matthew 6:10 (key verse). "Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven." The
  prayer for God's righteous rule that the new creation finally answers.
- Acts 1:11 (key verse). The promise that Jesus will return in the same way he ascended, the ground
  of the second coming.
- Romans 8:21 (key verse). Creation itself will be set free from decay, so redemption renews the
  world rather than discarding it.
- 1 Peter 5:10 (key verse). After we have suffered a little while, God will restore us, the chapter's
  word of hope in suffering.
- The two central images. Students study God on the throne and the slain Lamb (Revelation 5), the
  Lion of Judah who conquers as a Lamb who was slain.
- Genesis and Revelation. The chapter sets Genesis 1–3 beside Revelation 20–22 and traces how the new
  creation reverses the Fall: night, death, and the serpent's triumph give way to no night, no death,
  and the Lamb's victory, with access to the tree of life reopened.
- The six-act story. Chapter 12 is Act 6, Restoration Accomplished in the New Creation, the climax
  that completes the arc begun at Creation in Genesis 1–2.
