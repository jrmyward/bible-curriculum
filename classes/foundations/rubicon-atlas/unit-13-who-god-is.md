## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 13 opens Part 3 of the course, Knowing and Following God, and it begins Trimester 3. For two
trimesters the class told the whole story of the Bible, from Creation to New Creation. Now it turns
from the story to the God at its center and asks the first question: who is God, really? Most
Americans say they believe in God, but not the same God, so the chapter starts with four common views
and the way each one shapes a life. It then teaches that we cannot know God exhaustively but can know
him truly, because he reveals himself through creation, through Scripture, and above all through
Jesus. Students work through a dozen attributes that make God who he is, from aseity, his
self-existence, to his eternality, omniscience, omnipotence, holiness, righteousness, and love. The
chapter's hinge is the Trinity: one Being in three Persons, Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Students
learn why tritheism and modalism are false, and why the Trinity makes the Christian faith distinct.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What comes into your mind when you think about God, and why does that matter?
- Can a finite mind truly know an infinite God, and if so, how?
- How does God reveal himself, and what do his attributes tell us about who he is?
- What is the difference between believing in God and knowing God?
- What is the Trinity, and why do the common analogies for it fall short?
- Why does the Trinity make the Christian faith distinct?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Categorize the four ways people who believe in God view him, and explain how each view shapes a
  person's life.
- Conclude that although we cannot know God exhaustively, we can know him truly because he reveals
  himself through creation, Scripture, and Jesus.
- Explain perfect being theology as the view that God is the greatest possible being.
- Determine that God is self-existent (aseity), spirit and incarnate in Jesus, and personal rather
  than an impersonal force.
- Discuss God's eternality, omniscience, omnipotence, omnipresence, and immutability.
- Explain that God is holy, wise, righteous, and loving.
- Summarize the doctrine of the Trinity as one Being in three Persons, and argue that tritheism and
  modalism are false beliefs.
- Use Old and New Testament passages to support that God is Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.
- Give reasons the Trinity matters to the Christian faith, and conclude that good theology leads to
  worship, not just knowledge.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit turns the course from studying God's story to knowing God himself, and it treats that as
more than an academic exercise. As the chapter puts it, the goal of theology is not merely to think
more accurately about God but to love him and worship him. Students are pressed to notice that
everyone, even an atheist, holds some picture of God, and that our view of God both shapes and is
shaped by how we live. The chapter also equips students to answer honest, hard questions: whether God
can be known at all, why belief in the Trinity is not irrational, and how the Muslim rejection of the
Trinity differs from the Christian teaching, so that faith is examined rather than assumed. The
closing activities aim head-knowledge back at the heart, asking students to name reasons to worship
the God they have been studying.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter builds its account of God from the Bible, with five Key Verses anchoring it.

- Psalm 90:2 (key verse). "From everlasting to everlasting you are God." God's eternality and aseity,
  without beginning or end.
- Isaiah 46:9–10 (key verse). God declares the end from the beginning and says there is none like him,
  a support for his omniscience and sovereignty.
- Jeremiah 23:23–24 (key verse). God fills heaven and earth, a statement of his omnipresence.
- 1 John 4:8 (key verse). "God is love." His love is not one attribute among many but his nature, and
  to misunderstand it is to not know him.
- Matthew 3:16–17 (key verse). At Jesus' baptism the Father speaks, the Son is baptized, and the
  Spirit descends, all three Persons present at once, which is why modalism cannot be true.
- The Trinity in both Testaments. Students trace the Son prophesied by Isaiah as Immanuel (Isaiah
  7:14; 9:6) and the three Persons named together in the New Testament (Matthew 28:19), and learn why
  tritheism (three gods) and modalism (one God in changing modes) both fall short.
- God revealed. Because God is spirit and no one has seen him, he makes himself known through
  creation, through Scripture, and fully in Jesus, who is God become human (Hebrews 1:1–3).
