## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 9 is the story's interlude, not one of the six acts. It fills the roughly 400 silent years
between the last Old Testament prophet, Malachi, and John the Baptist, the gap between Act 3
(Israel) and Act 4 (Jesus). Students learn how the world Jesus stepped into got made. Daniel's
vision of four beasts names the four empires that ruled the Jews (Babylonian, Medo-Persian, Greek,
Roman); the exiles return under Cyrus to rebuild the temple and the walls; Alexander the Great
launches hellenization, the spread of Greek culture that split the Jewish community; Antiochus IV
desecrates the temple and the Maccabees revolt; and Rome takes over. The chapter then covers the
four Jewish symbols (temple, land, Torah, racial identity), the synagogue and the Samaritans, the
four factions (Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, Zealots), the literature of the period, and the five
hopes of Israel, including the military messiah most Jews expected but did not get.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- How did the roughly 400 years between the testaments shape the world Jesus was born into?
- What does hellenization show about the pressure to conform to a dominant culture, then and now?
- Why did the temple, the land, the Torah, and Jewish identity matter so much, and how could each
  become a danger?
- How did the four factions differ in the way they answered that pressure, and where did each go
  wrong?
- What did the Jewish people hope for, and why was Jesus not the messiah most of them expected?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Place this chapter as the interlude between Act 3 (Israel) and Act 4 (Jesus), the roughly 400
  years between the testaments.
- Name the four empires represented in Daniel's vision.
- Give examples of the challenges Jews faced returning from exile, and recount the rise of Greek
  culture and hellenization.
- Explain the factors that led to the Maccabean Revolt, from Antiochus IV to Mattathias and Judah
  Maccabee.
- Paraphrase how the rise of the Roman Empire set the stage for the birth, life, and death of
  Christ.
- Describe the four key Jewish symbols of the second temple period, and explain the role of the
  synagogue after the exile.
- Explain who the Samaritans were and why Jews and Samaritans despised one another.
- List and describe the four Jewish factions, Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, and Zealots, and how
  each responded to hellenization, and give examples of second temple literature.
- Restate the five hopes of the Jewish people and describe the kind of messiah most Jews expected.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit treats the silent years as anything but empty. Students learn that God was at work in the
gap, arranging the common language, the roads, and the longings the gospel would later use. The
material keeps turning personal: the clash of hellenism and Judaism becomes a mirror for the
culture students live in; the Jewish and Samaritan hatred becomes a prompt to name a person or
group they avoid; the four factions become a debate students argue from the inside. The aim is for
students to feel the pressure to conform that the second temple Jews felt, to see how each
faction's answer fell short, and to understand why the messiah God sent was better than the one
people wanted.

---

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter reads Scripture alongside the history written between the testaments.

- Daniel 7:13–14 (key verse). Daniel sees "one like a son of man" given an everlasting kingdom, a
  vision that looks past the four empires to the Messiah.
- Acts 17:2–3 (key verse). Paul reasons from the Scriptures that the Messiah had to suffer and
  rise, showing that Jesus fulfilled the hopes the second temple Jews held rather than the military
  rescue they expected.
- Galatians 4:4–5 (key verse). "When the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son ... to
  redeem." The long interlude was God's timing, and it ended in Act 4.
- Daniel 7:4–7. The four beasts represent the Babylonian, Medo-Persian, Greek, and Roman empires
  that ruled over the Jews.
- The five hopes of Israel. The people hoped for salvation from their enemies, a state of shalom,
  the arrival of God's kingdom, the forgiveness of sins, and a messiah to lead Israel.
- The interlude in the six-act story. This chapter sits between Act 3 (Redemption Initiated in
  Israel) and Act 4 (Redemption Accomplished in Jesus); it is the intermission, not one of the acts.
