## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 4 turns from what the Bible is to how to read it well. Students see that the Bible can be
hard to understand because it was written long ago, in other languages, to people whose culture
differed from ours, and yet reading it daily is worth the effort, because studying it is a
lifelong journey with real spiritual benefit. The chapter names postmodernism, the view that there
is no defining source for truth, and shows how it turns Bible reading into a mirror for the
reader's own opinions. Students learn five common mistakes people make, the difference between
reading Scripture as about God and reading it as about themselves, and a set of steps for reading
well: choose a good translation, understand the content and the historical and literary context,
look for relationships, use Bible helps, and pursue a response. It closes with three ways to apply
what they read, journal it, share it, and live it, and with how to handle honest doubt. The unit
gives students a skill they will use through the rest of the course.

## 2 · Essential Questions

- Can a passage mean one thing to me and the opposite to you, and both be right?
- What does postmodernism do to the way people read the Bible?
- What common mistakes keep people from understanding Scripture, and how do we avoid them?
- How do we read a passage in its context and still hear what it says to us today?
- What do we do with what we read, and how should we handle doubt?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain what goes wrong when people read the Bible carelessly and why personal Bible reading is
  necessary.
- Define postmodernism and give examples of how it has affected the study of the Bible.
- Identify the five common mistakes people make when reading the Bible.
- Define hermeneutics, context, and exegesis.
- Distinguish historical context from literary context, and eternal relevance from historical
  particularity.
- Name the three types of Bible translation, formal, functional or dynamic, and free, and give an
  example of each.
- Explain the descriptive and prescriptive distinction and the seven steps to reading the Bible
  well.
- Name three ways to apply Scripture, journal it, share it, and live it, and describe how to handle
  doubt well.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit gives students a skill, not only a doctrine: how to read God's Word for themselves.
Foundations assumes Scripture has a meaning its divine Author intended, so the chapter works
against the postmodern habit of making every passage about the reader. Students practice spotting
the common mistakes, tell the same Bible story in a self-centered and then a God-centered way to
feel the difference, and study a real passage using the chapter's method. The chapter also treats
doubt honestly, naming believers who wrestled with it and urging students to take their questions
to someone they trust rather than carry them alone. The aim is students who can open the Bible,
read it in its context, and let it shape them.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The chapter's Key Verses model the careful reading and the doing that it teaches.

- 2 Timothy 2:15 (key verse). Do your best to present yourself to God as a worker who correctly
  handles the word of truth. Reading well is a discipline, not a guess.
- 2 Timothy 3:16–17 (key verse). All Scripture is God-breathed and profitable, which is why no
  passage can be set aside for failing to fit our own theology.
- James 1:22 (key verse). Be doers of the word, and not hearers only. The point of reading is a
  changed life.
- 1 Thessalonians 2:13 (key verse). The Thessalonians received the message as the word of God,
  which is at work in those who believe. Scripture read rightly transforms the reader.
- Reading in context. Students see how ignoring context distorts a passage, as when Jeremiah 29:11
  or Matthew 18:19–20 is claimed apart from its setting.
