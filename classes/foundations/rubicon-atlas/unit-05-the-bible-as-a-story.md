## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 5 steps back from the individual Bible stories students know and asks what larger story
holds them together. It starts with what a narrative is (setting, characters, conflict, theme,
plot, point of view) and the three-act structure, then moves to the idea of a metanarrative, the
story behind all the stories that a worldview tells to make sense of life. Students compare the
Bible's metanarrative with five non-Christian ones (secularism, new spirituality, postmodernism,
Marxism, and Islam) and learn why the Bible is a historical narrative that is comprehensive,
coherent, and normative. The chapter lays out the whole six-act story from Creation to New
Creation and shows students how to place any passage inside it. This is the map for the rest of
the course: Chapters 6 through 12 walk the six acts one at a time, and this chapter is where
students first see the shape of the whole and are asked where they themselves fit in it.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- What story is the Bible telling, and how do its many books fit into one whole?
- What is a metanarrative, and how does the one I believe shape the way I read my own life?
- Why can't every worldview be equally true, and how do we tell which one fits reality?
- What makes the Bible comprehensive, coherent, and normative, and why does that matter?
- How does reading the Bible as one story change the way we handle its hard passages?
- Where do I fit in God's six-act story?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Define narrative, list the elements of a well-written story, and describe the three-act
  structure.
- Define metanarrative, give examples, and explain and critique the postmodern rejection of
  metanarratives as self-defeating.
- Compare and contrast the five common non-Christian worldviews (secularism, new spirituality,
  postmodernism, Marxism, and Islam) with Christianity.
- Explain and defend the Bible as a historical narrative that is comprehensive, coherent, and
  normative, and identify which attribute points to divine inspiration.
- Name the six acts of the biblical metanarrative in order, with the Second Temple interlude, and
  match well-known Bible stories to the right act.
- Identify biblical characters who knew they were caught up in God's larger story.
- Explain God's active role in the story and why knowing the metanarrative helps us interpret
  difficult passages.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit gives students the frame the whole course hangs on: the Bible is not a collection of
disconnected lessons but one true story about the way the world actually is. Foundations treats
that story as the Christian's map for questions about meaning, purpose, and how to live, and it
asks students to compare it honestly with the rival stories their culture tells. The teaching is
meant to form as well as inform. Students put the biblical metanarrative into their own words,
weigh other worldviews fairly, and consider where they themselves stand in God's story rather
than studying it from the outside. Learning to read a hard passage in light of the whole story,
instead of pulling a single verse out on its own, is a habit meant to carry well past the test.

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The Bible's own storyline is the content of this unit, and its Key Verses mark the turning points
of that story.

- Mark 1:14–15 (key verse). "The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand." Mark
  writes knowing that Jesus' arrival is the turning point of a story that began in Genesis.
- Romans 1:1–5 (key verse). Paul sees his own ministry as part of God's larger work, the gospel
  promised beforehand through the prophets and now opened to all nations.
- Galatians 4:4 (key verse). "When the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son."
  Redemption arrives at the appointed moment in the story, not by accident.
- Revelation 21:5 (key verse). "Behold, I am making all things new." The story ends in
  restoration, the sixth act.
- The six acts. Students trace the whole storyline: Creation, God's Good World (Genesis 1–2); the
  Fall, Sin and Its Consequences (Genesis 3–11); Israel, Redemption Initiated (Torah, Prophets,
  Writings); the Second Temple interlude (Ezra, Nehemiah, Esther, Daniel); Redemption Accomplished
  in Jesus (the Gospels); Restoration Initiated in the Church (Acts and the Epistles); and
  Restoration Accomplished in the New Creation (Revelation).
