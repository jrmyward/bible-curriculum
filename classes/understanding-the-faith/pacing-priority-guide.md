## Purpose

This guide helps you make real-time decisions about pacing throughout the year. With 17 fewer instructional days than originally planned, some material may need to be condensed or skipped. Use this guide to prioritize what matters most.

---

## Priority Levels

- **CORE** — Must cover fully. Essential to the course's learning goals. Do not skip.
- **IMPORTANT** — Should cover, but can condense if time is tight. Combine with other material or streamline activities.
- **SUPPLEMENTAL** — Nice to have, but can skip if behind schedule. Optional enrichment.

## Unit-by-Unit Priorities

### Unit 1: Ch 1–2 — Why Apologetics? What is Truth?
**Priority:** **CORE**  
**Reasoning:** This is the foundation for the entire course. Students must understand *why* apologetics matters and grasp the concept of objective truth before engaging with any other material. Without this, the rest of the course lacks grounding. Do not condense.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Discussion Brief #1 (Why apologetics?)
- Discussion Brief #2 (Can truth be relative?)
- Pair & Defend on objective truth

**Can Skip:**
- Case Study #1 (cultural encounter on truth claims) — save case studies for later units where application is more critical

---

### Unit 2: Ch 3–4 — Is the Bible Reliable and Authoritative?
**Priority:** **CORE**  
**Reasoning:** Biblical reliability and authority are foundational apologetics questions. Students will face these objections repeatedly in college and beyond. This unit builds confidence in Scripture's trustworthiness, which undergirds everything else in the course.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Discussion Brief #3 (strongest objection to biblical reliability)
- Discussion Brief #4 (how do we know the Bible is authoritative?)
- Video: "The Case for Reliability of the Gospels" (or equivalent)
- Capstone #1 (first major synthesis)

**Can Condense:**
- Pair & Defend on Bible's authority — if time is tight, use this as a class discussion instead of formal debate
- Case Study on early Christians defending Scripture — could be assigned as reading instead of in-class activity

---

### Unit 3: Ch 5–6 — Who is God? What is His Story?
**Priority:** **IMPORTANT**  
**Reasoning:** Theology of God and the biblical metanarrative are important, but students likely have *some* background from previous Bible classes. This unit deepens their understanding but isn't as novel as Units 1–2. Can condense if needed.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Discussion Brief #5 (attributes of God)
- Discussion Brief #6 (biblical metanarrative vs. other worldview stories)
- Overview of Creation-Fall-Redemption narrative

**Can Condense:**
- Pair & Defend on "Bible as history vs. theology" — can be a shorter class discussion instead of full debate
- Case Study on applying metanarrative to moral question — combine with another case study or assign as homework

**Can Skip:**
- Extended discussion of Trinity doctrine — students likely covered this in earlier Bible courses
- Detailed mapping of biblical storyline — provide a handout instead of in-class activity

---

### Unit 4: Ch 7–8 — Is God Christian? The Supernatural
**Priority:** **CORE**  
**Reasoning:** Religious pluralism ("all roads lead to God") is one of the most common objections students will face in college. The resurrection is the linchpin of Christian apologetics. This unit is essential for equipping students to defend the exclusivity of Christ and the reality of the supernatural.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Discussion Brief #7 (responding to "all religions lead to the same God")
- Discussion Brief #8 (strongest evidence for the resurrection)
- Video comparing world religions
- Pair & Defend on miracles (historically verifiable vs. faith-based)
- Capstone #2 (religious pluralism and the supernatural)

**Can Condense:**
- Case Study on defending Christ's exclusivity in interfaith dialogue — could be homework instead of full in-class activity

---

### Unit 5: Ch 9–10 — Good and Evil, Science and Faith
**Priority:** **CORE**  
**Reasoning:** The problem of evil and the science-faith debate are two of the most significant intellectual obstacles to Christian faith. Students *will* encounter these objections. Both chapters are essential.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Discussion Brief #9 (reconciling a good God with evil)
- Discussion Brief #10 (are science and Christianity compatible?)
- Pair & Defend on "science has disproven Christianity vs. supports Christian theism"
- Video: "The Case for a Creator" (or equivalent)

**Can Condense:**
- Case Study on Christian student in secular biology class — can assign as written reflection instead of group presentation

**Can Skip:**
- Extended discussion of young earth vs. old earth vs. theistic evolution — acknowledge the debate exists, but don't dive deep unless students ask

---

### Unit 6: Ch 11–12 — Truth Involvement, How to Read the Bible
**Priority:** **IMPORTANT**  
**Reasoning:** Hermeneutics (Ch 12) is important for long-term Bible study, but it overlaps with material students may have covered in other Bible courses. "Truth Involved" (Ch 11) is more about application than apologetics content. This unit can be condensed if time is tight.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Discussion Brief #12 (biggest mistake people make when reading the Bible)
- Basic hermeneutical principles: context, genre, authorial intent
- Capstone #3 (truth and interpretation synthesis)

**Can Condense:**
- Discussion Brief #11 ("How does understanding truth change how you live?") — combine with class discussion instead of separate written brief
- Pair & Defend on "truth claims demand action vs. are intellectual" — can be a shorter class discussion
- Case Study on historical misinterpretation (Galileo, slavery) — assign as reading or skip

**Can Skip:**
- Practice interpreting difficult passages — provide a handout with examples instead of in-class workshop

### Unit 7: Ch 13–15 — Loving God and Neighbors
**Priority:** **SUPPLEMENTAL**  
**Reasoning:** This unit shifts from *defending* the faith to *living* the faith. While important for discipleship, it's less central to an *apologetics* course. If time is tight, this unit can be significantly condensed or even skipped, with key points covered in a single class discussion.

**Non-Negotiables (if you keep the unit):**
- Discussion Brief #13 (loving God with all your mind)
- Connection between apologetics and discipleship (1 class discussion)

**Can Condense:**
- Discussion Brief #14 (apologetics and loving neighbors) — combine with Brief #13 into a single reflection
- Pair & Defend on "loving God is emotional vs. intellectual" — skip or shorten to class discussion
- Case Study on loving a hostile neighbor — skip or assign as written reflection

**Can Skip Entirely:**
- Chapter 15 (if it's primarily application/discipleship)
- All three chapters could be condensed into 1–2 days of discussion if you're behind schedule

**Alternative:** If you skip this unit, assign Chapters 13–15 as optional reading for students interested in the discipleship angle of apologetics.

### Unit 8: Ch 16–18 — Why People Walk Away, Conclusion
**Priority:** **CORE** (Ch 17), **IMPORTANT** (Ch 16, 18)  
**Reasoning:** Ch 17 (Why People Walk Away) addresses doubt and deconstruction — critically important for high school seniors heading to college. Ch 16 and 18 are more application-focused and can be condensed.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Discussion Brief #17 (most common reason people leave the faith, and how to respond)
- Video: "Dealing with Doubt"
- Pair & Defend on "doubt strengthens vs. undermines faith"
- Discussion Brief #18 (most important thing you've learned)
- Final Capstone (course synthesis)

**Can Condense:**
- Discussion Brief #16 (Ch 16 content) — if Ch 16 is application-focused, combine with another brief or skip
- Case Study on applying apologetics in workplace — assign as written reflection or skip

**Can Skip:**
- Extended work on Ch 16 if it overlaps with Unit 7 themes (practical application)

## Emergency Condensing Plan

If you fall significantly behind (5+ days), here's the order of cuts:

### First Cuts (Least Impact):
1. **Skip Unit 7 entirely** (Ch 13–15) — assign as optional reading
2. **Condense Ch 16** into 1 day of discussion (instead of full unit treatment)
3. **Skip all optional case studies** (save only the ones tied to capstones)

### Second Cuts (Moderate Impact):
4. **Reduce Pair & Defend exercises** — turn some into class discussions instead of formal debates
5. **Condense Unit 3** (Ch 5–6) to 1 week instead of 2–3 weeks
6. **Skip practice activities** in Ch 12 (hermeneutics) — provide handouts instead

### Third Cuts (Higher Impact, but still manageable):
7. **Reduce Capstone #3 presentations** — fewer students present, or shorter presentations (3–5 min instead of 5–7 min)
8. **Combine Discussion Briefs** — some weeks, combine two briefs into one longer reflection

### Last Resort (Significant Impact):
9. **Condense Ch 9 or Ch 10** (problem of evil OR science-faith) — cover only one in depth, summarize the other
10. **Shorten Final Capstone** — reduce presentation length or number of presentations

**Do NOT cut:**
- Units 1, 2, 4, or 5 (foundational apologetics content)
- Final Capstone (culminating assessment)
- Discussion Briefs #1, 2, 3, 4, 7, 8, 9, 10, 17, 18 (core engagement with key questions)

## Final Notes

1. **Monitor your pacing after each unit.** If you're more than 3 days behind, start condensing IMPORTANT units and skipping SUPPLEMENTAL units.

2. **Prioritize student engagement over content coverage.** It's better to cover fewer chapters well (with deep discussion and synthesis) than to rush through all 18 chapters superficially.

3. **Communicate with students.** If you need to skip or condense material, explain why and point them to resources for independent study.

4. **Adjust based on class needs.** If your students are particularly engaged with a topic (e.g., science-faith debate), it's worth spending extra time even if it means condensing elsewhere.

5. **Save the final 2 weeks (May 10–21) as buffer time.** Use these for final capstone presentations, review, and reflection. Don't plan new content for these weeks.
