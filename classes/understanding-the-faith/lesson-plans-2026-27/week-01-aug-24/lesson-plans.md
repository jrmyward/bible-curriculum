## **MONDAY, AUG 24 — Course Introduction**

### **Objectives:**
- Introduce course goals, structure, and expectations
- Explain the cohort teaching model and why it's different from lecture
- Build class culture around respectful dialogue and intellectual humility
- Preview Chapter 1 reading

### **Materials:**
- Syllabus (printed, one per student)
- Course overview slide deck (optional)
- Index cards for student info

### **Lesson Flow:**

#### **[0–10 min] Welcome & Course Overview**

**Say:**
> "Welcome to Understanding the Faith! This course is different from most Bible classes you've taken. We're not going to spend much time with me lecturing at you. Instead, you're going to read, think, write, and *talk* — a lot. This is a discussion-first class, which means I'm going to ask you hard questions, and you're going to defend your answers to each other and to me."

**Brief overview:**
- Course goal: Equip you to defend Christian faith intellectually and winsomely
- Not about winning arguments — about knowing *why* you believe what you believe
- You'll read the textbook, write Discussion Briefs, debate each other (Pair & Defend), solve Case Studies, and present Capstone projects

#### **[10–25 min] Syllabus Walkthrough**

**Cover:**
1. **Grading breakdown** (see syllabus)
   - Discussion Briefs: 20%
   - Case Studies: 15%
   - Pair & Defend: 10%
   - Capstones: 55% (five throughout the year)
2. **Cohort tools** — briefly explain what each is:
   - **Discussion Brief:** 1-page written response to a prompt before class discussion
   - **Pair & Defend:** Structured debate where you defend a position (sometimes not your own!)
   - **Case Study:** Group work applying concepts to real scenarios
   - **Capstone:** Major presentation synthesizing multiple chapters
3. **Expectations:**
   - Come prepared (reading must be done before class)
   - Engage respectfully (disagree without being disagreeable)
   - Take intellectual risks (it's okay to be wrong; it's not okay to be silent)

**Q&A:** Open floor for questions about the course

#### **[25–35 min] Build Class Culture — "Intellectual Humility" Activity**

**Activity: "I Used to Believe..."**

**Instructions:**
> "On your index card, write down one thing you used to believe that you no longer believe. It can be anything — something silly from childhood, a political view, a religious belief, whatever. You don't have to share it, but you can if you want."

Give students 3 min to write.

**Debrief (whole class):**
- "Who's willing to share?" (2–3 volunteers)
- After each: "What made you change your mind?"
- Transition: "This is what we're going to do all year — examine beliefs, test them, and be willing to change our minds if the evidence points elsewhere. That takes intellectual humility: being confident enough to question, humble enough to be wrong."

#### **[35–43 min] Preview Chapter 1**

**Say:**
> "This week we're starting with Chapter 1: *Why Apologetics?* Myers is going to introduce you to five key questions that shape how you see the world:
> 1. Does God exist?
> 2. Can we know truth?
> 3. How do we gain knowledge?
> 4. Where did we come from?
> 5. Why are we here?
> 
> These questions will come up over and over this year. Your job this week is to start wrestling with them."

**Preview Tuesday's class:**
> "Tomorrow we're doing a guided reading in class. I'm going to show you how to read this textbook efficiently — you won't read all 24 pages. I'll teach you how to find the core ideas and skip the filler. Bring your textbook and a pen."

#### **[43–45 min] Exit Ticket**

**Prompt (write on board):**
> "What's one question you hope this course answers for you?"

Students write on index cards and hand in as they leave.

## **TUESDAY, AUG 25 — Guided Core Reading (Chapter 1)**

### **Objectives:**
- Extract the five key worldview questions from Chapter 1
- Practice active reading with a note-catcher
- Understand the distinction between hard and soft authority
- Clarify the relationship between faith and reason
- Prepare for Discussion Brief #1

### **Materials:**
- Textbook (students bring their own)
- **Core Reading Guide** handout (see end of this document)
- **Note-Catcher** handout (see end of this document)
- Whiteboard/markers

### **Lesson Flow:**

#### **[0–5 min] Frame the Reading**

**Say:**
> "Chapter 1 is 24 pages long. If you tried to read every word carefully, you'd be here for 90 minutes and you'd forget half of it. Here's the secret: Myers is thorough, but not everything is essential *right now*. Some sections are examples, background, or asides we can come back to later.
> 
> Today, I'm teaching you how to read strategically. You're going to read about 8–10 pages in 30 minutes using this Core Reading Guide. It tells you exactly what to read and what to skip. As you read, fill out the Note-Catcher — it keeps you focused on the big ideas."

**Hand out:**
1. Core Reading Guide
2. Note-Catcher

**Quick scan:**
> "Look at the Core Reading Guide. Notice we're skipping Sections 5, 6, 8, 10, and 13–15. Why? Those are either stats-heavy, too abstract for Day 2, or administrative. We'll fold them in later when they're relevant. Today, focus on the five questions, authority, faith, and doubt."

#### **[5–15 min] Round 1: Sections 1–3 (The Five Questions)**

**Silent reading:** Students read pages 1–4 (Sections 1–3: NYC story, five questions intro, cat/dog dialogue)

**Teacher role:** Walk around. Check that students are filling out the Note-Catcher. Answer quick questions quietly.

#### **[15–17 min] Quick Check-In #1**

**Stop reading. Eyes up.**

**Ask:** "Raise your hand if you can name at least 3 of the five key questions."

Call on 2–3 students. Write the five questions on the board as they say them:

1. **God** — Does God exist?
2. **Truth** — Can we know truth?
3. **Knowledge** — How do we know?
4. **Origin** — Where did we come from?
5. **Meaning** — Why are we here?

**Say:** "These five questions are the backbone of the entire course. Every chapter connects to one or more of these. If you forget everything else, remember these five."

#### **[17–29 min] Round 2: Section 4 & 9 (Five Questions Breakdown + Authority)**

**Silent reading:** Students read pages 4–6 (Section 4: Five Questions detail) and pages 11–12 (Section 9: Hard/Soft Authority)

**Note:** They're skipping Sections 5–8. Remind them to check the Core Reading Guide if they look lost.

#### **[29–31 min] Quick Check-In #2**

**Ask:** "What's the difference between hard authority and soft authority?"

Call on 1–2 students.

**Clarify if needed:**
- **Hard Authority:** Power to compel behavior (government, police, parents when you're young)
- **Soft Authority:** Influence based on expertise or wisdom (teacher, pastor, mentor)

**Ask:** "Which kind of authority is the Bible? Why does that matter?"

(Let 1–2 students answer; don't over-explain — they'll write about this in Discussion Brief)

#### **[31–39 min] Round 3: Sections 11–12 + Conclusion (Faith, Doubt, Summary)**

**Silent reading:** Students read pages 14–16 (Sections 11–12: Faith ≠ blind belief; Thomas & John the Baptist) and page 22 (Conclusion: last 2 paragraphs)

#### **[39–45 min] Debrief & Assign Discussion Brief #1**

**No check-in for Round 3. Go straight to debrief.**

**Whole-class rapid-fire questions:**

1. **"What's one thing that surprised you in this reading?"**  
   (Take 2–3 responses)

2. **"Which of the five questions do you think is MOST important? Why?"**  
   (Take 2–3 responses; no right answer — just get them thinking)

3. **"Myers says biblical faith is NOT blind belief. What does he mean?"**  
   (1–2 responses. Clarify: Faith is trust *based on evidence*, not trust *despite* evidence. Thomas got evidence; John the Baptist got evidence.)

**Assign Discussion Brief #1:**

**Say:**
> "For homework, you're writing **Discussion Brief #1**. Here's the prompt:
> 
> **'Why should Christians be prepared to defend their faith?'**
> 
> Use what you read today. Think about:
> - The five questions (which ones do people challenge Christians on?)
> - Faith and reason (why isn't 'just have faith' always enough?)
> - Doubt (how did Jesus respond to Thomas and John?)
> 
> This is due **Tuesday night at 11:59 PM** via [Google Classroom/email]. I need to read them before Wednesday so I can give you feedback and we can discuss the format together."

**Hand out Discussion Brief Template** (see end of document)

**Preview Wednesday:**

## **WEDNESDAY, AUG 27 — Discussion Brief Format & Revision Workshop**

### **Objectives:**
- Understand what makes a strong Discussion Brief (thesis, reasoning, evidence, clarity)
- Analyze the rubric and identify areas for improvement
- Revise Discussion Brief #1 based on teacher feedback
- Preview Thursday's rhetoric lesson

### **Materials:**
- Students' Discussion Briefs with teacher feedback (returned at start of class)
- Projector (to display rubric and 1–2 example briefs)
- Whiteboard/markers
- Revision checklist handout

### **Lesson Flow:**

#### **[0–5 min] Return Briefs & Warm-Up Poll**

**As students enter:** Hand back Discussion Brief #1 with written feedback (you read these Tuesday night)

**Warm-up poll (show of hands):**
> "Quick poll about your first Discussion Brief:
> - How many of you found it **easy** to write?
> - How many found it **challenging**?
> - How many of you changed your mind while writing it?"

(Just get a sense of where they are — affirm that challenge is normal)

#### **[5–15 min] What Makes a Strong Brief? (Rubric Deep Dive)**

**Project the rubric on screen** (see end of document)

**Say:**
> "Discussion Briefs are the backbone of this class. Let me show you exactly what I'm looking for."

**Go through each criterion:**

1. **Thesis (4 points)**
   - **Strong:** "Christians should defend their faith because it strengthens both their own understanding and their witness to others."
   - **Weak:** "Apologetics is important." (Too vague)
   - **Ask:** "What makes the first one clearer?"

2. **Reasoning (4 points)**
   - **Strong:** "When Christians study apologetics, they encounter objections they've never considered, which forces them to think more deeply about *why* they believe, not just *what* they believe."
   - **Weak:** "It's good to know stuff."
   - **Ask:** "What's the difference?"

3. **Evidence (4 points)**
   - **Strong:** "In 1 Peter 3:15, Paul commands believers to 'always be prepared to give an answer.' This isn't optional — it's obedience."
   - **Weak:** "The Bible says we should."
   - **Ask:** "Why is the specific reference better?"

4. **Clarity (4 points)**
   - **Strong:** Organized paragraphs, transitions, easy to follow
   - **Weak:** Stream of consciousness, no clear structure
   - **Show an example:** (project one anonymous brief that's well-organized)

5. **Mechanics (4 points)**
   - Spelling, grammar, formatting
   - **Say:** "This is the easiest 4 points to get — proofread!"

#### **[15–25 min] Example Briefs (Anonymous Analysis)**

**Project 1–2 briefs anonymously** (choose ones that illustrate common strengths/weaknesses)

**For each brief:**

1. **Read it aloud** (or have a student read)
2. **Ask:** "What's this brief's thesis? Can you state it in one sentence?"
3. **Ask:** "What's the strongest piece of evidence?"
4. **Ask:** "If you were revising this, what's one thing you'd change?"

**Example Brief A (Strong thesis, weak evidence):**
> "Christians need apologetics because we live in a skeptical culture that challenges faith constantly."

**Class discussion:**
- Thesis: Clear and specific
- Evidence: Where's the support? What Scripture? What example?
- Revision tip: Add 1 Peter 3:15 or a cultural example (college campus, social media)

**Example Brief B (Good evidence, unclear thesis):**
> "Thomas doubted and Jesus showed him evidence. John the Baptist doubted and Jesus sent him evidence. Paul debated in the marketplace."

**Class discussion:**
- Evidence: Great examples!
- Thesis: What's the *point*? Why does this matter?
- Revision tip: Add a clear thesis at the top: "Jesus modeled apologetics by meeting doubt with evidence."

#### **[25–35 min] Revision Workshop**

**Say:**
> "You're going to revise Discussion Brief #1 right now. Look at my feedback on your brief. Most of you will see comments in one or two areas. Use the next 10 minutes to strengthen your brief."

**Revision checklist (hand out or project):**

- [ ] My thesis directly answers the prompt in 1–2 sentences
- [ ] I give at least 2 reasons *why* (not just *what*)
- [ ] I cite at least one Scripture verse or textbook reference
- [ ] My paragraphs have clear transitions
- [ ] I proofread for typos and grammar

**Teacher role:** Walk the room, answer questions, give real-time coaching

**Collect revised briefs at end of class** (optional — or let them finish for homework)

#### **[35–40 min] Preview Thursday: Rhetoric 101**

**Say:**
> "Tomorrow, we're stepping back to talk about *how* to make arguments — not just in this class, but anywhere. We're going to study **rhetoric** — the art of persuasive communication.
> 
> You'll learn:
> - The three types of persuasion: **Logos** (logic), **Pathos** (emotion), **Ethos** (credibility)
> - How to spot **logical fallacies** (bad arguments that *sound* good)
> - How the Apostle Paul used rhetoric to defend the Gospel in Romans
> 
> Then we'll come back to your Discussion Briefs and analyze them through a rhetoric lens. You're going to see your own arguments in a whole new way."

#### **[40–43 min] Preview Friday (Substitute Teacher Day)**

**Say:**
> "Heads up: I'll be out on Friday. You'll have a substitute. Here's what you'll be doing:
> - Watch a short video on apologetics in action (I'll leave instructions)
> - Reflect on what you've learned this week
> - Preview Chapter 2 reading
> 
> The sub will collect your reflections, and we'll start fresh on Monday with Chapter 2: *What is Truth?*"

#### **[43–45 min] Exit Ticket**

**Prompt (write on board):**
> "What's one thing you want to improve in your next Discussion Brief?"

Students write on index cards and hand in as they leave.

**Homework:**
> "If you didn't finish your revision in class, complete it and submit by **Thursday morning**. Come ready to learn about rhetoric tomorrow!"

## **THURSDAY, AUG 28 — Rhetoric 101 & Discussion Brief Debrief**

### **Objectives:**
- Learn the three appeals of rhetoric (Logos, Pathos, Ethos)
- Identify logical fallacies in arguments
- Analyze Discussion Briefs through a rhetoric lens
- See how Paul used rhetoric in Scripture

### **Materials:**
- **Rhetoric 101 handout** (print from `_shared/handouts/rhetoric-101.md` — one per student)
- 3–4 pre-selected Discussion Briefs (you chose these Tuesday night after reading submissions)
- Projector
- Whiteboard/markers

### **Lesson Flow:**

#### **[0–5 min] Introduction: Why Rhetoric Matters**

**Say:**
> "Yesterday you revised your Discussion Briefs. Today, I'm teaching you a skill that will transform how you read, write, and argue: **rhetoric**.
>
> Rhetoric isn't manipulation — it's the art of making truth clear, compelling, and defensible. Every time you write a Discussion Brief, defend your faith to a friend, or explain why you believe something, you're using rhetoric. The question is: are you using it *well*?"

**Hand out Rhetoric 101 handout**

**Preview:**
> "We're going to learn three things:
> 1. The three types of persuasion (Logos, Pathos, Ethos)
> 2. How to spot bad arguments (logical fallacies)
> 3. How the Apostle Paul used rhetoric in Scripture
>
> Then we'll analyze some of *your* Discussion Briefs through a rhetoric lens."

#### **[5–15 min] The Three Appeals: Logos, Pathos, Ethos**

**Direct students to handout** (Rhetoric 101, page 1)

**Teach the three appeals:**

**1. Logos (Logic)**

**Say:**
> "Logos appeals to **reason**. Facts, data, cause-and-effect. This is the backbone of apologetics."

**Example from handout:**
> "The Bible contains prophecies written centuries before Jesus that He fulfilled — therefore, it has predictive accuracy beyond human ability."

**Ask:** "What makes this logos? What's the logical structure?" (If A, then B; A is true; therefore B)

**2. Pathos (Emotion)**

**Say:**
> "Pathos appeals to **feelings** and **values**. Stories, vivid language, shared experiences. This isn't manipulation — it's connection."

**Example from handout:**
> "Imagine a world where truth is whatever the powerful say it is — where your voice, your conscience, your very self can be erased by someone else's definition."

**Ask:** "How does this make you *feel*? Why is that persuasive?"

**3. Ethos (Credibility)**

**Say:**
> "Ethos establishes **why you should be trusted**. It's about character, expertise, authority."

**Example from handout:**
> "C.S. Lewis was an atheist professor at Oxford before converting — he understands the objections from the inside."

**Ask:** "Why does Lewis's background make him more credible on this topic?"

**Key point:**
> "Good arguments use **all three**. Logos without pathos is cold. Pathos without logos is manipulative. Ethos without either is empty. You need all three working together."

#### **[15–25 min] Spotting Logical Fallacies**

**Direct students to handout** (Rhetoric 101, page 2–3: Common Logical Fallacies)

**Say:**
> "A fallacy is a flaw in reasoning. It *sounds* good, but it doesn't hold up. You'll see these everywhere — in debates, social media, even in sermons. Let's practice spotting them."

**Teach 3–4 key fallacies with examples:**

**1. Ad Hominem (Attack the Person)**

- Example: "You can't trust his view on ethics — he's a Republican."
- **Ask:** "What's wrong with this argument?" (The truth of a claim doesn't depend on who says it)

**2. Straw Man**

- Example: "Christians think you should never question anything and just blindly obey."
- **Ask:** "Is this what Christians actually believe, or a distortion?" (It's a distortion — easier to attack)

**3. Appeal to Popularity (Bandwagon)**

- Example: "Most people today don't believe in absolute truth, so it must not exist."
- **Ask:** "Does majority vote determine truth?" (No — truth isn't democratic)

**4. Begging the Question (Circular Reasoning)**

- Example: "The Bible is true because God wrote it, and I know God wrote it because the Bible says so."
- **Ask:** "What's circular here?" (Using the conclusion as the premise — no independent proof)

**Challenge:**
> "As you listen to arguments this year — in this class, online, from friends — ask yourself: Is this logos, pathos, or ethos? Is there a fallacy hidden in here?"

#### **[25–40 min] Analyzing Discussion Briefs Through a Rhetoric Lens**

**Say:**
> "Now let's look at your Discussion Briefs. I've picked 3–4 that show different rhetorical strategies. We're going to analyze them like rhetoricians — not to critique the writer, but to see what makes arguments *work*."

**Project Brief #1 anonymously**

**Ask these questions for each brief:**

1. **"Which appeal is strongest here: logos, pathos, or ethos?"**
2. **"Where do you see evidence (logos)? Where do you see emotion (pathos)? Where do you see credibility (ethos)?"**
3. **"Are there any logical fallacies?"**
4. **"How could this brief be even stronger?"**

**Example Analysis:**

> **[Brief A: "Christians should defend their faith because 1 Peter 3:15 commands it."]**
>
> - Strongest appeal: **Ethos** (biblical authority)
> - Logos: Scripture reference (but could use more *reasoning* about *why* the command matters)
> - Pathos: Missing — could add a story of someone whose faith was strengthened by apologetics
> - Fallacy check: None obvious
> - **Improvement:** Add logos (explain the logic of the command) and pathos (make it personal)

**Repeat for 2–3 more briefs**

**Key insight:**
> "Notice: the briefs that *only* use one appeal feel incomplete. The strongest briefs blend all three — Scripture (ethos), logic (logos), and personal relevance (pathos)."

#### **[40–43 min] Paul as Rhetorician**

**Direct students to handout** (Rhetoric 101, page 4: Rhetoric in Scripture)

**Say:**
> "The Apostle Paul was a master rhetorician. He studied Greek philosophy and debate. Look at how he structures Romans — it's like a legal case:
>
> 1. **Problem** (Romans 1–3): All have sinned
> 2. **Solution** (Romans 3–5): Justification by faith
> 3. **Objections** (Romans 6–8): What about grace leading to sin? What about the law?
> 4. **Application** (Romans 12–16): How to live
>
> He uses **diatribe** (imagined objections), **rhetorical questions**, **quotations from authority** (Old Testament), and **logical progression**. Paul didn't just *assert* the Gospel — he *argued* for it."

**Key takeaway:**
> "Rhetoric isn't un-biblical. Paul used it. Jesus used it (parables = pathos; 'Which is easier...' = logos). You're learning to defend truth the way Scripture does — with clarity, logic, and persuasion."

#### **[43–45 min] Preview Tomorrow (Friday — Substitute Day)**

**Say:**
> "Tomorrow I'll be out. You'll have a substitute. Here's what you'll do:
>
> - Watch a 15-min video on apologetics in action (Lee Strobel interview)
> - Write a reflection on what you learned this week
> - Get Chapter 2 reading materials
>
> The sub will collect everything. I'll see you Monday to start Chapter 2: *What is Truth?*"

**Exit Ticket:**
> "Name one logical fallacy from the handout and give an example of where you've seen it (social media, news, conversations, etc.)."

> 
> You've probably heard people say things like:
> - 'That's your truth, not my truth.'
> - 'Truth is whatever you believe.'
> - 'Who's to say what's really true?'
> 
> This is called *relativism* — the idea that truth is subjective, personal, or culturally constructed. Myers is going to argue that relativism is self-defeating and that *objective truth* exists."

**Introduce key concepts:**

1. **Relativism:** Truth is relative to the individual or culture
   - Example: "Lying is wrong *for you*, but not *for me*."

2. **Postmodernism:** Skepticism about objective truth; truth is a social construct
   - Example: "Science is just one way of knowing — it's not 'truer' than other ways."

3. **Objective Truth:** Truth that is true regardless of what anyone believes
   - Example: "2 + 2 = 4, even if everyone on earth believed it equaled 5."

**Ask:**
> "Quick poll: Do you think truth is relative or objective?"

(Show of hands — don't debate yet; just gauge the room)

**Explain Pair & Defend briefly:**
> "Here's how it works:
> 1. You'll be paired with a partner.
> 2. I'll assign you a position (pro or con).
> 3. You'll prepare a 3-minute opening statement defending your position.
> 4. You'll have 2 minutes to rebut your partner.
> 5. The class asks questions.
> 
> The goal isn't to 'win' — it's to make the *best possible argument* for your side, even if you don't personally agree with it. This is how lawyers, debaters, and thinkers train — by steelmanning the opposition."

---

**DISCUSSION BRIEF #1: "Why Should Christians Be Prepared to Defend Their Faith?"**

**Name:** ______________________  
**Date:** ______________________  
**Due:** Wednesday, Aug 26

---

**Instructions:**

Write a 1-page, double-spaced response to the prompt above. Your brief should include:

1. **A clear thesis** — Answer the question directly in 1–2 sentences
2. **Reasoning** — Explain *why* you hold this position (2–3 reasons)
3. **Evidence** — Support your reasoning with Scripture, textbook references, or examples
4. **Conclusion** — Restate your thesis and offer one implication or application

**Format:**
- 12-point font, Times New Roman or Arial
- Double-spaced
- 1-inch margins
- No heading beyond your name at the top

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Excellent (4) | Good (3) | Needs Improvement (2) | Incomplete (1) |
|-----------|---------------|----------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Thesis** | Clear, direct answer to prompt | Answer present but unclear | Implied but not stated | Missing |
| **Reasoning** | 2–3 well-developed reasons | 2 reasons, somewhat developed | 1 reason or poorly explained | No clear reasoning |
| **Evidence** | Scripture + textbook + examples | Scripture or textbook cited | Evidence present but weak | No evidence |
| **Clarity** | Easy to follow; well-organized | Mostly clear; minor issues | Hard to follow at times | Confusing or disorganized |
| **Mechanics** | No errors | 1–2 minor errors | 3–5 errors | 6+ errors or major issues |

**Instructions:**

Find **3 examples** of logical fallacies "in the wild" — social media posts, news articles, conversations, YouTube videos, advertisements, political speeches, etc.

For each example, write:

1. **The argument or claim** (quote it or summarize it)
2. **Which fallacy it commits** (use the names from the Rhetoric 101 handout)
3. **Why it's a fallacy** (explain what's wrong with the reasoning)

**Example:**

> **Argument:** "Everyone I know is voting for this candidate, so you should too."  
> **Fallacy:** Appeal to Popularity (Bandwagon)  
> **Why it's a fallacy:** Just because many people believe something doesn't make it true. Truth isn't decided by majority vote.
