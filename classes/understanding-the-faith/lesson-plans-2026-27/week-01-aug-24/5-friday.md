## SUBSTITUTE TEACHER INSTRUCTIONS

**Thank you for covering this class!** This is a high school apologetics course. Students have been learning about rhetoric and how to make arguments this week. Today is a video + reflection day. This is structured — students should be working the entire period.

### Lesson Flow:

#### [0–5 min] Attendance & Introduction

**Take attendance** (roster provided)

**Introduce yourself briefly**, then say:

> "Mr. Ward is out today. He left you a structured lesson: you'll watch a video about apologetics in action, then write a reflection on what you've learned this week. This is a working day — no free time. Let's get started."

#### [10–20 min] Address Common Concerns

**Based on Exit Tickets and Roses/Thorns, address 2–3 themes.**

**Example Theme 1: "The reading is too hard."**

**Say:**
> "I hear you. Myers writes at a college level, and some sections are dense. That's why we did guided reading this week — I showed you how to find the core ideas and skip the filler. As we go on, you'll get faster at this. And remember: you're not reading alone. We process it together in class."

**Example Theme 2: "I'm nervous about defending ideas I don't agree with in Pair & Defend."**

**Say:**
> "That's the point! Pair & Defend teaches you to see both sides of an argument. You can't refute an idea you don't understand. When you argue the opposite of your view, you get better at defending your *actual* view. Plus, it's way more interesting than everyone agreeing all the time."

**Example Theme 3: "I don't feel like I'm 'smart enough' for this class."**

**Say:**
> "This class isn't about being smart. It's about being *curious* and *willing to think hard*. I don't expect you to have all the answers — I don't have all the answers! I expect you to show up, engage, and try. That's it."

#### [20–35 min] Preview Chapter 2: "What is Truth?"

**Hook:**
> "Next week we're tackling one of the biggest questions in philosophy: *What is truth?* 
> 
> You've probably heard people say things like:
> - 'That's your truth, not my truth.'
> - 'Truth is whatever you believe.'
> - 'Who's to say what's really true?'
> 
> This is called *relativism* — the idea that truth is subjective, personal, or culturally constructed. Myers is going to argue that relativism is self-defeating and that *objective truth* exists."

**Introduce key concepts:**

1. **Relativism:** Truth is relative to the individual or culture
   - Example: "Lying is wrong *for you*, but not *for me*."

2. **Postmodernism:** Skepticism about objective truth; truth is a social construct
   - Example: "Science is just one way of knowing — it's not 'truer' than other ways."

3. **Objective Truth:** Truth that is true regardless of what anyone believes
   - Example: "2 + 2 = 4, even if everyone on earth believed it equaled 5."

**Ask:**
> "Quick poll: Do you think truth is relative or objective?"

(Show of hands — don't debate yet; just gauge the room)

**Say:**
> "We're going to dig into this next week. You'll read Chapter 2, write Discussion Brief #2, and do your first **Pair & Defend** on Thursday. The topic: *'Is objective truth possible in a pluralistic society?'* One of you will argue yes, one will argue no — and I'll assign your sides randomly."

**Explain Pair & Defend briefly:**
> "Here's how it works:
> 1. You'll be paired with a partner.
> 2. I'll assign you a position (pro or con).
> 3. You'll prepare a 3-minute opening statement defending your position.
> 4. You'll have 2 minutes to rebut your partner.
> 5. The class asks questions.
> 
> The goal isn't to 'win' — it's to make the *best possible argument* for your side, even if you don't personally agree with it. This is how lawyers, debaters, and thinkers train — by steelmanning the opposition."

(Take 2–3 questions)

> 2. **Write Discussion Brief #2** (prompt: 'Can truth be relative?') — due Tuesday.

>
