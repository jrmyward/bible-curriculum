1.
When it comes to the study of God (theology), what are the three basic options? [8.1]
Theism: the belief in the existence of a personal God or gods independent of creation.
Pantheism: the belief that all living beings and material objects are divine. God is the
sum of all things.
Atheism: the belief that God doesn't exist.
2. Can you summarize the theology of Secularism? [8.2]
The theology of Secularism is atheism, the belief that God doesn't exist. There is nothing,
or no one, higher than human beings.
[Secularism is] religion without God [that has] abandoned all conceptions of a
supernatural and all forms of cosmic support. (Harold H. Titus)
Like all atheists, Secularists are completely focused on the present. We are all there is,
and we should accept that.
[Atheism] can offer no hope of personal immortality, no threats of divine chastise-
ment, no promise of eternal recompense for injustices suffered, no blueprints to sure
salvation.... A tragic view of life is thus [an undeniable] ... ingredient in atheistic
thought. Ernest Nagel
A modern form of atheism, sometimes called New Atheism, is really more of an
anti-theism. It is brash, aggressive, and vindictive. It goes beyond denying the existence
of God to vehemently attacking religion and believers. Its main champions are Richard
Dawkins, Sam Harris, Daniel Dennett, and the late Christopher Hitchens.

6. Why is the war against capitalism also a war against religion to Marxists? [8.3]
Karl Marx believed that religion kept people from seeing the truth about capitalism's
inherent abusiveness. He couldn't be more blunt:
We want to sweep away everything that claims to be supernatural and superhu-
man, for the root of all untruth and lying is the pretension of the human and the
natural to be superhuman and supernatural. For that reason we have once and for
all declared war on religion and religious ideas and care little whether we are called
atheists or anything else. (Karl Marx)
The Communist Manifesto calls for the forcible overthrow of all existing social condi-
tions and the religion that created and sustains them. According to Marx, "Religion is
the sigh of the oppressed creature, the sentiment of a heartless world, as it is the spirit
of spiritless conditions. It is the opium of the people."
7. What is liberation theology and how is it related to Marxism? [8.3]
Liberation theology is a subset of Marxism tailored to Christians. It's a theological and
political movement that interprets the teachings of Jesus Christ as a call to liberate the poor.
[Liberation Theology is] an interpretation of Christian faith through the poor's
suffering, their struggle and hope, and a critique of society and the Catholic faith
and Christianity through the eyes of the poor. (Phillip Berryman)
Liberation theology has been called Christianized Marxism by some, but its Christian
adherents aren't atheists or materialists. They accept much of Marx's teachings but deny
they have to be atheists to embrace his critique of capitalism or that they have to accept
that the dialectic (the clash between the thesis and antithesis, producing a synthesis) is
an accurate description of how history operates.
8. What is Postmodernism's view of theology? [8.4]
Postmodernism doesn't start out with atheism, but it winds up there. Belief in God is
a socially constructed story of the world, one of many oppressive narratives. Since no
such story is true, it follows that no God exists.
Postmodernists are theologically suspicious about whether absolute truth exists, whether
there's a discernible foundation for knowledge, and whether any metanarratives are
actually true. The truth-claims of religion are just their unverifiable stories; they don't
accurately describe reality. Religion can be tolerated, but only as long as it's kept private.
In theology, as in everything else, most Postmodernists prefer ambiguity to certainty.
"Atheism is just as untenable as theism." (Gianni Vattimo)

9. What is wrong with the Postmodernist assertion that truth can't be objectively known?
[8.4]
By asserting that truth is unknowable, Postmodernists are claiming that this idea is true.
But how can they do that if truth is unknowable? This is a logical contradiction. It is
attempting to affirm a proposition while simultaneously denying it.
For religions to claim that their "truth" is universally true for everyone and not just for
themselves is seen by Postmodernists as an attempt to oppress others. But wouldn't that
make their insistence on the "truth" that truth is unknowable a form of oppression?

18. What are four scientific discoveries that point to the existence of God? [8.8]
Second Law of Thermodynamics: This law states, "although the total energy in the
cosmos remains constant, the amount of energy available to do useful work is always
getting smaller." The law assumes the universe is a closed system with nothing outside
it. The amount of useful energy is decreasing, therefore there must have been a time
when the energy clock began ticking. The universe had a beginning. And as the Kalam
cosmological argument clams, all beginnings have a cause. Therefore, the universe has
a cause we call God.
As one goes back in time, the amount of energy available for useful work would
eventually exceed the total energy in the universe, which, according to the First Law
of Thermodynamics, remains constant. This is an impossible condition, implying
that the universe had a beginning. (Walter Brown)
Spontaneous Generation: Spontaneous generation remains unproven. Modern experi-
ments don't support the idea that life somehow sprang into existence on its own through
chance and has managed through mutations and natural selection to organize itself and
became more complex over time, resulting in the variety of life we see today.
Because of the lack of irrefutable proof of how life started, many biology texts include
a short section on panspermia, the idea that life arrived from outer space. But how did
it get into space to begin with? This only pushes the question of origin back, it doesn't
answer it.
Genetic Information Theory: Recent genetics research reveals not only the presence
of information in DNA, but also indications of design. Francis Collins, who led the
Human Genome Project, says, DNA can be thought of metaphorically as the language
of God."
The most impressive arguments for God's existence are those that are supported
by recent scientific discoveries. ... The findings of more than fifty years of DNA
research have provided materials for a new and enormously powerful argument to
design. (Antony Flew)
Anthropic principle: The anthropic principle says we can observe the universe because it
exists in a way that allows us as observers to exist. The universe was deliberately designed
with properties that make the existence of intelligent life inevitable.
The anthropic principle is the most interesting development next to the proof of the
creation, and it is even more interesting because it seems to say that science itself
has proven, as a hard fact, that this universe was made, was designed, for man to
live in. It is a very theistic result. (Robert Jastrow)

A SELECTION FROM "WHY I AM NOT A CHRISTIAN"
by Bertrand Russell
The Moral Arguments for Deity
Now we reach one stage further in what I shall call the intellectual descent that the
Theists have made in their argumentations, and we come to what are called the moral
arguments for the existence of God. You all know, of course, that there used to be
in the old days three intellectual arguments for the existence of God, all of which
were disposed of by Immanuel Kant in the Critique of Pure Reason; but no sooner
had he disposed of those arguments than he invented a new one, a moral argument,
and that quite convinced him. He was like many people: in intellectual matters he
was skeptical, but in moral matters he believed implicitly in the maxims that he
had imbibed at his mother's knee. That illustrates what the psychoanalysts so much
emphasize-the immensely stronger hold upon us that our very early associations
have than those of later times.
Kant, as I say, invented a new moral argument for the existence of God, and that in
varying forms was extremely popular during the nineteenth century. It has all sorts
of forms. One form is to say that there would be no right and wrong unless God
existed. I am not for the moment concerned with whether there is a difference be-
tween right and wrong, or whether there is not: that is another question. The point
I am concerned with is that, if you are quite sure there is a difference between right
and wrong, then you are then in this situation: is that difference due to God's fiat or

1. What was the moral argument for God expounded by Immanuel Kant and why did
Russell reject it?
Immanuel Kant proposed that absolute right and wrong had to be based on an absolute
deity. Morality was universal because it extended from a good God's character and fiat
(word).
Russell's problem was that if you say "Cod is good, there has to be a standard of right
and wrong outside of God to which he conforms. Or if God is his own standard of right
and wrong, there is no difference to him between the two except his arbitrary choice.
The Bible sides with Kant on this. It teaches that moral absolutes are based on God's holy
character, not some higher standard of right and wrong. The difference between right
and wrong is based on what conforms to his holiness and what doesn't.
What was the remedying injustice argument for God and how did Russell refute it?
The concept of justice requires a day of reckoning when all the injustices in the universe
will be addressed. Throughout history, the righteous have suffered and the wicked have
prospered. This injustice and unfairness has to be righted in the end, which requires
an all-powerful judge. It also requires a heaven and hell as an eternal abode for the just
and unjust.
Russell believed the universe is just the way we find it; there's no basis for calling it un-
just" since there's no reason to expect there is a "just" standard to which reality has to
be aligned. This is wishful thinking projected onto the universe, not an objective reality
we can find anywhere outside ourselves.
The Bible presents God as the standard of justice and promises he will address all injustice
in the end and restore creation to a perfect state. The idea of justice exists because of the
idea of a just God and for no other reason.
3. According to Russell, why do most people believe in God?
Russell thought most people believed in God not because of superior moral or intellectual
arguments but simply because they had been taught to believe in him from infancy and
they hadn't outgrown this theistic worldview.
People also wanted a sense of safety that comes with having an all-powerful father or
big brother to watch out for them and make everything right in the end. Since people
wanted there to be a God, they invent reasons to believe in him, despite all the evidence
to the contrary.

What is "apologetics"?
Apologetics refers to content and methodologies that may be used by the Holy Spirit to
contribute toward the discipleship and mobilization of believers and the evangelism of
non-believers so that Christ is exalted and his kingdom is expanded.
Apologetics involves presenting, explaining, and defending the faith. It is a reasoned
defense of what we believe and why. The primary motive for apologetics is not the con-
version of people but the glory of God.
2. How do 1 Peter 3:15 and 1 Peter 2:15 relate to apologetics?
"But in your hearts revere Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to
everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with
gentleness and respect," (1 Peter 3:15).
"For it is God's will that by doing good you should silence the ignorant talk of foolish
people," (1 Peter 2:15).
First Peter 3:15 has to do with what we believe. First Peter 2:15 has to do with how we
behave. Both are important in apologetics. Words without consistent behavior are empty
and hypocritical, whereas a consistent life-witness gives power to the spoken word.

What are the Five Big Questions people ask? In what order would you prioritize them

outside and beyond themselves —an intelligent creator and a moral judge. These are known
as general revelation and are available to all mankind (Ecclesiastes 3:11; Romans 1:18-2:11).
