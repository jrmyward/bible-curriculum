## Unit Overview

**The Central Thesis:**  
Marxism didn't die when the Soviet Union collapsed — it evolved. Classical Marxism failed empirically (economically and politically), but Marxist *ideas* survived by "switching masks" through American Progressivism, the Frankfurt School, and finally Postmodernism. The core DNA remained constant: oppressor/oppressed dialectic, rejection of objective truth, and revolutionary liberation. Students *will* encounter these ideas repackaged as critical race theory, intersectionality, gender ideology, and "decolonize the curriculum" in college. Understanding the genealogy is essential.

**The Genealogy:**  
Marx (1848) → American Progressivism (1900s–1960s) → Frankfurt School (1930s–1960s) → Postmodernism (1960s–present) → Critical Theory in Practice (1980s–present)

## Week 1: Classical Marxism (The Foundation)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand Marxist theology (atheism, materialism)
- Explain dialectical materialism and historical determinism
- Identify the oppressor/oppressed framework (bourgeoisie vs. proletariat)
- Recognize why Marxism failed empirically but survived ideologically

### Key Content

**Marxist Theology:**
- Atheism: "Religion is the opiate of the masses" (Marx)
- Materialism: only matter exists, no soul, no God, no afterlife
- Contrast with Christianity: God exists, humans have immaterial souls, purpose beyond material world

**Marxist Philosophy:**
- Dialectical materialism: history moves through conflict (thesis → antithesis → synthesis)
- Historical determinism: economic forces drive history, not ideas or individuals
- Class struggle: all history is the history of class conflict (oppressor vs. oppressed)
- Contrast with Christianity: God directs history toward consummation, humans have agency and moral responsibility

**Marxist Economics:**
- Communism: abolition of private property, means of production controlled by the state
- Labor theory of value: capitalists exploit workers by extracting surplus value
- Revolution: workers must overthrow the bourgeoisie to establish a classless society
- Contrast with Christianity: private property is biblical (8th commandment), wealth is stewardship, work is dignified

**Why Marxism Failed (Empirically):**
- Soviet Union collapsed (1991)
- China adopted capitalism (1980s–present)
- Marxist economies produced poverty, not prosperity (Cuba, Venezuela, North Korea)
- Marxist politics produced tyranny, not liberation (gulags, purges, 100+ million deaths)

**Why Marxism Survived (Ideologically):**
- The oppressor/oppressed framework is emotionally compelling (identify the villain, liberate the victim)
- Marxist critique of "systems" (capitalism, patriarchy, etc.) persists even after Marxist solutions failed
- Marxism morphed into cultural Marxism, then postmodernism, then critical theory

### Discussion Brief #6
**Prompt:** "How does Marxism's view of history differ from Christianity's? Which view is more coherent? Why?"

### Activities
- Compare/contrast chart: Marxist vs. Christian views of theology, philosophy, economics
- Read excerpts from *Communist Manifesto* (Marx & Engels, 1848)
- Case study: Why did the Soviet Union collapse if Marxism is "scientifically inevitable"?

### Key Question for Students
"If Marxism failed economically and politically, why is it still influential in Western culture?"

## Week 2: American Progressivism (The Missing Link)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand how Marxist ideas entered American culture through Progressivism
- Identify the shift from biblical anthropology to progressive anthropology
- Explain Woodrow Wilson's rejection of natural law and the "living Constitution"
- Trace Margaret Sanger's role in the sexual revolution and rejection of biblical sexuality

### Key Content

**Woodrow Wilson (1913–1921): Progressive Political Theory**
- Rejected the Founders' view of fixed human nature and natural law
- Embraced Hegelian/Darwinian "living Constitution" — truth evolves, history progresses
- Expanded state power: "The state is the rational will of the people" (echoes Rousseau, Marx)
- Rejection of separation of powers: "The Constitution is outdated, we need expert administrators"
- **Key shift:** From biblical anthropology (fallen nature, limited government) to progressive anthropology (malleable nature, unlimited government)

**Margaret Sanger (1916–1966): Progressive Sexual Ethics**
- Founder of Planned Parenthood (1916)
- Eugenics advocate: "fit to breed" vs. "unfit to breed" (echoes Darwinian survival of the fittest)
- Sexual liberation as freedom from "oppressive" Christian sexual ethics
- Contraception and abortion as tools of liberation
- **Key shift:** From biblical sexuality (marriage, procreation, fidelity) to progressive sexuality (autonomy, contraception, abortion)
- **Marxist connection:** Sexual revolution as liberation from bourgeois family structure (Friedrich Engels: *The Origin of the Family, Private Property, and the State*, 1884)

**The Sexual Revolution (1960s): Cultural Marxism in Practice**
- Rejection of traditional sexual ethics (marriage, monogamy, binary gender)
- The Pill (1960), *Roe v. Wade* (1973), no-fault divorce (1970s), cohabitation
- "Free love" movement: sex without marriage, commitment, or procreation
- **Postmodern connection:** Sex and gender as social constructs, not biological realities (Judith Butler, Simone de Beauvoir)
- **Marxist connection:** Family as oppressive institution to be dismantled

**Progressive vs. Biblical Anthropology**

| Question | Biblical Anthropology | Progressive Anthropology |
|----------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| What is human nature? | Fixed, created in God's image, fallen | Malleable, evolving, perfectible |
| What is the problem? | Sin (rebellion against God) | Oppression (unjust systems) |
| What is the solution? | Redemption (Christ's atonement) | Liberation (revolution, reform) |
| What is the role of government? | Limited (humans are fallen, power corrupts) | Unlimited (experts can perfect society) |
| What is the purpose of sex? | Marriage, procreation, covenant faithfulness | Autonomy, pleasure, self-expression |

### Discussion Brief
**Prompt:** "How did American Progressivism (Wilson, Sanger) prepare the ground for postmodern sexual ethics (gender fluidity, abortion rights)? What's the worldview shift?"

### Activities
- Read excerpts from Woodrow Wilson's *The New Freedom* (1913)
- Read excerpts from Margaret Sanger's *Woman and the New Race* (1920)
- Debate: "The Constitution is a living document that must evolve / The Constitution is fixed and should be interpreted according to original intent"
- Case study: Trace the line from Margaret Sanger → Planned Parenthood → *Roe v. Wade* → contemporary abortion advocacy

### Key Question for Students
"How did Marxist ideas (liberation from oppression, rejection of fixed truth) enter American culture through Progressivism?"

## Week 3: The Frankfurt School & Critical Theory (The Bridge)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand the Frankfurt School's shift from economic Marxism to cultural Marxism
- Explain Gramsci's "cultural hegemony" and Marcuse's "repressive tolerance"
- Trace the sexual revolution to Marcuse and Wilhelm Reich
- Recognize "critical theory" as a method: expose and dismantle power structures

### Key Content

**Why the Frankfurt School Existed:**
- Classical Marxism predicted workers would revolt in industrialized nations (Germany, England, U.S.)
- Workers *didn't* revolt — they bought into capitalism (consumerism, middle class)
- Marxist intellectuals asked: "Why didn't the revolution happen? What went wrong?"
- Answer: "We need to win the *culture* first, then politics will follow" (Antonio Gramsci)

**Antonio Gramsci (1891–1937): Cultural Hegemony**
- Hegemony = cultural dominance (the ruling class controls ideas, not just economics)
- The church, family, and education perpetuate capitalist values
- To achieve Marxist revolution, you must first dismantle cultural institutions (church, family, traditional education)
- **Strategy:** "The long march through the institutions" (capture universities, media, schools, churches)

**Frankfurt School Thinkers (1930s–1960s):**
- Max Horkheimer & Theodor Adorno: *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (1947) — reason itself is oppressive
- Herbert Marcuse: *Eros and Civilization* (1955) — sexual repression is the foundation of capitalist oppression
- Wilhelm Reich: *The Sexual Revolution* (1936) — liberate sexuality to liberate society
- **Key shift:** From economic oppression (workers exploited by capitalists) to cultural oppression (traditional values oppress individuals)

**Herbert Marcuse & the Sexual Revolution:**
- Marcuse was the intellectual godfather of the 1960s counterculture
- "Make love, not war" = Marcuse's synthesis of sexual liberation and anti-capitalism
- Rejection of "repressive tolerance" — tolerance of traditional values perpetuates oppression
- Only by overthrowing traditional sexual ethics can society be truly free
- **Connection to contemporary culture:** Marcuse's ideas underpin LGBTQ+ activism, gender ideology, and sexual autonomy

**Critical Theory as Method:**
- Traditional theory: understand the world as it is
- Critical theory: expose how power structures oppress, then dismantle them
- No need to offer an alternative — just critique and deconstruct
- **Danger:** Critical theory is purely negative (it tears down but doesn't build up)

### Pair & Defend
**Proposition:** "Capitalism exploits workers and must be replaced (Marxism) / Capitalism creates prosperity and freedom, but must be tempered by biblical ethics (Christianity)"

### Activities
- Read excerpts from Herbert Marcuse's *Eros and Civilization*
- Watch: Interview clips with Herbert Marcuse (1960s)
- Debate: "Can you critique a system without offering an alternative?"
- Case study: How did the sexual revolution change American culture? (Marriage rates, divorce rates, out-of-wedlock births, STD rates, abortion rates)

### Key Question for Students
"Can you critique systems (capitalism, patriarchy, traditional family) without offering an alternative? Is criticism enough?"

## Week 4: Postmodernism (The Mask Change)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand postmodern philosophy (deconstruction, relativism, skepticism of grand narratives)
- Explain Foucault's power/knowledge framework and rejection of objective truth
- Identify the tension: postmodernism rejects metanarratives but smuggles in a Marxist liberation narrative
- Recognize how postmodernism borrowed Marxist oppressor/oppressed but rejected Marxist economics

### Key Content

**What is Postmodernism?**
- Reaction to modernism (Enlightenment, reason, science, progress)
- Rejects "metanarratives" (grand stories that claim universal truth): Christianity, Marxism, capitalism, science
- Rejects objective truth: "Truth is socially constructed, not discovered"
- Rejects objective morality: "Morality is power dynamics, not divine law"
- **Key claim:** "There is no objective truth" (but that's a truth claim — self-refuting)

**Michel Foucault (1926–1984): Power/Knowledge**
- All knowledge is a function of power ("Those in power decide what counts as truth")
- Truth claims are masks for oppression (science, religion, law = tools of control)
- Sexuality is performative, not biological (gender is socially constructed)
- **Marxist connection:** Oppressor/oppressed framework (those with power oppress those without)
- **Difference from Marx:** Marx believed in objective truth (historical materialism is scientifically true); Foucault rejects objective truth entirely

**Jacques Derrida (1930–2004): Deconstruction**
- Language doesn't refer to objective reality — meaning is unstable, context-dependent
- Deconstruction = exposing hidden power dynamics in texts
- No "correct" interpretation of any text (including the Bible, Constitution, etc.)
- **Danger:** If meaning is infinitely flexible, communication becomes impossible

**The Tension: Postmodernism's Inconsistency**
- Postmodernism claims: "There are no universal truths" (but that's a universal truth claim)
- Postmodernism claims: "Reject all metanarratives" (but smuggles in a Marxist liberation metanarrative)
- Postmodernism claims: "Power structures oppress" (but that requires objective moral standards to judge oppression)
- **Strategy for students:** Press postmodernists on their inconsistency ("If there's no objective truth, why should I believe your claim that power structures oppress?")

**How Postmodernism Borrowed from Marxism:**
- Oppressor/oppressed dialectic (bourgeoisie/proletariat → white/BIPOC, cis/trans, etc.)
- Rejection of traditional institutions (capitalism, family, church)
- Goal of liberation/revolution (overthrow systems of oppression)

**How Postmodernism Rejected Marxism:**
- Marx believed in objective truth (historical materialism is scientific)
- Postmodernism rejects objective truth (all truth is socially constructed)
- Marx believed in a universal metanarrative (history moves toward communist utopia)
- Postmodernism rejects metanarratives (no universal story)

### Discussion Brief #7
**Prompt:** "Can postmodernism's rejection of objective truth be coherently defended? Why or why not?"

### Activities
- Read excerpts from Michel Foucault's *Discipline and Punish*
- Read excerpts from Jacques Derrida's *Of Grammatology*
- Debate: "Truth is objective and knowable (Christianity) / Truth is socially constructed and relative (Postmodernism)"
- Case study: Analyze a postmodern claim ("Gender is a social construct") — what worldview assumptions underlie it?

### Key Question for Students
"If postmodernism rejects all metanarratives, why does it smuggle in a Marxist liberation narrative (oppressor/oppressed)?"

## Week 5: Modern Applications — Critical Theory in Practice

### Learning Objectives
- Trace critical race theory (CRT) to Marxist class struggle
- Understand intersectionality as Marxist oppression applied to multiple identity categories
- Explain equity vs. equality (Marxist "to each according to need" vs. biblical equal opportunity)
- Defend biblical justice against critical theory

### Key Content

**Critical Race Theory (CRT): Class Struggle → Race Struggle**
- Origins: Derrick Bell, Kimberlé Crenshaw, Richard Delgado (1980s–1990s)
- Core claim: Racism is not individual prejudice, but systemic oppression embedded in laws, institutions, culture
- Oppressor/oppressed: white people (oppressor) vs. BIPOC (oppressed)
- Solution: Dismantle "whiteness" and rebuild institutions around equity (not equality)
- **Marxist DNA:** Replace "bourgeoisie" with "white people," replace "proletariat" with "BIPOC" — same dialectic

**Intersectionality: Multidimensional Oppression**
- Kimberlé Crenshaw (1989): Oppression is not one-dimensional (race), but multidimensional (race + gender + sexuality + class + ability)
- Oppression is cumulative: a Black trans woman faces more oppression than a white gay man
- Privilege = inverse of oppression (white, male, cisgender, straight, wealthy = maximum privilege)
- **Marxist DNA:** Oppressor/oppressed framework applied to identity categories (not just economics)

**Equity vs. Equality**

| Concept | Equality | Equity |
|---------|----------|--------|
| Definition | Equal opportunity (same starting line) | Equal outcomes (same finish line) |
| Method | Remove barriers, allow merit to determine outcomes | Redistribute resources to ensure equal results |
| Biblical? | Yes (Lev 19:15 — "no partiality") | No (violates stewardship, rewards sloth) |
| Marxist? | No (bourgeoisie and proletariat don't have equal opportunity under capitalism) | Yes ("From each according to ability, to each according to need") |

**Gender Ideology: Foucault's Legacy**
- Gender as social construct (not biological reality)
- Sex assigned at birth (not observed)
- Gender fluidity (man, woman, non-binary, genderqueer, etc.)
- Rejection of binary (male/female) as oppressive
- **Marxist DNA:** Liberation from "oppressive" gender norms (echoes sexual revolution)
- **Postmodern DNA:** Gender is performative, not essential (Judith Butler, *Gender Trouble*, 1990)

**"Decolonize the Curriculum"**
- Claim: Western education perpetuates white supremacy and colonialism
- Solution: Center marginalized voices, dismantle "Eurocentric" knowledge
- **Marxist DNA:** Gramsci's cultural hegemony (ruling class controls education)
- **Postmodern DNA:** Rejection of objective truth (Western science is just one perspective, not universal)

**Biblical Justice vs. Critical Theory Justice**

| Question | Biblical Justice | Critical Theory Justice |
|----------|------------------|-------------------------|
| What is the problem? | Sin (individual rebellion against God) | Systemic oppression (unjust power structures) |
| What is the solution? | Repentance and redemption (individual transformation) | Revolution and equity (systemic transformation) |
| What is justice? | Impartiality (Lev 19:15), restitution, proportionality | Equity (equal outcomes), reparations, redistribution |
| Who is responsible? | Individuals (we all sin, we all need redemption) | Groups (oppressors vs. oppressed, collective guilt) |
| What is the goal? | Reconciliation (vertical with God, horizontal with others) | Liberation (overthrow oppressors, empower oppressed) |

### Pair & Defend
**Proposition:** "Systemic injustice requires systemic solutions (equity, reparations, redistribution) / Individual sin requires individual repentance (equality, restitution, transformation)"

### Case Study
**Prompt:** Analyze a "decolonize the curriculum" proposal or "anti-racist" training document. Identify the Marxist and postmodern worldview assumptions. How would a biblical worldview respond?

### Activities
- Read excerpts from *Critical Race Theory: An Introduction* (Delgado & Stefancic)
- Read excerpts from Kimberlé Crenshaw's "Mapping the Margins" (intersectionality)
- Watch: Clips from DEI trainings or "anti-racist" seminars
- Debate: "Racism is systemic (CRT) / Racism is individual sin (Christianity)"

### Capstone #3 Assignment
**Prompt:** "Defend the biblical view of truth and justice against critical theory. Choose one of the following:
1. Defend objective truth against postmodern relativism (Foucault, Derrida)
2. Defend biblical justice (impartiality, individual responsibility) against critical race theory (equity, systemic oppression)
3. Defend biblical sexuality (binary gender, marriage) against gender ideology (fluidity, social construction)

Engage with at least one critical theorist (Foucault, Derrida, Marcuse, Crenshaw, Butler) and demonstrate how the biblical worldview is more coherent."

**Format:** 5–7 minute presentation + Q&A

## Unit Summary: The Genealogy Revealed

By the end of this unit, students should be able to trace the following genealogy:

1. **Marx (1848):** Oppressor (bourgeoisie) vs. oppressed (proletariat) → economic revolution
2. **American Progressivism (1900s–1960s):** Wilson (living Constitution, unlimited state) + Sanger (sexual liberation) → cultural shift
3. **Frankfurt School (1930s–1960s):** Gramsci (cultural hegemony) + Marcuse (sexual revolution) → cultural Marxism
4. **Postmodernism (1960s–present):** Foucault (power/knowledge) + Derrida (deconstruction) → rejection of objective truth
5. **Critical Theory (1980s–present):** CRT (race struggle) + intersectionality (multidimensional oppression) + gender ideology (sex as social construct) → contemporary social justice movements

**The Core DNA (unchanged from Marx to CRT):**
- Oppressor/oppressed dialectic
- Rejection of objective truth (or truth as tool of oppression)
- Goal of liberation through revolution (economic, cultural, or systemic)

**Why This Matters:**
Students will encounter critical theory in college (required DEI trainings, "anti-racist" curriculum, gender ideology). If they don't understand the Marxist → postmodern genealogy, they'll be confused and vulnerable. If they *do* understand the genealogy, they can say: "That's not a new idea — that's Marxism in postmodern clothing."
