## Priority Levels

## Unit-by-Unit Priorities

### Unit 1: Christianity (Biblical Theism)
**Priority:** **CORE**  
**Reasoning:** Christianity is the baseline for all comparisons. Students must have a thorough understanding of the biblical worldview across all 10 disciplines (theology, philosophy, ethics, biology, psychology, sociology, law, politics, economics, history) before engaging with competing worldviews. This is the foundation — do not condense.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- All 10 worldview questions answered from a Christian perspective
- Discussion Brief #1 (How does the existence of God shape reality?)
- Pair & Defend #1 (morality grounded in God vs. independent of God)
- Case Study #1 (applying biblical worldview to policy issue)

**Can Condense:**
- Nothing. This unit sets the template for all other worldviews. Cover it fully.

### Unit 2: Mormonism (LDS)
**Priority:** **CORE** (SE Idaho context)  
**Reasoning:** In SE Idaho, students regularly encounter LDS missionaries and peers who claim Mormonism is Christian. Students must be able to articulate the theological differences between Christian orthodoxy and LDS teaching. This is not optional in this region — it's essential apologetics training.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- LDS theology vs. biblical Christianity (key differences: nature of God, Christology, soteriology)
- Discussion Brief #2 (How to respond to "We're Christians too")
- Pair & Defend #2 (LDS as Christian denomination vs. distinct religion)

**Can Condense:**
- Reduce from 2 weeks to 1 week: cover origins in 1 day, theology in 1 day, Pair & Defend in 1 day
- Skip detailed history of Joseph Smith and LDS church growth — focus only on theological differences

**Cannot Skip:**
- This unit is required for SE Idaho context. Do not skip.

### Unit 3: Islam
**Priority:** **CORE**  
**Reasoning:** Islam is the second-largest religion in the world and the fastest-growing. Students will encounter Muslim friends, coworkers, and neighbors. Understanding Islamic theology, law (Sharia), and politics is essential for cultural engagement and apologetics. This is a major worldview — do not skip.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Islamic theology (Allah, Muhammad, Quran)
- Islamic law (Sharia) and politics (theocracy vs. democracy)
- Discussion Brief #3 (God of Islam vs. God of Christianity)
- Pair & Defend #3 (Sharia compatible with Western democracy or not)
- Case Study #2 (responding to Islamic apologetics or evangelizing a Muslim)
- Capstone #1 (Christianity vs. Islam comparison)

**Can Condense:**
- Islamic history (early caliphates, spread of Islam) — provide a handout or short video instead of full class discussion
- Modern sectarian divisions (Sunni vs. Shia) — acknowledge but don't dive deep

**Can Skip:**
- Extended discussion of Islamic contributions to science/culture during the Middle Ages — interesting but not essential for worldview comparison

### Unit 4: Buddhism & Hinduism
**Priority:** **CORE** (condensed to 1 week)  
**Reasoning:** Eastern religions represent the predominant worldview of ~2 billion people globally. Buddhism and Hinduism offer fundamentally different metaphysics (pantheism/atheism vs. theism) and frameworks for understanding suffering, salvation, and human purpose (karma/reincarnation vs. sin/redemption). Students cannot claim to understand world religions without covering these worldviews. However, 1 week is sufficient for essential coverage.

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Core concepts: karma, samsara, moksha/nirvana
- Buddhism: Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path
- Hinduism: Brahman, caste system
- Discussion Brief #4 (Buddhism's diagnosis of the human condition vs. Christianity's)
- Pair & Defend #4 (suffering is central to life vs. an intrusion)

**Can Condense:**
- Reduce from 2 weeks to 1 week: cover Buddhism in 1–2 days, Hinduism in 1–2 days, Pair & Defend in 1 day
- Skip detailed history of the Buddha and Hindu scriptures — focus only on worldview comparison
- Combine Case Study #3 (sharing gospel with Buddhist/Hindu) with another case study or skip

**Cannot Skip:**
- This unit is required. 2 billion people hold these worldviews — they must be covered.

Understanding secular theology (atheism), philosophy (materialism), ethics (utilitarianism), and biology (Darwinian evolution) is absolutely essential. This is a major worldview — do not skip or condense.

**Non-Negotiables:**

- Pair & Defend #5 (Morality requires God vs. can exist without God)
- Capstone #2 (Defend biblical worldview against secular objection)

### Unit 6: Marxism → Postmodernism (Combined: The Evolution of Critical Theory)
**Priority:** **CORE** (5 weeks; can condense to 4 weeks)  
**Reasoning:** Marxism didn't die—it evolved. Classical Marxism failed empirically (Soviet collapse, China went capitalist), but Marxist *ideas* survived by "switching masks" through American Progressivism, the Frankfurt School, and finally postmodernism. The core DNA remained: oppressor/oppressed dialectic, rejection of objective truth, and revolutionary liberation. Students *will* encounter these ideas repackaged as critical race theory, intersectionality, gender ideology, and "decolonize the curriculum" in college. Understanding the full genealogy from Marx → American Progressivism (Wilson, Sanger) → Frankfurt School (Marcuse) → Postmodernism (Foucault/Derrida) → Critical Theory (CRT, intersectionality) is essential. This combined unit reveals the throughline and fills gaps left by the *Understanding the Times* textbook.

**Week 1: Classical Marxism (The Foundation)**
- Marxist theology (atheism, religion as opiate)
- Marxist philosophy (dialectical materialism, historical determinism, class struggle)
- Marxist economics (communism, abolition of private property)
- Why Marxism failed empirically but survived ideologically
- **Key question:** "If Marxism failed economically, why is it still influential?"

**Week 2: American Progressivism (The Missing Link)**
- Woodrow Wilson: rejection of natural law, "living Constitution," expansion of state power
- Margaret Sanger: eugenics, sexual liberation, Planned Parenthood
- Progressive vs. biblical anthropology (malleable nature vs. fallen nature)
- How Marxist ideas entered American culture through Progressivism
- **Key question:** "How did American Progressivism prepare the ground for postmodern sexual ethics?"

**Week 3: The Frankfurt School & Critical Theory (The Bridge)**
- Gramsci's cultural hegemony ("long march through the institutions")
- Adorno/Marcuse: winning culture before politics
- Marcuse and the sexual revolution (1960s)
- Introduction to "critical theory" as a method (expose and dismantle power)
- **Key question:** "Can you critique systems without offering an alternative?"

**Week 4: Postmodernism (The Mask Change)**
- Foucault: power/knowledge, truth as social construct, sexuality as performance
- Derrida: deconstruction, no objective meaning
- How postmodernism *borrowed* Marxist oppressor/oppressed but *rejected* Marxist metanarrative
- **Key tension:** Postmodernism is philosophically inconsistent (rejects metanarratives but pushes liberation narrative)

**Week 5: Modern Applications (Critical Theory in Practice)**
- Critical race theory (class struggle → race struggle)
- Intersectionality (multidimensional oppression)
- Gender ideology (sex as social construct, Foucault's legacy)
- Equity vs. equality (Marxist "to each according to need" vs. biblical impartiality)
- **Case Study:** Analyze a "decolonize the curriculum" or "anti-racist" proposal through biblical vs. Marxist/postmodern lens
- **Capstone #3:** Defend biblical view of justice/truth against critical theory

**Non-Negotiables:**
- Marxist theology, philosophy, economics (Week 1)
- Critical theory as bridge (Week 2)
- Postmodern philosophy (deconstruction, relativism) (Week 3)
- Modern applications: CRT, intersectionality, gender ideology (Week 4)
- Discussion Brief, Pair & Defend, Capstone #3 (Week 5)

**Can Condense:**
- Reduce from 5 weeks to 4 weeks: combine Week 2 (Frankfurt School) with Week 1 (Classical Marxism)
- Skip detailed history of Marx, Derrida, Foucault — focus on *ideas*, not biography
- Marxism in practice (Soviet Union, China, gulags) — brief overview only
- Postmodern theology (God as social construct) — summarize in 1 day

**Cannot Skip:**
- This combined unit is essential. Students must understand the Marxist → postmodern genealogy to recognize critical theory in contemporary culture.

**Non-Negotiables:**

- Debate tournament (students defend assigned worldviews on abortion, justice, meaning of life)

## Final Notes

1. **Monitor your pacing after each unit.** If you're more than 3 days behind, start condensing using the Emergency Condensing Plan above.

2. **Prioritize depth over breadth.** It's better to cover 7 worldviews deeply (with thorough comparison and synthesis) than to rush through superficially. However, all 7 are essential — none can be skipped.

3. **The textbook covers many more worldviews** (New Spirituality, etc.). Focus on the 7 listed in this teaching map — they're the most culturally relevant for high school seniors heading to college in SE Idaho.

4. **Communicate with students.** If you need to condense material, explain why. For example, if condensing Marxism → Postmodernism from 5 weeks to 4 weeks, explain that you're focusing on the genealogy rather than biographical details.

5. **Adjust based on class needs.** If your students are particularly engaged with a topic (e.g., Marxism → Postmodernism, Islam), it's worth spending extra time even if it means condensing elsewhere (e.g., Contemporary Issues).

6. **Save the final 2 weeks (May 10–21) as buffer time.** Use these for final capstone presentations, exam review, and reflection. Don't plan new content for these weeks.

7. **The worldview comparison chart (Unit 7) is non-negotiable.** This is where students consolidate everything they've learned. All 7 worldviews must be included in the comparison chart.
