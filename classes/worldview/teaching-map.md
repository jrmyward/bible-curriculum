## Design Philosophy

This course uses Myers & Noebel's 10-discipline analytical framework as its spine but **restructures the delivery from discipline-first to worldview-first.**

Myers & Noebel organize their book in two passes: Part 1 introduces each worldview in isolation (Ch 1–7); Part 2 compares all worldviews on each of the 10 disciplines (Ch 8–17). That two-pass structure is correct, but the publisher's *order* (worldview chapters first, then discipline chapters) is a poor fit for a high schooler's first encounter — discipline-comparative chapters assume schema students don't yet have. This course preserves the two-pass structure but reorders it for cognitive load: **Pass 1** is worldview-first instruction (Units 1–9), letting students build coherent mental models of one worldview at a time through 10 lenses; **Pass 2** is discipline-comparative instruction during three "Discipline Pass" periods at trimester boundaries (T1 Discipline Pass, T2 Discipline Pass, and T3 Finals), where the rows of the grid get walked across deliberately at increasing breadth. Pass 2 is real teaching, not review — the publisher devotes 100 days of their 180-day course to it; we devote 49.

Two assessment commitments anchor the design to the publisher's curriculum: each per-unit chapter test on Day 9 is a **blended test** combining the publisher's matching worldview-comprehensive Ch X test with that worldview's relevant questions extracted from Ch 8–17. And the publisher's **Ch 18 Test functions as the dual-credit written final**, administered in T3 finals.

The worldview-first delivery also enables what is otherwise hard: **a per-worldview Gospel conversation.** Each unit ends with a roleplayed conversation tailored to the language, longings, and objections of that worldview's adherents. The textbook is apologetic; this course is also evangelistic.

**The outcome we're building toward:** by May, students should be able to sit across a coffee shop table from a thoughtful proponent of any of these 8 worldviews, represent that person's view back to them in a way they would recognize and affirm as fair, and present the Gospel in a way that is meaningful and impactful to *that person specifically* — not a generic tract.

## The 10 Analytical Lenses

Every worldview unit examines the worldview through all 10 of these disciplines. The Christianity unit establishes the baseline answers. Every subsequent unit compares back to that baseline through Day 7's "Four-Test + vs. Christianity" exercise.

1. **Theology** — What is God (or ultimate reality)?
2. **Philosophy** — What is real? How do we know?
3. **Ethics** — What is good? On what basis?
4. **Biology** — What is life, and where did it come from?
5. **Psychology** — What is the self? What makes a person whole or broken?
6. **Sociology** — How should people live together?
7. **Law** — What is justice, and where does authority come from?
8. **Politics** — What is the role of the state?
9. **Economics** — What is wealth, and how should it be stewarded?
10. **History** — Where is history going? What is its meaning?

See [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md) for the full 8 × 10 reference matrix. Students see the grid on Day 1 of Unit 0 and return to it throughout the year.

## The Four-Test Framework

Myers & Noebel's evaluative framework, taught explicitly in Unit 0 and applied in Day 7 of every worldview unit:

1. **Reason** — Is the worldview internally coherent? Does it contradict itself?
2. **Outer World** — Does the worldview match what we observe in nature, history, and human experience?
3. **Inner World** — Does the worldview match our deepest moral, aesthetic, and relational intuitions?
4. **Real-World Consequences** — When lived out at scale, what fruit does the worldview bear?

The four tests are the apologetic spine of the course's "defend the faith" goal. Every worldview gets pressure-tested through them on Day 7 against the Christian baseline.

## Standing Commitments Across Every Unit

Four commitments run continuously, independent of the unit rhythm:

1. **Per-worldview reading packets** replace cross-chapter textbook navigation. Each unit has a stapled packet (~10–20 pages) containing the worldview's Myers/Noebel chapter or excerpt, the relevant slices of Ch 8–17 in lens order, primary-source readings, and a blank slice of the worldview grid. Each packet also includes a "Publisher Vocabulary" terminology section pulling every defined term from the matching chapter test, so students prep for the dual-credit final's recall format every week. Students never "find" content in the textbook during a worldview unit. (See [teaching-methodology.md](teaching-methodology.md) for the rationale and prep cost.)
2. **Weekly retrieval quizzes** every Friday starting Week 4: 5 cells from the worldview grid drawn from any unit already covered. Cumulative across the year. By May, the quizzes test the full 8 × 10 grid in random-access fashion — exactly what the dual-credit final demands.
3. **Adopt the publisher's assessment infrastructure for chapters it covers.** For Units 0, 1, 3, 5–9 (which map to Myers Ch 1, 2, 3, 6, 4, 5, 7, plus the Ch 8–17 discipline-comparative chapters), use the publisher's chapter tests, writing assignments (Doug Letters), and supplemental quizzes directly. Build only what the publisher doesn't cover: 4 Doug Letters and 4 chapter tests for Units 2 (Judaism), 4 (Mormonism), and the distinct-treatment portions of 5 (Hinduism) and 6 (Buddhism). Built-from-scratch materials use the publisher's format and tone as templates.
4. **Charitable representation** is the curricular norm. Every Pair & Defend assigns positions students don't personally hold. Every Day 1 framing presents the worldview in its strongest form. The Capstone rubric weights charity #1. *Note:* The publisher's tone is more polemical than ours; charitable framing is *additive*, not *replacement*. Day 7's Four-Test + vs. Christianity exercise is where students learn to switch into the publisher's clarity register for the test.

## Unit 1 — Christianity

**Dates:** Sep 9 – Sep 28, 2026 (13 instructional days — baseline depth)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 2
**Framing Question:** *What is the Christian answer to each of the 10 disciplines, and why?*

### Purpose

Establish the Christian worldview as the baseline answer to each of the 10 disciplines. Every subsequent unit compares back to this. Students finish Unit 1 able to articulate Christianity's answer to every discipline in one clear sentence with at least one Scripture anchor.

This unit is the longest (13 days instead of 9) because students need to know the baseline *well* before they can meaningfully compare anything to it.

### Key Theological Anchors

- **Trinitarian Monotheism** — one God eternally existing in three persons (Father, Son, Holy Spirit)
- **Imago Dei** — every person is made in God's image and has inviolable dignity (Gen 1:26–27)
- **Special Creation** — the world and life are created by God, not self-generated
- **Natural Law** — moral reality is built into creation and knowable by reason
- **Redemptive Narrative** — Creation → Fall → Redemption → Restoration (four-act story)
- **Sphere Sovereignty** — God-ordained spheres (family, church, state) have distinct, non-overlapping authority
- **Agape Love** — the ethic of self-giving love grounded in God's own character
- **Biblical Stewardship** — we steward what God owns; we do not own outright

## Unit 2 — Judaism

**Dates:** Sep 29 – Oct 12, 2026 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** **Not covered.** Build from primary sources.
**Framing Question:** *What is Judaism on its own terms — across four stages of reinterpretation — and how does it answer its own central questions about covenant, atonement, and the meaning of history?*

### Purpose

Treat Judaism as the theological arc it actually is, not a static category. Modern Rabbinic Judaism is not a faltering remnant of Temple Judaism — it is a self-contained 2,000-year-old system with its own robust theology of atonement (teshuvah, tefilah, tzedakah; the Yom Kippur liturgy; suffering as expiation in some traditions). Students learn that system on its own terms first. The Christian apologetic conversation — Jesus as the fulfillment of the sacrificial system — arrives explicitly on Day 8 (Gospel Conversation), not in the unit's framing.

### Key Anchors — The Four Stages

1. **Covenantal / Patriarchal Judaism** (Abraham–Moses) — the covenant framework; God chooses a people.
2. **Temple / Sacrificial Judaism** (Moses–70 AD) — the Levitical system as the divinely-prescribed mechanism for atonement. Blood sacrifice as the means by which sin is covered.
3. **The Rupture of 70 AD** — the Temple is destroyed. The Levitical sacrificial system ends permanently. The Babylonian Talmud (Yoma 39b) records that the scarlet cord stopped turning white for 40 years before the destruction — a sign, in rabbinic memory, that atonement was no longer being received. *The theological question students will wrestle with: if sacrifice was God's prescribed mechanism, what does its cessation mean?*
4. **Rabbinic Judaism** (70 AD – present) — the reconstruction. Prayer, Torah study, and acts of lovingkindness as substitutes for sacrifice, grounded in Hosea 6:6 (*"For I desired mercy, and not sacrifice"*). Then the spectrum: Reform, Conservative, Orthodox, Messianic.

### Gospel Conversation Focus

The question of atonement. Judaism's own scriptures (Leviticus, the Prophets) establish that sin requires blood sacrifice for forgiveness; after 70 AD, that mechanism ended. Rabbinic Judaism answered the rupture from inside the tradition (Hosea 6:6, the three pillars). Christianity's claim is that Jesus completes what the Temple system was a shadow of. Teach students to make that case *from the Jewish scriptures themselves*, not as an outside critique. Tone matters enormously — Jewish students and Jewish friends have heard anti-Semitic versions of this argument their whole lives. Christian supersessionism at its worst has blood on its hands. Students must learn to make the theological point without the historical cruelty, and they must be able to represent the Jewish response (Rabbinic Judaism's own atonement theology) fairly before they advance the Christian one.

## Unit 3 — Islam

**Dates:** Oct 13 – Oct 26, 2026 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 3
**Framing Question:** *How does the Muslim understanding of God and prophet-hood differ from the Christian understanding — and how do we talk about Jesus across that difference?*

### Purpose

Engage Islam as a major world religion (1.8+ billion adherents) on its own terms. Charitable representation is essential — students must be able to articulate Islamic theology in a way a Muslim scholar would recognize as fair. This unit also establishes the **angel-prophet-new-scripture revelation pattern** that will reappear in Unit 4 (Mormonism). Teach the pattern carefully here; Mormonism reuses the same template, and the apologetic move is the same.

### Key Theological Anchors

- **Umma** — the global Muslim community; Islam is corporate before it is individual.

Jesus is the entry point, not the wall. Islam has more positive theology about Jesus than most students realize. Treat Mormonism (LDS) as a distinct theological system, not a variant of Christianity. The critical teaching point: **Mormon vocabulary overlaps with Christian vocabulary but refers to categorically different things.** Students must learn to recognize and name the shift.

## Unit 5 — Hinduism

**Dates:** Dec 3 – Dec 15, 2026 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 6 (partial — acknowledge limitation)
**Framing Question:** *What is the self, and what is it for?*

### Purpose

Treat Hinduism as the sophisticated, 3,000-year-old philosophical and religious tradition it is — not as "many gods and reincarnation." The philosophical depth (Vedanta, Advaita, the Brahman–Atman relation) requires genuine engagement. This is the first unit where Myers/Noebel's "New Spirituality" category is explicitly named as a teaching limitation: tell students that the textbook collapses Hinduism, Buddhism, and Western syncretism into a single category and that this course refuses that move. Unit 7 will handle the genuine scope of the textbook's "New Spirituality" (Tolle, Chopra, Byrne).

### Key Anchors

- **Brahman** — ultimate reality; the one true being from which all appearance derives
- **Atman** — the self; in Advaita Vedanta, ultimately identical with Brahman
- **Maya** — illusion; the appearance of separation and multiplicity that conceals the unity of Brahman
- **Dharma** — duty; the right action for one's caste (*varna*) and stage of life (*ashrama*)
- **Karma** — moral causation; actions in this life determine circumstances in the next
- **Samsara** — the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth
- **Moksha** — liberation; release from samsara through realization of Atman-Brahman unity
- **Three paths** — knowledge (*jnana*), devotion (*bhakti*), action (*karma yoga*)
- **Key texts** — Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita

### Day-by-Day Schedule

| Day | Date | Activity |
|-----|------|----------|
| 1 | Thu Dec 3 | Framing. Why Hinduism matters. Why "many gods and reincarnation" is not enough. The Myers "New Spirituality" category — teach students to recognize and name inadequate categorization. |
| 2 | Fri Dec 4 | Deep Dive Pt 1 (Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Biology, Psychology). Brahman, Atman, Maya; dharma, karma, the cycle of rebirth; the self as ultimately illusory. Read excerpt from the Chandogya Upanishad ("*Tat tvam asi* — thou art that") and the Bhagavad Gita Ch 2. |
| 3 | Mon Dec 7 | Deep Dive Pt 2 (Sociology, Law, Politics, Economics, History). The caste system and its theological justification. Yuga cycles. The four purusharthas. Dharmic vocation. |
| 4 | Tue Dec 8 | Socratic Seminar. Student questions. Likely tensions: how do Christians evaluate a system that offers a compelling account of suffering (karma) that is morally coherent on its own terms? |
| 5 | Wed Dec 9 | Pair & Defend (two-round both-sides): *"Karma is a more morally satisfying explanation of suffering than the Christian answer"* vs. *"Karma puts moral weight on victims in a way Christianity does not."* |
| 6 | Thu Dec 10 | Case Study: A Hindu friend loses his mother. In his worldview, her death is explained by her karma. How does this frame suffering differently than Christianity does? What does each worldview offer the grieving person? |
| 7 | Fri Dec 11 | Four-Test + vs. Christianity. Particular attention: the inner-world test on Atman-Brahman unity (does it match the intuition that love requires distinct persons?); the consequences test on the caste system's lived fruit. |
| 8 | Mon Dec 14 | Gospel Conversation. The question of the self is the hinge. Hinduism offers dissolution of the self into Brahman; Christianity offers the resurrection of the self into glorified personhood. Sin, grace, and personal love are categorically different frames. Roleplay. |
| 9 | Tue Dec 15 | Worldview Brief + Unit 5 Assessment. Bridge to Unit 6: another Indian tradition that *departs* from Hinduism on exactly the question of the self — Buddhism. |

### Gospel Conversation Focus

The self. Hinduism offers ultimate dissolution of the individual self into the impersonal Brahman; Christianity offers the ultimate preservation and glorification of the individual self in relationship with a personal God. The Gospel's appeal to the Hindu worldview is often grief — the loss of someone we love. Hinduism's answer (they've been reborn; the karmic cycle continues) does not satisfy the intuition that the *specific* person mattered eternally. Christianity says: yes, this specific person, resurrected bodily, known by name. That is a very different offer.

## Unit 6 — Buddhism

**Dates:** Dec 16, 2026 – Jan 12, 2027 (9 instructional days; spans Christmas break)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 6 (partial — acknowledge limitation)
**Framing Question:** *What is suffering, and what would it mean to be free of it?*

### Purpose

Engage Buddhism as a serious philosophical and religious tradition, distinct from Hinduism. The philosophical claims (no-self, dependent origination, impermanence) are genuinely different from Hinduism's Brahman-Atman metaphysics. Do not reduce Buddhism to "mindfulness and meditation" — that is the Western retail version, which Unit 7 will name and critique.

### Key Anchors

- **The Four Noble Truths** — (1) life is suffering (*dukkha*), (2) suffering is caused by craving/attachment, (3) suffering can cease, (4) the Eightfold Path leads to cessation
- **The Eightfold Path** — right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration
- **Three Marks of Existence** — *dukkha* (suffering), *anicca* (impermanence), *anatta* (no-self)
- **Nirvana** — cessation; the extinguishing of craving and thereby of suffering
- **Dependent Origination** — nothing has independent existence; all things arise in causal relation
- **Schools** — Theravada (the "Way of the Elders"), Mahayana (the "Great Vehicle"), Vajrayana (Tibetan)

### Core Philosophical Tension with Christianity

**The self.** Buddhism teaches *anatta* — the self is an illusion to be seen through and ultimately dissolved. Christianity teaches that every person is a uniquely created image-bearer of God with eternal significance. This is not a peripheral disagreement; it is categorical.

### Day-by-Day Schedule

Christmas break (Dec 21 – Jan 1) falls after Day 3. The pre-break days are framing + the two Deep Dives so students return from break with the conceptual scaffolding already in place; post-break days are discussion, application, and synthesis.

| Day | Date | Activity |
|-----|------|----------|
| 1 | Wed Dec 16 | Framing. The life of Siddhartha Gautama. Why Buddhism emerged from Hinduism but departs from it. |
| 2 | Thu Dec 17 | Deep Dive Pt 1 (Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Biology, Psychology). The Four Noble Truths; the Three Marks of Existence (*dukkha*, *anicca*, *anatta*); how *anatta* differs from Hindu Atman. Primary source: selections from the *Dhammapada* and the Buddha's first sermon (*Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta*). |
| 3 | Fri Dec 18 | Deep Dive Pt 2 (Sociology, Law, Politics, Economics, History). The Eightfold Path. Theravada, Mahayana, Vajrayana. The Sangha. Right Livelihood. Dependent origination as a metaphysics of history. |
| — | Dec 21 – Jan 1 | **Christmas Break — no school.** *(Mon Jan 4 = Teacher Work Day.)* |
| 4 | Tue Jan 5 | Socratic Seminar. Student questions. Likely tension: the Christian claim that suffering comes from *sin* vs. the Buddhist claim that suffering comes from *attachment*. |
| 5 | Wed Jan 6 | Pair & Defend (two-round both-sides): *"Buddhist non-attachment is wiser than Christian longing"* vs. *"Christian longing recognizes the goodness of what we love; Buddhism requires us to deny it."* |
| 6 | Thu Jan 7 | Case Study: A Buddhist friend dies. Her Buddhist community does not mourn in the way Christians mourn — attachment is what Buddhism is escaping from. How does each worldview honor the dead? What does each say to the grieving? |
| 7 | Fri Jan 8 | Four-Test + vs. Christianity. Particular attention: the inner-world test on *anatta* (does the no-self doctrine match the intuition that *I* am the one suffering?); the reason test on the dependent-origination chain. |
| 8 | Mon Jan 11 | Gospel Conversation. The Buddhist diagnosis of suffering is partly right — we do suffer from attachment, from craving. The Christian response: yes, but the answer is not to extinguish desire, it is to *redirect* desire toward its proper end (God). The Gospel does not dissolve the self; it heals and fulfills the self. Roleplay. |
| 9 | Tue Jan 12 | Worldview Brief + Unit 6 Assessment. Bridge to Unit 7: now that we've taken Hinduism and Buddhism on their own terms, what happens when their categories get blender'd into Western self-help? |

### Gospel Conversation Focus

Desire and suffering. Buddhism and Christianity both take suffering with utter seriousness — but they diagnose it differently and prescribe categorically different solutions. Buddhism: suffering → attachment → extinguish attachment. Christianity: suffering → sin and separation from God → reunion with God through Christ, with desire redeemed rather than extinguished. The Buddhist convert to Christianity is often surprised that Christianity *affirms* desire (properly ordered) rather than denies it — and that Christianity preserves the *self* rather than dissolves it. Both moves are counterintuitive if you've internalized the Buddhist frame.

## Unit 7 — New Spirituality

**Dates:** Jan 13 – Jan 21, 2027 (5 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 6 (proper scope — Western syncretism)
**Framing Question:** *What happens when Eastern categories get blender'd into Western self-help?*

### Purpose

Now that students have engaged Hinduism and Buddhism on their own terms, they're equipped to recognize **what New Spirituality actually is**: a Western syncretism that borrows vocabulary from Eastern traditions while stripping them of their philosophical depth, communal structure, and ascetic demands. The wellness/manifestation industry is the lived face of this worldview. Tolle, Chopra, Byrne, and the broader self-help canon are the named voices.

This is not a 9-day unit because the heavy lifting was done in Units 5–6. Five days is enough to name the syncretism, distinguish it from its sources, and equip students for the conversations it actually produces — which are not "should I become a Buddhist monk?" but "should I trust my manifestation journal?" and "isn't God just the universe, and aren't we all one?"

### Key Anchors

- **Pantheism / Panentheism (popularized)** — God is the universe; the universe is God; we are sparks of the divine
- **Self-divinization** — the self is fundamentally good, divine, and the source of its own truth
- **Manifestation / Law of Attraction** — thoughts are causal forces in physical reality; positive thinking reshapes the world
- **Spiritual-but-not-religious** — institutional religion is suspect; personal spiritual experience is authoritative
- **Four basic beliefs** (per Myers Ch 6): all is one; all is divine; humanity is divine; consciousness creates reality
- **Distinguishing from sources:** authentic Hinduism affirms institutional dharma and caste; authentic Buddhism demands the Sangha and ascetic practice; New Spirituality rejects both in favor of self-curated practice

### Day-by-Day Schedule

| Day | Date | Activity |
|-----|------|----------|
| 1 | Wed Jan 13 | Framing. What New Spirituality actually is — Western syncretism, not Eastern religion. The four basic beliefs (Myers). Read short excerpts from Tolle (*The Power of Now*), Chopra (*Seven Spiritual Laws*), and Byrne (*The Secret*). What's borrowed, what's stripped, what's added? |
| 2 | Thu Jan 14 | Deep Dive (compressed — all 10 lenses in one day). Treat New Spirituality through the grid quickly. The wellness industry, manifestation culture, "spiritual but not religious" demographics. Why this is the *operative* spirituality of much of contemporary American culture. |
| 3 | Tue Jan 19 | *(Fri Jan 15 = Teacher Work Day; Mon Jan 18 = MLK Day.)* Combined Pair & Defend + Case Study. Pair & Defend: *"New Spirituality is just Buddhism for Westerners"* vs. *"New Spirituality is its own worldview that borrows Buddhist vocabulary."* Case study: a friend tells you she's "manifesting" a romantic partner. What's actually going on? |
| 4 | Wed Jan 20 | Four-Test + vs. Christianity + Gospel Conversation (combined). Apply the four tests to New Spirituality (the consequences test is particularly devastating — manifestation theology is pastorally cruel when manifestation fails). The Gospel response: God is *personal*, not the universe; suffering has meaning, not just bad-vibration causes; we are loved without performance, not divine. Roleplay. |
| 5 | Thu Jan 21 | Worldview Brief + Unit 7 Assessment. Bridge: T2 Discipline Pass begins. Then T3 opens with the dominant secular alternative — Secularism / New Atheism. |

### Gospel Conversation Focus

Personal love vs. impersonal universe. New Spirituality offers a friendly but ultimately impersonal cosmos that mirrors back whatever the self projects. Christianity offers a personal God who initiates, who knows the specific person, who loves without depending on the person's vibration. The conversation often comes when manifestation fails — when the desired thing didn't materialize despite the journaling and the visualization. The pastoral entry point is not "your worldview is wrong"; it's "you are loved already, before you manifest anything, and in ways that don't depend on your performance."

### Contemporary Resources

1. Eckhart Tolle — *The Power of Now* (a few pages — primary source)
2. Rhonda Byrne — *The Secret* (excerpt — primary source)
3. A contemporary critique: Alisa Childers or similar on the Christian/New Spirituality interface

## Unit 8 — Secularism / New Atheism

**Dates:** Mar 1 – Mar 11, 2027 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 4
**Framing Question:** *If there is no God, on what basis do human rights exist?*

### Purpose

Engage the dominant worldview of Western academia, media, and professional culture. Distinguish two flavors:

1. **General Secularism** — naturalism, humanism, science as the only reliable path to truth, religion as private preference
2. **New Atheism** — Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris, Dennett; more aggressive, specifically anti-Christian, argues religion is actively harmful

Both share: materialism, no transcendent meaning, ethics derived from reason / evolution / social contract rather than divine command. Students will encounter secular professors, coworkers, and friends their whole adult lives — this is not hypothetical.

### Key Anchors

- **Naturalism / Materialism** — only the physical world exists; no supernatural
- **Scientism** — science is the only reliable path to truth
- **Methodological doubt** — skepticism as epistemology
- **Utilitarianism** — ethics grounded in outcomes (maximize well-being, minimize harm)
- **Humanism** — human dignity without divine grounding
- **The grounding problem** — if there is no God, on what basis do human rights, human dignity, or objective moral claims exist?

### Day-by-Day Schedule

| Day | Date | Activity |
|-----|------|----------|
| 1 | Mon Mar 1 | Framing. Who are secular people, and what do they actually believe? Distinguish functional secularism (most of culture) from New Atheism (specific thinkers). |
| 2 | Tue Mar 2 | Deep Dive Pt 1 (Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Biology, Psychology). Naturalism, materialism, the limits of science, utilitarianism, neo-Darwinism, the grounding problem for morality and human dignity. Read excerpt from Dawkins (*The God Delusion*) or Harris (*The Moral Landscape*). |
| 3 | Wed Mar 3 | Deep Dive Pt 2 (Sociology, Law, Politics, Economics, History). Personal autonomy. Legal positivism. Progressivism (preview — full treatment in Unit 9). Social progress as secular eschatology. |
| 4 | Thu Mar 4 | Socratic Seminar. Student questions. Likely tension: can a secular worldview coherently ground objective morality? |
| 5 | Fri Mar 5 | Pair & Defend (two-round both-sides): *"Morality requires God"* (theistic position) vs. *"Morality can exist without God — utilitarianism, social contract, etc."* (secular position). |
| 6 | Mon Mar 8 | Case Study: A secular friend faces a terminal diagnosis. Her worldview has no resurrection, no transcendent meaning. How does she make sense of her death? What does she need to hear? |
| 7 | Tue Mar 9 | Four-Test + vs. Christianity. Particular attention: the inner-world test on the grounding problem (where do moral intuitions come from on a materialist account?); the reason test on whether scientism is itself a scientific claim. |
| 8 | Wed Mar 10 | Gospel Conversation. Meaning, morality, and death — the three places where a secular worldview has no transcendent answer. Most secular people feel these absences even if they don't name them. The Gospel as a story that explains the longings secularism cannot account for. Roleplay. |
| 9 | Thu Mar 11 | Worldview Brief + Unit 8 Assessment. Bridge: the intellectual movement that took secular materialism political and remade Western institutions — Revolutionary Lineage. |

### Gospel Conversation Focus

Meaning, morality, and death. The secular worldview struggles at exactly the places where human longing is strongest. Most secular people have functioning moral intuitions (human dignity, horror at injustice, belief in love's significance) that their own worldview cannot ground. The conversation is not "atheists are immoral" — most aren't, precisely because they're borrowing Christian moral capital. The conversation is: "where do your moral intuitions come from, and does your own worldview explain them?" This is winsome because it honors what is true about the secular person's ethical life while revealing its unacknowledged sources.

### Contemporary Resources

1. Tim Keller — *The Reason for God* (chapters 1–3 on secular objections)
2. Richard Dawkins — *The God Delusion* chapter excerpt (primary source from the other side)
3. A Bishop Barron video response to New Atheism (winsome example)

## Unit 9 — The Revolutionary Lineage: Marxism → Progressivism → Postmodernism

**Dates:** Mar 15 – Apr 8, 2027 (13 instructional days; spans Spring Break)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 5 (Marxism) + Ch 7 (Postmodernism) + primary sources on American Progressivism (not in textbook)
**Supplemental Outline:** [unit-09-revolutionary-lineage-outline.md](unit-09-revolutionary-lineage-outline.md)
**Framing Question:** *How did class struggle become identity struggle, and why is "there are no metanarratives" itself a metanarrative?*

### Purpose

Teach the three movements as a genealogical arc, not three separate worldviews. The unifying thread: **critical deconstruction of existing power structures, a materialist or anti-realist view of truth, and a vision of human liberation through revolutionary change.**

This unit receives 13 days (instead of the default 9) because it covers three movements and because the American Progressive bridge (which Myers/Noebel does not cover) is essential for students to understand how European philosophical movements shaped American law, education, medicine, and family life.

### The Three Movements

**Movement 1 — Marxism** (Ch 5)
- Dialectical materialism; class conflict as the engine of history; religion as "opium of the people"; capitalism as exploitation; inevitable proletarian revolution
- Why it matters pedagogically: students need the philosophical root before they can recognize the branches

**Movement 2 — American Progressivism** (not in textbook — the bridge)
- Woodrow Wilson (administrative state; rejection of natural law and the Constitution as fixed)
- John Dewey (education as social reconstruction; rejection of transcendent truth)
- Margaret Sanger (eugenics as social progress; Planned Parenthood's founding ideology)
- Herbert Croly (nationalism + socialism hybrid)
- *Critical teaching point:* American Progressivism is not simply "liberal politics." It is a specific intellectual project that imported Hegelian and Marxist categories into American political life and explicitly rejected the Founders' framework. This is intellectual history, not partisan politics — teach it that way.

**Movement 3 — Postmodernism** (Ch 7)
- Nietzsche (death of God; will to power)
- Foucault (knowledge as power; all truth claims are power moves)
- Derrida (deconstruction; no stable meaning)
- Lyotard ("incredulity toward metanarratives")
- Inherits from Marxism: the critical deconstruction impulse; suspicion of all existing structures as oppressive
- Departs from Marxism: abandons the optimism about where history is going. There is no proletarian paradise — there is only deconstruction
- Contemporary expression: Critical Theory, CRT, gender ideology as a worldview framework, the lived campus culture students will enter

### Day-by-Day Schedule

Spring Break (Mar 22–26) falls between Days 5 and 6. Movement 1 (Marxism) is complete before the break — a clean conceptual pause. Movements 2 (Progressivism) and 3 (Postmodernism) plus integration come after.

### Gospel Conversation Focus

Truth and metanarrative. Postmodernism's greatest vulnerability is self-referential: *"there are no metanarratives"* is itself a metanarrative. The Gospel as a true story about the world — not merely a private religious preference — is the direct response. Christians must be able to say: yes, we are making a universal claim about reality; yes, we know that offends postmodern sensibilities; no, we will not reduce it to private preference to make it socially acceptable. The conversation requires courage and intellectual honesty in roughly equal measure.

### Contemporary Resources

1. Carl Trueman — *The Rise and Triumph of the Modern Self* (the Frankfurt School and Foucault chapters)
2. Foucault — *Discipline and Punish* or *History of Sexuality* (primary source excerpt)
3. James Lindsay or Helen Pluckrose (contemporary critic of critical theory — e.g., *Cynical Theories*) — paired with a Christian critique such as Voddie Baucham's *Fault Lines*

## Unit 10 — Capstone (Oral Final integrated)

**Dates:** Apr 15 – Apr 27, 2027 (8 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** N/A — synthesis across the full year
**Framing Question:** *Can you defend the Christian worldview — charitably and intellectually — against the worldview that most challenges you?*

### Purpose

Integrate the full year. Students present a sustained defense of the Christian worldview against the worldview they found most personally challenging, demonstrating that they can (1) represent the opposing worldview charitably, (2) identify the decisive differences through the 10 lenses and the four tests, (3) present the Christian response with intellectual honesty, and (4) articulate a Gospel conversation appropriate for a thoughtful proponent of that worldview.

**The Capstone presentation IS the oral component of the dual-credit final.** This is deliberate. The Capstone tests integrative synthesis under public pressure — the only place in the course that does. It is graded once, recorded once, and serves both purposes.

### Capstone Structure

**Presentation (10 minutes per student):**

1. **Charitable Representation (5 min):** State the worldview you are addressing. Represent it fairly — your "steel-man" version, one a proponent would recognize and affirm. Use the 10-lens grid and the four-test framework to identify the 2–3 disciplines where this worldview most challenges Christianity.
2. **Q&A (3 min):** Classmates and teacher ask questions that probe the presentation. Expect challenges from the class — that's the point.
3. **Live Gospel Conversation (2 min):** Roleplay the Gospel conversation with a peer playing the worldview adherent. This is the part that doubles as the dual-credit oral final.

**Final Brief (handed in separately):** a 2-page written version of the presentation, more developed than a unit Worldview Brief, with at least 3 primary-source citations. Due at the start of the first presentation day.

# Appendix A — Cohort Tools

This course uses four cohort tools throughout. Full specifications are in [_shared/cohort-tools.md](../../_shared/cohort-tools.md). Summary below:

- **Discussion Brief** — 1-page written response; collected at start of class period; graded on completion and thoughtfulness. Used as the Worldview Brief in this course (1 per unit).
- **Pair & Defend** — two-round simultaneous pair debate; sides assigned, then swapped so every student argues both sides; ~24 minutes total; debrief question: *"What was the strongest argument you heard from the side you started disagreeing with?"*
- **Case Study** — groups of 3–4; real-world scenarios; produce summary + recommended response + strongest counterargument; class vote; teacher debrief.
- **Capstone** — summative; 10-minute presentation (charitable rep + Q&A + Gospel roleplay) + Final Brief; doubles as dual-credit oral final.

# Appendix B — Notes for the Teacher

1. **The worldview grid is the anchor.** Use [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md) as the physical reference (printed poster or handout) students return to every unit. By May, students should be able to fill in every cell from memory.

2. **The reading packets are load-bearing.** See [teaching-methodology.md](teaching-methodology.md). One per worldview unit, 10–20 pages, built once over the summer. Without them, class time gets consumed doing what packets should have done, and the course quietly drifts toward the textbook's discipline-first structure. Plan the summer prep work in.

3. **SUB days are instructional.** There are 8 sub days in the year (Aug 28, Sep 3, Sep 21, Apr 5, Apr 12, Apr 19, Apr 26, May 3). Each is marked **SUB DAY** in the day-by-day schedules above with a specific activity suitable for a substitute to facilitate. Generate detailed substitute plans via `/generate-substitute-plan <week> <day>` when needed.

4. **Units 2 and 4 require primary source sourcing.** Judaism and Mormonism are not covered in Myers/Noebel. Budget prep time to assemble primary source excerpts before each unit begins. Unit 9 (Revolutionary Lineage) has [unit-09-revolutionary-lineage-outline.md](unit-09-revolutionary-lineage-outline.md) as a draft resource for the Progressivism bridge.

5. **Charitable representation is the curricular commitment.** This is the first criterion on the capstone rubric and the central pedagogical norm. A student who cannot represent a worldview fairly cannot engage it faithfully. Discipline this from Day 1 of Unit 0.

6. **Pair & Defend assigns positions students don't personally hold.** Several debates ask Christian students to defend positions critical of Christianity (e.g., *"Christianity's claim to be the one true worldview is arrogant"*; *"Karma is more morally satisfying than the Christian answer"*; *"Buddhist non-attachment is wiser than Christian longing"*). The two-round structure ensures every student argues both sides, so the "argue against Christianity" assignment is always paired with an "argue for Christianity" assignment for the same student. This is pedagogically essential for charitable engagement, but parents should be informed at the start of the year via the syllabus or a dedicated parent letter so the framing isn't a surprise.

7. **Breaks fall mid-unit.** Thanksgiving falls inside Unit 4 (Mormonism) — six days before, three days after. Christmas Break falls inside Unit 6 (Buddhism) — three days before, six days after. Spring Break falls inside Unit 9 (Revolutionary Lineage) — five days before (Marxism complete), eight days after (Progressivism + Postmodernism + integration). Each pre-break stretch reaches a natural pause point.

8. **The Discipline Pass weeks are real instruction, not buffer.** T1 Discipline Pass (10 days), T2 Discipline Pass (22 days), and T3 Finals (17 days) constitute Pass 2 — the discipline-comparative teaching the publisher devotes 100 days to in their Ch 8–17 sequence. Each Pass 2 lesson re-teaches the same material at higher altitude (discipline as the unit of analysis instead of worldview). Publisher Ch 8–17 chapter tests are administered as **ungraded practice** during T3 finals window, after all 8 worldviews are taught. The high-stakes Pass 2 summative is the **Ch 18 Test as dual-credit written final** on May 10. Per-unit Day 9 tests are **blended tests** that combine the publisher's matching worldview-comprehensive test (Ch 2/3/4/5/6/7) with that worldview's relevant questions extracted from Ch 8–17, so each per-unit assessment covers both holistic and discipline-by-discipline mastery.
