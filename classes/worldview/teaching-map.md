## Design Philosophy

This course uses Myers & Noebel's 10-discipline analytical framework as its spine but **restructures the delivery from discipline-first to worldview-first.**

Myers & Noebel organize their book in two passes: Part 1 introduces each worldview in isolation (Ch 1–7); Part 2 compares all worldviews on each of the 10 disciplines (Ch 8–17). That structure is pedagogically elegant but weak for our purposes in two specific ways:

1. It does not directly compare competing worldviews with the Christian worldview on the terms of the person who holds the competing view.
2. It does not equip students to present the Gospel to someone who holds a competing worldview in a way that's meaningful to *them*.

This course reorganizes around those two goals. Each worldview gets its own two-week unit, during which students:

1. Meet the worldview in its best, most charitable form (no straw men, no Christian filter).
2. Examine it through all 10 disciplines using the worldview-grid.
3. Compare it directly with the Christian worldview, discipline by discipline.
4. Practice the Gospel conversation specific to that worldview's adherents.

**The outcome we're building toward:** by May, students should be able to sit across a coffee shop table from a thoughtful proponent of any of these 9 worldviews, represent that person's view back to them in a way they would recognize and affirm as fair, and present the Gospel in a way that is meaningful and impactful to *that person specifically* — not a generic tract.

## The 10 Analytical Lenses

Every worldview unit examines the worldview through all 10 of these disciplines. The Christianity unit establishes the baseline answers. Every subsequent unit compares back to that baseline.

1. **Theology** — What is God (or ultimate reality)?
2. **Philosophy** — What is real? How do we know?
3. **Ethics** — What is good? On what basis?
4. **Biology** — What is life, and where did it come from?
5. **Psychology** — What is the self? What makes a person whole or broken?
6. **Sociology** — How should people live together?
7. **Law** — What is justice, and where does authority come from?
8. **Politics** — What is the role of the state?
9. **Economics** — What is wealth, and how should it be stewarded?
10. **History** — Where is history going? What is its meaning?

See [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md) for the full 9 × 10 reference matrix. Students see the grid on Day 1 of Unit 0 and return to it throughout the year.

## Unit Rhythm (The 9-Day Pattern)

Every worldview unit (Units 2–8) follows this 9-day rhythm. Unit 1 (Christianity, 13 days), Unit 9 (Revolutionary Lineage, 13 days), Unit 0, and Unit 10 have custom rhythms documented in their sections below. The vs.-Christianity comparison is woven into the Deep Dives and the Gospel Conversation rather than getting its own day.

| Day | Phase | What Happens |
|-----|-------|--------------|
| **1** | **Framing (Lecture)** | Who actually holds this worldview? Why? Present it in its best form. No strawmen. Assign Myers/Noebel chapter + primary source reading at home. |
| **2** | **Deep Dive Pt 1 (Lecture)** | First five lenses — Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Biology, Psychology. Begin filling in this worldview's column on the grid; flag where it agrees and disagrees with Christianity as it goes. |
| **3** | **Deep Dive Pt 2 (Lecture)** | Remaining five lenses — Sociology, Law, Politics, Economics, History. Complete the column. Students submit 2–3 questions they still have. |
| **4** | **Socratic Seminar** | Students drive discussion from their reading and questions. Teacher facilitates only. Cold-call: *"What would you say to that?"* Students must argue the worldview charitably — no easy dismissals. |
| **5** | **Pair & Defend** | Two-round simultaneous pair debate (see [_shared/cohort-tools.md](../../_shared/cohort-tools.md)). Every student argues both sides. Debrief: *"What was the strongest argument from the side you started disagreeing with?"* |
| **6** | **Case Study (Inside the Worldview)** | Real scenario: a person who genuinely holds this worldview encounters a life event (suffering, injustice, death, meaning). How does this worldview respond on its own terms? What does it offer? What does it fail to offer? |
| **7** | **Contemporary Resource** | Teacher-chosen current clip, interview, or essay from someone who actually holds this worldview. Discussion: does this change how you see it? |
| **8** | **Gospel Conversation** | The capstone day. How does the Gospel speak specifically to someone who holds this worldview? What is the entry point? What is the offense? Roleplay the conversation in pairs. |
| **9** | **Worldview Brief + Assessment** | Students write the Worldview Brief (1 page) at the start of class: charitable summary, most compelling feature, deepest contradiction with Christianity, sketch of the Gospel conversation. Short unit test (10 questions on the 10 lenses) follows. Preview next worldview's framing question. |

This rhythm is a default, not a cage. Reorder, combine, or extend days as the material requires — the goal is optimal learning, not pattern fidelity. When a unit spans a break (Thanksgiving, Christmas, Spring Break), the natural pause point depends on the unit; see each unit's day-by-day schedule for the specific split.

# TRIMESTER 1 — Foundations and the Christian Baseline

T1 establishes the analytical framework (Unit 0), the Christian worldview as the baseline (Unit 1), and then examines two traditions that share theological territory with Christianity in different ways: Judaism, the covenantal tradition Christianity claims to fulfill, and Mormonism, a 19th-century American restoration movement that uses Christian vocabulary while teaching categorically different content. Islam — a third tradition that also claims the same prophetic lineage — begins T2.

## Unit 0 — Foundations

**Dates:** Aug 24 – Sep 8, 2026 (10 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 1 — Battle of Ideas
**Framing Question:** *What do you actually believe — and where did that come from?*

### Purpose

Establish the analytical framework of the course before examining any worldview in depth. Introduce the 10 disciplines. Show students the [worldview-grid](worldview-grid.md) on Day 1 as a preview of the year. Most importantly: build the cohort norms that make charitable engagement possible.

### Key Anchors

- A worldview is the set of answers — examined or unexamined — a person gives to the 10 ultimate questions
- Everyone has a worldview; the question is whether it is coherent, honest, and examined
- Understanding a worldview is not the same as agreeing with it — this distinction is the foundation of intellectual charity
- Christianity will be examined first as the baseline, then every other worldview will be compared against it

## Unit 1 — Christianity

**Dates:** Sep 9 – Sep 28, 2026 (13 instructional days — baseline depth)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 2
**Framing Question:** *What is the Christian answer to each of the 10 disciplines, and why?*

### Purpose

Establish the Christian worldview as the baseline answer to each of the 10 disciplines. Every subsequent unit compares back to this. Students finish Unit 1 able to articulate Christianity's answer to every discipline in one clear sentence with at least one Scripture anchor.

This unit is the longest (13 days instead of 10) because students need to know the baseline *well* before they can meaningfully compare anything to it.

### Key Theological Anchors

- **Trinitarian Monotheism** — one God eternally existing in three persons (Father, Son, Holy Spirit)
- **Imago Dei** — every person is made in God's image and has inviolable dignity (Gen 1:26–27)
- **Special Creation** — the world and life are created by God, not self-generated
- **Natural Law** — moral reality is built into creation and knowable by reason
- **Redemptive Narrative** — Creation → Fall → Redemption → Restoration (four-act story)
- **Sphere Sovereignty** — God-ordained spheres (family, church, state) have distinct, non-overlapping authority
- **Agape Love** — the ethic of self-giving love grounded in God's own character
- **Biblical Stewardship** — we steward what God owns; we do not own outright

### 10-Lens Summary

Christianity's column from [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md). Students should be able to explain each cell with one sentence of elaboration by Day 13.

### Day-by-Day Schedule

| Day | Date | Activity |
|-----|------|----------|
| 1 | Wed Sep 9 | Framing. Why start with Christianity? Because we cannot compare anything to what we do not know. Read Myers Ch 2 (assigned for home). |
| 2 | Thu Sep 10 | Deep Dive Part 1: Theology, Philosophy, Ethics. What does the Christian faith claim about God, truth, and goodness? |
| 3 | Fri Sep 11 | Deep Dive Part 2: Biology, Psychology, Sociology. Imago Dei, the Fall, sphere sovereignty. |
| 4 | Mon Sep 14 | Deep Dive Part 3: Law, Politics, Economics, History. Natural law, subsidiarity, biblical stewardship, the redemptive narrative. |
| 5 | Tue Sep 15 | Socratic Seminar. Students drive discussion from their reading. Questions submitted in advance shape the conversation. |
| 6 | Wed Sep 16 | Pair & Defend (two-round both-sides): *"Christianity's claim to be the one true worldview is reasonable"* vs. *"Christianity's claim to be the one true worldview is arrogant."* |
| 7 | Thu Sep 17 | Case Study: a 22-year-old Christian who just lost her father to cancer. How does the Christian worldview answer her at each of the 10 lenses? Where does it comfort? Where does it demand hard truth? |
| 8 | Mon Sep 21 | *(Fri Sep 18 = Teacher Work Day.)* The Christian worldview vs. itself: what do we do with the differences inside Christianity (Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox)? Brief survey. Why do these differences matter or not matter at the worldview level? |
| 9 | Tue Sep 22 | Contemporary Resource: Tim Keller sermon or Francis Collins interview (teacher-selected). How is a thoughtful Christian articulating the worldview today? |
| 10 | Wed Sep 23 | The Gospel in one conversation. How would you articulate the Gospel to someone who has no Christian background? Practice in pairs. This is the foundational exercise we will return to for every subsequent unit. |
| 11 | Thu Sep 24 | Review day. Fill in the Christianity column of the worldview grid from memory (quiz). Discuss anything still unclear. |
| 12 | Fri Sep 25 | Worldview Brief — students write the Christianity brief. This becomes their reference document for the rest of the year. |
| 13 | Mon Sep 28 | Unit 1 Assessment. Short test (20 questions + 1 short essay). Bridge to Unit 2: what does Christianity claim about its own origins? That leads us to Judaism. |

### Gospel Conversation Focus

This is the unit where students learn to *present* the Gospel, not just defend it. Keep the framing positive and invitational: the Gospel as the story that makes sense of their own longing, grief, joy, and moral intuition. Every subsequent unit will adapt this Gospel conversation for a specific worldview's adherents, so getting the basic version right here matters.

## Unit 2 — Judaism

**Dates:** Sep 29 – Oct 12, 2026 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** **Not covered.** Build from primary sources.
**Framing Question:** *What is Judaism on its own terms — across four stages of reinterpretation — and how does it answer its own central questions about covenant, atonement, and the meaning of history?*

### Purpose

Treat Judaism as the theological arc it actually is, not a static category. Modern Rabbinic Judaism is not a faltering remnant of Temple Judaism — it is a self-contained 2,000-year-old system with its own robust theology of atonement (teshuvah, tefilah, tzedakah; the Yom Kippur liturgy; suffering as expiation in some traditions). Students learn that system on its own terms first. The Christian apologetic conversation — Jesus as the fulfillment of the sacrificial system — arrives explicitly on Day 8 (Gospel Conversation), not in the unit's framing.

### Key Anchors — The Four Stages

1. **Covenantal / Patriarchal Judaism** (Abraham–Moses) — the covenant framework; God chooses a people.
2. **Temple / Sacrificial Judaism** (Moses–70 AD) — the Levitical system as the divinely-prescribed mechanism for atonement. Blood sacrifice as the means by which sin is covered.
3. **The Rupture of 70 AD** — the Temple is destroyed. The Levitical sacrificial system ends permanently. The Babylonian Talmud (Yoma 39b) records that the scarlet cord stopped turning white for 40 years before the destruction — a sign, in rabbinic memory, that atonement was no longer being received. *The theological question students will wrestle with: if sacrifice was God's prescribed mechanism, what does its cessation mean?*
4. **Rabbinic Judaism** (70 AD – present) — the reconstruction. Prayer, Torah study, and acts of lovingkindness as substitutes for sacrifice, grounded in Hosea 6:6 (*"For I desired mercy, and not sacrifice"*). Then the spectrum: Reform, Conservative, Orthodox, Messianic.

### 10-Lens Summary

Judaism's column from [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md). Note: this summarizes modern Rabbinic Judaism. The earlier stages (Covenantal, Temple) are the *history* that produced this column, and students need that history to understand the column.

### Day-by-Day Schedule

| Day | Date | Activity |
|-----|------|----------|
| 1 | Tue Sep 29 | Framing. The four stages of Judaism (Covenantal, Temple, Rabbinic reconstruction, modern expressions). Why this is not a static category. |
| 2 | Wed Sep 30 | Deep Dive Pt 1 (Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Biology, Psychology). Covenantal and Temple Judaism on its own terms. Read Leviticus 16 (Day of Atonement). |
| 3 | Thu Oct 1 | Deep Dive Pt 2 (Sociology, Law, Politics, Economics, History). The rupture of 70 AD and the Rabbinic reconstruction. Read Josephus on the Temple's destruction; Talmud Yoma 39b (the scarlet cord); Hosea 6:6 and the three pillars (teshuvah, tefilah, tzedakah). Mishnah and Talmud as the post-Temple framework. |
| 4 | Mon Oct 5 | *(Fri Oct 2 = Teacher Work Day.)* Socratic Seminar. Student questions drive discussion. Likely tensions: Is Rabbinic Judaism the same religion as Temple Judaism? On Jewish terms, what is atonement today? |
| 5 | Tue Oct 6 | Pair & Defend (two-round both-sides): *"Rabbinic Judaism is a faithful continuation of the covenant"* vs. *"Rabbinic Judaism is a reconstruction after the original atonement system ended."* Both positions have serious Jewish proponents; neither is anti-Semitic. |
| 6 | Wed Oct 7 | Case Study: walk through Yom Kippur 2026 with a devout Jewish family. The fast, Kol Nidre, the Ashamnu and Al Chet confessions, the Avodah service that retells the Temple liturgy, the sealing of the Book of Life. What is happening theologically on Jewish terms? How does Rabbinic Judaism understand atonement today? |
| 7 | Thu Oct 8 | Modern Judaism overview (Reform, Conservative, Orthodox, Messianic) + Contemporary Resource: interview or essay from a contemporary rabbi (teacher-selected). |
| 8 | Fri Oct 9 | Gospel Conversation. *Now* the apologetic conversation. Jesus as the fulfillment of the Levitical sacrificial system (Hebrews 9–10), made *from the Jewish scriptures themselves*. Roleplay: how do you have this conversation with a Jewish friend without being offensive or triumphalist? |
| 9 | Mon Oct 12 | Worldview Brief + Unit 2 Assessment. Bridge to Unit 3: another tradition that claims to restore a lost covenant — Mormonism. |

### Gospel Conversation Focus

The question of atonement. Judaism's own scriptures (Leviticus, the Prophets) establish that sin requires blood sacrifice for forgiveness; after 70 AD, that mechanism ended. Rabbinic Judaism answered the rupture from inside the tradition (Hosea 6:6, the three pillars). Christianity's claim is that Jesus completes what the Temple system was a shadow of. Teach students to make that case *from the Jewish scriptures themselves*, not as an outside critique. Tone matters enormously — Jewish students and Jewish friends have heard anti-Semitic versions of this argument their whole lives. Christian supersessionism at its worst has blood on its hands. Students must learn to make the theological point without the historical cruelty, and they must be able to represent the Jewish response (Rabbinic Judaism's own atonement theology) fairly before they advance the Christian one.

### Contemporary Resources

1. Michael Brown — *Answering Jewish Objections to Jesus* series (one-chapter excerpt)
2. David Baron — *Rays of Messiah's Glory* (classic; one essay)
3. Modern: an interview or op-ed from a contemporary Messianic Jewish voice (e.g., Jews for Jesus writing)

## Unit 3 — Mormonism

**Dates:** Oct 13 – Oct 26, 2026 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** **Not covered.** Build from primary sources.
**Framing Question:** *How do you have a meaningful conversation with someone who uses Christian language but means something categorically different?*

### Purpose

Treat Mormonism (LDS) as a distinct theological system, not a variant of Christianity. The critical teaching point: **Mormon vocabulary overlaps with Christian vocabulary but refers to categorically different things.** Students must learn to recognize and name the shift. This unit also teaches the parallel with Islam's revelation structure — the same apologetic framework applies to both, which students will see when they reach Unit 4.

### Key Contrasts with Christianity

| | Christianity | Mormonism |
|---|---|---|
| **God** | Trinity — one God in three persons, eternal | The Godhead: three separate divine beings (Father, Son, Holy Ghost); the Father once lived a mortal life and progressed to exaltation |
| **Jesus** | Eternal Son of God, second person of the Trinity, fully God | Firstborn spirit Son of God the Father; a divine being, but not eternally one in being with the Father; not the Trinitarian God of historic Christianity |
| **Salvation** | Grace alone through faith in Christ alone (Eph 2:8–9) | Grace + works + ordinances + temple covenants + exaltation (2 Nephi 25:23) |
| **Scripture** | Protestant canon (66 books) | Bible (with Joseph Smith Translation "corrections") + Book of Mormon + Doctrine & Covenants + Pearl of Great Price |
| **Revelation mechanism** | Closed canon; revelation completed in Christ | Angel Moroni → Joseph Smith → golden plates (continuing revelation) |

### Parallel with Islam (Teach Explicitly)

| | Mormonism | Islam |
|---|---|---|
| **Revelation** | Angel Moroni to Joseph Smith | Angel Jibreel to Muhammad |
| **Prior scripture** | Bible corrupted; Book of Mormon corrects | Bible corrupted; Quran supersedes |
| **Jesus** | Prophet / created god — not *the* eternal God | Prophet — not divine |
| **Trinity** | Rejected | Rejected (*shirk*) |
| **Founder** | Joseph Smith as prophet / restorer | Muhammad as final prophet |

The apologetic response to the "corrupted scripture + new prophet" pattern is the same for both. Teach the pattern once here; apply it again in Unit 4.

### 10-Lens Summary

Mormonism's column from [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md).

### Day-by-Day Schedule

| Day | Date | Activity |
|-----|------|----------|
| 1 | Tue Oct 13 | Framing. The vocabulary-overlap problem: "God," "Jesus," "salvation" mean different things in LDS theology. Read a page of LDS official teaching (from churchofjesuschrist.org) and compare to a Christian systematic theology page on the same topic. |
| 2 | Wed Oct 14 | Deep Dive Pt 1 (Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Christology). LDS doctrine of eternal progression, God as exalted man, eternal matter; why the LDS Jesus is a different Jesus. Primary sources: excerpts from *Doctrine & Covenants* and Joseph Smith's King Follett discourse. |
| 3 | Thu Oct 15 | Deep Dive Pt 2 (Soteriology, Sociology, Scripture, Revelation, History). Why "grace + works + ordinances + temple covenants" is not the Christian gospel. The Book of Mormon's origin, Angel Moroni, the Joseph Smith Translation. Teach the parallel with Islamic revelation structure explicitly here — the apologetic pattern returns in Unit 4. |
| 4 | Mon Oct 19 | *(Fri Oct 16 = Teacher Work Day.)* Socratic Seminar. Student questions. Likely tension: how do you engage with LDS friends charitably when the disagreement is this deep? |
| 5 | Tue Oct 20 | Pair & Defend (two-round both-sides): *"Mormonism is a Christian denomination"* (LDS official position) vs. *"Mormonism is a distinct religion that uses Christian vocabulary."* |
| 6 | Wed Oct 21 | Case Study: You are at a friend's house for dinner. Her parents are faithful LDS. They ask you what you believe about Jesus. What do you say? How do you honor them as people while being clear about what you actually believe? |
| 7 | Thu Oct 22 | Contemporary Resource: an interview or presentation from a former LDS member (e.g., Rob Bowman Jr., Lynn Wilder, or similar). How do they describe what they left? |
| 8 | Fri Oct 23 | Gospel Conversation. The entry point is usually Jesus — LDS Mormons honor Jesus but mean something different. Start there. The offense is usually scripture sufficiency — that God's word is complete in Christ and does not require a new prophet. Roleplay. |
| 9 | Mon Oct 26 | Worldview Brief + Unit 3 Assessment. Bridge: T1 buffer begins. Preview T1 review. |

### Gospel Conversation Focus

Vocabulary clarification is the first move. Before any meaningful conversation, students need to ask: "When you say 'Jesus,' who do you mean?" and "When you say 'salvation,' what are you being saved from, and by what mechanism?" The conversation is gentle, not combative. LDS Mormons are often warmer, more family-centered, and more morally serious than many of the Christian students will encounter — *that's part of what makes this unit formative.* The goodness of LDS people is not evidence that the theology is true. Helping students hold both "they are lovely neighbors" and "the theology is categorically different" without collapsing either is the formation work.

### Contemporary Resources

1. Rob Bowman Jr. — any of his comparative theology lectures on LDS doctrine
2. Lynn Wilder — *Unveiling Grace* (excerpt — former BYU professor's exit from LDS)
3. An official LDS source (churchofjesuschrist.org) paired with a critique from a Christian theologian — teach students to read both sides in the original

## T1 Buffer & Review (Oct 27 – Nov 10, 2026)

## Unit 4 — Islam

**Dates:** Nov 13 – Dec 2, 2026 (9 instructional days; spans Thanksgiving break)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 3
**Framing Question:** *How does the Muslim understanding of God and prophet-hood differ from the Christian understanding — and how do we talk about Jesus across that difference?*

### Purpose

Engage Islam as a major world religion (1.8+ billion adherents) on its own terms. Charitable representation is essential — students must be able to articulate Islamic theology in a way a Muslim scholar would recognize as fair. Then, with that groundwork, examine the genuine disagreements with Christianity.

The parallel revelation structure with Mormonism (Unit 3) is recalled here: angel → prophet → new scripture → prior scripture is said to be corrupted. The apologetic pattern students learned in Unit 3 applies again.

### Key Theological Anchors

- **Tawhid** — the absolute oneness of God. The Trinity is rejected as *shirk* (associating partners with God).
- **Muhammad as the "Seal of the Prophets"** — final and greatest prophet; the Quran is the direct, uncorrupted word of Allah.
- **The Five Pillars** — Shahada (confession), Salat (prayer), Zakat (almsgiving), Sawm (Ramadan fast), Hajj (pilgrimage).
- **Sharia** — the moral and legal framework derived from Quran + Hadith.
- **Umma** — the global Muslim community; Islam is corporate before it is individual.
- **Jesus (Isa) in Islam** — revered prophet; born of a virgin; performed miracles; did not die on the cross (substitutionist view in most traditions); will return at the end of time. *Not divine.*

## Unit 5 — Hinduism

**Dates:** Dec 3 – Dec 15, 2026 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 6 (partial — "New Spirituality" — acknowledge limitation)
**Framing Question:** *What is the self, and what is it for?*

### Purpose

Treat Hinduism as the sophisticated, 3,000-year-old philosophical and religious tradition it is — not as "many gods and reincarnation." The philosophical depth (Vedanta, Advaita, the Brahman–Atman relation) requires genuine engagement. This is the first unit where Myers/Noebel's "New Spirituality" category is explicitly named as a teaching limitation: tell students that the textbook collapses Hinduism and Buddhism into a single category and that this course refuses that move.

### Key Anchors

- **Brahman** — ultimate reality; the one true being from which all appearance derives
- **Atman** — the self; in Advaita Vedanta, ultimately identical with Brahman
- **Maya** — illusion; the appearance of separation and multiplicity that conceals the unity of Brahman
- **Dharma** — duty; the right action for one's caste (*varna*) and stage of life (*ashrama*)
- **Karma** — moral causation; actions in this life determine circumstances in the next
- **Samsara** — the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth
- **Moksha** — liberation; release from samsara through realization of Atman-Brahman unity
- **Three paths** — knowledge (*jnana*), devotion (*bhakti*), action (*karma yoga*)
- **Key texts** — Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita

### Gospel Conversation Focus

The self. Hinduism offers ultimate dissolution of the individual self into the impersonal Brahman; Christianity offers the ultimate preservation and glorification of the individual self in relationship with a personal God. The Gospel's appeal to the Hindu worldview is often grief — the loss of someone we love. Hinduism's answer (they've been reborn; the karmic cycle continues) does not satisfy the intuition that the *specific* person mattered eternally. Christianity says: yes, this specific person, resurrected bodily, known by name. That is a very different offer.

## Unit 6 — Buddhism

**Dates:** Dec 16, 2026 – Jan 12, 2027 (9 instructional days; spans Christmas break)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 6 (partial — "New Spirituality" — acknowledge limitation)
**Framing Question:** *What is suffering, and what would it mean to be free of it?*

### Purpose

Engage Buddhism as a serious philosophical and religious tradition, distinct from Hinduism. The philosophical claims (no-self, dependent origination, impermanence) are genuinely different from Hinduism's Brahman-Atman metaphysics. Do not reduce Buddhism to "mindfulness and meditation" — that is the Western retail version.

### Key Anchors

- **The Four Noble Truths** — (1) life is suffering (*dukkha*), (2) suffering is caused by craving/attachment, (3) suffering can cease, (4) the Eightfold Path leads to cessation
- **The Eightfold Path** — right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration
- **Three Marks of Existence** — *dukkha* (suffering), *anicca* (impermanence), *anatta* (no-self)
- **Nirvana** — cessation; the extinguishing of craving and thereby of suffering
- **Dependent Origination** — nothing has independent existence; all things arise in causal relation
- **Schools** — Theravada (the "Way of the Elders"), Mahayana (the "Great Vehicle"), Vajrayana (Tibetan)

### Core Philosophical Tension with Christianity

**The self.** Buddhism teaches *anatta* — the self is an illusion to be seen through and ultimately dissolved. Christianity teaches that every person is a uniquely created image-bearer of God with eternal significance. This is not a peripheral disagreement; it is categorical.

| 2 | Thu Dec 17 | Deep Dive Pt 1 (Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Biology, Psychology). The Four Noble Truths; the Three Marks of Existence (*dukkha*, *anicca*, *anatta*); how *anatta* differs from Hindu Atman. ### Gospel Conversation Focus

Desire and suffering. Buddhism and Christianity both take suffering with utter seriousness — but they diagnose it differently and prescribe categorically different solutions. Buddhism: suffering → attachment → extinguish attachment. Christianity: suffering → sin and separation from God → reunion with God through Christ, with desire redeemed rather than extinguished. The Buddhist convert to Christianity is often surprised that Christianity *affirms* desire (properly ordered) rather than denies it — and that Christianity preserves the *self* rather than dissolves it. Both moves are counterintuitive if you've internalized the Buddhist frame.

## Unit 8 — Expressive Individualism / Moralistic Therapeutic Deism

**Dates:** Mar 1 – Mar 11, 2027 (9 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** **Not covered.** Build from primary sources (Smith, Rieff, Trueman).
**Framing Question:** *What happens to a worldview when the self becomes the ultimate authority?*

### Purpose

**This is arguably the most important unit for student formation** because it is the actual operative worldview of most of their peers — including many Christian-schooled peers who have absorbed it without recognizing it. Students are far more likely to lose their faith to Moralistic Therapeutic Deism than to New Atheism.

### Key Concepts

**Moralistic Therapeutic Deism (MTD)** — coined by sociologist Christian Smith (*Soul Searching*, 2005):

1. A God exists who created the world and watches over human life.
2. God wants people to be good, nice, and fair to each other.
3. The central goal of life is to be happy and feel good about yourself.
4. God does not need to be involved in your life except when you need him.
5. Good people go to heaven when they die.

**Expressive Individualism** — the broader cultural framework (Philip Rieff, *Triumph of the Therapeutic*; Carl Trueman, *The Rise and Triumph of the Modern Self*):

- The self is the primary locus of truth and meaning
- Authenticity (being true to your inner self) is the highest virtue
- External moral authorities (tradition, church, scripture) are suspect
- Identity is self-constructed, not received
- Tolerance is the primary social virtue
- Therapy has replaced theology as the framework for understanding the human condition

### Why This Unit Matters

- Students are more likely to lose their faith to MTD than to atheism — MTD is Christian-adjacent, and Christian-schooled students can absorb it without knowing.
- The language is Christian-adjacent, making it hard to identify. ("Don't judge." "God just wants me to be happy." "Be true to yourself.")
- It produces people who say they're Christian but functionally live MTD.
- The Gospel is genuinely *offensive* to this worldview — it claims the self is not the ultimate authority and that we are not basically fine as we are.

Teach students to hold both the offense and the appeal together.

## Unit 9 — The Revolutionary Lineage: Marxism → Progressivism → Postmodernism

**Dates:** Mar 15 – Apr 8, 2027 (13 instructional days; spans Spring Break)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** Ch 5 (Marxism) + Ch 7 (Postmodernism) + primary sources on American Progressivism (not in textbook)
**Supplemental Outline:** [unit-06-marxism-to-postmodernism-outline.md](unit-06-marxism-to-postmodernism-outline.md) *(already drafted under the old unit number; the file should be renamed `unit-09-revolutionary-lineage-outline.md` when next revised)*
**Framing Question:** *How did class struggle become identity struggle, and why is "there are no metanarratives" itself a metanarrative?*

### Purpose

Teach the three movements as a genealogical arc, not three separate worldviews. The unifying thread: **critical deconstruction of existing power structures, a materialist or anti-realist view of truth, and a vision of human liberation through revolutionary change.**

This unit receives 13 days (instead of the default 9) because it covers three movements and because the American Progressive bridge (which Myers/Noebel does not cover) is essential for students to understand how European philosophical movements shaped American law, education, medicine, and family life.

### The Three Movements

**Movement 1 — Marxism** (Ch 5)
- Dialectical materialism; class conflict as the engine of history; religion as "opium of the people"; capitalism as exploitation; inevitable proletarian revolution
- Why it matters pedagogically: students need the philosophical root before they can recognize the branches

**Movement 2 — American Progressivism** (not in textbook — the bridge)
- Woodrow Wilson (administrative state; rejection of natural law and the Constitution as fixed)
- John Dewey (education as social reconstruction; rejection of transcendent truth)
- Margaret Sanger (eugenics as social progress; Planned Parenthood's founding ideology)
- Herbert Croly (nationalism + socialism hybrid)
- *Critical teaching point:* American Progressivism is not simply "liberal politics." It is a specific intellectual project that imported Hegelian and Marxist categories into American political life and explicitly rejected the Founders' framework. This is intellectual history, not partisan politics — teach it that way.

**Movement 3 — Postmodernism** (Ch 7)
- Nietzsche (death of God; will to power)
- Foucault (knowledge as power; all truth claims are power moves)
- Derrida (deconstruction; no stable meaning)
- Lyotard ("incredulity toward metanarratives")
- Inherits from Marxism: the critical deconstruction impulse; suspicion of all existing structures as oppressive
- Departs from Marxism: abandons the optimism about where history is going. There is no proletarian paradise — there is only deconstruction

### Gospel Conversation Focus

Truth and metanarrative. Postmodernism's greatest vulnerability is self-referential: *"there are no metanarratives"* is itself a metanarrative. The Gospel as a true story about the world — not merely a private religious preference — is the direct response. Christians must be able to say: yes, we are making a universal claim about reality; yes, we know that offends postmodern sensibilities; no, we will not reduce it to private preference to make it socially acceptable. The conversation requires courage and intellectual honesty in roughly equal measure.

## Unit 10 — Capstone

**Dates:** Apr 15 – Apr 29, 2027 (10 instructional days)
**Myers/Noebel Reference:** N/A — synthesis across the full year
**Framing Question:** *Can you defend the Christian worldview — charitably and intellectually — against the worldview that most challenges you?*

### Purpose

Integrate the full year. Students present a sustained defense of the Christian worldview against the worldview they found most personally challenging, demonstrating that they can (1) represent the opposing worldview charitably, (2) identify the decisive differences through the 10 lenses, (3) present the Christian response with intellectual honesty, and (4) articulate a Gospel conversation appropriate for a thoughtful proponent of that worldview.

### Capstone Structure

**Presentation (5 minutes):**
1. State the worldview you are addressing. Represent it fairly (your "steel-man" version — a proponent would recognize and affirm this).
2. Identify the central point of tension with the Christian worldview through the 10-lens grid (pick 2–3 disciplines; do not try to cover all 10).
3. Present the Christian response.
4. Articulate the Gospel conversation: how would you talk about Jesus with a thoughtful proponent of this worldview?

**Q&A (3 minutes):** classmates and teacher ask questions that probe the presentation. Expect challenges from the class — that's the point.

**Final Brief (handed in separately):** a 2-page written version of the presentation, more developed than a Worldview Brief, with at least 3 primary-source citations.

# Appendix A — Cohort Tools

This course uses four cohort tools throughout. Full specifications are in [_shared/cohort-tools.md](../../_shared/cohort-tools.md). Summary below:

- **Discussion Brief** — 1-page written response; collected at start of class period; graded on completion and thoughtfulness. Used as the Worldview Brief in this course (1 per unit).
- **Pair & Defend** — two-round simultaneous pair debate; sides assigned, then swapped so every student argues both sides; ~24 minutes total; debrief question: *"What was the strongest argument you heard from the side you started disagreeing with?"*
- **Case Study** — groups of 3–4; real-world scenarios; produce summary + recommended response + strongest counterargument; class vote; teacher debrief.
- **Capstone** — summative; 5-minute presentation + Q&A + final brief; graded on clarity of thought, evidence quality, and charitable engagement.

# Appendix B — Notes for the Teacher

1. **The worldview grid is the anchor.** Use [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md) as the physical reference (printed poster or handout) students return to every unit. By May, students should be able to fill in every cell from memory.

2. **SUB days are instructional.** There are 7 sub days in the year (Aug 28, Sep 3, Apr 5, Apr 12, Apr 19, Apr 26, May 3). Each is marked **SUB DAY** in the day-by-day schedules above with a specific activity suitable for a substitute to facilitate. Generate detailed substitute plans via `/generate-substitute-plan <week> <day>` when needed.

3. **Units 2, 3, 8 require primary source sourcing.** Judaism, Mormonism, and Expressive Individualism / MTD are not covered in Myers/Noebel. Budget prep time to assemble primary source excerpts before each unit begins. Unit 9 (Revolutionary Lineage) has [unit-06-marxism-to-postmodernism-outline.md](unit-06-marxism-to-postmodernism-outline.md) as a draft resource — the file should be renamed `unit-09-revolutionary-lineage-outline.md` when next revised, and the cross-references in this teaching map updated accordingly.

4. **Charitable representation is the curricular commitment.** This is the first criterion on the capstone rubric and the central pedagogical norm. A student who cannot represent a worldview fairly cannot engage it faithfully. Discipline this from Day 1 of Unit 0.

5. **Pair & Defend assigns positions students don't personally hold.** Several debates ask Christian students to defend positions critical of Christianity (e.g., *"Christianity's claim to be the one true worldview is arrogant"*; *"Karma is more morally satisfying than the Christian answer"*; *"Buddhist non-attachment is wiser than Christian longing"*). The two-round structure ensures every student argues both sides, so the "argue against Christianity" assignment is always paired with an "argue for Christianity" assignment for the same student. This is pedagogically essential for charitable engagement, but parents should be informed at the start of the year via the syllabus or a dedicated parent letter so the framing isn't a surprise.

6. **Breaks fall mid-unit.** Thanksgiving falls inside Unit 4 (Islam) — six days before, three days after. Christmas Break falls inside Unit 6 (Buddhism) — three days before, six days after. Spring Break falls inside Unit 9 (Revolutionary Lineage) — five days before (Marxism complete), eight days after (Progressivism + Postmodernism + integration). Each pre-break stretch reaches a natural pause point.

7. **Unit 8 is the most formative.** Expressive Individualism / MTD is the worldview most likely to be the students' own unexamined worldview. Expect pushback. Create room for honest self-recognition.

8. **The Gospel conversation rehearsals accumulate.** By Unit 10, each student has done 9 Gospel-conversation roleplays (one per worldview). The capstone tests their ability to do one well under pressure. Hold the bar high.

9. **Review dates once per trimester.** Re-verify against [_shared/school-calendar-2026-27.md](../../_shared/school-calendar-2026-27.md) at the start of each trimester.

10. **Assessment philosophy.** Grade on clarity of thought, quality of evidence, and charitable engagement — not polish or rhetoric. The Worldview Briefs are often more revealing than the tests; read them carefully.

11. **Resource flag — Ravi Zacharias.** Zacharias is cited in Unit 5 (Hinduism) and Unit 6 (Buddhism) as a contemporary resource. His work remains substantive but the 2020–21 RZIM disclosures are a live issue for many Christian schools. Revisit before the academic year and decide: keep, replace, or pair with a contextual note. Worth flagging now so the decision is intentional, not default.
