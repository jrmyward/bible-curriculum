**Unit:** Unit 0 — Foundations (Day 1 of 10)
**Topic:** First day of school · Course introduction · Opening question

## Learning Objectives

By end of class, students should be able to:

1. Articulate in one sentence what this course is about
2. Name the cohort norms (charitable engagement, steel-manning, no easy dismissals)
3. Offer at least one answer to the opening question: *"What shapes how you see the world?"*

## Class Schedule (45 min)

### 0:00–0:05 · Welcome & Attendance

- Greet students at the door as they enter
- Attendance, seating plan (if assigning)
- Brief intro: name, room norms, bathroom/water policy

### 0:05–0:15 · Syllabus Walk-Through

- Course title and what it IS: *Understanding the Times — A Survey of Competing Worldviews*. This year we study 9 worldviews including Christianity, Judaism, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Secularism, and the dominant worldviews shaping modern culture.
- What it is NOT: a Sunday school class, a lecture series where you memorize and move on, or a "Christian superiority" course. We will take every worldview seriously on its own terms.
- What will be expected of you: do the reading, show up prepared, engage charitably, present a worldview fairly even when you disagree with it.
- How you'll be graded: Worldview Briefs (1 per unit), Pair & Defend participation, unit tests, Capstone presentation.
- What you'll walk out with in May: the ability to represent any of 9 worldviews fairly and present the Gospel meaningfully to someone who holds one.

### 0:15–0:25 · Opening Question — *"What shapes how you see the world?"*

- Write the question on the board if not already.
- Ask each student to think silently for 1 minute. Then share with a partner for 2 minutes. Then open to the whole class.
- Possible student answers: family, church, school, friends, phone/internet, personal experiences, books, pain, culture.
- **Teacher move:** do not evaluate any answer. Just catch them and name them. Your only job today is to get students talking.
- End the discussion with: *"This is what a 'worldview' is — the lens through which you see everything. Everyone has one. The question is whether yours is examined."*

### 0:25–0:35 · What Is a Worldview? (brief lecture)

- A worldview is the set of answers — examined or unexamined — a person gives to life's ultimate questions.
- Everyone has a worldview. The question is not whether you have one; it's whether you've looked at it.
- We'll learn 9 in depth this year. We'll examine each through 10 lenses (preview the 10 disciplines briefly — Theology, Philosophy, Ethics, Biology, Psychology, Sociology, Law, Politics, Economics, History). Don't worry about memorizing them today.
- Essential course norm: **charitable engagement.** When we examine Islam in September–November, we will represent it the way a thoughtful Muslim would recognize as fair. Same for every worldview. If we cannot represent a view fairly, we cannot meaningfully disagree with it.

### 0:35–0:45 · Preview the Year & Assign Homework

- Show the worldview grid poster briefly (no need to explain every cell — that happens Thursday).
- Preview: this Friday is a sub day (teacher out). Sub will give you a video and a reflection journal. Treat this like any normal class day — the reflection journal is your first graded assignment.
- Assign reading: **Myers Ch 1 (Battle of Ideas) pp 1–15, due Wednesday.** Bring one question from the reading to class.
- End with a small mystery: *"Ch 1 is called 'Battle of Ideas.' Think about why. We'll unpack that Wednesday."*

## Teacher Notes

- Day 1 is mostly culture-setting. Don't try to cover too much content. The course is 154 days long; pacing starts tomorrow.
- Watch for: which students engage, which sit silent, which project confidence they don't have. Take mental notes for seating and pairings later.
- **The opening question sets the tone for the year.** Every student should feel that their answer was heard and respected, even if it was surface-level. That signals "this is a classroom where you can think out loud."
- If textbooks aren't in student hands yet, print Myers Ch 1 excerpts for Wednesday's class — don't delay the reading.
- If class runs long or short, the flex goes in Section 0:25–0:35 (the lecture).
