# Day 4 — Thu Aug 27, 2026

**Unit:** Unit 0 — Foundations (Day 4 of 10)
**Topic:** Preview the 9 worldviews · Guided tour of the worldview grid

---

## Learning Objectives

By end of class, students should be able to:

1. Name all 9 worldviews the course will cover
2. Place each on a rough map (how many adherents, where in the world, religious vs. secular, etc.)
3. Identify which worldview(s) they'd like to spend more time on — curiosity, not judgment

---

## Materials

- Full worldview grid (9 × 10) as handout — 1 per student
- Worldview grid wall poster (same content, large format)
- "Primary-source starter links" handout for Friday's assignment — 1 per student (or digital)
- Optional: world map showing approximate distributions (where Islam is dominant, where Hinduism is dominant, etc.)

## Class Schedule (45 min)

### 0:00–0:05 · Check-In

- Attendance
- Quick share-out: *"Yesterday we talked about the 'battle of ideas.' Did anyone notice a worldview claim in the wild — news, conversation, social media — since we last met? Share one in 15 seconds."*
- Transition: *"Today we meet the nine combatants in Myers' battle."*

### 0:05–0:35 · Guided Tour of the Worldview Grid (30 min)

Hand out the grid. Walk through each column at ~3 minutes each. Keep it conversational — this is a preview, not a deep dive.

For each worldview, cover briefly:
1. **Name** and any common aliases
2. **Rough number of adherents worldwide**
3. **Where you'd most likely meet someone who holds it** (geography or social context)
4. **One surprising or counterintuitive fact**

Suggested order (matches teaching order):

1. **Christianity** (~2.4 billion adherents, global) — *"This is the baseline. Everything else in this course compares back to this column. We'll spend three weeks just on this one."*
2. **Judaism** (~15 million, global) — *"Christianity came out of Judaism. They share the Old Testament, but they went in different directions at a specific historical moment — 70 AD, when the Temple was destroyed. We'll unpack that."*
3. **Mormonism / LDS** (~17 million, concentrated in western U.S.) — *"Claims to be Christian. Uses the same vocabulary — 'God,' 'Jesus,' 'salvation.' But means categorically different things by them. In southeast Idaho, you'll meet Mormons constantly. This unit matters."*
4. **Islam** (~1.9 billion, world's second-largest and fastest-growing) — *"Shares a lot of the same prophetic lineage — Abraham, Moses. But a completely different view of who God is and who Jesus is. We'll meet Islam on its own terms, charitably."*
5. **Hinduism** (~1.2 billion, concentrated in India) — *"The world's oldest surviving major religion. Philosophically sophisticated. Nothing like Western monotheism."*
6. **Buddhism** (~520 million, concentrated in East and Southeast Asia) — *"Came out of Hinduism but went in a different direction. Denies the existence of a permanent self. Has quietly reshaped Western culture in ways most people don't notice."*
7. **Secularism / New Atheism** (~1.2 billion globally explicit; the unexamined worldview of much of the Western professional class) — *"The dominant worldview in American universities, media, and courts. You will encounter secular professors, coworkers, and friends your whole adult life."*
8. **Expressive Individualism / Moralistic Therapeutic Deism** (operative worldview of most American teens, including Christian-schooled teens) — *"This is probably the worldview your friends actually hold, even if they'd call themselves Christian. The idea that God exists but is basically a distant cheerleader, that morality is being nice, and that the goal of life is to be true to yourself. This is the most important unit in the course for your own formation."*
9. **The Revolutionary Lineage** (Marxism → Progressivism → Postmodernism — framework influencing much of contemporary academic and cultural thought) — *"One unit covering three connected movements. This is what's behind CRT, gender ideology, and the current cultural conversation about 'deconstructing power.' We'll trace the genealogy."*

### 0:35–0:45 · Questions + Friday Setup

- Open for questions. Likely ones:
  - *"What about [X worldview] that's not here?"* → Honest answer: we had to choose 9 that matter most for your lives. The grid is not exhaustive.
  - *"Which one are WE going to conclude is right?"* → Christianity. This is a Christian school and this teacher is openly Christian. But the goal is not to "win" against other worldviews — it's to understand them so well that you can speak to them with integrity.
  - *"Will we read anyone who actually holds these views?"* → Yes. Every unit includes primary source readings. Starting with tomorrow's homework.
- Hand out the primary-source starter links for Friday.
- **Friday is a sub day.** Remind them: *"I won't be here Friday. A substitute will run the class. There's a video and a reflection journal. Treat it like any normal day — the journal is graded."*

## Teacher Notes

- Day 4 is breadth, not depth. The temptation is to say too much about any one worldview — resist it. Students will get 10–15 days on each one later.
- **Watch for:** students who react strongly (positively or negatively) to a specific worldview preview. That's a clue to how they'll engage when that unit arrives.
- The "which one are we going to conclude is right?" question deserves a direct, honest answer. Be clear: the pedagogy is charitable engagement; the teacher's convictions are Christian; the student's task is to examine, not to perform a predetermined answer.
- **Sub day prep:** before leaving Thursday, confirm the sub folder is ready. See [substitute-plan-friday.md](substitute-plan-friday.md) for the checklist.
