# Day 3 — Wed Aug 26, 2026

**Unit:** Unit 0 — Foundations (Day 3 of 10)
**Topic:** Read Myers Ch 1 (Battle of Ideas) in class · Socratic discussion

---

## Learning Objectives

By end of class, students should be able to:

1. Summarize Myers' central claim in Ch 1 ("there is an actual battle over ideas, and every person is a combatant whether they know it or not")
2. Identify at least one place where their own worldview was shaped without their awareness
3. Begin to distinguish "things I've been told" from "things I've examined"

---

## Materials

- Myers Ch 1 printed excerpts (in case textbooks aren't yet distributed — 3–4 key passages highlighted)
- Discussion questions on slides (see below)
- Collect yesterday's homework paragraph ("Which of the 10 questions do you find hardest?")

---

## Class Schedule (45 min)

### 0:00–0:05 · Collect & Preview

- Collect homework paragraphs at the door as students enter.
- Read 1–2 strong submissions aloud to kick off discussion (with permission — ask beforehand if possible).
- Bridge: *"Most of you said some version of 'it's hard to answer because I've never really thought about it.' That's exactly Myers' point in Ch 1."*

### 0:05–0:20 · Read-Aloud — Myers Ch 1 Key Passages (15 min)

- Distribute printed excerpts (if textbooks not yet in hand) OR ask students to open Ch 1 in their books.
- Select 3–4 key passages (teacher-chosen; suggestions below) and read them aloud:
  - Myers' opening: a scene or anecdote that frames "worldview as battleground"
  - Myers' definition of worldview (typically in first 5 pages)
  - A passage on why ideas have consequences (why worldview matters, not just for academics)
  - A passage on the "battle of ideas" metaphor — who's fighting, for what
- **Teacher move:** pause after each passage and invite a reaction. Don't over-explain — let students sit with the quote.

### 0:20–0:40 · Socratic Discussion (20 min)

Drive the discussion from students' questions (the ones they brought from the homework) and these prompts:

1. *"Myers uses 'battle' as his metaphor. What's true about that metaphor? What's misleading?"*
2. *"Where do most people get their worldview? Is it examined or inherited?"*
3. *"Can a person have a coherent worldview without knowing it? Can they have an incoherent one without knowing?"*
4. *"Myers assumes there IS a right answer to these 10 questions. Some worldviews would disagree — they'd say every answer is just your opinion. What's the difference between those two claims?"*

**Teacher move:** let students talk to each other. If a student says something sharp, ask a classmate to respond before you do. Your job is to redirect, not resolve.

Possible pushbacks students may raise — prepare for these:
- *"Why does it matter what people believe? Can't we just let everyone believe what they want?"* → Great question. Answer: because beliefs produce actions, and actions produce consequences. We'll spend the year seeing how.
- *"Isn't calling it a 'battle' too aggressive?"* → Legitimate point. Distinguish Myers' rhetorical frame from the actual pedagogical approach of the course, which is charitable engagement.

### 0:40–0:45 · Closing & Bridge

- Pull the thread together: *"Worldviews aren't neutral, aren't automatic, and aren't obvious. That's why we spend a year on them."*
- Bridge to tomorrow: *"Tomorrow I'll show you the 9 worldviews we'll study. Some you've heard of. Some you haven't. All have real people holding them. Our job is to take all of them seriously."*
- Homework reminder for Friday: *"Choose ONE worldview on the grid you know nothing about. I'll hand out a resource list Thursday. Read one primary-source paragraph before Friday."*

## Teacher Notes

- Ch 1 is dense. Don't expect students to have mastered it — you're mostly introducing the framing vocabulary ("worldview," "battle of ideas") and getting them thinking.
- **If textbooks haven't arrived yet:** printed excerpts are essential. Don't push the Ch 1 reading to next week — students need the vocabulary in hand.
- If discussion is dead (common on a Day 3 of the year), go to the pushback questions above and turn them into a Pair & Defend-lite: split the class in half, give one side the "beliefs don't matter, just be nice" position and the other the "beliefs shape everything" position, give them 90 seconds to confer, then 2 minutes of whole-class back-and-forth.
- **Pacing warning:** the Socratic section eats time. If it's 0:35 and you've only gotten through 1 question, land the plane — move to closing/bridge.
