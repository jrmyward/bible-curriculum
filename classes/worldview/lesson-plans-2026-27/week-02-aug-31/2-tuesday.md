# Day 7 — Tue Sep 1, 2026

**Unit:** Unit 0 — Foundations (Day 7 of 10)
**Topic:** The Gospel as a year-long thread · distinguishing "the Gospel" from "Christian culture"

---

## Learning Objectives

By end of class, students should be able to:

1. Articulate the Gospel in 3 sentences or fewer (God, sin, Christ, redemption — in their own words)
2. Distinguish what is ESSENTIAL to the Gospel from what is Christian cultural expression
3. Understand that every worldview unit this year ends with a Gospel conversation specific to that worldview

---

## Materials

- Gospel outline handout — 1 per student (see [handouts/](handouts/))
- Two short case studies (or roleplays) for comparing "Christian culture" vs. "the Gospel" — see below
- Blank note cards for Gospel-summary drafting

## Class Schedule (45 min)

### 0:00–0:10 · What Is the Gospel? (tight lecture)

- Hand out the Gospel outline.
- Teach a clean version — don't over-qualify.

**The Gospel in one paragraph:**
> God created a good world and placed people in it to know Him, love Him, and reflect His character. People rebelled — this is what the Bible calls sin — and the relationship with God was broken. God did not abandon us; He came Himself, in the person of Jesus, fully God and fully human. Jesus lived the faithful human life we couldn't, died a death we deserved, and rose again, defeating sin and death. Because of Jesus, anyone who trusts Him is reconciled to God, given new life now, and promised a renewed creation to come. This is not earned by our performance — it is received as a gift.

**The Gospel in four words:** God. Sin. Christ. Redemption.

**Not the Gospel (but sometimes confused with it):**
- American civic religion
- "Be a good person"
- "God wants you happy"
- Voting a particular way
- Going to church
- Not doing specific sins

None of these are the Gospel. Some are fine things; some are distortions.

### 0:10–0:25 · Case Study: Christian Culture vs. The Gospel (15 min)

Two short scenarios. Read each aloud. Ask students to identify what's "culture" and what's "Gospel."

**Scenario 1:**
> A student invites his friend from another school to his youth group. The evening includes worship music, pizza, a talk by the youth pastor about "living a Christian life that honors God," and closing prayer. The friend leaves and says, *"That was nice, but I still don't really know what you believe or why."*
>
> **Question:** What was missing?

Possible class discussion:
- The friend encountered Christian *culture* (songs, community, a talk about how to live) without hearing the Gospel (who Jesus is and what He accomplished).
- This isn't a criticism of youth group — it's a warning that Christian spaces can operate without anyone naming the Gospel explicitly.

**Scenario 2:**
> A coworker of your dad's is going through a hard time — his marriage is falling apart. Your dad doesn't attend church, but when the coworker asks *"do you think God is real?"*, your dad says *"yeah, I think so, and I think He loves you. Why do you ask?"* That's the start of a conversation that eventually leads to the coworker meeting Jesus.
>
> **Question:** Was that an evangelism conversation? What was present, and what wasn't?

Discussion:
- No church service, no songs, no tract. Just honesty and openness.
- The Gospel doesn't require a stage. It requires someone willing to be honest about what they believe and why.

### 0:25–0:35 · The Gospel Conversation — Year-Long Thread (10 min)

Preview: *"Every unit this year ends with a 'Gospel conversation' day. You will learn to articulate the Gospel 9 different times — adapted for the worldview of the person you're talking to. A conversation with a Muslim friend sounds different from a conversation with a secular friend from a conversation with a Buddhist friend. Not because the Gospel changes — it doesn't — but because the entry point does, and the objections do, and the language that lands does."*

- Christianity unit: practice the baseline Gospel conversation.
- Judaism unit: focus on atonement (the Temple-sacrifice question).
- Islam unit: focus on who Jesus is.
- Hinduism unit: focus on the self and personal love.
- Buddhism unit: focus on suffering and desire.
- Secularism unit: focus on meaning, morality, and death.
- Expressive Individualism unit: focus on the offense of the cross to "I'm basically fine."
- Revolutionary Lineage unit: focus on truth as metanarrative.

You'll practice each one. The final exam has an oral Gospel-conversation component.

### 0:35–0:45 · Draft Your 3-Sentence Gospel Summary

- Hand out blank note cards.
- **Task:** In 3 sentences or fewer, write your own articulation of the Gospel. Don't copy from the handout. Use your own words.
- Collect. These are NOT graded — the teacher is reading to understand the class's starting point. Students will rewrite this summary at the end of Unit 1 (Christianity) and again at the end of Unit 10.

## Teacher Notes

- The Gospel-summary collection is diagnostic. Read through them Tuesday evening. Some will be solid. Some will be culture-not-Gospel. Some will be MTD in disguise ("God wants me to be a good person"). Do NOT correct publicly — notice and use as teacher baseline.
- **The year-long Gospel thread is the single most distinctive feature of this course.** Tell students that directly. They've been through enough youth ministry and Bible curricula to think "yeah, I know the drill." Make clear this isn't that.
- Resist the urge to make Day 7 into a full Gospel presentation. That comes in Unit 1. Today is the *framing* that the Gospel is a through-line — not an exhaustive teaching.
- If a student says *"I'm not a Christian,"* this is a formation moment, not a problem. Affirm: *"Good. Then you'll be the one arguing the 'no' side in debates, and you'll make them better. This course isn't about conversion — it's about examination. Stay honest with us and we'll stay honest with you."*
