# Steel-Manning vs. Straw-Manning

**The First Curricular Commitment of This Course**

---

## The Two Moves

### Straw-Manning

**Definition:** Misrepresenting an opposing position to make it easier to knock down.

**Examples:**
- *"Atheists just want to sin without feeling guilty."*
- *"Christians believe the earth is flat because the Bible says so."*
- *"Muslims want to take over the world."*
- *"Progressives want to destroy the family."*

**What's wrong with it:** In each case, there are real people who hold the opposing view for reasons you haven't considered. By picking the worst version, you ensure you never actually engage the strong version. You "win" the argument against your own imagination.

### Steel-Manning

**Definition:** Representing an opposing position in its **strongest**, most **charitable** form — the version the person holding it would nod at and say *"yes, that's what I mean."*

**Examples:**

| Straw version | Steel-manned version |
|---|---|
| *"Atheists just want to sin without feeling guilty."* | *"Many atheists believe the evidence for God's existence is inconclusive, and that living morally and meaningfully without religious belief is both possible and intellectually honest."* |
| *"Muslims want to take over the world."* | *"Many Muslims believe Islam provides a complete framework for both private devotion and public life, and that a society governed by Islamic principles would be more just than one governed by secular ones."* |
| *"Progressives want to destroy the family."* | *"Many progressives believe traditional family structures have historically excluded or harmed people (women, LGBTQ individuals, divorced parents), and that expanding our definition of 'family' protects more people from those harms."* |

Notice: in each steel-manned version, we're not agreeing with the view. We're representing it the way its strongest proponent would.

---

## Why This Is the Rule of This Classroom

1. **Because it's the first ethical move.** People who hold views you disagree with have reasons. Taking those reasons seriously treats them as agents, not opponents.

2. **Because it makes real disagreement possible.** If you only know the straw version, you're arguing with your own imagination. You'll never change anyone's mind, and you'll never test whether your own view survives serious challenge.

3. **Because it's the skill you need.** This year ends with you sitting across from someone who holds one of these worldviews and trying to articulate the Gospel in a way that's meaningful to them. You cannot do that if you only know the caricature.

4. **Because it models intellectual humility.** The strongest form of an opposing view is often more defensible than the straw version you pictured. Sometimes you'll change your mind about *something* — not the whole worldview, but a nuance. That's growth, not weakness.

---

## The Steel-Man Test

Before you engage an opposing view, ask yourself: **"If a thoughtful proponent of this view were standing here right now, would they hear my summary as fair?"**

- If **yes** → you're steel-manning.
- If **no** → you're straw-manning. Go back and try again.

---

## Practice (In Class Today)

You'll be paired with a partner and assigned a side on a light topic. Your job:

1. **For 2 minutes:** Argue your assigned side. Steel-man it. Make the strongest case for a view you may not personally hold.
2. **Your partner's job:** Listen carefully. At the end of 2 minutes, they'll try to restate your argument in a way YOU would affirm as fair.
3. **Swap.** Then debrief as a class.

**Topics** (your pair will draw from these):

- *"Pineapple belongs on pizza"* vs. *"Pineapple does not belong on pizza"*
- *"Cats are better pets than dogs"* vs. *"Dogs are better pets than cats"*
- *"Summer is the best season"* vs. *"Fall is the best season"*
- *"Video games are a good use of teenage time"* vs. *"Video games are a bad use of teenage time"*

These are low-stakes on purpose. The SKILL you're learning is steel-manning — not how to argue about pizza.

---

## One Rule You Cannot Break

**You may not refuse your assigned side.**

If you refuse to argue a position because you don't personally hold it, you've missed the point of the exercise — and of the course. The exercise is not about endorsement. It's about representation.

This rule applies to every Pair & Defend we do this year. When we get to harder topics (Islam, CRT, atheism, MTD), you will be assigned sides you may strongly disagree with. That's the point. If you refuse to steel-man a view, you cannot meaningfully disagree with it.

---

## The Big Idea

> **Understanding a position is not the same as agreeing with it.**
>
> The strongest critique of any worldview comes from someone who has represented it fairly first.

This is how we're going to do this year.
