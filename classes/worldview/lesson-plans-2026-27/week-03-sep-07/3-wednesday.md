# Day 1 — Wed Sep 9, 2026

**Unit:** Unit 1 — Christianity (Day 1 of 13) · First day of the worldview baseline
**Topic:** Framing the unit + the worldview-as-a-whole content (publisher Ch 02)
**Class Period:** 50 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By end of class, students should be able to:

1. Recognize C. S. Lewis's framing of Christianity as *"the great myth that turned out to be true"* and explain what that claim entails (not metaphor; not advice; the one story of the world that actually happened)
2. Tell the **Christian Story in Four Acts** — Creation → Fall → Redemption → Restoration — with one anchor verse for each
3. State Christianity's claim that God is **personal, transcendent, immanent, and relational** (the Trinity foreshadowed)
4. Distinguish **general revelation** from **special revelation** and name what each is for
5. Identify the four apologetics passages (Col 2:8 · Acts 17 · 2 Cor 10:5 · 1 Pet 3:15) and recognize each quote on sight

Narrate the four acts with board diagram. *Creation → Fall → Redemption → Restoration*. Hit testable claims: companionship/stewardship/work as creative design (Q3); sin infects humans + creation but not God himself (Q4); Adam and Eve broke three relationships (Q5); eternity is *redemption* of the physical, not escape (Q20); Bible as universal history (Q18). General revelation vs. special revelation. Board diagram. Publisher's exact wording for each (Q30, Q31 fill-in-the-blank). Testable claims: general revelation removes excuses (Q21); must know what passage means before applying (Q22); three resources to understand the Bible — careful study, Holy Spirit, body of Christ (Q7). ## Teacher Notes

- This is a **content-heavy day** — about half the Day-13 test comes from today's worldview-as-a-whole material. Don't shortchange it.
- The Tolkien/Lewis opening sets the unit's emotional register. Adolescents perk up at "an actual story of two real men on a real night in 1931" in a way they don't perk up at "Christianity claims X." Tell the story; don't summarize it.
- **Pacing risk:** the Four-Act story tempts a teacher into preaching. Don't. Narrate. Hit the testable claims explicitly so students can mark them in their outlines.
- The Eight Anchors preview at 0:46–0:49 is just names — *don't* try to define them. Each anchor gets its own treatment in Days 2–4 in its corresponding lens.
