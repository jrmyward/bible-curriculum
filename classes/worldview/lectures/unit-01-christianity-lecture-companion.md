## Day 1 — Framing + The Worldview as a Whole [50 min]

**Goal:** Students leave with (a) the motivation for why we start with Christianity and the shape of the 13 days, and (b) the **worldview-as-a-whole content** from publisher Ch 02 — the four-act story, the eight anchors with substance, general/special revelation, the personal-transcendent-immanent God, eternity-as-redemption, and the four apologetics passages.

**This is a content-heavy day.** About half the Day-13 test comes from the Ch 02 worldview-comprehensive material, not from the 10 lenses. Days 2–4 cover Ch 8–17 (the lens-by-lens material). Today covers Ch 02. Don't shortchange it.

**On the board before students arrive:**

```
Unit 1 — Christianity
"What is the Christian answer to each of the 10 disciplines,
 and why?"
```

### Opening hook — Tolkien's walk with Lewis [6 min] *(testable: Q1)*

The reading packet opens with C. S. Lewis calling Christianity *"the great myth that turned out to be true."* That phrase came from a real night. September 19, 1931. Lewis, Tolkien, and Hugo Dyson walked Addison's Walk at Magdalen College, Oxford, talking until 3 a.m. Lewis was a theist by then but still couldn't accept that Christianity was anything more than one myth among many. Tolkien — a devout Catholic — argued that Christianity *was* myth in the literary sense: a story that captures the deepest patterns of human longing. The difference was that this one *happened*. The Gospels are the myth of the dying-and-rising God, except in this case the dying-and-rising God walked out of a real tomb in real Judea on a real Sunday morning.

Twelve days later, Lewis wrote to his friend Arthur Greeves: *"I have just passed on from believing in God to definitely believing in Christ. My long night talk with Dyson and Tolkien had a good deal to do with it."*

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *"The great myth that turned out to be true" — not metaphor, not advice, but the one story of the world that actually happened.*

That's the claim. Make sure students hear it cleanly: Christianity is not "Christianity is metaphorically true the way Greek myths teach us lessons." It is not "true if we have faith." It is the claim that the deepest pattern of every human story — sacrifice, love, return, restoration — happens to be the literal pattern of cosmic history.

### Quick orientation [6 min]

"Why this unit is 13 days, why we start here, and what to expect."

- **Why first?** Christianity is the baseline. Every other worldview in this course will be examined by comparison. Without the baseline in hand, the comparisons don't work.
- **13 days?** Three lecture days (today, 2, 3, 4), five discussion/practice days (5, 6, 7, 8, 11), three reflection/synthesis days (9, 10, 12), and the test on 13. I'll tell you exactly what's on the test as we go. No trick questions.
- **Bring this every day:** the [learning-outline-student.md](../handouts/unit-01-christianity-learning-outline-student.md) blank fill-in. Hand it out now if you haven't.

### The Christian Story in Four Acts [12 min] *(testable: Q2, Q3, Q4, Q5, Q18, Q20)*

**WRITE ON BOARD:**

```
CREATION → FALL → REDEMPTION → RESTORATION
   |          |         |             |
 Gen 1     Gen 3    Gospels        Rev 21
```

The Christian worldview is *centered on the person of Jesus Christ.* John Stott: *"Christ is the centre of Christianity; all else is circumference."* Now narrate the four acts. Don't just name them.

**Creation [3 min].** Genesis 1:31 — God looks at what he made and calls it *meod tob* — "very good." That Hebrew phrase means "exceedingly, heartbreakingly, abundantly, richly, immeasurably good." The world was *made* good, not neutral. Three things were part of the creative design for humans (write on board): **companionship · stewardship · work** *(Q3)*. We were made to be in relationship, to take care of what God made, and to participate in the ongoing creative work.

**Fall [3 min].** Genesis 3. The first humans broke faith with their Creator. The theological word is *sin.* Cornelius Plantinga defines it as *"the smearing of a relationship, the grieving of one's divine parent and benefactor, a betrayal of the partner to whom one is joined by a holy bond."* Augustine described it as *incurvatus in se* — Latin for "curved in on itself." We pursue everything for our own sakes; the spiral is always downward. The fall infected (write on board): **human beings · God's creation** — but **not God himself** *(Q4)*. And Adam and Eve's disobedience broke (write on board): **their relationship with God · with each other · all human relationships** *(Q5)*.

**Redemption [3 min].** God did not abandon what he made. From the moment of the fall — Genesis 3:15 promises the serpent will be crushed — through the Passover, the Law, the Prophets, finally through the incarnation, death, and resurrection of his Son, God redeems his wayward creation. The serpent who said *"Take, eat"* in the garden is defeated by the Savior who said *"Take, eat"* in the upper room. This is the central act. Christ has come, has died, has risen.

**Restoration [3 min].** And the story doesn't end there. *"Behold, I am making all things new"* (Rev. 21:5). Read this verse twice. Note the word order — it's not "all new things." It is "all *things* new." The same things, restored. Eternity is the **redemption** of the physical world, **not the escape** from it *(Q20)*. That is a Christian distinctive. Most religions promise escape; Christianity promises *renewal*.

**WRITE ON BOARD:** The Bible is not simply a book of religion — it is an explanation of **universal history** *(Q18)*.

### The Personal, Transcendent, Immanent God [6 min] *(testable: Q14, Q15, Q16, Q17, Q44)*

Christianity claims four things about God that distinguish it from every other worldview. Write them on the board as you teach them:

```
1. PERSONAL          — self-aware, creative, volitional, rational,
                         relational, communicative
2. TRANSCENDENT      — beyond and separate from the world
3. IMMANENT          — and yet present in the world
4. RELATIONAL        — God IS relationship (Trinity)
```

The publisher's phrasing matters. Christianity teaches that God is **a person to be known, not a force to be used** *(Q15)*. The existence of mankind suggests God is **personal**, not impersonal *(Q14)*. He is both *transcendent* (beyond and separate from the world) and *immanent* (in the world) *(Q16)*. And he is *relational* — not solitary *(Q17)*. The Trinity is the Christian claim that relationship is not something God *does* but something God *is*. *(We'll go deeper on the Trinity in Lens 1 tomorrow.)*

### How Christians Know What They Know [10 min] *(testable: Q6, Q7, Q21, Q22, Q30, Q31, Q45)*

Christians believe God has revealed himself in **two ways.** Write them on the board:

```
GENERAL REVELATION                 SPECIAL REVELATION
"to all people"                    "to particular people"
through creation,                  through Scripture,
conscience, the                    miraculous events,
structure of human                 and supremely
existence                          Jesus Christ
Ps 19:1–6 · Rom 1:18–20            Ps 19:7–11 · 2 Tim 3:14–17 · Jn 1:1–18
```

**Define each cleanly (publisher's exact wording — they are fill-in-the-blank items, Q30 and Q31):**

- **General revelation** [2.4] — *God's revelation of himself to all people through creation.*
- **Special revelation** [2.4] — *God's revelation of himself through the Bible, miraculous events, and Jesus Christ.*

**The key distinction (testable, Q6, Q21, Q45):** General revelation can tell us *that* God exists — it can lead a person *to* God by revealing something about his nature. But basic knowledge through general revelation is limited; a *personal and saving* knowledge comes only through *special* revelation. General revelation's job, in part, is to **remove people's excuses for not believing in God** *(Q21)*. The two work together. Christianity does not ask people to take a blind leap; it asks them to consider both kinds of evidence.

**Reading the Bible well.** We must know what a biblical passage **means** before we can accurately apply it to our lives *(Q22)*. What resources help us understand the Bible? Three (Q7): **careful study · the Holy Spirit · the body of Christ** (the community of Christians, past and present).

### The Four Apologetics Passages [6 min] *(testable: Q32, Q33, Q34, Q35 — fill-in-the-blank)*

These four Scripture passages appear on the Day-13 test as fill-in-the-blank: students must identify which passage each quote comes from. Walk through them in order. Have students underline each one in the reading packet.

**WRITE ON BOARD:**

```
Col 2:8        — "Don't be taken captive by philosophy"
Acts 17        — Paul vs. Epicureans + Stoics in Athens
2 Cor 10:5     — "Take every thought captive to Christ"
1 Pet 3:15     — "Always be prepared to make a defense"
```

Briefly narrate each one (1 min apiece + 2 min intro and close):

- **Colossians 2:8** — *"See to it that no one takes you captive by philosophy and empty deceit, according to human tradition, according to the elemental spirits of the world, and not according to Christ."* Paul is not against philosophy. He's against *bad* philosophy that displaces Christ.
- **Acts 17** — Paul in Athens at the Areopagus. He engages the two dominant philosophical schools of his day: the *atheistic* Epicureans and the *pantheistic* Stoics. He quotes their own poets back to them. He doesn't run from the philosophers; he engages them on their own ground.
- **Second Corinthians 10:5** — *"We destroy arguments and every lofty opinion raised against the knowledge of God, and take every thought captive to obey Christ."* This is the apologetic charge — Christians are to use their minds offensively, not just defensively.
- **First Peter 3:15** — *"But in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy, always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you; yet do it with gentleness and respect."* The full Christian apologetic posture: ready to defend, but with gentleness and respect. Both halves matter.

"You will see all four of these on the test. The format will be: I give you the quote, you tell me which passage it came from. There are no trick questions; just memorize the four pairings."

## Day 2 — Deep Dive Pt 1: Theology · Philosophy · Ethics [50 min]

**Total budget:** 50 min · 5 min opening · 12 min × 3 lenses · 9 min synthesis/transition

**On the board before students arrive:**

```
Day 2 — Three lenses
1. Theology — Who is God?
2. Philosophy — What is real?
3. Ethics — What is good?
```

### Opening review [5 min]

"What's the one claim Christianity makes that, if it's wrong, makes everything else collapse?" Take 2–3 student answers. The answer you're listening for: *the resurrection* (1 Cor. 15:14). If no one says it, name it.

This is the through-line for the entire unit. Christianity is *falsifiable* in a way most religions are not. That's a feature, not a bug. Pivot to today's lenses.

### Lens 1 — Theology [12 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Trinitarian Monotheism — one God, three persons, eternal relationship.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity claims God is one Being who has existed forever as three coequal, coeternal Persons (Father, Son, Holy Spirit), in eternal relationship with one another.* Relationship is not something God *does*. It is something God *is*.

This matters because: every other major theistic worldview gets at least one of those words wrong. Islam says God is one — but rejects the Trinity (Unit 3). Mormonism uses "Trinity" language but means three separate beings (Unit 4). Hinduism affirms divinity everywhere but it's impersonal (Unit 5). Christianity is the only one that says God is *both* radically one *and* eternally relational *and* personal.

**Story to tell (not in reading packet):** The Trinity wasn't invented at Nicaea in AD 325 — it was *defined* there because a priest named Arius was teaching that Jesus was a *created* being, the first and greatest of God's works but not God himself. The council pulled together bishops from across the Roman Empire, some of whom still bore scars from the recent persecutions under Diocletian. Athanasius, then a young deacon, argued the case for what we now call Trinitarian orthodoxy: Jesus is *of one Being with the Father* (*homoousios*, in Greek). One letter — an *iota* — separated Athanasius's *homoousios* (same substance) from the Arian *homoiousios* (similar substance). That's where the English idiom "not one iota of difference" comes from. The Nicene Creed is the result.

**KEY VOCAB (board, then students fill in their outline):**
- **Trinitarian Monotheism** [8.6] — one God recognized as three separate and distinct persons: Father, Son, and Spirit
- **General revelation** [2.4] — God's universal self-disclosure through creation (Ps. 19:1–6; Rom. 1:18–20)
- **Special revelation** [2.4] — God's particular self-disclosure through Scripture, miraculous events, and supremely Jesus Christ
- **Personal God** [2.3a] — God possesses the characteristics of a person: self-aware, creative, volitional, rational, relational, communicative

**Scripture anchor (write on board):** *Isaiah 44:6 — "I am the first, and I am the last; besides me there is no god."*

### Lens 2 — Philosophy [12 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Realism + Mind/Body Dualism — reality exists outside our heads; we are both body and soul.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity claims the world is real, knowable, and given to us — not constructed by our minds. And humans are not just brains in skulls; we are an integrated unity of immaterial soul and material body.*

This matters because: most modern philosophy has gone in the opposite direction. Postmodernism (Unit 9) says we *construct* reality through language and power. Secularism (Unit 8) says the mind *is* the brain — nothing more. Buddhism (Unit 6) says the enduring self is an illusion. Christianity disagrees with all three on the same grounds: God made a real world, and made us real persons in it.

**Story to tell:** When the early Christian apologists encountered Greek philosophy, they didn't run from it. Paul went to the Areopagus in Athens (Acts 17) — the meeting place of the city's philosophers — and quoted *their own poets* back to them: *"In him we live and move and have our being… we are indeed his offspring."* He critiqued the atheistic Epicureans and the pantheistic Stoics from inside their own categories. Christianity has *never* been anti-philosophy. It has been anti–*bad* philosophy. There's a difference.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Realism** [9.7] — objective reality exists independent of human perception
- **Mind/body dualism** [9.7c, 12.8] — humans have both an immaterial mind and a material body
- **Metaphysics** [9.1] — the study of ultimate reality
- **Epistemology** [9.1] — the study of knowledge
- **Christian sources of knowledge** [9.7a] — science, history, experience, and God

**Scripture anchor:** *John 1:1 — "In the beginning was the Word…"*

### Lens 3 — Ethics [12 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Agape — self-giving love, grounded in God's character. "Love God; love your neighbor."*

The thing you're teaching: *Christian ethics is grounded in God's character, not in human consensus, divine command, or evolutionary advantage. Right and wrong are real. The summary command is to love God with everything and to love your neighbor as yourself.*

This matters because: every alternative — moral relativism (Unit 9), evolutionary ethics (Unit 8), karmic ethics (Units 5–6) — locates morality somewhere other than in a personal, loving God. Each of those alternatives runs into a wall: relativism cannot condemn the Nazis; evolutionary ethics cannot tell you why you *should* do what evolution selected for; karmic ethics blames the suffering for their suffering. Christianity grounds morality in the character of someone who is *himself* good.

**Quote to read aloud (it's in the reading packet but worth saying out loud):** Jürgen Habermas — a German philosopher and *not* a Christian — wrote: *"Egalitarian universalism, from which sprang the ideas of freedom and social solidarity… of human rights and democracy, is the direct heir of the Judaic ethic of justice and the Christian ethic of love. To this day, there is no alternative to it."*

Make the point: human rights are not free-standing. They have a history. That history is Judeo-Christian. You can keep the fruit when you cut down the tree, for a while. Habermas's question is whether the fruit lasts.

**Local example:** Idaho Falls has a robust nonprofit sector — Community Food Basket, the Domestic Violence Sexual Assault Center, hospice care at EIRMC. Walk down the list of who founded each one. Notice the pattern. Then ask: *"What would have to be true for these institutions to keep running if everyone forgot why they were started?"*

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Agape** [10.8] — morality built on two foundational moral absolutes: love of God and love of neighbor
- **Greatest commandment** [10.8] — Luke 10:27
- **Sermon on the Mount** [10.8] — Matthew 5–7
- **Universal lawgiver** [10.1] — common ethical norms across cultures point to a common source

**Scripture anchor:** *Romans 12:9 — "Let love be genuine. Abhor what is evil; hold fast to what is good."*

### Synthesis check [5 min]

Stop. Quick verbal check: "Across these three lenses — theology, philosophy, ethics — what's the through-line?" Take 2–3 student answers.

The through-line you're listening for: *God's character is the source.* Theology: God is one being in three persons. Philosophy: reality is what God made it. Ethics: good is what conforms to God's character. The three lenses cohere because they all start in the same place.

### Bridge to Day 3 [4 min]

"Tomorrow: Biology, Psychology, Sociology. We move from *who God is* to *who we are*. Read Part 2 §4–6 of the reading packet tonight. Bring your outline."

## Day 3 — Deep Dive Pt 2: Biology · Psychology · Sociology [50 min]

**Total budget:** 50 min · 5 min opening · 12 min × 3 lenses · 9 min synthesis/transition

**On the board before students arrive:**

```
Day 3 — Three more lenses
4. Biology — What is life, and where did it come from?
5. Psychology — What is the self?
6. Sociology — How should we live together?
```

### Opening review [5 min]

"Yesterday we said God's character is the source. Today we trace what that means for what *we* are. One sentence: what does Christianity say it means to be human?" Take 2–3 student answers. Land on: *image of God* (Imago Dei) — fallen but redeemable.

### Lens 4 — Biology [12 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Special Creation in the image of God — life is the deliberate work of a Creator; humans are categorically different.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity claims that life and the universe are created by God, not self-generated. Humans are categorically different from the rest of creation because we are image-bearers. Christians disagree about the timing and mechanism (young-age, progressive, theistic evolution) but unite on the claim that humans are made in God's image.*

This matters because: if naturalism is true — if life arose from non-life through random processes — then human dignity has no metaphysical anchor. You can still *invent* dignity, the way societies invent currencies, but it's a convention. Christianity says dignity is *discovered*, not invented, because it was put there by Someone.

**Story to tell:** Francis Collins — director of the Human Genome Project, later head of the NIH — was an atheist as a young scientist. The thing that changed his mind wasn't a Christian apologist. It was reading C. S. Lewis's *Mere Christianity*. He was struck that he, a scientist, kept making moral judgments — that the genocide in Rwanda was *actually* wrong, not just culturally disapproved — and yet had no framework that could ground those judgments. He came to faith through the moral argument, then spent the rest of his career arguing that there is no necessary conflict between rigorous science and Christian faith. He's a theistic evolutionist; many Christians disagree with him on the *mechanism*. But on the *anchor* — humans as image-bearers — they all agree.

**Local note (use sparingly):** SE Idaho has a young-earth-creationist majority among practicing Christians. Don't condescend; don't bait. The unit's goal isn't to settle the creation debate — it's to teach students that the *categorical* claim (humans are image-bearers) holds across all three Christian positions on timing and mechanism. Make space for that.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Special creation** [11.8] — the cosmos and all life were brought into existence by the creative act of God
- **Young-age creationism** / **Progressive creationism** / **Theistic evolution** [11.8] — three positions held by serious Christians
- **Intelligent design movement** [11.7] — argument from information and complexity in life
- **Irreducible complexity** [11.8] — complex systems can't function without all components in place

**Scripture anchor:** *Genesis 1:27.*

### Lens 5 — Psychology [12 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Mind/Body Dualism, Fallen but Redeemable — we are body + soul; bent inward by sin; not beyond redemption.*

The thing you're teaching: *The self is real. It includes both the body and the soul. We are fallen — bent inward, curved on ourselves (Augustine's incurvatus in se) — but not worthless and not beyond rescue. The source of psychological suffering is rebellion against God and neighbor, not society alone.*

This matters because: every alternative collapses the self in some way. Buddhism (Unit 6) calls the self an illusion. Hinduism (Unit 5) dissolves it into Brahman. Secularism (Unit 8) reduces it to brain activity. New Spirituality (Unit 7) divinizes it. Christianity holds the middle: the self is real, valuable, fallen, redeemable.

**Story to tell:** Henri Nouwen, a Dutch Catholic priest and Yale professor, walked away from Yale in 1985 to live in a L'Arche community in Toronto — a community for adults with severe intellectual disabilities. He spent the last decade of his life caring for a man named Adam who couldn't speak, couldn't feed himself, couldn't move without help. Nouwen wrote that Adam taught him what it meant that every human being is an image-bearer — not because of what Adam could *do*, but because of who he *was*. Christian psychology starts here: *being* precedes *doing*. You don't earn the image of God by performing well. You bear it because God put it there.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Mind/body dualism** [12.8] — humans are immaterial mind + material body
- **The soul accounts for…** [12.8] — identity, free will, our proclivity to do wrong
- **Three sources of temptation** [12.8] — the world, the flesh, and the devil (Eph. 2:1–3)
- **Positive psychology** [12.8] — focus on promoting mental health, not only treating illness

**Scripture anchor:** *Matthew 10:28 — "Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul."*

### Lens 6 — Sociology [12 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Sphere Sovereignty — family, church, state. Each sphere has its own authority. The family holds society together.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity holds that society functions best when organized around the family, with church and state as distinct, God-ordained spheres of authority. Sin — rebellion against God's design — is the best explanation for social breakdown.*

This matters because: the question "who is sovereign over society?" gets a different answer in every worldview. Marxism (Unit 9): the class collective. Islam (Unit 3): the *umma*. Secularism (Unit 8): the autonomous individual. Christianity says: no single sphere is sovereign over the others — each has its own God-given jurisdiction, and government's job is to *secure* rights, not *create* them.

**Quote to read aloud:** Abraham Kuyper, Dutch Reformed theologian (and Prime Minister of the Netherlands), said it best:

> *"There is not one square inch of the entire creation about which Jesus Christ does not cry out, 'This is mine! This belongs to me!'"*

Now, Kuyper *also* said that the church doesn't run the state, the state doesn't run the family, and the family doesn't run the church. Lordship is universal; jurisdiction is not. That's sphere sovereignty.

**Contemporary example:** Talk briefly about the ongoing American conversation over what the family is. Christianity has a specific answer: father, mother, children, related to each other by blood and covenant, formed by the marriage of one man and one woman. That's not a 2026 culture-war position — it's a 4,000-year-old position. The question for students isn't whether the culture has shifted (it has); it's whether the historic Christian definition is *true*. We'll come back to this in Units 4 (Mormonism extends the family eternally), 6 (Buddhism deprioritizes it), 8 (Secularism redefines it), and 9 (Marxism replaces it with the collective).

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Sphere sovereignty** [13.8 / 15.9b] — God ordained distinct spheres (family, church, state), each with its own authority
- **Subsidiarity** [13.8 / 15.9b] — decisions made at the level closest to those affected
- **Government's responsibility** [13.8] — to *secure* human rights, not create them
- **Source of social problems** [13.8] — sin (rebellion against God's design)

**Scripture anchor:** *Gen. 2:18, 24 (foundational anthropology) and Acts 2:42–47 (church as community).*

### Synthesis check [5 min]

"Across these three lenses — biology, psychology, sociology — what's the through-line?" The through-line you're listening for: *humans bear God's image — body and soul, individually and in community.* Biology says we're made on purpose. Psychology says we're more than brains. Sociology says we're made for one another. The image of God is the common thread.

### Bridge to Day 4 [4 min]

"Tomorrow: the last four lenses — Law, Politics, Economics, History. Then on Day 5 we put the textbook down and you start arguing. Read Part 2 §7–10 tonight. The reading is dense; mark what you don't understand and bring those questions to the Socratic seminar."

## Day 4 — Deep Dive Pt 3: Law · Politics · Economics · History [50 min]

**Total budget:** 50 min · 5 min opening · 10 min × 4 lenses · 5 min synthesis

**On the board before students arrive:**

```
Day 4 — The last four lenses
7. Law         — What is justice?
8. Politics    — What is the state for?
9. Economics   — What is wealth, for?
10. History    — Where is it going?
```

### Opening review [5 min]

"In two sentences: what has Christianity claimed about God so far, and about us?" Take one or two answers. Land on something like: *God is one being in three persons, eternal, personal, the source of truth and love. We are image-bearers — body and soul, made for relationship, fallen but redeemable.* Pivot to today: how does that change how we organize ourselves into societies, into governments, into economies, and how we understand where we're going?

### Lens 7 — Law [10 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Natural Law grounded in God's character — laws are discovered, not invented.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity holds that moral law is grounded in God's character. We don't make law up; we discover it. Government's role is to enforce it justly. Two kinds of revelation help us see it: general (conscience, nature) and special (Scripture).*

**Quote:** Carl F. H. Henry: *"God is the only Legislator. Earthly rulers and legislative bodies are alike accountable to Him from whom stems all obligation — religious, ethical and civil."*

**Story to tell:** William Blackstone, the 18th-century English jurist whose *Commentaries on the Laws of England* shaped American constitutional thinking, wrote: *"Man, considered as a creature, must necessarily be subject to the laws of his creator… This will of his maker is called the law of nature."* The American founders, including those who weren't traditional Christians, assumed a Blackstonian framework. The Declaration of Independence appeals to "the Laws of Nature and of Nature's God" — which is Blackstone's natural-law language. When students hear "America was founded on Christian principles," what's *actually* true is that America was founded on a *natural-law* framework that Christianity had been carrying for centuries.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Natural law** [14.3] — laws based on an internal code of morality possessed by all people
- **Source of divine law** [14.9] — the character and nature of God
- **Order vs. justice** [14.3] — administration (order) vs. enforcement (justice)
- **Christian sources of law** [14.9] — both general AND special revelation

**Scripture anchor:** *Romans 2:14–15.*

### Lens 8 — Politics [10 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Subsidiarity / Limited Government — protect justice, punish evil, secure rights. Politics is real but penultimate.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity says government's primary mission in a fallen world is to preserve justice and punish evil. Rights are protected, not created, by the state. Christians engage in politics with humility — knowing politics is real, but penultimate.*

**Contemporary example:** Take 90 seconds on the Christians-and-politics dilemma in 2026. American Christians have given politics enormous weight in the last 50 years — left and right — and it has not been entirely good for the church. Walk through the publisher's five problematic positions briefly (retreat, "society isn't worth redeeming," "politics can't change hearts," two-kingdoms theology, partisan capture) and then land on the sixth view: *involved, with humility, knowing politics is real but penultimate.* The phrase to underline is *penultimate*. The kingdom of heaven is not coming to America in November.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Subsidiarity** [15.9b] — decisions at the closest level
- **Sphere sovereignty** [15.9b] — family / church / state distinct
- **Government's primary mission** [15.8] — preserve justice, punish evil
- **Acts 5:29** — *"We must obey God rather than men."* The limit on civil submission.

**Scripture anchor:** *Romans 13:1–7; Acts 5:29.*

### Lens 9 — Economics [10 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Biblical Stewardship — God owns it all. The right to property stems from the duty to work.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity holds that all property ultimately belongs to God; we steward it. Private property is real, presupposed by the commandments against stealing and coveting. Work is dignified, not cursed. Generosity is a virtue.*

**Quick illustration:** Read Proverbs 10:4, 14:23, and 16:8 aloud back-to-back. Notice the pattern: *work matters, talk doesn't, and a small honest income beats a large unjust one.* Christian economics has always been morally serious about *both* productivity *and* justice. Either one without the other is a half-gospel.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Biblical stewardship** [16.10] — managing what ultimately belongs to God
- **Right to property** [16.10] — stems from the duty to work
- **Accountability** [16.10] — to God, not to no one
- **Private property** [16.10] — presupposed by commandments against stealing and coveting

**Scripture anchor:** *Psalm 24:1 ("The earth is the Lord's, and everything in it"); Prov. 10:4.*

### Lens 10 — History [10 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Redemptive Narrative — Creation → Fall → Redemption → Restoration. History is linear and going somewhere. Christ is the center.*

The thing you're teaching: *Christianity holds that history is not random and not cyclical — it is the unfolding of God's story. Creation → Fall → Redemption → Restoration. History is rooted in real events that happened in real places. If the resurrection didn't happen, the faith collapses (1 Cor. 15:14). And Christ is the center: "When the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son" (Gal. 4:4).*

**Story to tell:** N. T. Wright, a major contemporary New Testament scholar (Anglican bishop, Oxford professor), has spent his career arguing that Christianity's central claim is not "we go to heaven when we die" but "Christ has risen, and one day God will renew all things." The Christian hope is not *escape* from the world but its *redemption*. That's why Christians built hospitals, started universities, fed the hungry, and worked for the abolition of slavery — not because this world doesn't matter, but because it does, and the world to come will be its glorification, not its replacement. Revelation 21:5 isn't *"Behold, I am making all new things."* It's *"Behold, I am making all things new."* The difference matters.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Redemptive narrative** [17.9] — the four-act story
- **Linear conception of history** [17.9b] — Christianity's view
- **Cyclical conception of history** [17.9b] — rejected by Christianity (held by Hindu/Buddhist)
- **Christianity is rooted in history** [17.9a] — the faith stakes itself on what actually happened

**Scripture anchor:** *Galatians 4:4; Revelation 21:5; 1 Cor. 15:14.*

### Synthesis check + bridge to Days 5–8 [5 min]

"All ten lenses, three days. By Day 12 you should be able to write the Christianity column from memory. Tomorrow is Socratic seminar — *your* day. Bring two or three questions from this week's reading and lectures, written down. The discussion goes where you take it."

Hand back any prep questions students submitted; remind them to bring written questions Friday.

## Day 8 — Four-Test Self-Application [10 min lecture frame, then small-group facilitation]

**Goal:** Equip students to apply Myers's four tests (Reason, Outer World, Inner World, Real-World Consequences) to *Christianity itself*. This is the **model exercise** for the entire course — every Day 7 of every later unit follows this template. Get the rhythm right here.

**Lecture frame (10 min) — small group structure is in [facilitation-guide.md](../handouts/unit-01-christianity-facilitation-guide.md) Day 8.**

### Opening [3 min]

"Today we do something that may feel strange. We're going to apply the four tests — the same four tests we'll use against every other worldview — to Christianity itself. Not to attack it. To *test* it. The premise is simple: a worldview that can't survive its own tests probably shouldn't be defended. Let's see how Christianity does."

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *The Four Tests*

```
1. REASON          — Is it internally coherent? Does it contradict itself?
2. OUTER WORLD     — Does it match what we observe?
3. INNER WORLD     — Does it match our deepest intuitions?
4. REAL-WORLD CONSEQUENCES — When lived at scale, what fruit does it bear?
```

### The frame: charity AND honesty [4 min]

"Two things have to be true at once today. First — *charity*. We are testing Christianity, not auditing it for failure. Take the strongest, smartest version of every Christian claim. Second — *honesty*. If a test surfaces a real pressure point, name it. Don't hand-wave. The Christian intellectual tradition has handled real pressure for 2,000 years; you don't have to protect it from your honest questions."

"Here's the structure for each test. For each one we ask two things — (a) where does Christianity *pass cleanly*, and (b) where is there a *hard question* a thoughtful Christian must be ready to answer?"

### What "passes cleanly" and "hard question" look like [3 min]

Walk through Test 1 (Reason) as the example:

- **Where it passes cleanly:** Christianity offers a coherent four-act story — creation, fall, redemption, restoration. It grounds objective morality in God's character rather than in human consensus. It accounts for both the dignity *and* the brokenness of human beings. The basic worldview hangs together.
- **Hard question:** The Trinity (one being, three persons) and the Incarnation (Jesus as fully God and fully man) are mysteries — claims that exceed human reason. Are they *contradictions*? The classical answer is no — a contradiction asserts and denies the same thing in the same sense at the same time, which the Trinity does not. But the response *requires articulating it*. A Christian needs to be able to say "this is a mystery, not a contradiction, and here's the difference."

"That's the shape. Apply it to two of the four tests. Now go to your groups."

→ Hand off to small-group activity. See [facilitation-guide.md](../handouts/unit-01-christianity-facilitation-guide.md) Day 8 for the worksheet, group structure, and debrief.

## Day 9 — The Christian Family: Catholic · Orthodox · Protestant [50 min]

**Goal:** Students leave able to distinguish where the three traditions agree (Theology, Biology, History — the Nicene Creed level) from where they diverge (authority, salvation, sacraments). They should also be able to explain why Mormonism and Jehovah's Witnesses, despite using Christian vocabulary, sit on the worldview grid as separate columns.

**On the board before students arrive:**

```
The Christian Family
   ROMAN CATHOLIC · EASTERN ORTHODOX · PROTESTANT
What they share | Where they diverge | Where the line ends
```

### Opening [5 min]

"Christianity, in this course, is one column on the worldview grid. But Christianity in the actual world has three major branches. Today is the survey of what those branches share and where they differ. The goal is *not* to settle the differences — that's seminary, not high school. The goal is to know enough to recognize each tradition when you meet a thoughtful adherent."

### What all three share (the Nicene minimum) [10 min]

Walk through the Nicene Creed (it's in the reading packet, Part 3 Source 2). All three traditions confess every clause. The shared claims:

1. **One God in three persons** (Father, Son, Holy Spirit) — Trinitarian Monotheism
2. **Jesus is fully God and fully man** — incarnation, virgin birth, crucifixion, bodily resurrection, ascension, future return
3. **The Holy Spirit** as God, giver of life, speaker through the prophets
4. **One holy catholic and apostolic Church** ("catholic" here means *universal*, not denominationally Roman Catholic)
5. **Baptism for the forgiveness of sins**
6. **The resurrection of the dead and the life of the world to come**

"If any tradition denies these, they have stepped off the historic Christian map. The Nicene Creed is the *minimum* — not the *maximum* — of Christianity."

### Where they diverge [25 min — about 8 min per tradition]

#### Roman Catholic (8 min)

- **Authority:** Scripture + Sacred Tradition + the Magisterium (the teaching office of the Church, with the Pope as head)
- **Salvation:** Justified by grace through faith, but faith *formed by love* — meaning works of love are part of saving faith. The Council of Trent (1547) anathematized "faith alone."
- **Sacraments:** Seven (baptism, confirmation, Eucharist, reconciliation, anointing, holy orders, matrimony). The Eucharist is the *real presence* — transubstantiation.
- **Mary:** Honored as Theotokos (God-bearer), assumed bodily into heaven, immaculately conceived (free from original sin).
- **Visible body:** ~1.4 billion globally. The largest single Christian tradition.

**One-sentence summary:** *The visible, hierarchical, sacramental Church preserves and teaches the deposit of faith.*

#### Eastern Orthodox (8 min)

- **Authority:** Scripture + Holy Tradition + the seven ecumenical councils (no Pope; collegial bishops)
- **Salvation:** *Theosis* — the gradual transformation of the believer into the likeness of God, by grace, through participation in the divine life
- **Sacraments:** Seven (same as Catholic), but theology emphasizes *mystery* over precise definition (they would say "the Eucharist *is* the body and blood; *how* is a mystery")
- **Iconography:** Icons are venerated (not worshipped) as windows into divine reality
- **Visible body:** ~220 million globally; the dominant tradition in Russia, Greece, Eastern Europe, parts of the Middle East
- **The Great Schism:** Split with Rome in 1054 — primarily over papal authority and the *filioque* clause in the Creed (whether the Spirit proceeds from "the Father" or "the Father *and the Son*")

**One-sentence summary:** *The Church is the living continuation of the apostolic mystery; salvation is participation in God.*

#### Protestant (8 min)

- **Authority:** Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) — Scripture is the supreme and final authority; tradition is helpful but subordinate
- **Salvation:** *Sola fide* — justified by grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone. Works are the *fruit* of saving faith, not its *foundation*.
- **Sacraments:** Two — baptism and the Lord's Supper (with major internal disagreement about what happens in the Supper: Lutheran consubstantiation, Reformed spiritual presence, Baptist memorial)
- **Origin:** 1517 — Martin Luther's 95 Theses, protesting indulgences. Then a multi-generational unraveling and re-formation across Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican, Baptist, Methodist, Pentecostal, non-denominational lines
- **Visible body:** ~900 million globally; the dominant Christian tradition in the U.S. (and at Watersprings)

**One-sentence summary:** *Scripture alone is the final authority; salvation is by grace through faith in Christ alone.*

### Where the line ends — and why [8 min]

"All three of those traditions confess the Nicene Creed. They argue about authority, salvation, and sacraments — *inside* the family. But the family has an edge."

**WRITE ON BOARD:**

```
INSIDE THE FAMILY        OUTSIDE
Catholic                  Mormonism (different Jesus, different God)
Orthodox                  Jehovah's Witnesses (no Trinity, no full deity of Christ)
Protestant                Christian Science, Unity, etc.
```

Mormonism uses Christian vocabulary — "Jesus," "God," "salvation" — but means categorically different things (we'll spend two weeks on this in Unit 4). Jehovah's Witnesses reject the Trinity and the full deity of Christ. Both groups would have been excluded from the Council of Nicaea.

"How do you tell the difference? Run the Nicene Creed test. If a group denies any of its central claims — Trinity, incarnation, bodily resurrection, the one apostolic Church — they're a different worldview, not a Christian denomination. That doesn't mean their members aren't lovely people. It means the theology is categorically distinct."

### Bridge [2 min]

"Day 10 is the contemporary resource. Day 11 we practice the Gospel conversation. Read whatever the teacher posts tonight. Bring questions tomorrow."

## Day 10 — Contemporary Resource [50 min]

**Goal:** Show students *how a thoughtful Christian articulates the worldview today.* This is the only day of the unit where the material is teacher-curated and content varies. Not a content day so much as a *demonstration* day — students should leave thinking "Christians who are smart, honest, and contemporary exist."

### Three suggested resources (pick one)

1. **Tim Keller — "Skeptics Welcome" sermon** (~35 min, available on Gospel in Life; YouTube versions of related Keller talks also work). Keller was a Manhattan pastor for 30 years who specialized in articulating Christianity for people who didn't grow up with it. His tone is warm, intellectually serious, and gently challenging. Pair with the Q&A questions in the facilitation guide.

2. **Francis Collins — interview on *The Language of God*** (multiple versions on YouTube; pick a 30–40 min interview). Collins is the geneticist who led the Human Genome Project. He came to faith from atheism via Lewis's *Mere Christianity*. He's a theistic evolutionist, which some students' families will disagree with; that's fine — the point is *how* he reasons, not whether you adopt his position.

3. **Rebecca McLaughlin — a video from her *Confronting Christianity* series** (typically 10–15 min apiece on YouTube; pick the one on "Is Christianity homophobic?" or "Hasn't science disproven Christianity?" depending on what your students are wrestling with). McLaughlin is a Cambridge-trained theologian who specializes in the questions teenagers and young adults actually ask.

**Sub-friendliness note:** If you're handing this day to a sub, queue up the video in advance, leave the link in your sub plan, and use the facilitation-guide.md Day 10 prompts.

### Structure [50 min]

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|

| 35 min | Watch the resource. (If shorter than 35 min, use remaining time for paired discussion.) |
| 8 min | Debrief — go around the room, take one of (a) and one of (b) from each student |
| 2 min | Bridge to Day 11 |
