# Day 1 — Framing the Christian Worldview [target: 45 min]

**Reading due today:** Part 1 of the Christianity reading packet.

## Opening hook [5 min]

Tell C. E. M. Joad's story (Myers Ch 2, §1):

- British radio philosopher in the 1940s. Witty, popular, dismissive of Christianity.
- Caught riding trains without paying. Fired. Diagnosed with cancer.
- Wrote *The Recovery of Belief* shortly before his death: *"It is because… the religious view of the universe seems to me to cover more of the facts of experience than any other that I have been gradually led to embrace it."*
- He had been reading C. S. Lewis. Maybe he saw Edmund in himself, and Aslan in Christ.

**Hook line for the class:** *"This is a course about the maps people use to navigate reality. Some maps work. Some don't. We start with the one this course will compare every other map to."*

## What this worldview actually is [15 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Christianity is a theistic worldview centered on the person of Jesus Christ that derives its understanding of the world through the teachings of the Holy Bible.* (Myers Ch 2, §2)

Spoken summary (steel-man, no critique):

- Christianity is *not* primarily a moral system, a tribe, or a Sunday habit. It is a claim that *something happened* — that the God who made the world stepped into it as a man, died, and rose again.
- The basics (Myers Ch 2, §2): there is a God; through Jesus Christ, God created the world; humans were made in God's image but fell into sin; the fall affected all creation; Jesus Christ paid for sin and bought redemption with his own life.
- The center is a *person*. Quote John Stott on the board if there's room: *"Christ is the centre of Christianity; all else is circumference."*

**The reversal** that distinguishes Christianity from most religions: *other religions tell you how to search for God. Christianity says God came to find you* (Myers Ch 2, §2). Walked in the garden. Dwelled in the temple. Became flesh in Jesus. *Immanuel* — God with us.

**The four-act story** (Myers Ch 2, §3) — write the four words on the board:

**WRITE ON BOARD:**
1. **Creation** — *meod tob* (very good)
2. **Fall** — *incurvatus in se* (curved in on itself)
3. **Redemption** — the serpent who said "Take, eat" defeated by the Savior who said "Take, eat"
4. **Restoration** — "Behold, I am making all things new" (Rev. 21:5)

**KEY VOCAB** (blank in student version):

- **Christianity** [2.2] — a theistic worldview centered on the person of Jesus Christ that derives its understanding of the world through the teachings of the Holy Bible
- **Theism** [8.1] — the belief in the existence of a God or gods
- **General revelation** [2.4] — God's revelation of himself to all people through creation (Ps. 19:1–6; Rom. 1:18–20)
- **Special revelation** [2.4] — God's revelation of himself through the Bible, miraculous events, and Jesus Christ (Ps. 19:7–11; 2 Tim. 3:14–17; John 1:1–18)

## The framing question [10 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *What is the Christian answer to each of the 10 disciplines, and why?*

Spoken framing:

- This unit is the longest of the year — 13 days — because every other unit compares back to this one.
- By Day 12 (Worldview Brief day), every student should be able to articulate Christianity's answer to each of the 10 disciplines in **one sentence with one Scripture anchor**.
- That's the bar. The lectures are the scaffolding to get you there.

**Two big claims that organize everything that follows:**

1. *God exists, is personal, and has revealed himself.* (vs. Secularism, Marxism, postmodernism, New Spirituality)
2. *History is going somewhere — toward the renewal of all things in Christ.* (vs. cyclical religions, vs. nihilism)

## Anticipated student questions [10 min]

- *"Why are we starting with Christianity? Isn't that biased?"*
  → **A:** Every comparative course starts somewhere. Starting with the baseline you'll compare everything else to is the most honest approach — and it lets us catch our own assumptions early. By Unit 8 (Secularism), every student will have to argue Secularism's strongest case. Charity isn't reserved for views we like.
- *"What if I'm not a Christian? What if my family isn't?"*
  → **A:** This unit teaches what Christians actually claim, in their own words. You don't have to agree to understand. By the end, you should be able to represent Christianity to a Christian in a way they would recognize as fair. That's the same standard we'll hold for every other worldview.
- *"Christians disagree about a lot. Which Christianity are we doing?"*
  → **A:** Day 9 is dedicated to that exact question — Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox. For now, we focus on what unites them: the four-act story, the Trinitarian God, the bodily resurrection of Jesus. The Nicene Creed (Source 2 in your packet) is the shared confession.

## Lens 1 — Theology [13 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *Who is God?*: **Trinitarian Monotheism**

**From Myers Ch 2 (the worldview's own chapter):**
- God is *personal* — possesses intellect, emotion, volition; rational, relational, communicative (Myers Ch 2, §3a).
- God is *transcendent* (beyond and separate from the world) and *immanent* (in the world) (Myers Ch 2, §3b; Gen. 1:1; John 1:1; Col. 1:16–17).
- God is *triune* — relationship is not something God does but something God *is* (Myers Ch 2, §3b).
- Aslan-not-safe-but-good line from Lewis (Myers Ch 2, §3a). Use it. Students remember stories.

**From Ch 8 (Theology), Christianity slice:**
- The publisher's exact phrase for Christianity's theological position: *Trinitarian monotheism* — "the belief in one God recognized as three separate and distinct persons: Father, Son, and Spirit" (Myers Ch 8, §6).
- Christianity expects to find *order* in nature — not chaos — because God created it (Myers Ch 8, §8).
- The God of love is *also* the God of wrath (Myers Ch 8, §9b). Don't soften this; the publisher won't.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Trinitarian Monotheism** [8.6] — belief in one God recognized as three separate and distinct persons: Father, Son, and Spirit
- **Theism** [8.1] — belief in the existence of a God or gods
- **Monotheism** [8.6] — belief in the existence of one God
- **Holy** [8.9c] — sacred / set apart / consecrated
- **Anthropic principle** [8.8d] — the universe was designed for human beings
- **Genetic information theory** [8.8c] — complexity and information in DNA molecules reveal evidence for design

**Scripture anchor:** Isaiah 44:6 — *"I am the first, and I am the last; besides me there is no god."*

**STUDENTS FILL IN GRID:** Christianity column, Theology row.

## Lens 2 — Philosophy [13 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *What is real, and how do we know?*: **Realism + Mind/Body Dualism**

**From Myers Ch 2:**
- Christianity claims reality exists for us to know (Myers Ch 9, §7 — but flagged here in the Ch 2 chapter as well).
- Faith is not opposed to reason. Quote Neidhardt: *"Faith correctly viewed is that illumination by which true rationality begins."*

**From Ch 9 (Philosophy), Christianity slice:**
- *Realism* (Myers Ch 9, §7) — "the belief that an objective reality exists independently from human perception or language."
- *Mind/body dualism* (Myers Ch 9, §7c) — "the belief that human beings are composed of immaterial minds and material bodies."
- According to the Christian view of metaphysics, **mind came BEFORE matter** — not after (Myers Ch 9, §7b). John 1:1 — *"In the beginning was the Word."*
- Christians believe knowledge can come from science, history, experience, *and* God (Myers Ch 9, §7a).
- Mention Al-Ghazali's *Kalam* cosmological argument (Myers Ch 9, §6a) as a classical argument shared by Muslim and Christian theists.

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Philosophy** [9.1] — the study of knowledge, truth, and the nature of ultimate reality
- **Metaphysics** [9.1] — the study of ultimate reality
- **Epistemology** [9.1] — the study of knowledge
- **Realism** [9.7] — the belief that an objective reality exists independently from human perception or language
- **Dualism** [9.7] — the belief that reality is ultimately composed of two essential substances
- **Mind/body dualism** [9.7c] — the belief that human beings are composed of immaterial minds and material bodies

**Scripture anchor:** John 1:1 — *"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."*

**STUDENTS FILL IN GRID:** Christianity column, Philosophy row.

## Lens 3 — Ethics [14 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *What is good, on what basis?*: **Agape / Natural Law**

**From Myers Ch 2:**
- Quote Schaeffer (use the board): *"He exists; He has a character; and not all things are the same to Him. Some things conform to His character, and some are opposed to His character."*
- Faith and reason in apologetic posture: 1 Pet. 3:15, Acts 17, 2 Cor. 10:5, Col. 2:8 (Myers Ch 2, §9).

**From Ch 10 (Ethics), Christianity slice:**
- Christian ethics is grounded in **God's nature**, not God's decree (Myers Ch 10, §8). This matters: it's not "God said so"; it's "this conforms to who God is."
- The publisher defines *agape* exactly: "the belief that morality is built upon two foundational moral absolutes: love of God and love of neighbor" (Myers Ch 10, §8).
- The greatest commandment: Luke 10:27. The apex of Christ's ethical teaching: the **Sermon on the Mount** (Matt. 5–7).
- Romans 12:9 — *"Let love be genuine. Abhor what is evil; hold fast to what is good."*
- Christians see common moral norms across cultures (e.g., murder, adultery, lying universally condemned) as evidence of a **universal lawgiver** (Myers Ch 10, §1).

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Ethics** [10.2] — the study of moral conduct, values, duties, and goodness
- **Agape** [10.8] — the belief that morality is built upon two foundational moral absolutes: love of God and love of neighbor
- **Sermon on the Mount** [10.8] — the apex of Christ's ethical teaching (Matt. 5–7)
- **Greatest commandment** [10.8] — love God with all your heart, soul, mind, strength, and love your neighbor as yourself (Luke 10:27)
- **Universal lawgiver** [10.1] — common moral norms across cultures point to one
- **Christian ethics task** [10.8] — to determine what conforms to God's character and what does not

**Scripture anchor:** Luke 10:27.

**STUDENTS FILL IN GRID:** Christianity column, Ethics row.

# Day 3 — Deep Dive Pt 2: Biology, Psychology, Sociology [target: 50 min]

**Reading due today:** Part 2 §§4–6 of the reading packet.

## Opening review [3 min]

Cold-call: *"What's the publisher's name for Christianity's theological position?"* (Trinitarian monotheism.)
*"What's Christianity's epistemology?"* (Realism — reality exists independent of our minds.)

## Lens 4 — Biology [14 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *What is life, and where did it come from?*: **Special Creation / Imago Dei**

**From Myers Ch 2:**
- The cosmological argument (Myers Ch 2, §3): order and arrangement of the universe point to a first cause. Energy alone doesn't make plans.
- Genesis 1:27 — humans created *in his own image*.
- Quote: Fred Hoyle (atheist!) said the chance of life arising by accident is less than "producing a Boeing 747 via a junkyard explosion."

**From Ch 11 (Biology), Christianity slice:**
- The publisher's exact term for Christianity's biology: **Special creation** — "the belief that the cosmos and all life were brought into existence by the creative act of God" (Myers Ch 11, §8).
- Three legitimate Christian positions, all under the special-creation umbrella (Myers Ch 11, §8):
  - **Young-age creationism** — six literal days, ~6,000 years
  - **Progressive creationism** — progressive stages over a long time
  - **Theistic evolution** — billions of years, guided evolution
- The **intelligent design movement** (Myers Ch 11, §7) and **irreducible complexity** (Myers Ch 11, §8) are scientific arguments many Christians cite.
- Microevolution is a fact of science; macroevolution is the disputed claim (Myers Ch 11, §3).

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Biology** [11.2] — the study of life and living organisms
- **Special creation** [11.8] — the belief that the cosmos and all life were brought into existence by the creative act of God
- **Young-age creationism** [11.8] — God created the cosmos and all life in six literal days, around six thousand years ago
- **Progressive creationism** [11.8] — God created the cosmos and all life in progressive stages, over a long period of time
- **Theistic evolution** [11.8] — God created the cosmos and all life billions of years ago and then guided the process of evolution
- **Intelligent design movement** [11.7] — a scientific movement positing an intelligent designer best accounts for design and complexity in life
- **Irreducible complexity** [11.8] — complex biological systems could not gradually evolve because without all components, complex systems don't function

**Pedagogical note for teacher:** Don't pick a side among the three creation views in this unit. Many Christian families have strong feelings here. The unifying claim — *humans are categorically different because they bear God's image* — is what Day 13 will test.

**STUDENTS FILL IN GRID:** Christianity column, Biology row.

## Lens 5 — Psychology [13 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *What is the self?*: **Mind/Body Dualism (Fallen but Redeemable)**

**From Myers Ch 2:**
- The fall (Myers Ch 2, §3): sin distorted but did not destroy the image of God in us. Light bulb with a broken filament — still a light bulb.
- Augustine's *incurvatus in se* — "curved in on itself."

**From Ch 12 (Psychology), Christianity slice:**
- The publisher's framing: **mind/body dualism** — humans are composed of immaterial minds and material bodies (Myers Ch 12, §8). Same term as Lens 2; here applied to human persons.
- The Christian view of the soul accounts for *identity*, *free will*, and our *proclivity to do wrong* (Myers Ch 12, §8).
- The source of psychological problems is *our rebellion against God and against our fellow man* — not society alone (Myers Ch 12, §8). Don't reduce sin to "bad upbringing."
- Three sources of temptation (Eph. 2:1–3): the **world**, the **flesh**, the **devil** (Myers Ch 12, §8).
- Matthew 10:28 — *"Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul."*
- The publisher commends **positive psychology** as compatible with Christian anthropology (Myers Ch 12, §8).

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Psychology** [12.1] — the study of the human soul
- **Mind/body dualism** [12.8] — the belief that human beings are composed of immaterial minds and material bodies
- **Imago Dei / Image of God** — every person bears God's image; not destroyed by the fall (Myers Ch 2, §3d)
- **Three sources of temptation** [12.8] — the world, the flesh, the devil
- **Positive psychology** [12.8] — focuses on promoting mental health and happiness rather than only treating illness

**Scripture anchor:** Matthew 10:28.

**STUDENTS FILL IN GRID:** Christianity column, Psychology row.

## Lens 6 — Sociology [15 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *How should people live together?*: **Sphere Sovereignty**

**From Myers Ch 2:**
- Genesis 1–4 sequence: marriage → family → worship → government → urbanization → agriculture → music → metallurgy. Society's basic institutions were there from the start.
- Lesslie Newbigin's Hindu friend: *"There is nothing else in the whole religious literature of the world to put alongside [the Bible's interpretation of universal history]."*

**From Ch 13 (Sociology), Christianity slice:**
- The publisher's exact phrase: **Sphere sovereignty** — "the belief that God ordained certain spheres of society, such as family, church, and state, and that society functions best when each sphere is properly managing its own area of responsibility" (Myers Ch 13, §8).
- The **family** is what holds society together (Myers Ch 13, §8).
- Diagnosis: the source of society's problems is *sin* — rebellion against God's design (Myers Ch 13, §8).
- Government's responsibility is to **secure** human rights, not **create** them (Myers Ch 13, §8).
- The church's primary responsibility is to **model community by loving one's neighbor** (Myers Ch 13, §8).
- **Subsidiarity** (Myers Ch 13, §8) — decisions handled at the lowest competent level. (Same term will return in Lens 8 / Politics.)

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Sociology** [13.1] — the study of human societies and institutions
- **Sphere sovereignty** [13.8] — God ordained spheres (family, church, state); society functions best when each manages its own responsibility
- **Subsidiarity** [13.8 / 15.9b] — decisions are best handled at the level closest to the issues or persons affected
- **Family as society's anchor** [13.8] — Christianity's claim about what holds society together

**Pedagogical note for teacher:** Use Kuyper's "three balloons in a box" image. Students remember it. Then his most-quoted line: *"There is not one square inch of the entire creation about which Jesus Christ does not cry out, 'This is mine!'"*

**STUDENTS FILL IN GRID:** Christianity column, Sociology row.

## Lens 9 — Economics [11 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *What is wealth, and how should it be stewarded?*: **Biblical Stewardship**

**From Myers Ch 2:**
- Genesis 2:15 — work is part of God's *very good* design, not a curse from the fall.
- Dorothy Sayers: work *"for the sake of doing well a thing that is well worth doing."*

**From Ch 16 (Economics), Christianity slice:**
- The publisher's exact phrase: **Biblical stewardship** — "the act of managing and caring for resources that ultimately belong to God" (Myers Ch 16, §10).

**Key Proverbs (Myers Ch 16, §10):**

**KEY VOCAB:**
- **Economics** [16.1] — the study of the management of resources
- **Biblical stewardship** [16.10] — the act of managing and caring for resources that ultimately belong to God
- **Right to property** [16.10] — stems from our duty to work

## Lens 10 — History [11 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Christianity's answer to *Where is history going?*: **Redemptive Narrative**

**From Myers Ch 2:**
- The four-act structure (revisit from Day 1): Creation → Fall → Redemption → Restoration.
- Galatians 4:4 — *"When the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son."* The language is of a pregnancy and a birth. History had a center, and the center is a person.

**From Ch 17 (History), Christianity slice:**
- The publisher's exact phrase: **Redemptive narrative** — "the Christian metanarrative that describes the course of history unfolding according to the biblical chronicle of God's creative act, humanity's rebellion, Christ's redemptive sacrifice, and God's eventual restoration of his creation" (Myers Ch 17, §9).

# Day 8 — Four-Test Self-Application [lecture frame: 10 min, then small-group: 35 min]

**Note on Day 8 placement.** In the standard 9-day rhythm, the Four-Test exercise lands on Day 7. For Unit 1 (the 13-day baseline), the case study has already happened on Day 7, and the Four-Test self-application takes Day 8. Same content, different slot.

## Lecture frame [10 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *The four tests*
1. **Reason** — Is the worldview internally coherent?
2. **Outer World** — Does it match what we observe in nature, history, human experience?
3. **Inner World** — Does it match our deepest moral, aesthetic, and relational intuitions?
4. **Real-World Consequences** — When lived at scale, what fruit does it bear?

Spoken framing:

- These are Myers's four tests (Myers Ch 1). Every later unit's Day 7 will apply them to that worldview *against the Christian baseline*.
- Today is the **model exercise**. We're applying them to Christianity itself. *"This is what we'll do for Islam, Hinduism, Marxism — but you have to see how it works on the home worldview first, where the stakes feel personal."*
- This is not "tear apart your faith." It is *"learn to see your faith from the outside, so you can defend it from the inside."*

**Three highest-value tension points to surface for the small groups:**

1. *Reason:* Christianity claims the Trinity and the Incarnation are mysteries (true but not fully comprehensible). Is "I can't fully explain this" different from "this is contradictory"? (Suggested answer: yes — compare wave-particle duality in physics. But students should land on the answer themselves.)
2. *Outer World:* Christianity claims a real, bodily resurrection. The 1 Cor. 15:14 line cuts both ways — it grounds the claim in falsifiable history, but also stakes the entire faith on it. How do Christians defend the historicity of the resurrection?
3. *Real-World Consequences:* Christianity has produced hospitals, universities, abolition movements, civil rights advances — and inquisitions, crusades, and church scandals. How do Christians account for the failures? (Aim for: *the tree is judged by its fruit, but the gardener is also accountable for the weeds.*)

## Hand off to small groups [35 min]

Groups of 3–4. Each group takes one test and applies it to Christianity. 25 min of small-group work, 10 min for reports.

**Worksheet for each group:**

# Day 9 — Christianity vs. Itself: Catholic / Protestant / Orthodox [target: 45 min]

This day exists to handle the question students always ask — *"which Christianity?"* — without derailing the unit. The aim is **literacy, not partisanship**.

## Opening framing [5 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *What unites all Christians? What divides them?*

Spoken intro:
- The Nicene Creed (Source 2 in your packet) is what unites the three main branches. Re-read it now if anyone's foggy on it.
- The differences are real but operate at a *different level* than the differences between Christianity and Islam, or Christianity and Buddhism.
- The publisher's textbook treats Christianity as a single column on the worldview grid because the Trinitarian, Christ-centered, scripturally-grounded core is shared. We're following that move.

## The three traditions, in their own terms [25 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:** Three branches table (compact):

| | Roman Catholic | Eastern Orthodox | Protestant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Authority | Scripture + Tradition + Magisterium (Pope) | Scripture + Tradition + Ecumenical Councils | Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) |
| Salvation | Grace + Faith + Sacraments | Theosis (becoming partakers of divine nature) | Grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone |
| Sacraments | Seven | Seven (called "mysteries") | Two (baptism + Lord's Supper) — most traditions |
| Visible center | Pope, Vatican | Patriarchs, councils | Local congregation / denomination |
| Numbers | ~1.3 billion | ~220 million | ~900 million |

**Spoken treatment** (8 min per tradition; charitable, not comparative):

**Roman Catholic** (8 min):
- The largest Christian tradition. Continuous institutional presence since the early church.
- Key emphasis: the church is the *visible body of Christ*, with apostolic succession running from Peter to the current Pope.
- Sacramental theology: God's grace is communicated through ordinary physical things (bread, wine, water, oil) made holy by Christ's presence.
- *Magisterium* — the teaching authority of the Pope and the bishops in communion with him.

**Eastern Orthodox** (8 min):
- Originated in the Christian East — Greek, Russian, Coptic, Syrian, Ethiopian Orthodox churches.
- Split from Roman Catholicism in the Great Schism of 1054 (over the *filioque* clause and papal authority).
- Key emphasis: *theosis* — salvation as becoming, by grace, what Christ is by nature. Not earning salvation, but being transformed *into* the divine life.
- Ecumenical councils (especially the first seven, AD 325–787) are the high authority — not a Pope.
- Heavy emphasis on the Liturgy, icons, and continuity with the early church.

**Protestant** (8 min):
- Originated in the 16th-century Reformation (Luther, Calvin, Cranmer, others). Now 900M+ across thousands of denominations.
- Five solas: *sola scriptura* (Scripture alone), *sola fide* (faith alone), *sola gratia* (grace alone), *solus Christus* (Christ alone), *soli Deo gloria* (glory to God alone).
- Spectrum: Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican, Methodist, Baptist, Pentecostal, non-denominational.
- Decentralized authority. Strength: every Christian reads Scripture for themselves. Weakness: fragmentation.

## Synthesis: what worldview-grid cells stay constant? [10 min]

Cold-call this section. Aim to surface:

- *Theology:* Trinitarian Monotheism — same for all three.
- *Biology:* Special creation in God's image — same.
- *History:* Redemptive narrative ending with Christ's return — same.
- *Sociology:* Sphere sovereignty — varies by Catholic / Reformed / Lutheran tradition, but family/church/state distinction holds.
- *Authority and salvation* are where they diverge — these are *intra-Christian* questions, not *Christianity vs. other worldviews* questions.

**The take-home for the unit:** *"When we put Christianity on the worldview grid against Islam or Marxism or Buddhism, we are answering questions where the three Christian traditions agree. The differences between them matter — but they matter inside the family."*

## Anticipated student questions [5 min]

- *"Are Catholics Christians?"* → Yes. Catholic Christianity predates Protestant Christianity by 1,500 years. Disagreements are real, but they fall inside the Trinitarian, Christ-centered, scripturally-grounded confession.
- *"What about Mormons / Jehovah's Witnesses?"* → They use Christian vocabulary but reject the Nicene Creed (specifically, the deity of Christ in the Trinitarian sense). They sit on the worldview grid as separate columns (Mormonism is Unit 4). Christianity, here, means Trinitarian Christianity.
- *"Which is right?"* → That's a question for your church, family, and conscience — and it's a question you SHOULD wrestle with. This course teaches you to recognize the family resemblance first; the family disagreements are real, but they are family disagreements.
