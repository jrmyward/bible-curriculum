## Part 1 — What Is a Worldview?

### The Definition

Jeff Myers and David Noebel — the authors of the textbook this course is built around — define a worldview this way:

> **A worldview is a pattern of ideas, beliefs, convictions, and habits that help us make sense of God, the world, and our relationship to God and the world.** (Myers Ch 1, §5)

That definition is doing several jobs at once. Notice:

- **A pattern, not a list.** A worldview is not a checklist of opinions. It's an organized set of answers that hang together — pull one and the others move.
- **Ideas, beliefs, convictions, and habits.** The publisher is careful here: worldviews are not just thoughts. They show up in *what you do* on a Tuesday afternoon as much as in *what you say* in a debate.
- **God, the world, and our relationship to God and the world.** Every worldview answers questions in all three categories — even worldviews that deny God exists. *Atheism is a worldview about God*. It just answers "no."

### A Mental Map

The publisher uses the analogy of a map. Your worldview is *like a map or a GPS program*: it tells you where you are, where you need to go, and the best route to get there (Myers Ch 1, §5). Two important points follow:

1. **Your worldview is *descriptive*.** It describes what you think the world is.
2. **Your worldview is *prescriptive*.** It tells you how you should act and respond in any situation.

The publisher emphasizes this on the chapter test: *"a worldview is descriptive as well as prescriptive."* That distinction matters because most people are aware that they hold *opinions* about how things are. Fewer are aware that those opinions *control* what they do.

### Everyone Has One

This is the publisher's most counterintuitive claim, and it appears on the Day-10 test:

> **Everyone has a worldview.** (Myers Ch 1, §5)

Not everyone has *examined* their worldview. Not everyone could *articulate* it on demand. But everyone operates from one. You inherited yours from family, culture, school, the internet, the friends you spend time with, and the experiences you've had. The question of this class is not *whether* you have a worldview — it's whether yours is **examined, coherent, and honest**.

### The Evidence Is in Your Behavior

If everyone has a worldview but most people can't articulate it, how do we *see* worldviews at work? The publisher's answer: **behavior**. The evidence of a person's worldview *physically plays out in their behavior* (Myers Ch 1, §5). When someone says "I believe X" but consistently does Y, the X may be lip service and the Y is the actual worldview. We're studying not what people *say* they believe but what people's lives reveal they actually believe.

## Part 2 — Why Study Worldviews?

### Ideas Have Consequences

The publisher's central argument: **ideas have consequences** (Myers Ch 1, §6). Worldviews are not abstractions. They shape the kind of people who hold them, the kind of families they raise, the kind of governments they build, and the kind of futures they bring about.

Myers and Noebel make this point with examples most students don't expect:

- **Nazism** was, before it was a regime, *an idea*. It systematically exterminated approximately 21 million people, in addition to the tens of millions who died in battles initiated by the Nazi regime (Myers Ch 1, §6).
- **Communist regimes** — built on the *idea* called Marxism — slaughtered well over 100 million people in the twentieth century (Myers Ch 1, §6).

The point is not to score political points. It is to make a philosophical claim: **bad ideas cost lives**. Conversely, good ideas — *human beings made in the image of God*, *the rule of law*, *equality before God*, *freedom of conscience* — build the kinds of societies most people want to live in.

### The Battle of Ideas

The chapter title is *The Battle of Ideas* because the author treats the contest of worldviews as a real contest with real stakes. David Noebel — the original author — got the title from a chapel speaker named Fred Schwarz, who argued that communism spread so quickly because it was *religious* in shape: it answered ultimate questions, inspired devotion, and gave meaning to people's lives (Myers Ch 1, §3). That insight became the founding observation of this entire curriculum.

So this is not a class where we study worldviews because it's interesting (though it is). We study them because they're how the world is being shaped — for good and for ill — *right now*.

### The Cost of Not Studying Them

What happens when people *don't* learn to think about worldviews? They pick up beliefs the way they pick up viruses — by being around other people, without consciously choosing. The publisher uses this exact analogy: *people pick up their beliefs like they catch colds — by being around other people* (Myers Ch 1, §5).

This isn't a metaphor we should be comfortable with. It means most people's deepest convictions are *inherited* without examination. By the time they're old enough to examine what they believe, the worldview is already running their life. The point of this class is to make the inherited worldview *visible* so that, if you keep it, you keep it because you've looked at it — and if you change it, you change it because you've thought about it.

## Part 3 — The 10 Analytical Disciplines

Every worldview in this course is examined through the same 10 lenses. The publisher calls these the *ten basic disciplines, the seeds from which most things in academia grow* (Myers Ch 1, §10). Memorize them. They are the vocabulary of the rest of the year.

### The 10 Disciplines, in Order

| # | Discipline | The question it asks | Greek/Latin root |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Theology** | Who is God? | *theos* = God |
| 2 | **Philosophy** | What is real? How do we know? | *philo* (love) + *sophia* (wisdom) |
| 3 | **Ethics** | What is good? On what basis? | *ethos* = goodness |
| 4 | **Biology** | What is life, and where did it come from? | *bios* = life |
| 5 | **Psychology** | What is the self? | *psyche* = soul |
| 6 | **Sociology** | How should people live together? | *socios* = society |
| 7 | **Law** | What is justice? Where does authority come from? | *lagu* (Old English) = ordinance |
| 8 | **Politics** | What is the role of the state? | *polis* = city |
| 9 | **Economics** | What is wealth? How should it be stewarded? | *oikonomia* = household management |
| 10 | **History** | Where is history going? What is its meaning? | *historia* = inquiry |

### Why These 10?

The publisher's claim is not that these are the *only* important disciplines — engineering, medicine, art, and music all matter. The claim is that these 10 are *foundational*: they ask the questions that every other field of study inherits answers from. A school of architecture is downstream of theology (what is beauty?), philosophy (what is real?), and ethics (what is good?). A medical school is downstream of biology (what is life?), psychology (what is a person?), and ethics (what does it mean to heal?).

If you can describe a worldview's answer to all 10 of these, you can predict — fairly accurately — what that worldview will say about any other field. That's the design principle of this course.

### The Grid

Every worldview occupies one column on a 9 × 10 grid: 9 worldviews × 10 disciplines = 90 cells. By May you should be able to fill in every cell of that grid from memory. You'll see the grid on Day 7 of this unit and again at the start of every worldview unit. *Cells are starting points for conversations, not the end of them.* "Trinitarian Monotheism" is not the Christian view of God — it's the three-word pointer to the Christian view of God. Your job is to know what's behind every pointer.

## Part 5 — The Four Tests

How do you tell whether a worldview is *true*? Not just *appealing*, not just *familiar*, not just *the one you grew up with* — but actually true?

Myers and Noebel adopt a four-test framework from the book *Making Sense of Your World* by W. Gary Phillips, William E. Brown, and John Stonestreet (Myers Ch 1, §12). Memorize these four. They appear on the Day-10 test and will appear in every later unit's Day 7 (or, for Unit 1, Day 8).

### The Four Tests

| # | Test | The question it asks |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Reason** | Is the worldview internally coherent? Can it be logically stated and defended without contradicting itself? |
| 2 | **Outer World** | Is there external, corroborating evidence to support it? Does it match what we observe in nature, history, and human experience? |
| 3 | **Inner World** | Does it adequately address our deepest moral, aesthetic, and relational intuitions? The "victories, disappointments, blessings, crises, and relationships of our everyday world"? |
| 4 | **Real-World Consequences** | When the worldview is lived out at scale, what fruit does it bear? Are its real-world consequences good or bad? |

### How to Apply Them

The tests work the same way against any worldview, including your own:

- The **Reason test** asks if the worldview is *internally* consistent. (Example: A worldview that says "there are no absolute truths" fails the test of reason because that very statement is itself an absolute truth claim.)
- The **Outer World test** asks if the worldview matches *external* evidence — what we observe. (Example: A worldview that says "the universe is eternal" must contend with strong scientific evidence that the universe had a beginning.)
- The **Inner World test** asks if the worldview matches our *internal* intuitions — what we feel and know about ourselves. (Example: A worldview that says "morality is just biology" must explain why we *all* believe that cruelty to children is wrong, not just culturally disapproved.)
- The **Real-World Consequences test** asks what kind of *world* the worldview produces when lived at scale. (Example: A worldview that says "the state is the source of all rights" has, every time it's been tried at scale, produced regimes that kill people who disagree.)

A worldview that passes all four cleanly is — at minimum — a worldview worth taking seriously. A worldview that fails one or more is worth examining hard before accepting.

### Honest Stakes

On Day 8 of every worldview unit (Day 8 in Unit 1, Day 7 in later units), we will apply the four tests *to Christianity itself*. This is not a betrayal. It is the only way to know whether Christianity actually deserves the place you give it.

The course's claim is that Christianity passes all four tests — but only when you're honest about the hard questions. We don't pretend the hard questions aren't there. We learn to *answer* them.

## Part 6 — Steel-Manning: The Standing Rule of This Classroom

### The Two Moves

There are two opposite ways to engage with a view you disagree with. One is *honest* and *useful*. The other is *cheap* and *common*.

#### Straw-Manning

**Definition:** Misrepresenting an opposing position to make it easier to knock down.

Examples of straw-man caricatures you'll encounter (and may have made yourself):

- *"Atheists just want to sin without feeling guilty."*
- *"Christians believe the earth is flat because the Bible says so."*
- *"Muslims want to take over the world."*
- *"Progressives want to destroy the family."*

In each case, there are real people who hold the opposing view for reasons you haven't considered. By picking the worst version, you ensure you never actually engage the strong version. You "win" the argument — against your own imagination.

#### Steel-Manning

**Definition:** Representing an opposing position in its **strongest**, most **charitable** form — the version the person holding it would nod at and say *"yes, that's what I mean."*

| Straw version | Steel-manned version |
|---|---|
| *"Atheists just want to sin without feeling guilty."* | *"Many atheists believe the evidence for God's existence is inconclusive, and that living morally and meaningfully without religious belief is both possible and intellectually honest."* |
| *"Muslims want to take over the world."* | *"Many Muslims believe Islam provides a complete framework for both private devotion and public life, and that a society governed by Islamic principles would be more just than one governed by secular ones."* |
| *"Progressives want to destroy the family."* | *"Many progressives believe traditional family structures have historically excluded or harmed people, and that expanding our definition of 'family' protects more people from those harms."* |

Notice: in each steel-manned version, we're not *agreeing* with the view. We're representing it the way its strongest proponent would. *Understanding is not endorsement.*

### Why This Is the Rule of This Classroom

1. **It's the first ethical move.** People who hold views you disagree with have reasons. Taking those reasons seriously treats them as agents, not opponents.
2. **It makes real disagreement possible.** If you only know the straw version, you're arguing with your own imagination. You'll never change anyone's mind, and you'll never test whether your own view survives serious challenge.
3. **It's the skill you need.** This year ends with you sitting across from someone who holds one of these worldviews and trying to articulate the Gospel in a way that's meaningful to them. You cannot do that if you only know the caricature.
4. **It models intellectual humility.** The strongest form of an opposing view is often more defensible than the straw version you pictured. Sometimes you'll change your mind about *something* — not the whole worldview, but a nuance. That's growth, not weakness.

### The Steel-Man Test

Before you engage an opposing view, ask yourself: *"If a thoughtful proponent of this view were standing here right now, would they hear my summary as fair?"*

- If **yes** → you're steel-manning.
- If **no** → you're straw-manning. Go back and try again.

### The One Rule You Cannot Break

**You may not refuse your assigned side.**

When this class does *Pair & Defend* (you'll learn this format on Day 8 of Unit 0), you will be assigned positions you don't personally hold. If you refuse to argue a position because you don't personally hold it, you've missed the point of the exercise — and of the course. The exercise is not about *endorsement*. It is about *representation*.

This rule applies to every Pair & Defend we do this year. When we get to harder topics — Islam, atheism, postmodernism, the family debate — you will be assigned sides you may strongly disagree with. That's the point. *If you refuse to steel-man a view, you cannot meaningfully disagree with it.*

## Part 7 — Primary Source: Plato's Cave

Plato (427–347 BC) was one of the most influential philosophers of all time. The *Allegory of the Cave*, from Book VII of his *Republic*, is a dialogue between his teacher Socrates and a student named Glaucon. It is the oldest worldview text in this course — about 2,400 years old — and it is included here because it asks the question this entire unit is built around: *What if your view of reality is wrong, and how would you know?*

### The Setup

Imagine prisoners chained from birth in a cave, facing a wall. Behind them is a fire and a low wall, and along that wall, other people carry statues and objects. The fire casts shadows of those objects on the cave wall in front of the prisoners. The prisoners have never seen anything else. To them, the shadows on the wall *are* reality. They name them. They study them. They reward each other for predicting which shadow will appear next.

One prisoner is freed. He's forced to turn around and face the fire. It blinds him at first — the light is painful. He's dragged up out of the cave into the sunlight. It blinds him again. But slowly his eyes adjust. He sees the objects themselves, then the trees and the people, then the sun.

He realizes the shadows weren't reality. They were *shadows of* reality.

He goes back into the cave to tell the others. They don't believe him. He can't see the shadows as well as before — his eyes have been ruined by the sun. They think he's gone mad. If they could, Plato writes, they would kill him.

### The Excerpt

> **[Socrates:]** Imagine human beings living in an underground, cavelike dwelling, with an entrance a long way up, which is both open to the light and as wide as the cave itself. They've been there since childhood, fixed in the same place, with their necks and legs fettered, able to see only in front of them, because their bonds prevent them from turning their heads around. Light is provided by a fire burning far above and behind them. Also behind them, but on higher ground, there is a path stretching between them and the fire. Imagine that along this path a low wall has been built, like the screen in front of puppeteers above which they show their puppets.
>
> **[Glaucon:]** I'm imagining it.
>
> **[Socrates:]** Then also imagine that there are people along the wall, carrying all kinds of artifacts that project above it — statues of people and other animals, made out of stone, wood, and every material. And, as you'd expect, some of the carriers are talking, and some are silent.
>
> **[Glaucon:]** It's a strange image you're describing, and strange prisoners.
>
> **[Socrates:]** They're like us. Do you suppose, first of all, that these prisoners see anything of themselves and one another besides the shadows that the fire casts on the wall in front of them?
>
> *[…]*
>
> **[Socrates:]** Consider, then, what being released from their bonds and cured of their ignorance would naturally be like if something like this came to pass. When one of them was freed and suddenly compelled to stand up, turn his head, walk, and look up toward the light, he'd be pained and dazzled and unable to see the things whose shadows he'd seen before.
>
> *[…]*
>
> **[Socrates:]** And if someone dragged him away from there by force, up the rough, steep path, and didn't let him go until he had dragged him into the sunlight, wouldn't he be pained and irritated at being treated that way? And when he came into the light, with the sun filling his eyes, wouldn't he be unable to see a single one of the things now said to be true? […] I suppose, then, that he'd need time to get adjusted before he could see things in the world above. At first, he'd see shadows most easily, then images of men and other things in water, then the things themselves.
>
> *(Republic, Book VII, trans. G. M. A. Grube, rev. C. D. C. Reeve. Hackett Publishing, 1992.)*

### Reading Questions

1. The prisoners spend their entire lives studying shadows and rewarding each other for predicting which shadow will appear next. What is Plato saying about the kind of expertise we develop *inside* a flawed worldview?
2. When the freed prisoner first sees the sun, it is *painful*. Why? What is Plato saying about the experience of having your worldview challenged?
3. When the freed prisoner returns to the cave to tell the others, they refuse to believe him. They think he's gone mad. *If they could, they would kill him.* Where in history, or in your own life, have you seen this pattern?
4. Plato wrote this 2,400 years ago. The Christian Gospel — written ~500 years later — also involves someone bringing light into a dark world and being killed for it. Is that pattern accidental? Or is Plato describing something true about human nature that the Gospel addresses?

## Part 8 — Doug Letter Preview

On Day 10 (Tue Sep 8), you will hand in your written response to "Doug." Doug is a fictional college freshman whose questions you will answer in every unit of this course. The publisher provides his Unit 0 letter; you will receive the printed copy in class and have time over the unit to draft your response. The Doug Letter for Unit 0 is short and orienting — it's your first one, and it establishes the format for the rest of the year.

**The setup:** Doug has just moved into his college dorm. His roommate Nathan mentions he's taking a World Religions class, and Doug signs up too. On the first day, the professor — a friendly older man who dresses like a hippie and brings a ukulele to class — asks each student to share their religion. Many say Christianity; others say Islam, Judaism, atheism. The professor announces that by the end of the semester, *most of the professed Christians would believe something different.*

Doug, shocked, talks to the professor after class. The professor asks him whether Christianity is the only way to God. Doug says yes — *Christianity is the only religion that acknowledges Jesus as both God and Savior.* The professor counters: *all religions ultimately lead to God; they're just different paths to the same destination.* Doug stammers a reply. The professor asks: *what do you know about other religions?* Doug admits, *not much.* The professor's parting line: *"If you don't know what other religions believe, then how do you know that other religions don't lead to God as well?"*

**Doug asks you:** *What is a worldview? Why should we study worldviews? What does it mean to have a Christian worldview? And — do you know why you are a Christian?*

**Format expectations** (matching the publisher's rubric):

- 1–2 pages, double-spaced
- Address Doug directly (it's a letter, not an essay)
- Use this unit's vocabulary: *worldview, descriptive vs. prescriptive, the 10 disciplines, the four tests, steel-manning*
- Quote at least one Scripture (Romans 12:2 and 1 Peter 3:15 are the unit's two anchor verses — see below)
- Conclude with what you'd actually say if you were Doug in that hallway

**The two anchor verses for Unit 0:**

- **Romans 12:2** — *"Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind, that by testing you may discern what is the will of God, what is good and acceptable and perfect."*
- **1 Peter 3:15** — *"But in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy, always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you; yet do it with gentleness and respect."*

Doug doesn't need a sermon. He needs a friend who can think clearly with him.

### The Parable of the Elephant

A story used in introductory comparative-religion classes: several blind men touch different parts of an elephant — the trunk, a leg, the tusk, the side — and each describes the elephant differently (a snake, a tree, a spear, a wall). The *popular moral* is that every worldview captures part of reality, so no worldview should make exclusive claims. The *publisher's response* is that this moral is self-contradictory: the only way to know everyone is touching the same elephant is to *be the person who can see the whole elephant.* The parable secretly assumes the very position (a view of the whole) that it claims is impossible. The Christian claim is that someone — God — *does* see the whole elephant, and has revealed it to us.

This parable is tested on the publisher's Day-10 test (Q23, Q24, Q25 — all three are short essay items).
