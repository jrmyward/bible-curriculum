## Section 1 — What a Worldview Is

### Definition (memorize verbatim — appears on Day-10 test, Q20)

> A worldview is **a pattern of ideas, beliefs, convictions, and habits that help us make sense of God, the world, and our relationship to God and the world.** (Myers Ch 1, §5)

### Four Claims About Worldviews (each is testable)

1. **Everyone has a worldview.** *(Day-10 test, Q16 — True)* Not everyone has examined theirs; not everyone could articulate it; but everyone operates from one.
2. **A worldview is descriptive AND prescriptive.** *(Day-10 test, Q19 — fill-in-blank)* It both describes what we think the world is AND prescribes how we should act in it.
3. **A worldview is like a mental map.** *(Day-10 test, Q17 — True)* It tells us where we are, where we need to go, and the best route to get there.
4. **The evidence of a worldview plays out in behavior.** *(Day-10 test, Q11 — multiple choice)* When what someone *says* and what someone *does* differ, the *doing* is the actual worldview.

### Religion as a Worldview Category (testable, Q12)

Islam, Marxism, and Secularism are *all* religious worldviews under the publisher's definition. *Any* system of belief that prescribes responses to the existence (or nonexistence) of the divine is religious. Atheism is religious in this sense. So is "I don't care about ultimate questions."

## Section 2 — Why Worldviews Matter

### "Ideas Have Consequences" (Day-10 test, Q13)

The phrase means: *our worldview affects how we live*. Not that atheists are immoral or theists are moral. Not that ideology determines behavior in some mechanical way. The claim is more modest and more serious: what you think about ultimate questions shapes the kind of person you become, the kind of family you raise, and the kind of world you build.

### The Worldview Questions (Day-10 test, Q14)

A worldview answers questions like:
- Who is God?
- What does it mean to be human?
- What is the foundation for right and wrong?

The publisher tests this as an "all of the above" multiple-choice item. The point: worldviews don't address one domain — they address all of them.

### The Stakes — Historical Examples

- **Nazism:** ~21 million systematically exterminated, plus tens of millions in WWII combat (Myers Ch 1, §6).
- **Communist regimes:** well over 100 million killed in the 20th century (Myers Ch 1, §6).

Both began as *ideas*. Bad ideas, at scale, cost lives.

## Section 3 — The 10 Analytical Disciplines

The 10 lenses we will apply to every worldview in this course. Match each to its publisher-defined definition (Day-10 test, Q1–Q10, all matching).

| Discipline | Publisher's definition (memorize verbatim — these are the matching items) |
|---|---|
| **1. Theology** | The study of God |
| **2. Philosophy** | The study of knowledge, truth, and the nature of ultimate reality |
| **3. Ethics** | The study of moral conduct, values, and duties |
| **4. Biology** | The study of life and living organisms |
| **5. Psychology** | The study of the human mind (or soul) |
| **6. Sociology** | The study of human societies and institutions |
| **7. Law** | The study of ordinances designed to help citizens coexist peacefully |
| **8. Politics** | The study of community governance |
| **9. Economics** | The study of the management of resources |
| **10. History** | The study of past places, persons, and events |

### Why These 10

These are the *foundational* disciplines — the ones every other field of study inherits answers from. A school of architecture is downstream of theology, philosophy, and ethics. A medical school is downstream of biology, psychology, and ethics. By understanding a worldview's answer to all 10, you can predict — fairly accurately — what that worldview will say about any other field.

### The Worldview Grid (introduced Day 7)

Every worldview occupies one column on a **9 × 10 grid**: 9 worldviews × 10 disciplines = 90 cells. By May you should be able to fill in every cell of that grid from memory. Cells are *starting points for conversations*, not the end of them.

## Section 5 — The Four Tests for Evaluating a Worldview

Memorize these four. They appear on the Day-10 test (Q22, essay — 4 points) and in *every* later unit's Day-7 (or Day-8 for Unit 1) Four-Test exercise.

| # | Test | What it asks |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Reason** | Is the worldview internally coherent? Can it be logically stated and defended without contradicting itself? |
| 2 | **Outer World** | Is there external evidence to support it? Does it match what we observe? |
| 3 | **Inner World** | Does it adequately address personal experience — our deepest moral, aesthetic, and relational intuitions? |
| 4 | **Real World** | Does the worldview lead to positive or negative consequences when lived out at scale? |

### Application

A worldview that *passes all four cleanly* is — at minimum — worth taking seriously. A worldview that *fails one or more* is worth examining hard before accepting.

We will apply these four tests to Christianity itself on Day 8 of Unit 1, and to every later worldview on its Day 7. Today's job is to learn the tests well enough to use them.

## Section 6 — The Rule of This Classroom: Steel-Manning

### Definitions

- **Straw-manning** — misrepresenting an opposing position to make it easier to knock down.
- **Steel-manning** — representing an opposing position in its *strongest*, most *charitable* form: the version its proponent would nod at and say *"yes, that's what I mean."*

### Why Steel-Manning Is the Rule

1. It's the first ethical move — taking opposing reasons seriously treats people as agents, not opponents.
2. It makes real disagreement possible — you cannot meaningfully disagree with a view you've never honestly represented.
3. It's the skill the course is preparing you for — articulating the Gospel to someone who holds a different worldview requires understanding their actual view.
4. It models intellectual humility — sometimes the strongest form of an opposing view is more defensible than the straw version you pictured.

### The Standing Rule

**You may not refuse your assigned side** in any Pair & Defend exercise. Understanding is not endorsement. You will be assigned positions you don't personally hold. That is the point.

## Section 7 — How Ideas Spread (and How to Resist Bad Ones)

### The Publisher's Inoculation Theory (Day-10 test, Q15)

Ideas spread the way viruses spread. The publisher (drawing on William McGuire's 1950s research) identifies six possible defenses, ordered from worst to best:

| Defense | Effectiveness |
|---|---|
| Indoctrination (continually reinforcing the same position) | **Worst** — *worse than no preparation at all* |
| No preparation | Bad |
| Reinforcement of previous preparation | Bad |
| Warning of attack | Better |
| Inoculation (exposure to opposing positions) | Better |
| Inoculation + Refutation | Better |
| **Inoculation + Refutation + Preparation** | **Best** |

**Indoctrination is NOT a way to guard against bad ideas.** Testable as Q15 on the Day-10 test (the question asks which of the listed defenses is *NOT* effective — the answer is "indoctrination").

The publisher's argument: *repeating a message over and over again, even with increasing fervency and emotion, is actually counterproductive.* People who have been equipped only with the conclusion (and not with the *defense* of the conclusion) fall for falsehoods more easily than people with no preparation.

### What This Means for This Class

This class is built on inoculation + refutation + preparation. You will be exposed to other worldviews on their own terms. You will learn the strongest objections to Christianity. You will be given the thinking tools to work through both. That's not undermining your faith — that's the only known way to actually build resistance to bad ideas.

## Section 8 — The Parable of the Elephant

### The Parable (Day-10 test, Q23 — short essay)

Several blind men encounter an elephant. Each touches a different part (trunk, leg, tusk, side) and describes the elephant differently (snake, tree, spear, wall). None has the whole picture.

### The Popular Moral (Q24)

*Every worldview is correct in its own way; no single worldview has the entire picture, so worldviews should not make exclusive claims.*

### The Fundamental Problem (Q25)

**The popular moral is self-contradictory.** It claims that everyone is limited to a small part of the elephant — yet the only way to *know* everyone is limited is to *be the person who can see the whole elephant.* The parable secretly assumes the very view-of-the-whole it claims is impossible.

The Christian response: someone *does* see the whole elephant — God — and has revealed enough of it for us to know what kind of world we live in.
