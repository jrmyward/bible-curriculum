## Day 5 — Socratic Seminar [50 min]

**Goal:** Students drive the discussion from their reading and from questions they bring. Teacher facilitates only — guiding, redirecting, cold-calling — but does not lecture and does not resolve. By the end, students should have engaged at least two genuinely contested questions about Christianity at substantive depth.

**Materials needed:**
- Each student brings 2–3 written questions (homework from Day 4)
- Chairs in a circle (or two concentric circles for fishbowl variant)
- A timer (you'll use it)
- A clipboard for tracking participation

### Opening [5 min]

"Today is the first day in this class where I will say almost nothing. The discussion goes where you take it. Three rules:

1. **Cite evidence.** When you make a claim, point to where it came from — the reading packet, a Scripture, something from this week's lectures, or your own experience. 'I think' is not enough; tell us *why* you think it.
2. **Steel-man before you object.** Before you disagree with someone, restate their position in a way they would recognize as fair. If they say 'yes, that's what I meant,' you've earned the right to push back.
3. **Make space, take space.** If you've spoken three times, hold off. If you haven't spoken, find your moment. I will cold-call if I need to."

"Here is the opening question. We'll take it as far as it goes; if it runs out, I have backup prompts."

### Anchor prompt [opens the seminar]

**WRITE ON BOARD:**

> *Christianity claims to be true — not just personally meaningful, but objectively, historically, universally true. Is that claim defensible in 2026?*

Let it sit. Wait. Resist the urge to fill silence. Adolescents will speak; they just need longer than you think. If 30 seconds passes with no one volunteering, cold-call: *"[Student name], what's your gut on this?"*

### Anticipated discussion paths

Most opening prompts of this kind go in one of three directions. Be ready for all three.

**Direction A — historical: "If the resurrection happened, Christianity stands. If it didn't, it falls."**
Push points:
- 1 Cor. 15:14 — Christianity stakes itself on a specific historical event. Is that strength or weakness?
- What kind of evidence would settle the question? What kind couldn't?
- If a student asserts "you can't prove the resurrection," ask: *"What would count as proof of an event 2,000 years ago? How do we know anything about ancient history?"*

**Direction B — moral: "Even if Christianity isn't 'true,' isn't it good for society?"**
Push points:
- Habermas's claim (covered Day 2) — that human rights have a Christian genealogy. Does usefulness count as truth?
- If a student says "all religions teach the same thing," ask: *"Have you read the actual sacred texts of any two? What did they say?"*
- The "good for society" question collapses into the truth question quickly — push there.

**Direction C — personal: "I grew up Christian. Why am I being asked to defend it?"**
Push points:
- The class is not asking students to *abandon* their faith but to *articulate* it. Acknowledge the discomfort.
- *"If someone asked you tomorrow why you believe what you believe, what would you say?"* — that's the question this unit is preparing them for.
- Differentiate inherited belief from owned belief — both can be Christian; the second is what college will test.

### Backup prompts (if the anchor runs out)

1. *"The reading packet quoted Habermas — a non-Christian philosopher — saying there is 'no alternative' to the Judeo-Christian ethic of human rights. Is he right, and if so, what does that mean for societies trying to keep the ethic without the theology?"*
2. *"Christianity says God is personal. New Spirituality and Hinduism would say God is impersonal. Buddhism would say there is no 'God' at all in the personal sense. What is at stake in the question of whether the divine has a face?"*
3. *"The Nicene Creed is 1,700 years old. It is recited every Sunday by hundreds of millions of Christians across denominations that disagree about many other things. What explains that durability?"*
4. *"Why does the resurrection — not the crucifixion, not the teachings — get treated as the load-bearing claim of Christianity?"*

### Cold-call menu

When a student has been quiet too long, or when the discussion is sliding into agreement and needs friction, use one of these:

- *"[Name], what would you say to that?"*
- *"[Name], steel-man the opposite position."*
- *"[Name], you haven't said anything yet. What's been the most interesting thing you've heard?"*
- *"[Name], is anyone in this room saying something you actually disagree with? Tell us."*

### Closing debrief [last 5 min]

"Two questions:

1. **What was the strongest argument you heard from the side you started disagreeing with?** Hand-raise; take 3 answers.
2. **What is one question you are now carrying that you weren't carrying when class started?** Have students write this on the back of their reading packet. Don't collect — they keep it."

### What to collect

**Nothing graded.** Track participation on your clipboard during class. If you want a written artifact, have students hand in their 2–3 prepared questions on the way out.

### Trouble spots

- **"No one is talking."** Acknowledge it: *"This is the first one. It's awkward. Talk anyway."* Then wait. Then cold-call.
- **"One student is dominating."** Use the make-space-take-space rule, said warmly: *"[Name], you've done a lot of the heavy lifting. Let's hear from someone who hasn't been in yet."*
- **"The discussion turns into a Sunday-school recitation."** Push for friction: *"What is the *hardest* version of this objection? Don't soften it."*
- **"A student says something that's theologically off."** Don't correct in the moment unless it's wildly off. Note it for the closing 2 minutes: *"One thing I want to come back to — earlier someone said X. That's a common framing, but here's where the historic Christian position actually lands…"*
- **"A student attacks Christianity sincerely."** Welcome it. This is the kind of pressure the unit is designed to produce. *"That's a real challenge. Who wants to take a swing at responding?"* If no one does, you take it briefly — but only after waiting.

### Sub-friendliness notes

A substitute can run this day if they hold the line on not lecturing. The anchor prompt is provided; the backup prompts are provided; the rules are provided. The sub's job is to enforce the rules and run the clock. If the sub is uncomfortable with theology, that's actually fine for this day — they're not supposed to teach. They're supposed to keep students talking.

## Day 6 — Pair & Defend [50 min]

**Format:** two-round simultaneous pair debate. See [cohort-tools.md §B](../../../_shared/cohort-tools.md) for the protocol (5 min setup · 8 min Round 1 · 6 min Round 2 · 5 min debrief = 24 min). With two runs of the protocol and transitions, the period fills cleanly.

**Goal:** Every student argues *both* sides of two contested claims about Christianity. By the end, students should be able to articulate the strongest opposing position on each — not just dismiss it.

**Materials needed:**
- Scratch paper for brainstorming (5 min before Round 1)
- Two prompts ready to put on the board (below)
- Timer

### Opening [3 min]

"Today every one of you will argue against Christianity for part of the period, and *for* it for the other part. This is not a trick. The goal is not to convert anyone to anything. The goal is to make sure you have *heard* the strongest version of the position you disagree with, and that you can *make* the strongest version of the position you agree with.

Pair up. I'll assign starting sides. Brainstorm both sides on paper for 5 minutes before anyone speaks."

### Prompt 1 [20 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:**

> *Christianity's claim to be the one true worldview is reasonable.*
> *vs.*
> *Christianity's claim to be the one true worldview is arrogant.*

Assign sides (alternate down the row). Brainstorm 5 min, Round 1 (8 min), Round 2 — swap sides — (6 min), brief class debrief (1 min: "what was the strongest argument from the side you started disagreeing with?").

**Anticipated student arguments to listen for:**

*For "reasonable":*
- The exclusivity claim follows from the historical claim — if Jesus rose from the dead, Christianity is the only worldview that has its founder vindicated. Refusing to claim truth is itself dishonest.
- Every worldview makes exclusive claims (atheism claims theism is wrong; Buddhism claims Christianity misunderstands the self). Christianity is no more arrogant than any other.
- The exclusivity is *invitational*, not coercive — Christianity says everyone is welcome, but the door is Christ.

*For "arrogant":*
- Claiming exclusive truth about cosmic questions, when there are 2.5 billion Muslims and 1 billion Hindus and 500 million Buddhists with their own ancient traditions, requires significant intellectual humility — humility Christianity often does not display.
- Historically, exclusive-truth claims have been used to justify violence (crusades, inquisitions, colonial missions). The claim has fruit.
- A more humble framing — Christianity is *my* path, others have *theirs* — would still let Christians be devout without making cosmic-scale judgments.

### Prompt 2 [20 min]

**WRITE ON BOARD:**

> *If naturalism is true, human beings have no inherent dignity.*
> *vs.*
> *Human beings have inherent dignity regardless of whether God exists.*

Same protocol. Reassign sides so partners argue something new.

**Anticipated student arguments:**

*For "no inherent dignity if naturalism is true":*
- Dignity is a metaphysical claim — "this being is to be treated with reverence." If we are just chemicals, what grounds the reverence? Atoms aren't reverent.
- Secular philosophers like Yuval Noah Harari have argued exactly this — that "human rights" are useful fictions. The Christian objection is that *if you mean what you say*, the position collapses morally.
- The 20th-century regimes that took naturalism most seriously (Soviet, Maoist, Nazi) treated humans as biological raw material. That's not coincidence; that's logic.

*For "dignity regardless":*
- Dignity can be grounded in capacities (rationality, autonomy, the ability to suffer) rather than in metaphysics. Many secular ethicists hold this.
- Christianity's claim that dignity comes from being made in God's image was *itself* a development — older religions didn't grant universal human dignity either. Maybe we don't need the original argument to keep the conclusion.
- Empirically, plenty of atheists live as if humans have dignity. That's evidence the conclusion doesn't depend on the premise.

### Debrief [5 min]

Whole-class question: *"Which prompt was harder to argue against? Why?"* Take 3–4 answers. Don't resolve. Land on: *"Both prompts will return in later units. You're going to meet thoughtful people who hold every one of these positions. The earlier you've argued each position out loud, the better you'll do in those conversations."*

### What to collect

Students hand in their brainstorming scratch paper at the end (graded for completion only — evidence that both sides were brainstormed).

### Trouble spots

- **"My partner won't argue their side."** Coach them: *"You agreed to take that side for 8 minutes. After that you can disagree. For now, make the strongest case you can think of."*
- **"This feels dishonest — I'm not allowed to lie."** Reframe: *"You're not lying. You're representing a position someone actually holds. Christians are commanded to be 'always prepared to give a defense' (1 Pet. 3:15). You can't defend against an argument you've never made."*
- **"I'm a Christian; arguing against Christianity feels wrong."** Acknowledge it warmly. Then: *"Jesus's own brother James said in James 1:19 to be 'quick to hear, slow to speak.' Today's exercise is making sure you've actually heard. If you can't articulate the other side, you haven't heard it."*

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub can run this if they keep the clock. The brainstorm phase (5 min before speaking) is critical — don't let students skip it. Without brainstorming, Round 1 is just talking off the top of one's head.

## Day 7 — Case Study: The 22-Year-Old Who Lost Her Father [50 min]

**Format:** Case Study per [cohort-tools.md §C](../../../_shared/cohort-tools.md). 10 min read + discuss in groups · 5 min × group present · 5 min vote + debrief. With Unit 1's case there's also a Day 6 publisher supplemental quiz (~5 min individual quiz) per the teaching-map's Day 6 — administer it at the start of class.

**Goal:** Move from abstract worldview content to a concrete situation where Christianity is asked to give an answer. Students see *what their worldview costs and what it provides* in a moment of real human suffering.

**Materials needed:**
- Printed case-study handout (one per student — content below)
- Publisher's Ch 02 supplemental-reading quiz (Gospel of Mark, ~5 min) — from `_source-text/_cd/UTT Unit 02 Files/`
- Worksheet (one per group — template below)

### Opening: supplemental quiz [5 min]

Administer the publisher's Gospel-of-Mark supplemental quiz from the CD. 5 min, individual, closed-book. Collect at end.

### The case [3 min]

Read the case aloud once. Then hand out the printed copy.

> **The case.** Hannah is 22, a senior at Boise State, majoring in social work. Her father — a quiet, faithful Christian who never missed a Sunday — was diagnosed with stage-4 pancreatic cancer in March. He died on a Tuesday afternoon in June, ten weeks after the diagnosis. Hannah was holding his hand. She is the oldest of three; she's now the one her younger brothers call when something falls apart.
>
> Two weeks after the funeral she's sitting in a coffee shop with her best friend Sarah, who is not a Christian. Sarah is gentle and direct: "Hannah, I love you, and I want to ask you something honestly. Where was your God when your dad was dying? Why didn't he heal him? Your dad was *good*. He didn't deserve this. And now you're carrying it. What is the Christian story supposed to do for you in this moment?"
>
> Hannah doesn't have a script. She has her actual faith. What does it offer her?

### Group work [10 min — groups of 3–4]

Each group works through the worksheet below. They must produce *all three* sections; don't let groups skip the second one.

**Worksheet:**

```
The Case: Hannah & Sarah, the coffee shop

1. WHAT THE CHRISTIAN WORLDVIEW OFFERS (across the 10 lenses)
   Pick 3 lenses that speak most directly to this moment. For
   each, write one sentence that names what Christianity gives
   Hannah that another worldview wouldn't.

   Lens: __________  →  __________________________________
   Lens: __________  →  __________________________________
   Lens: __________  →  __________________________________

2. WHERE THE CHRISTIAN ANSWER IS HARD
   Identify one place where the Christian answer is hard, costly,
   or doesn't give comfort easily. Don't soften it. What is
   Christianity asking Hannah to hold that another worldview
   wouldn't ask her to hold?

   ____________________________________________________________

3. WHAT HANNAH MIGHT ACTUALLY SAY TO SARAH
   Draft 3–4 sentences Hannah could say in response. Not a
   sermon. A real human answer.

   ____________________________________________________________

   ____________________________________________________________
```

### Anticipated group responses to push on

**Section 1 — likely lenses chosen:** History (the redemptive narrative — death is not the end), Psychology (the soul outlives the body, Matt. 10:28), Theology (a personal God who himself suffered loss in the death of his Son), Sociology (the church as a family of families — Hannah is not alone).

**Section 2 — likely hard answers:** The Christian answer is not "your dad was healed." It is "your dad has died, AND death has been defeated, AND he will rise." That requires Hannah to hold both grief and hope at the same time. Other worldviews resolve the tension by choosing one (Buddhist non-attachment dissolves grief; pure naturalism resolves the tension by denying meaning to either life or death). Christianity asks more.

**Section 3 — what to listen for:** Honest sentences, not theology lectures. *"I don't have an answer for why now. I have an answer for what's true. My dad isn't gone in the way he would be if there were no God. I have hope I'll see him again. And right now I am also incredibly sad. Both are real."* That's a strong response. Push back on responses that flatten into either "everything happens for a reason" (cheap) or pure grief without Christian content (also incomplete).

### Group presentations [15 min — adjust to group count]

Each group presents (2–3 min). At minimum: which 3 lenses they chose, the hard answer they identified, and Hannah's draft response. After each group, take one question from the class.

### Class vote + debrief [10 min]

Vote: *"Which group's response to Sarah would you trust most in a real conversation?"* Brief teacher debrief — name what was strong, what was missing.

Land the closing point: *"You are going to have a version of this conversation in your actual life. Maybe sooner than you think. The Christian answer is not 'we know why.' It is 'we know who.' Hannah doesn't get an explanation. She gets a person — Jesus — who himself wept at his friend's tomb (John 11:35) before he raised him. That's the Christian shape of grief."*

### Final 2 min

Bridge: "Tomorrow is Day 8 — Four-Test self-application. We will apply Myers's four tests to Christianity itself. Re-read Part 1 of the reading packet tonight, especially the Eight Anchors. Tomorrow's small-group work assumes you have it cold."

### What to collect

- Supplemental quiz (graded — publisher's answer key)
- Worksheets (one per group — graded for completion + thoughtfulness)
- Each student also hands in a 3-sentence written response: *"What would YOU say to Sarah?"*

### Trouble spots

- **"This is too sad / too heavy."** Acknowledge: *"It is. Real Christian witness happens in moments like this — not in apologetics debates. The reason we do this case is that the abstract content of the last week has to mean something at a coffee shop with a friend who is hurting."*
- **"A student in the room has actually lost a parent recently."** Be aware in advance. Talk with that student privately before class — they can opt out, or participate, but they shouldn't be ambushed.
- **"A group writes a Hallmark response that flattens the theology."** Push: *"Where in your answer does the actual Christian gospel show up? If it could have been written by any kind, non-Christian friend, you haven't reached Christianity yet."*

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub can run this — the case is printed, the worksheet is printed, the structure is timed. The sub's only theology call is during the debrief; coach the sub to *summarize what groups said* rather than offer their own commentary if they're not confident.

## Day 8 — Four-Test Self-Application: Small-Group Worksheet [35 min after 10-min lecture frame]

**Goal:** Every student practices applying *two* of the four tests to Christianity itself, in a small group, with a worksheet, with a whole-class synthesis at the end. This is the **model exercise** for the rest of the course — every later unit's Day 7 follows this template. Take it seriously.

**The lecture frame (first 10 min) is in [lecture-companion.md](../lectures/unit-01-christianity-lecture-companion.md) §Day 8.** This file covers the small-group work that follows.

**Materials needed:**
- Worksheet (one per group — template below)
- The four tests on the board (from the lecture frame)
- A timer

### Group assignment [3 min]

Form groups of 3–4. Assign each group *two* of the four tests:

- Groups 1, 2: **Reason** + **Outer World**
- Groups 3, 4: **Inner World** + **Real-World Consequences**
- (Or distribute differently based on group count — but every group gets two tests, and at least two groups cover each test so the debrief has cross-perspectives.)

### Group work [25 min — about 12 min per test]

**Worksheet template:**

```
Group ____________   Tests assigned: ________ + ________

TEST #1: ____________________________________________
(Question this test asks: ___________________________)

(a) WHERE CHRISTIANITY PASSES CLEANLY
    Name 2 places. Be specific. Use vocabulary from the unit.

    1. ________________________________________________
    2. ________________________________________________

(b) WHERE CHRISTIANITY RAISES A HARD QUESTION
    Name 1 hard question. Don't soften it. Don't pretend it
    isn't there.

    ___________________________________________________

(c) HOW A THOUGHTFUL CHRISTIAN RESPONDS
    Write 2–3 sentences. The response doesn't have to make
    the hard question go away; it has to show how someone
    can hold the question and the faith at the same time.

    ___________________________________________________

    ___________________________________________________

[Repeat for Test #2 below.]
```

### What to listen for at each table (teacher rotates)

**REASON (internal coherence):**
- Passes: Coherent four-act story. Objective morality grounded in God's character. Accounts for both dignity and brokenness. Christianity has no internal contradiction in its core claims.
- Hard question: The Trinity (one being, three persons) and Incarnation (Jesus fully God and fully man) appear paradoxical. Are they contradictions?
- Response: A contradiction asserts and denies the same thing in the same sense at the same time. The Trinity does not (one being / three persons — different categories). The Incarnation does not (one person / two natures). They are *mysteries* — claims that exceed reason — not violations of reason.

**OUTER WORLD (matches what we observe):**
- Passes: The bodily resurrection is rooted in real history. The Imago Dei doctrine correctly predicted universal human dignity. Fine-tuning of physical constants. The moral universals across cultures.
- Hard question: The problem of natural evil (earthquakes, cancer, suffering of innocents) — if God is good and powerful, why this?
- Response: Christianity has the most robust answer in the religious world to the *fact* of suffering (the fall, the cross, the resurrection). It does not always offer an answer to specific *instances* of suffering. The Christian answer is "we know who, not always why" — God himself entered the suffering and defeated it.

**INNER WORLD (matches our deepest intuitions):**
- Passes: Accounts for guilt, longing, beauty, the universal sense that the world is "not as it should be." Explains why love demands relationship (the Trinitarian God *is* love-in-relationship eternally).
- Hard question: The doctrine of eternal punishment / hell — does this match the moral intuition that God is loving?
- Response: Different Christian traditions handle this differently (eternal conscious torment, conditional immortality, hopeful universalism). All agree God is just and loving. The historic mainstream holds that hell is the consequence of a freely-chosen alienation from God — God respects the no.

**REAL-WORLD CONSEQUENCES (fruit when lived at scale):**
- Passes: Hospitals, universities, abolition, modern science, the language of human rights, end-of-life care, mass literacy via Scripture translation.
- Hard question: Crusades, inquisitions, the wars of religion, residential schools, church abuse scandals, slavery defended on Biblical grounds.
- Response: The fruit goes both ways. Christianity makes a strong claim — that it is true — and so its failures are particularly damning. The Christian answer is not "ignore the failures" but "the same Scripture used to defend slavery was used to abolish it; the difference was who was reading it more honestly." The faith is not invalidated by its abusers; it is exposed by its faithful.

### Whole-class synthesis [5 min]

Quick popcorn-style debrief. For each test:

- *"What did your group identify as the strongest Christian pass?"* (1 group answers)
- *"What did your group identify as the hardest pressure point?"* (1 group answers)
- *"How would a thoughtful Christian respond?"* (1 group answers)

Don't take time for everyone. Move fast. The point is exposure to all four, not depth on any one.

Land the closing point: *"This is the exercise we'll repeat for every worldview. Today you applied the four tests to Christianity itself — and you found that it can survive its own tests, but only when you're honest about the hard questions. That honesty is what we'll demand of every later worldview too."*

### What to collect

Group worksheets — graded for completion + thoughtful engagement with both halves (passes + hard questions). If a group only wrote on passes and skipped the hard questions, that's a 50%. The hard questions are the whole point.

### Trouble spots

- **"A group refuses to name a hard question."** Push: *"If you can't name a hard question, you're not testing Christianity — you're praising it. The exercise requires both. Try again."*
- **"A student suggests a hard question that's actually a confusion."** Use it as a teaching moment: *"Tell me more — what specifically is the question?"* Usually clarification surfaces what they actually mean. If it's truly off, note it for the close.

### Sub-friendliness notes

This day is doable for a sub *because* it's structured. The lecture frame is 10 min and scripted in the companion. The small groups are self-directing with the worksheet. The sub's job is timekeeping + rotation through groups to make sure everyone is engaging the hard questions.

## Day 11 — Gospel Conversation Practice [50 min]

**Goal:** Students practice articulating the Gospel — God, sin, Christ, redemption — to someone who has no Christian background. This is the **foundational exercise** that returns in every subsequent unit, adapted to each worldview's adherents. Get the basic version right today.

**Materials needed:**
- Roleplay cards (template below) — printed, two per pair
- The four Doug-Letter questions written on the board (covered in lecture-companion Day 1; rehearse them today)

### Opening [5 min]

"You've spent ten days learning what Christianity claims. Today you practice what to *say* about it.

Two ground rules:

1. **No Christianese.** No 'washed in the blood,' no 'accept Jesus into your heart,' no 'born again' unless you're prepared to define it in 30 seconds for someone who has never heard the phrase. The vocabulary the unit has given you is what you use.
2. **Listen first.** Before you talk *about* the Gospel, you find out what your conversation partner already thinks. The most important sentence you can say in this conversation is 'tell me more about that.'"

**WRITE ON BOARD:** *Four things the Gospel says — God · Sin · Christ · Redemption*

### The four words [8 min — quick teacher review]

For each, a one-line student-facing version:

- **GOD** — There is a God. He is personal, loving, transcendent, and immanent. He made you on purpose, and you matter to him.
- **SIN** — Something has gone wrong with the human heart — and with mine. We are bent inward. We hurt each other. We hurt ourselves. We can't fix it.
- **CHRIST** — God didn't leave us in the mess. He came in person, in Jesus, lived among us, died for our sins, and rose from the dead. The resurrection is the central historical claim of the Christian faith.
- **REDEMPTION** — The invitation is to trust Jesus. Not to clean yourself up first. Not to become religious. To trust that Jesus has done for us what we couldn't do for ourselves, and to let that change everything.

"Those four words are not the whole Christian story. They are the *spine*. Memorize them. You can hang the rest on them in any conversation."

### Roleplay setup [3 min]

Pair up. One partner is *Christian*; one partner is *Skeptic*. The Skeptic is given a card with their persona and three of their actual questions. The Christian has no card — they have to respond live, using the four words and the unit content.

After 8 minutes, swap roles. The previous Skeptic becomes Christian and gets a new card.

### Skeptic card #1 — Sarah, the biochemistry major

> *You are Sarah, a senior biochemistry major at Boise State. You were raised in a vaguely Catholic family but stopped going to church in high school. You're not hostile to religion, but you find it intellectually unconvincing.*
>
> *Three questions you bring:*
>
> *1. "If God exists and is good, why isn't he obvious? Why hide?"*
>
> *2. "I work with cells every day. I see how evolution works. I don't see God doing anything that nature isn't already doing. What would God add?"*
>
> *3. "I'm a good person. I'm honest. I love my family. I help people. Why do I need to be 'saved' from anything?"*

### Skeptic card #2 — Marcus, the cynic

> *You are Marcus, 19, taking a year off before college. You grew up in a Christian family in eastern Idaho but stopped believing when you saw the church protect an abusive youth pastor. You're not looking to debate; you're tired.*
>
> *Three questions you bring:*
>
> *1. "I've heard the verses. Don't quote Bible at me. Tell me why the Church I grew up in protected an abuser instead of his victims."*
>
> *2. "If the Gospel is true, why does Christianity produce so many people who don't act like it?"*
>
> *3. "Honestly — I think I'd rather just be a decent person and not deal with the religion part. What does Christianity offer that being a decent person doesn't?"*

### Skeptic card #3 — Priya, the curious Hindu

> *You are Priya, an international student from Mumbai. You are a practicing Hindu — Vaishnava, specifically — devoted to Krishna. You are open and curious. You like your Christian friend and want to understand their faith.*
>
> *Three questions you bring:*
>
> *1. "We have many manifestations of the divine in Hinduism. Why is your tradition so insistent on only one — and on him being a Jewish man from 2,000 years ago?"*
>
> *2. "I believe in karma. It is just — what you sow, you reap. Christianity offers forgiveness instead. But isn't forgiveness without consequence … unjust?"*
>
> *3. "I've read the Sermon on the Mount. It is beautiful. Why isn't that enough? Why do I need to believe Jesus rose from the dead?"*

### Skeptic card #4 — Daniel, the secular Jewish friend

> *You are Daniel, 21, raised in a Jewish family in New York. You don't keep kosher; you don't go to synagogue except for High Holidays. You consider yourself culturally Jewish, secular in practice. Your roommate is a Christian who has just started asking you questions.*
>
> *Three questions you bring:*
>
> *1. "My grandparents survived the Holocaust. Most of my extended family did not. Where was the Christian God in 1944?"*
>
> *2. "Jews have been told by Christians for 2,000 years that we need to accept Jesus. Christians have killed us when we said no. Why should I take your invitation seriously now?"*
>
> *3. "If your messiah came 2,000 years ago, why is the world still broken? What did he actually accomplish?"*

### The roleplay rounds [20 min total — 8 min × 2 rounds + 4 min reset]

Round 1: 8 min. Hand out cards. The Skeptic asks their first question and the Christian responds — using the four words, listening first, no Christianese. The Skeptic may ask the second and third questions in turn, or stay with the first.

After 8 min: swap roles. New card to the new Skeptic.

Round 2: 8 min.

### Debrief [10 min]

Three full-class questions:

1. *"What was the hardest question you faced as the Christian, and how did you handle it?"* (Take 3 answers.)
2. *"What did your Skeptic partner say that you weren't expecting?"* (Take 3 answers.)
3. *"What would you do differently next time?"* (Take 3 answers.)

Close: *"Every later worldview unit has a Day 8 Gospel Conversation. We will adapt the Skeptic card for the worldview we're studying — a Muslim friend in Unit 3, an LDS friend in Unit 4, a Hindu friend in Unit 5. The four words don't change. What changes is the *entry point* — what part of the Gospel best meets that person where they are. Today you practiced the baseline. From here on, you adapt."*

### What to collect

3×5 card from each student at the end: *"One sentence about what you learned today."*

### Trouble spots

- **"My partner won't take the Skeptic role seriously."** Coach: *"Your job is to ask the questions on the card as if they were actually yours. If you make it easy, you waste your partner's practice."*
- **"A Christian student gets defensive."** Pause the pair, coach: *"Take a breath. The Skeptic is asking what real people ask. Don't argue — listen, then respond using one of the four words."*
- **"A student starts proselytizing in the debrief."** Redirect: *"This is a class on understanding, not a witnessing session. What did you *learn* from the conversation?"*
- **"A student tells you they had a real conversation like this last week."** This is a feature. Welcome it: *"What worked? What didn't?"*

### Sub-friendliness notes

The Skeptic cards are printed; the structure is timed; the four words are on the board. A sub can run this if they enforce no-Christianese and listen-first. The sub should NOT try to model the Christian role themselves unless they are confident — coach from the side.

## Day 12 — Worldview Brief Writing [50 min — independent]

**Goal:** Every student writes their own Christianity column of the worldview grid in their own words. This becomes their reference document for the rest of the year — they will return to it in Unit 10 (Capstone) and notice how their thinking has matured.

### Opening [5 min]

"Today you write. No discussion. No partner. Just you, your reading packet, your filled-in learning outline, and a pen. By the end of the period you will have a Christianity column you wrote yourself. We will come back to it nine times this year — every time we study a new worldview, you compare it back to this column. So put real thought in."

### The assignment [print and distribute]

> **Worldview Brief — Unit 1: Christianity**
>
> Write a brief that gives Christianity's answer to each of the 10 disciplines. Your brief must contain:
>
> - One paragraph (3–5 sentences) per discipline
> - One Scripture anchor per discipline (chapter and verse — quote it or paraphrase it accurately)
> - In your own words. Don't copy from the reading packet. If a sentence sounds like Myers/Noebel wrote it, rewrite it.
> - At least three of the *Eight Theological Anchors* (Trinitarian Monotheism, Imago Dei, Special Creation, Natural Law, Redemptive Narrative, Sphere Sovereignty, Agape Love, Biblical Stewardship) appearing somewhere in your brief, named explicitly.
>
> **Length:** 2–3 pages handwritten, or 600–900 words typed.
>
> **What you may use:** reading packet, learning outline, your filled-in student outline, your Bible.
>
> **What you may not use:** another student's brief, the internet, the lecture companion.
>
> **Submit:** by end of period today. Keep a copy — you will refer back to it many times this year.
>
> **Grading:** completion + accuracy + own-voice. Briefs that read as paraphrased Myers/Noebel will be returned for revision.

### Body [40 min — students work independently]

Walk the room. Answer specific questions one-on-one. Don't deliver mini-lectures. If three students ask the same question, pause and address it briefly to the whole room.

### Closing [5 min]

Two reminders:

1. *"Tomorrow — Day 13 — your Doug Letter is due at the start of class. After you hand it in, you take the unit test. Tonight: re-read your reading packet's Publisher Vocabulary section. Quiz yourself on every term. If you can define each in your own words AND match it to the publisher's exact phrase, you're ready."*
2. *"You will refer back to this brief many times. Don't lose it. Keep it in your packet."*

### What to collect

The brief — graded as described above. Return graded briefs by Day 14 (the first day of Unit 2). Students keep their briefs in their notebooks.

### Trouble spots

- **"I don't know how to start."** Coach: *"Start with what you remember best. Pick any discipline. Write one sentence. Then write another."*
- **"I don't think I believe all of this."** *"Your job today is to articulate the Christian position accurately, not to defend it personally. You can write the Christian column without being a Christian — and that distinction is, in fact, what this whole class is teaching you to do."*
- **"Can I just copy the learning outline?"** *"No. You may use it as a reference. The brief must be in your voice. The reason: the brief is a thinking exercise. Copying is not thinking."*

### Sub-friendliness notes

This day is the easiest day to sub. The assignment is printed. Students work independently. The sub's job is to enforce silence and answer logistical questions only.

## Day 13 — Doug Letter Due + Unit Assessment [50 min]

**Goal:** Collect the Doug Letter (started by students last week, finalized over Days 11–12). Administer the Day-13 blended chapter test ([test-student.md](../assessments/unit-01-christianity-test-student.md)). Preview Unit 2 in the last 5 minutes.

### Opening: Doug Letter collection [5 min]

"Please hand in your Doug Letter at the front of the room as I call your name." (Call alphabetically. Mark off who handed in and who didn't on a clipboard.)

If a student hands in a partial letter, accept it; mark it for late credit. Don't make a scene.

### Test administration [40 min]

Distribute the printed [test-student.md](../assessments/unit-01-christianity-test-student.md).

"This is a single-class-period test. You have 40 minutes. Answer every question. The True/False section requires you to alter the underlined word if the statement is false — don't just write 'F.' The essay at the end is worth 4 points; don't skip it.

You may not use your reading packet, your outline, or your notes. You may not talk to a neighbor. Place your phone face-down on the corner of your desk.

When you finish, turn your test over and sit quietly. If you have time at the end, re-check your answers. Begin."

**Walk the room.** Do not answer content questions. If a student asks "what does this word mean," redirect: *"You'll need to use your best judgment."* If a student is genuinely stuck on a phrasing issue, you may clarify the *phrasing* (not the content).

### Bridge to Unit 2 [5 min after test collection]

After collecting tests:

"Two weeks ago we started with the question *'what is the Christian answer to each of the 10 disciplines?'* You can now answer that question. From here on, every unit asks the comparison version of the same question: *'how does this worldview answer differently, and why?'*

Tomorrow we open Unit 2 — Judaism. Not the Judaism of the Old Testament — *modern* Judaism. The tradition that, after the destruction of the Temple in AD 70, rebuilt itself around prayer, study, and acts of lovingkindness instead of sacrifice. The question we'll ask is: *what does Judaism do with the cessation of the sacrificial system?* That question is going to set up the Christian apologetic conversation that arrives on Day 8 of Unit 2.

Read the first half of the Unit 2 reading packet for tomorrow. It's posted on the class portal. See you then."

### What to collect

- Doug Letters (graded against the publisher's rubric — 30 points)
- Tests (graded against [test-teacher.md](../assessments/unit-01-christianity-test-teacher.md) — 83 points)

### Trouble spots

- **A student didn't finish the Doug Letter.** Take what they have, give partial credit, talk to them after class. Don't shame them in front of the room.
- **A student didn't finish the test.** Standard test policy. Score what's there.
- **A student tries to cheat.** Quietly remove the test, mark a zero, talk to them after class. Document.

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub absolutely can administer this day. The Doug Letter rubric is the publisher's; the test answer key is in [test-teacher.md](../assessments/unit-01-christianity-test-teacher.md). The sub's only judgment call is the "what does this word mean" question — coach the sub to redirect rather than answer.
