# Worldview — Custom Syllabus

**Base Textbook**: Understanding the Times: A Survey of Competing Worldviews by Jeff Myers  
**Series**: Understanding the Times Series (Summit)  
**Supplemental Content**: Buddhism, Hinduism, Mormonism (LDS)  
**Created**: 2026-04-15

---

## Course Overview

This custom syllabus extends the official Understanding the Times curriculum to include additional worldviews critical for understanding the modern religious landscape. Students will analyze 8 major worldviews across multiple academic disciplines.

### Worldviews Covered

| # | Worldview | Global Population | Notes |
|---|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| 1 | **Christianity** (Biblical Theism) | ~31% (~2.4B) | Baseline worldview; includes Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox |
| 2 | **Islam** | ~24% (~1.9B) | Second-largest religion; fastest growing |
| 3 | **Secularism** (Naturalism) | ~16% (~1.2B) | Includes atheists, agnostics, secular humanists; dominant in academia/media |
| 4 | **Hinduism** | ~15% (~1.2B) | Primarily India; oldest major religion *(supplemental)* |
| 5 | **Buddhism** | ~7% (~520M) | East/Southeast Asia; overlaps with other religions *(supplemental)* |
| 6 | **Postmodernism** | ~5-10% (cultural influence) | Not a formal religion; pervasive in Western culture/education |
| 7 | **Marxism** | ~3-5% (ideology) | Declining as formal system; still influential in politics/economics |
| 8 | **Mormonism** (LDS) | ~0.2% (~17M) | Concentrated in U.S.; significant cultural/political influence *(supplemental)* |

**Note**: Population percentages are approximate (2024 estimates) and represent global adherents. Many people hold hybrid worldviews or cultural religion without deep conviction. In the U.S., Christianity (~65%), Secularism (~26%), and Mormonism (~2%) have outsized influence relative to global numbers.

### Academic Disciplines Analyzed

Each worldview is examined through:
- Theology (view of God/ultimate reality)
- Philosophy (epistemology, metaphysics)
- Ethics (moral framework)
- Biology (origins, nature of life)
- Psychology (nature of humanity)
- Sociology (community and family)
- Law (source of authority)
- Politics (governance)
- Economics (stewardship and resources)
- History (purpose and direction)

## Structure

### Interleaved Structure (Core + Supplemental)
**Weeks 1-36**

Supplemental worldviews are integrated immediately after related core content for better compare-and-contrast learning.

| Unit | Weeks | Worldview | Type | Focus |
|------|-------|-----------|------|-------|
| 1 | 1-3 | **Christianity** | Core | Baseline worldview — theology, philosophy, ethics across all disciplines |
| 2 | 4-5 | **Mormonism (LDS)** | **Supplemental** | Claims to be Christian — immediate contrast with orthodox theology |
| 3 | 6-8 | **Islam** | Core | Major Abrahamic monotheism — theology, law, politics |
| 4 | 9-11 | **Buddhism & Hinduism** | **Supplemental** | Eastern religions — karma, reincarnation, enlightenment vs. monotheism |
| 5 | 12-16 | **Secularism** | Core | Western naturalism — philosophy, ethics, biology, psychology |
| 6 | 17-20 | **Marxism** | Core | Atheistic materialism — economics, politics, history |
| 7 | 21-24 | **Postmodernism** | Core | Relativism and deconstruction — philosophy, ethics, truth claims |
| 8 | 25-28 | **Cross-Worldview Synthesis** | Application | Law, politics, economics, history across all 8 worldviews |
| 9 | 29-32 | **Contemporary Issues** | Application | Bioethics, justice, technology through multiple worldview lenses |
| 10 | 33-36 | **Capstones & Final Exam** | Assessment | Student presentations and cumulative exam |

**Key Pedagogical Strategy**: Supplemental content is interleaved for immediate comparison:
- **Mormonism** follows **Christianity** (claimed Christian identity)
- **Buddhism/Hinduism** follows **Islam** (major non-Christian world religions)
- **Western philosophies** grouped together (Secularism → Marxism → Postmodernism)

## Detailed Supplemental Unit Plans

### Unit 2: Mormonism / LDS (Weeks 4-5) — SUPPLEMENTAL

**Placement Rationale**: Immediately after Christianity deep dive (Weeks 1-3) to create sharp contrast between orthodox Christianity and a movement claiming Christian identity.

#### Week 4: Origins and Claims

**Placement Rationale**: After studying Abrahamic monotheism (Islam, Weeks 6-8), students examine Eastern pantheism and non-theism for maximum contrast.

**Overview**: Eastern religions offer a radically different framework from Western worldviews. Key concepts include karma, reincarnation, enlightenment, and the nature of reality (maya).

#### Week 9: Introduction to Eastern Thought
**Topics**:
- Historical origins (India, ~1500 BC for Hinduism, ~500 BC for Buddhism)
- Core concepts: karma, samsara (cycle of rebirth), moksha/nirvana (liberation)
- Contrast with Western linear history and monotheism

**Resources**:
- *The Universe Next Door* by James Sire, Ch 5 (Eastern Pantheistic Monism)
- Articles or videos on Hinduism and Buddhism basics
- Guest speaker (if available): Buddhist or Hindu practitioner, or missionary to Asia

**Key Questions**:
- What is ultimate reality in Eastern thought?
- How does karma differ from Christian concepts of justice and grace?
- What does "enlightenment" mean, and how is it achieved?

#### Week 10: Buddhism — The Four Noble Truths
**Topics**:
- Life of Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha)
- Four Noble Truths: suffering, cause, cessation, path
- Eightfold Path to enlightenment
- Key branches: Theravada, Mahayana, Zen

**Resources**:
- Primary text: excerpts from the Dhammapada
- Comparison: Buddhist view of suffering vs. Christian view of suffering
- Case study: How does a Buddhist approach death and suffering?

**Assignments**:
- Discussion Brief: "How does Buddhism's diagnosis of the human condition compare to Christianity's?"
- Pair & Defend: "Suffering is central to life (Buddhism) vs. Suffering is an intrusion into God's good creation (Christianity)"

#### Week 11: Hinduism — Many Gods, One Ultimate Reality
**Topics**:
- Sacred texts: Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita
- Pantheon of gods (Brahma, Vishnu, Shiva) and avatars
- Caste system and its theological justification
- Paths to liberation: knowledge (jnana), devotion (bhakti), action (karma)

**Resources**:
- Primary text: excerpts from the Bhagavad Gita
- Comparison: Hindu view of God (Brahman) vs. Christian Trinitarian God
- Case study: How does Hinduism shape Indian culture and society?

**Assignments**:
- Discussion Brief: "What are the ethical implications of karma and reincarnation?"
- Case Study: Analyze caste system through a Christian worldview lens

**Assessment**: Quiz on Buddhism and Hinduism (Week 11, Day 5)

### Unit 2 Details: Mormonism / LDS (Weeks 4-5)

**Overview**: The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints (LDS) is a significant American religious movement that claims to be Christian but departs from historic orthodox Christianity in key areas. Understanding Mormonism is essential for evangelism and cultural engagement in the U.S.

#### Week 4: Origins and Claims
**Topics**:
- Life of Joseph Smith and the founding of the LDS church (1830)
- Key claims: additional scripture (Book of Mormon, D&C, Pearl of Great Price), modern prophets, restoration of true Christianity
- Sacred history: ancient Israelites in America, gold plates, translation process
- Growth and cultural influence (Utah, missionary program, political impact)

**Resources**:
- *Kingdom of the Cults* by Walter Martin, chapter on Mormonism
- Documentary or video overview of LDS history
- Primary texts: excerpts from the Book of Mormon, Articles of Faith
- Guest speaker (if available): former Mormon or missionary to Mormons

**Key Questions**:
- What are the LDS church's core claims about authority and revelation?
- How does the Book of Mormon's account of ancient America hold up historically?
- What is the LDS view of salvation and the afterlife?

**Assignments**:
- Discussion Brief: "How would you respond to a Mormon missionary who says, 'We're Christians too'?"

#### Week 5: Theology and Scriptures
**Topics**:
- View of God: "God was once a man," exaltation to godhood
- Christology: Jesus as spirit-brother of Lucifer, different from historic Christian doctrine
- Additional scriptures: Book of Mormon, Doctrine & Covenants, Pearl of Great Price
- Temple practices and sacred ordinances (baptism for the dead, eternal marriage)

**Resources**:
- Comparison chart: LDS theology vs. historic Christian doctrine
- Analysis of key Book of Mormon passages
- Archaeological and historical challenges to the Book of Mormon

**Key Questions**:
- How does LDS theology of God differ from biblical monotheism?
- What is the role of grace vs. works in LDS soteriology?
- How do Mormons use the Bible, and what hermeneutical challenges arise?

**Assignments**:
- Pair & Defend: "The LDS church is a Christian denomination vs. The LDS church is a distinct religion"
- Research project: Compare a key LDS doctrine (choose from: God, Jesus, salvation, scripture) with biblical Christianity

#### (Integrated into Week 5): Evangelism and Cultural Engagement
**Topics**:
- How to engage Mormons with the gospel (relational evangelism, doctrinal conversations)
- Common apologetic approaches: archaeology, prophecy, grace vs. works
- Cultural insights: why Mormonism appeals, family values, community
- Case studies: conversations with Mormon friends, family, or missionaries

**Resources**:
- *Reasoning from the Scriptures with the Mormons* by Ron Rhodes
- Testimony videos from former Mormons who came to Christ
- Role-play scenarios: responding to LDS missionaries

**Assignments**:
- Case Study: Small groups practice responding to common LDS truth claims
- Discussion Brief: "What is the most compelling way to share the gospel with a Mormon?"

**Assessment**: Test on Mormonism (Week 5, Day 5)

## Comparative Analysis and Application (Weeks 25-32)

Now integrated into Units 8-9. See "Week-by-Week Outline" above for details.

### Week 25-28: Cross-Worldview Synthesis
**Topics**:
- Create comparison charts across all 8 worldviews
- Identify common ground and irreconcilable differences
- Analyze cultural impact: which worldviews are growing, declining, and why?
- Discuss: "Why does worldview matter?"

**Activities**:
- Debate tournament: students defend assigned worldviews on key issues (abortion, justice, meaning of life, etc.)
- Worldview grid: students fill in answers to 10 life questions across all 8 worldviews

### Week 29-32: Application to Contemporary Issues
**Topics**:
- Choose 3-5 contemporary issues and analyze through multiple worldview lenses:
  - Bioethics (abortion, euthanasia, genetic engineering)
  - Social justice (poverty, racism, immigration)
  - Technology (AI, transhumanism, social media)
  - Religious freedom and pluralism

**Activities**:
- Panel discussions: students represent different worldviews in mock town halls
- Essay: "How does a biblical worldview inform my response to [chosen issue]?"

## Final Projects and Assessment (Weeks 33-36)

### Week 33-35: Capstone Projects
Students choose one of the following:

1. **Worldview Presentation**: Deep dive on one worldview (7-10 min presentation + Q&A)
2. **Comparative Essay**: Analyze a contemporary issue through 3 worldviews (5-7 pages)
3. **Evangelism Case Study**: Create a gospel presentation tailored to a specific worldview (Buddhist, Hindu, or Mormon)
4. **Documentary Project**: Interview practitioners of different worldviews and analyze (video or podcast)

### Week 36: Final Exam and Reflection
- Cumulative final exam covering all 8 worldviews
- Reflection essay: "How has this course shaped your worldview?"

## Notes

- This custom syllabus maintains the rigor of the Understanding the Times framework while addressing significant gaps in coverage.
- Buddhism, Hinduism, and Mormonism are essential for cultural engagement in a pluralistic society.
- Teachers can adjust pacing based on class needs — some may spend more time on Eastern religions, others on Mormonism.
- The condensed core content (Weeks 1-24) prioritizes depth on key disciplines rather than exhaustive coverage of all 10 for each worldview.
