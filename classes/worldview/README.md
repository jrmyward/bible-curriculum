# Understanding the Times — A Survey of Competing Worldviews

**Grade Level:** 11–12
**School:** Watersprings School
**School Year:** 2026–2027
**Source Text:** *Understanding the Times* by Jeff Myers & David Noebel
**Instructor:** Jeremy Ward

---

## Course Description

A year-long comparative worldview course for high school juniors and seniors, offered with an optional dual-credit track. The course uses Myers & Noebel's 10-discipline analytical framework + four-test framework as its spine but restructures delivery from **discipline-first** (the textbook's order) to **worldview-first** — each worldview receives its own one-to-two-week unit examined through all 10 disciplines, pressure-tested against Christianity using the four tests, and culminating in a practiced Gospel conversation specific to that worldview.

Discipline-comparative recall — what the textbook's Ch 8–17 build toward and what the dual-credit final is most likely to test — is preserved as **synthesis review at trimester boundaries**, where the rows of the grid get walked across deliberately, three times, with cumulative breadth.

### Stated Goal

By May, every student should be able to sit across from a thoughtful proponent of any of these 8 worldviews, represent that person's view back to them in a way they would recognize as fair, and present the Gospel in a way that is meaningful and impactful to *that person specifically* — not a generic tract.

---

## Design Philosophy

**Worldview-first delivery, discipline-first synthesis.** Myers & Noebel introduce each worldview in isolation (Part 1) and then compare all worldviews on each discipline (Part 2). High schoolers can build a coherent mental model of one worldview through 10 lenses; making them juggle 8 partial models per lens is harder, not easier. This course delivers worldview-first day to day, then walks the discipline rows at each trimester boundary so the cross-comparative recall the dual-credit final demands gets exercised three times across the year.

**Charitable engagement is the curricular commitment.** A student who cannot represent a worldview fairly cannot engage it faithfully. Every unit includes a "framing" day where the worldview is presented in its best, most charitable form with no strawmen — and the Capstone rubric weights charitable representation as the first scoring category.

**The Gospel is a conversation, not a script.** Students practice 8 different Gospel conversations across the year, one tailored to the language, longings, and objections of each worldview's adherents. The Capstone presentation — which doubles as the dual-credit oral final — includes a live Gospel-conversation roleplay.

**Hybrid pedagogy by intention.** ~40% direct instruction (lecture for cross-chapter synthesis), ~50% cohort (Socratic, Pair & Defend, Case Study, Gospel roleplay), ~10% independent. Per-worldview reading packets replace cross-chapter textbook navigation. Weekly retrieval quizzes keep the grid alive in long-term memory. See [teaching-methodology.md](teaching-methodology.md).

## The 10 Analytical Lenses

Every unit examines the worldview through all 10:
Theology · Philosophy · Ethics · Biology · Psychology · Sociology · Law · Politics · Economics · History

The four tests (reason, outer world, inner world, real-world consequences) are applied against the Christian baseline on Day 7 of every worldview unit.

Mormonism is placed after Islam (Unit 3 → Unit 4) so the angel-prophet-new-scripture revelation pattern taught in Islam can be reused for LDS theology. Hinduism, Buddhism, and New Spirituality are taught as three distinct units rather than collapsed into Myers's "New Spirituality" category.
