# The Worldview Grid

**9 Worldviews × 10 Analytical Disciplines**

This grid is the visual anchor of the course. Students see it on Day 1 of Unit 0, return to it at the end of every worldview unit, and defend it from memory in the Unit 10 capstone. By May, every student should be able to explain every cell in their own words.

The 10 disciplines are drawn from Myers & Noebel's *Understanding the Times*. The 9 worldviews extend that framework to include traditions the textbook doesn't cover on their own terms — Judaism, Mormonism, Hinduism and Buddhism as distinct religions, Expressive Individualism / Moralistic Therapeutic Deism, and the Marxism → Progressivism → Postmodernism lineage as a single genealogical arc.

Cells are single-concept summaries — not the whole picture. Each unit unpacks its column across two weeks of teaching.

---

## How to Read the Grid

- **Row** = one of the 10 disciplines (the analytical lenses)
- **Column** = one of the 9 worldviews (the units of this course)
- **Cell** = the dominant answer that worldview gives to that discipline's core question
- **Christianity column** = the baseline every other worldview is compared against

When a cell looks similar to Christianity's cell (e.g., "Special Creation" appears in Christianity, Judaism, Mormonism, and Islam for Biology), the interesting work is in the *differences underneath the shared term*. The cell is the starting point for the conversation, not the end of it.

## Teaching Notes on the Grid

### On the "Revolutionary Lineage" column

Combining Marxism, American Progressivism, and Postmodernism into a single column is a pedagogical choice, not a claim that they are identical. They share a genealogical arc and a common core (deconstruction of existing structures, materialist or anti-realist metaphysics, liberation through revolutionary change) that is obscured when they are treated as three separate worldviews. The unit traces the evolution: Marx → Wilson/Sanger/Dewey → Frankfurt School → Foucault/Derrida → Critical Theory. Students should be able to identify which movement a given idea comes from, and how the movements depart from each other as well as connect.

### On "New Spirituality" being split into Hinduism and Buddhism

Myers & Noebel combine Hinduism, Buddhism, New Age, and assorted Eastern-influenced Western spirituality under a single "New Spirituality" column. That category flattens 2,500-year-old religious traditions with distinct textual canons, philosophical schools, and internal disagreements into a single cell. This course treats Hinduism and Buddhism as the distinct, intellectually serious religions they are. Where the "New Spirituality" label applies (Chopra, Tolle, Byrne), we'll name it inside those units as a Western appropriation of Eastern categories, not as the same thing as Buddhism or Hinduism proper.

### On "Expressive Individualism / MTD"

This column does not exist in Myers/Noebel. It is arguably the most urgent worldview for student formation because it is the operative belief system of most of their peers — including Christian-schooled peers who have absorbed it without recognizing it. Moralistic Therapeutic Deism (Christian Smith) and Expressive Individualism (Philip Rieff, Carl Trueman) together describe a worldview that uses Christian vocabulary while denying Christian substance: God exists but is distant, morality is being nice, the self is the ultimate authority, authenticity is the highest virtue. Students are far more likely to drift into this worldview than into atheism. Name it clearly so they can recognize it when they meet it — and when they catch themselves in it.

### On "Mormonism" as distinct from Christianity

Mormon vocabulary overlaps heavily with Christian vocabulary — but the referents are categorically different. "God" in LDS theology is an exalted man who once lived a mortal life, not the eternal Trinitarian God. "Jesus" is a created being and spirit-brother of Lucifer, not the eternal Son. "Salvation" is grace + works + ordinances + exaltation toward godhood, not grace alone. This column exists so students can recognize the categorical shift beneath the shared vocabulary. The parallel with Islam's revelation structure (Angel Moroni → Joseph Smith → Book of Mormon // Angel Jibreel → Muhammad → Quran) is taught explicitly so students see the pattern.

### On "Judaism" as a theological arc

Judaism is not a static category. The course traces four stages: Covenantal (Abraham–Moses), Temple/Sacrificial (Levitical atonement mechanism), the rupture of 70 AD (Temple destroyed, sacrificial system ends permanently), Rabbinic reconstruction (prayer, Torah study, acts of lovingkindness as substitutes for sacrifice, citing Hosea 6:6), and modern expressions (Reform, Conservative, Orthodox, Messianic). The column shows modern Rabbinic Judaism's answers. Christianity is presented not as an attack on Judaism but as the fulfillment of its own internal narrative logic — particularly the unanswered question of atonement after the Temple's destruction.

## Using the Grid in Class

**On Day 1 of Unit 0:** Show students the full grid. Don't explain every cell. Let them sit with the scope of what they're going to learn.

**On the first day of every worldview unit:** Circle that column. This is what we're filling in this week.

**On the last day of every worldview unit:** Students should be able to speak to every cell in that column from memory, with at least one sentence of elaboration for each.

**In Unit 10 (Capstone):** Students defend the Christianity column against the worldview they found most challenging. The grid is the scaffolding for their thesis.

**As a takeaway:** Print the grid as a one-page reference. Students should leave the course with this page and the ability to use it in conversations — not as a weapon, but as a map of where the real disagreements live.
