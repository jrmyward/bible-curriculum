# The Worldview Grid

**8 Worldviews × 10 Analytical Disciplines**

This grid is the visual anchor of the course. Students see it on Day 1 of Unit 0, return to it at the end of every worldview unit, and defend it from memory in the Unit 10 Capstone. By May, every student should be able to explain every cell in their own words.

The 10 disciplines are drawn from Myers & Noebel's *Understanding the Times*. The 8 worldviews extend that framework to include traditions the textbook doesn't cover on their own terms — Judaism, Mormonism, Hinduism and Buddhism as distinct religions, and the Marxism → Progressivism → Postmodernism lineage as a single genealogical arc. New Spirituality is preserved with its proper textbook scope: Western syncretism (Tolle, Chopra, Byrne), not as a stand-in for Hinduism or Buddhism.

Cells are single-concept summaries — not the whole picture. Each unit unpacks its column across one to two weeks of teaching.

---

## How to Read the Grid

- **Row** = one of the 10 disciplines (the analytical lenses)
- **Column** = one of the 8 worldviews (the units of this course)
- **Cell** = the dominant answer that worldview gives to that discipline's core question
- **Christianity column** = the baseline every other worldview is compared against

When a cell looks similar to Christianity's cell (e.g., "Special Creation" appears in Christianity, Judaism, Mormonism, and Islam for Biology), the interesting work is in the *differences underneath the shared term*. The cell is the starting point for the conversation, not the end of it.

## Teaching Notes on the Grid

### On the "Revolutionary Lineage" column

Combining Marxism, American Progressivism, and Postmodernism into a single column is a pedagogical choice, not a claim that they are identical. They share a genealogical arc and a common core (deconstruction of existing structures, materialist or anti-realist metaphysics, liberation through revolutionary change) that is obscured when they are treated as three separate worldviews. The unit traces the evolution: Marx → Wilson/Sanger/Dewey → Frankfurt School → Foucault/Derrida → Critical Theory. Students should be able to identify which movement a given idea comes from, and how the movements depart from each other as well as connect.

### On Hinduism, Buddhism, and New Spirituality as three distinct columns

Myers & Noebel collapse Hinduism, Buddhism, New Age, and assorted Eastern-influenced Western spirituality under a single "New Spirituality" column. That category flattens 2,500-year-old religious traditions with distinct textual canons, philosophical schools, and internal disagreements into a single cell.

This course separates them. Hinduism (Ch 6 partial) and Buddhism (Ch 6 partial) get their own units as the distinct, intellectually serious religions they are. New Spirituality (Ch 6 proper scope) gets its own shorter unit as the Western syncretism it actually is — Tolle, Chopra, Byrne, the wellness/manifestation industry. Treating them as three separate columns honors what each actually is and lets the comparison work go cleanly.

### On "Mormonism" as distinct from Christianity

Mormon vocabulary overlaps heavily with Christian vocabulary — but the referents are categorically different. "God" in LDS theology is an exalted man who once lived a mortal life, not the eternal Trinitarian God. "Jesus" is a created being and spirit-brother of Lucifer, not the eternal Son. "Salvation" is grace + works + ordinances + exaltation toward godhood, not grace alone. This column exists so students can recognize the categorical shift beneath the shared vocabulary. The parallel with Islam's revelation structure (Angel Jibreel → Muhammad → Quran // Angel Moroni → Joseph Smith → Book of Mormon) is taught explicitly — Islam is studied first (Unit 3) so the pattern is established before Mormonism (Unit 4) re-applies it.

### On "Judaism" as a theological arc

Judaism is not a static category. The course traces four stages: Covenantal (Abraham–Moses), Temple/Sacrificial (Levitical atonement mechanism), the rupture of 70 AD (Temple destroyed, sacrificial system ends permanently), Rabbinic reconstruction (prayer, Torah study, acts of lovingkindness as substitutes for sacrifice, citing Hosea 6:6), and modern expressions (Reform, Conservative, Orthodox, Messianic). The column shows modern Rabbinic Judaism's answers. Christianity is presented not as an attack on Judaism but as the fulfillment of its own internal narrative logic — particularly the unanswered question of atonement after the Temple's destruction.

### On the absence of an "Expressive Individualism / MTD" column

A previous version of this course included Expressive Individualism / Moralistic Therapeutic Deism as a ninth column, drawing from Christian Smith, Philip Rieff, and Carl Trueman. It was removed in the 2026-05-08 revision when the course committed to dual-credit alignment with the Myers/Noebel framework. MTD is not absent from teaching — it surfaces as an aspect of New Spirituality (the therapeutic-self piece) and Secularism (the moralistic-deism piece) — but it does not get its own unit or column.

## Using the Grid in Class

**On Day 1 of Unit 0:** Show students the full grid. Don't explain every cell. Let them sit with the scope of what they're going to learn.

**On the first day of every worldview unit:** Circle that column. This is what we're filling in this week.

**On the last day of every worldview unit:** Students should be able to speak to every cell in that column from memory, with at least one sentence of elaboration for each.

**During T1, T2, and T3 synthesis review:** Students walk *across* the rows of the grid, comparing how all studied worldviews answer a single discipline. This is the discipline-comparative recall the dual-credit final will test.

**In Unit 10 (Capstone):** Students defend the Christianity column against the worldview they found most challenging. The grid is the scaffolding for their thesis and the structure of their oral final.

**As a takeaway:** Print the grid as a one-page reference. Students should leave the course with this page and the ability to use it in conversations — not as a weapon, but as a map of where the real disagreements live.
