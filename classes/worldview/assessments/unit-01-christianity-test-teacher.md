## How This Test Is Structured

This is a *blended* test in two halves:

- **Worldview-comprehensive items** — drawn verbatim from the publisher's UTT Chapter 02 Test (the dual-credit alignment standard for Christianity). These test holistic mastery of the Christian worldview as a whole. Reference codes like `[2.5]` point to the publisher's Ch 02 chapter test, Section 5.
- **Discipline-comparative items** — drawn from the publisher's Ch 8–17 chapter tests, filtered for items that specifically test Christianity's view of theology, philosophy, ethics, biology, psychology, sociology, law, politics, economics, and history. Reference codes like `[8.6]` point to Ch 8 Theology, Section 6. Items originally cross-comparative ("Which worldviews deny X?") have been adapted to per-worldview phrasing where needed.
- **One locally-built essay item** at the end. Reference code: `[U1.Day8]` — built from the Day 8 Four-Test exercise.

Question types follow the publisher's exact format: MC (1pt) → T/F with correction (1pt) → Fill-in-blank (2pt) → Short answer (3pt) → Essay (4pt). No matching section, since the publisher's Ch 02 test does not include one.

## Short Answer [3 points each — 8 questions]

*Answer each question with at least one complete sentence.*

**43.** Summarize the Christian worldview.
- *Christianity is a theistic worldview centered on the person of Jesus Christ that derives its understanding of the world through the teachings of the Holy Bible.* [2.2]

**44.** What does it mean to say that God is personal?
- *To say that God is personal is to say that God is a person — that is, he possesses the characteristics of a person. Unlike the god-force of New Spirituality, the Christian God is self-aware, creative, volitional, rational, relational, and communicative.* [2.3a]

**45.** Can general revelation lead people to God?
- *General revelation can "lead people to God" by revealing something about his nature, but there is only so much we can infer about God from observing his creation. Basic knowledge comes through general revelation, but a personal and saving knowledge only comes through his special revelation.* [2.6]

**46.** Define Trinitarian monotheism.
- *Trinitarian monotheism is the belief in one God recognized as three separate and distinct persons: Father, Son, and Spirit.* [8.6]

**47.** Define agape.
- *Agape is the belief that morality is built upon two foundational moral absolutes: love of God and love of neighbor.* [10.8]

**48.** Define special creation.
- *Special creation is the belief that the cosmos and all life were brought into existence by the creative act of God.* [11.8]

**49.** Define biblical stewardship.
- *Biblical stewardship is the act of managing and caring for resources that ultimately belong to God.* [16.10]

**50.** Define redemptive narrative.
- *Redemptive narrative is the Christian metanarrative that describes the course of history unfolding according to the biblical chronicle of God's creative act, humanity's rebellion, Christ's redemptive sacrifice, and God's eventual restoration of his creation.* [17.9]

## Essay [4 points — 1 question]

*Answer in at least one well-developed paragraph.*

**51.** Christianity claims to be a true worldview that holds up under intellectual scrutiny. Apply Myers's four tests — Reason, Outer World, Inner World, and Real-World Consequences — to Christianity. Choose **two** of the four tests. For each, identify (a) where Christianity passes the test cleanly, and (b) where it raises a hard question a thoughtful Christian must be ready to answer. [U1.Day8]

**Sample answer (4 pts):** *Students should select two of the four tests and address each with both its strengths and its tensions. Strong responses might note: on REASON, Christianity offers a coherent four-act story (creation, fall, redemption, restoration) and grounds objective morality in God's character — yet must be prepared to explain how the Trinity and Incarnation can be true mysteries without being contradictions. On OUTER WORLD, Christianity claims a bodily resurrection rooted in real history (1 Cor. 15:14) and predicted human-image-of-God dignity, fine-tuning, and moral universals — yet stakes the entire faith on the historicity of the resurrection. On INNER WORLD, Christianity gives a robust account of guilt, longing, beauty, and our universal sense that the world is "not as it should be" — yet must address the problem of evil. On REAL-WORLD CONSEQUENCES, Christianity has produced hospitals, universities, abolition, and human-rights frameworks — yet must account for crusades, inquisitions, and church scandals. Full credit requires honest engagement with both halves: where Christianity passes, AND where the pressure point is real.*

**Rubric:**
- 4 pts: Two tests addressed; both strengths and tensions named for each; specific publisher vocabulary used (e.g., redemptive narrative, image of God, special revelation); a sentence on how a thoughtful Christian responds to the tension.
- 3 pts: Two tests addressed but only one half (strengths or tensions) developed.
- 2 pts: Only one test addressed, or both addressed superficially.
- 1 pt: Names the four tests but doesn't apply them to Christianity.
- 0 pts: Off-topic or blank.
