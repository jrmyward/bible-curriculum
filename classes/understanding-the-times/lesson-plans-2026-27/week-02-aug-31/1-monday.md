# Day 6 — Mon Aug 31, 2026

**Unit:** Unit 0 — Foundations (Day 6 of 10)
**Topic:** Steel-manning — the first curricular commitment of the course

---

## Learning Objectives

By end of class, students should be able to:

1. Define steel-manning and distinguish it from straw-manning
2. Articulate a position they disagree with in a way a proponent would recognize as fair
3. Recognize why charitable engagement is the first ethical move, not a concession

## Class Schedule (45 min)

### 0:00–0:10 · Return Journals + Read Aloud

- Hand back Friday's reflection journals with brief comments.
- Read 2 strong submissions aloud (with students' permission — ask beforehand if possible):
  - One that was honest about an unexamined inherited belief
  - One that named a worldview the student grew up in and what it assumed
- **Teacher move:** highlight what made these strong. *"Honest. Specific. Didn't try to sound smart. That's what I'm looking for all year."*

### 0:10–0:20 · Steel-Manning — Lecture (10 min)

Cover two moves:

1. **Straw-manning** — misrepresenting a position to make it easier to knock down. *"Nobody actually believes X,"* *"People who believe Y are just doing it for Z reason."* Straw-manning wins arguments cheaply. It also ensures you never actually understand your opponent, so you can't meaningfully disagree with them.

2. **Steel-manning** — representing a position in its strongest, most charitable form — the version the person who holds it would nod at and say *"yes, that's what I mean."* Steel-manning is harder. It requires you to actually understand the opposing view well enough to articulate it faithfully.

**Why steel-manning is the rule of this classroom:**
- It's the first ethical move: treat people, including people you disagree with, as thoughtful agents who have reasons for what they believe.
- It makes real disagreement possible. If you only know the straw version, you're arguing with your own imagination.
- It builds the skill you'll need in May when you have a Gospel conversation with someone who doesn't share your worldview.

### 0:20–0:35 · Practice (15 min)

- Pair students up (random — use name cards, playing cards, or whatever mechanism).
- Each pair draws a topic from a stack. Topics are light — non-worldview. Examples:
  - *"Pineapple belongs on pizza"* vs. *"Pineapple does not belong on pizza"*
  - *"Cats are better pets than dogs"* vs. *"Dogs are better pets than cats"*
  - *"Summer is the best season"* vs. *"Fall is the best season"*
  - *"Video games are a good use of teenage time"* vs. *"Video games are a bad use of teenage time"*
- Students are ASSIGNED a side — they don't pick. If the assigned side is the one they agree with, they get a different card.
- **Task:** argue your assigned side for 2 minutes. The other partner listens and, at the end, tries to steel-man what they just heard (not what they believe — what their partner just argued).
- Swap sides. Repeat.

### 0:35–0:45 · Debrief (10 min)

- Whole class. Questions:
  1. *"What was hard about arguing the side you disagreed with?"*
  2. *"How did it feel when your partner steel-manned your position — even though you disagreed with them?"*
  3. *"What's the difference between steel-manning and agreeing?"*
- Close with: *"This is the rule for the year. In every unit, we will argue for worldviews we don't hold. Not because we believe them — but because if we can't represent them, we can't meaningfully disagree with them. Charitable engagement is the first move, not the last."*

## Teacher Notes

- Steel-manning is the single most important habit of the course. Teach it like a skill (not a suggestion).
- Students will initially find it easier to sarcastically argue a position than to genuinely steel-man it. Correct that in real time: *"That's the straw version. Give me the strong one."*
- **Watch for:** students who refuse to argue a side they disagree with. This will happen. Private conversation: the exercise is about learning to understand, not to endorse. Arguing a position faithfully for 2 minutes does not commit you to it.
- The light-topics choice is intentional. Starting with "should God exist" or "is abortion wrong" sets students up to fail this first attempt. The goal is the SKILL of steel-manning, independent of how much the topic matters to them.
- Keep a stack of ~15 topic cards ready; you'll reuse them later in the year.
