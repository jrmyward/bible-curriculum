# Day 2 — Tue Aug 25, 2026

**Unit:** Unit 0 — Foundations (Day 2 of 10)
**Topic:** The 10 worldview questions — students draft their own answers

---

## Learning Objectives

By end of class, students should be able to:

1. Name the 10 analytical disciplines the course will use to examine every worldview
2. Articulate their own current answers to at least 5 of the 10 questions (not polished — honest)
3. Recognize that their own answers come from *somewhere* (family, culture, experience) and that the point of the course is to examine where

---

## Materials

- "10 Worldview Questions" handout — one per student (each question phrased plainly, with 1–2 sentences of context). See [handouts/](handouts/).
- Blank worldview-grid template (the Christianity column filled in as an example of what a column looks like — but don't draw attention to it yet)
- Large whiteboard / slides with the 10 disciplines displayed

---

## Class Schedule (45 min)

### 0:00–0:05 · Warm-Up

- Invite 2–3 students to share a single sentence from yesterday's discussion: "What shapes how you see the world?"
- Bridge: *"Today we're going to get more specific. What exactly is a worldview answering?"*

### 0:05–0:20 · The 10 Worldview Questions (lecture, 15 min)

Present each discipline as a question. Give 60–90 seconds each. Use slides if available.

1. **Theology** — Is there a God? What is God like?
2. **Philosophy** — What is real? How do we know what we know?
3. **Ethics** — What is good? On what basis?
4. **Biology** — What is life, and where did it come from?
5. **Psychology** — What is a person? What makes someone whole or broken?
6. **Sociology** — How should people live together?
7. **Law** — What is justice? Where does authority come from?
8. **Politics** — What is the role of government?
9. **Economics** — What is wealth? How should it be used?
10. **History** — Where is history going? What is its meaning?

After presenting all 10, clarify: *"These are the 10 lenses we'll use on every worldview. Every column in the grid I showed you yesterday is a set of answers to these 10 questions. By May, you'll know every column."*

### 0:20–0:35 · Independent Work — *Your* Answers (15 min)

- Hand out the "10 Worldview Questions" sheet.
- **Directions:** *"For each of the 10 questions, write your own current answer in 1–2 sentences. Do not look anything up. Do not try to give a 'right' answer. What do YOU actually think, right now? If you're not sure, write 'I don't know' — that's a legitimate answer. Be honest."*
- Students work silently for 15 minutes.
- **Teacher move:** circulate, but do not coach. This is a baseline. If a student writes "I don't know" for all 10, that's an honest starting point and we'll work with it.

### 0:35–0:45 · Quick Share-Out (10 min)

- Ask 3–4 volunteers to share one answer each from their sheet — NOT "here's what I wrote for Theology" but "here's one question I had a confident answer to, and here's one where I had to write 'I don't know.'"
- **Teacher move:** resist the urge to grade or correct any answer. The goal is to normalize uncertainty and invite curiosity. There is always a student who thinks they have it all figured out and a student who feels they have nothing — both need to hear that both responses are okay at Day 2.
- Close with: *"Hold onto this sheet. In May, at the end of the course, you'll rewrite it. The point isn't that your answers change — some will, some won't. The point is that you'll be able to say why."*

---

## Teacher Notes

- The 10 disciplines will feel abstract today. That's fine — they get concrete when applied to an actual worldview next week.
- Some students will have sophisticated answers to all 10. Some will have none. Both are starting points. Don't over-reward the confident ones.
- If a student writes something you disagree with (e.g., a Theology answer that denies God), note it but do not correct it. Today is a baseline, not a correction session.
- **Pacing warning:** the temptation is to spend 3–4 minutes per discipline. Don't — you need the 15 minutes of independent work. Keep the lecture section tight.
