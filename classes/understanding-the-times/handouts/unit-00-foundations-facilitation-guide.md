## Day 1 — The Opening Question (within Day 1's 45-min flow)

This is the **15-minute facilitation segment** that runs from 0:15–0:30 of Day 1. It's the structural opener of the entire course; the rest of the period is teacher-led (see [lecture-companion.md §Day 1](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md)).

**Goal:** Every student in the room speaks at least once. Every student's answer is captured on the board. Every student feels heard. By the end, the word *worldview* has been earned, not announced.

**Materials needed:**
- Board open across the whole front of the room (you'll need it for ~20 student answers)
- One marker, two colors helpful (one for student answers, one for follow-ups)
- A clipboard or attendance sheet for tracking who has spoken

### Setup [before class starts]

Write on the board, large: ***"What shapes how you see the world?"***

Don't add anything else. Just the question. Leave the rest of the board blank.

### Step 1 — Silent thinking [1 min]

*"Look at the question on the board. I want you to think — silently — for one full minute. Don't write yet. Just think. What are three things that have shaped how you see the world?"*

Set a timer. Watch the clock. **Do not break the silence.** Adolescents are not used to one minute of silence; some will fidget. Let them. The point is to slow them down before they speak.

### Step 2 — Pair share [2 min]

*"Now turn to the person next to you. Each of you takes 1 minute. Share one of the three things you thought of. Listener — don't fix, don't comment, just listen."*

Set a timer for 2 min. Walk the room. Don't intervene unless a pair is completely silent.

### Step 3 — Whole-class capture [12 min]

*"Now we share with the room. I'm going to go around — every single one of you will share at least one of your answers. Just one or two words is fine. I'll write each one on the board."*

Go in seating order. When a student speaks, write their answer on the board in one or two words: *family · pastor · my mom · books · TikTok · pain · church · video games · 9/11 · COVID · sports · my dad · social media · friends · the internet · my grandparents.*

**Teacher rules during capture:**

- **Don't evaluate.** No "great answer." No "really?" No raised eyebrows. Just write.
- **Don't ask follow-up questions** unless an answer is genuinely unclear and you need a single word to write. *(Even then — make the student pick the word, not you.)*
- **If a student passes:** *"I'll come back to you."* Then come back at the end. Don't let any student opt out of speaking entirely.
- **If an answer is repeated:** *"Already on the board — give me a different one."*
- **If a student gives a joke answer:** Accept it and write it down. Adolescents test boundaries on day 1. Don't take the bait. Move on. They'll calibrate by Day 2.

By the end you should have 15–25 things on the board.

### Step 4 — The teaching moment [land it cleanly]

Stand back. Look at the board with the class. Then deliver the line (verbatim if you want it to land):

> *"Look at this list. Every single thing on this board is something that's been shaping how you see the world — most of it without your permission, most of it without your awareness. That's what we mean by a* **worldview** *— the lens you've been looking through. Some of you knew you were looking through a lens. Most of you didn't. Both groups are about to find out a lot more about what's been on the lens."*

Don't follow up immediately. Let the line sit. Then move into the next segment (*What a worldview is — preview*, in [lecture-companion.md §Day 1](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md)).

### What to collect

Nothing. Take a photo of the board after class if you want a record — it's useful evidence for parent conferences if anyone asks what students did the first day.

### Trouble spots

- **"What if students give weird answers (drugs, sex, etc.)?"** Accept it without flinching. Write it down. Don't moralize. *"On the board."* You'll handle the larger questions over the year; the first day is not the day to enforce content boundaries on what they say has shaped them.
- **"What if one student dominates?"** Use the seating-order rule strictly. *"One answer each. We're going around the room."*
- **"What if no one speaks?"** Call on a student you've already taught (if any) or the most confident-looking face. Once one person speaks, the rest follow. Wait through the awkward silence — don't fill it.

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub could run Day 1 — but it would be a poor first impression. If at all possible, the teacher of record should run Day 1. If a sub must, the structure above is enough; the sub's main risk is filling the silence (don't) and evaluating answers (don't).

## Day 2 — Independent Writing on the 10 Questions

This is the **20-minute writing block** of Day 2 (0:10–0:30). The lecture-companion covers the framing (0:00–0:10) and the closing reflection (0:30–0:45). This file covers the writing block itself.

**Goal:** Every student produces a first-draft, honest, in-their-own-words answer to each of the 10 worldview questions, with no outside input. This becomes their baseline Worldview Brief; they will rewrite it in Unit 10 (May).

**Materials needed:**
- Printed copies of [10-worldview-questions handout](../lesson-plans-2026-27/week-01-aug-24/handouts/10-worldview-questions.md) (one per student)
- Pencils (some students will need spares)
- A timer at the front of the room

### Set-up

Distribute the handout. Each student also has the [learning-outline-student.md](unit-00-foundations-learning-outline-student.md) blank fill-in from Day 1.

### Rules (read aloud before starting)

> *"The rules are simple. Number one — no looking things up. Phones face-down on your desk. No textbook. No Bible. Number two — don't try to give a 'right' answer. Give your answer. Number three — if your honest answer is 'I don't know,' write 'I don't know.' That's a legitimate answer; it tells us where the work is. Number four — 1 to 2 sentences per question. Don't over-write. You'll have 20 minutes."*

Start the timer. Visible to the room.

### During the writing

Walk the room. Be present. Do not read over shoulders (this triggers performance anxiety). If a student looks paralyzed, lean in and ask one of these:

- *"Pick any question — start there. Doesn't have to be Q1."*
- *"What would you say if you weren't worried about being right?"*
- *"Write 'I don't know' for now. Move to the next."*

If a student finishes early, do not let them pull out a phone. *"Re-read your answers. Underline the one you find most surprising."*

### Closing the writing block

At 20 minutes: *"Pencils down. Don't change anything else."*

Pivot into the pair-share + whole-class reflection segment (see [lecture-companion.md §Day 2](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md), 0:30–0:40).

### What to collect

**The handout does NOT get collected or graded.** Tell students explicitly: *"This is yours. Put it in your folder. You'll rewrite it in May. The point is not what you wrote — the point is that you wrote it before anyone told you what to write."*

### Trouble spots

- **"A student writes only 'I don't know' for every question."** Acceptable today. Talk to them privately after class — is this anxiety, disengagement, or actual honesty? Calibrate your response to which.
- **"A student writes deeply troubling things (despair, threats of harm to self or others)."** Take it seriously. Pull them aside after class. Follow school protocols. The exercise is designed to surface honesty; sometimes honesty surfaces things that need a counselor.
- **"A student is clearly copying their answer from somewhere."** *"This is supposed to be yours. Close your phone. Start over."* No drama. Move on.

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub can run this segment. The structure is fully scripted. The sub's only judgment call is the "I don't know" / troubling answers contingency — coach the sub to take any troubling answer seriously and pass it to you the next morning.

## Day 4 — Four-Tests Small-Group Practice (within Day 4's 45-min flow)

This is the **12-minute small-group practice** that runs from 0:30–0:42 of Day 4 (after the four-tests lecture). The lecture-companion covers the teaching frame; this file covers the activity.

**Goal:** Every group applies at least two of the four tests to a *familiar, non-religious* worldview — building the skill on something low-stakes before the four-tests practice on Christianity in Unit 1.

**Materials needed:**
- Printed worksheet (template below) — one per group
- Timer

### Group formation [1 min]

Groups of 3–4. Self-selected today (low stakes); you'll assign groups starting Unit 1.

### Worksheet template

```text
Group: ___________  Worldview tested: ___________________

We chose this worldview because it's familiar to us. Examples:
  - a movie or sitcom character's life philosophy
  - a song or song lyric we know well
  - a TikTok / Instagram influencer's stated worldview
  - a friend's stated belief
  - a meme

STATE the worldview in 1-2 sentences (in its own terms, not yours):

________________________________________________________________

________________________________________________________________

TEST #1 — Which one? ___________________________________

  Where does this worldview pass?
  _____________________________________________________

  Where does this worldview run into trouble?
  _____________________________________________________

TEST #2 — Which one? ___________________________________

  Where does this worldview pass?
  _____________________________________________________

  Where does this worldview run into trouble?
  _____________________________________________________

ONE-SENTENCE SUMMARY:
"This worldview is strong on _______, but weak on _______."
```

### Facilitation [12 min]

You walk the room. At each group:

- *"What worldview did you pick?"* — make sure it's actually a worldview, not a hobby.
- *"What test are you applying first?"* — make sure they've named one of the four explicitly.
- *"Where does it pass?"* — push for specificity. *"Be precise. Where exactly?"*
- *"Where does it fail?"* — same.

Don't lecture. Just nudge.

### Pre-debrief check [during the work]

Listen for groups that have picked something pedagogically rich. The best groups to call on for the whole-class share are ones that:

- Picked a worldview the room recognizes (a song or character most students know)
- Surfaced a genuinely interesting *failure* in a test
- Articulated the pass *and* the fail without flinching

### Whole-class synthesis [3 min, separate from facilitation but happens at 0:42–0:45]

See [lecture-companion.md §Day 4](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md).

### What to collect

Group worksheets — graded for completion + thoughtful engagement with both halves (pass + fail). If a group only wrote on "where it passes" and skipped "where it fails," that's a 50%. The fail side is where the learning is.

### Trouble spots

- **"A group picks Christianity."** *"Save it. We test Christianity in Unit 1. Today's worldview should be non-religious."* This rule matters — Christianity gets the full four-test treatment on Day 8 of Unit 1; don't pre-empt it.
- **"A group can't name a worldview to test."** Suggest one: *"The worldview of the 'YOLO' philosophy — you only live once, so do what feels good. Try that one."*
- **"A group is sailing past the 'where it fails' question."** Push: *"You've named the strength. The exercise requires the weakness. Try again."*

### Sub-friendliness notes

This day's structure works for a sub. The worksheet is printed; the timing is fixed; the sub's role is to facilitate groups, not teach.

## Day 6 — Steel-Manning Pair Practice (within Day 6's 45-min flow)

This is the **10-minute pair practice** at 0:33–0:43 of Day 6 (after the steel-manning lecture). The lecture-companion covers the teaching; this file covers the practice.

**Goal:** Every student practices steel-manning on a *light, low-stakes* topic. The skill they're building today (representing an assigned view in its strongest form) is the load-bearing skill for every Pair & Defend the rest of the year.

**Materials needed:**
- Topic list (below) — printed at the front of the room or on a slide
- Timer

### Set-up [1 min]

Pair students up. **Random pairing today** (deal a deck of cards if you want; same number = same pair). Random pairing avoids friend pairs choosing matching positions.

### Topic assignment [1 min]

Each pair draws one of these topics. Within the pair, the partner whose first name comes first alphabetically argues the FIRST side; the other partner argues the SECOND.

| Topic | Side A | Side B |
|---|---|---|
| Pizza | *"Pineapple belongs on pizza."* | *"Pineapple does not belong on pizza."* |
| Pets | *"Cats are better pets than dogs."* | *"Dogs are better pets than cats."* |
| Seasons | *"Summer is the best season."* | *"Fall is the best season."* |
| Games | *"Video games are a good use of teenage time."* | *"Video games are a bad use of teenage time."* |
| Phones | *"Smartphones have made our generation better off."* | *"Smartphones have made our generation worse off."* |

### Round 1 [2 min — Student A argues Side A]

*"Side A — you have two minutes. Make the strongest possible case for your assigned side. Steel-man it. Side B — your only job is to listen and remember what the strongest argument was. You'll have to restate it in a moment."*

Set timer. Walk room.

### Round 2 [1 min — Student B restates Student A's argument]

*"Side B — restate your partner's argument. The goal isn't to win. The goal is for Student A to nod and say 'yes, that's what I meant.'"*

Side A confirms or corrects. *"Did your partner get it right? If not, what did they miss?"*

### Swap [2 min — Student B argues Side B]

Same structure, partners swap.

### Round 4 [1 min — Student A restates Student B's argument]

Same protocol.

### Whole-class debrief [3 min — happens at 0:43–0:45 of the period]

See [lecture-companion.md §Day 6](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md). One question: *"Did anyone restate their partner's argument better than the partner did?"* (This usually surfaces a moment of genuine charity.)

### What to collect

Nothing today. The skill is the artifact.

### Trouble spots

- **"My partner won't argue their side."** Coach the pair: *"You agreed to take that side for 2 minutes. After that you can disagree. For now, make the strongest case."*
- **"This is silly — pineapple on pizza isn't worth debating."** Reframe: *"The topic is intentionally silly. The skill is steel-manning. We're building the muscle on something low-stakes before harder topics. Take the practice seriously."*
- **"I literally can't think of a steel-man for [topic]."** Coach: *"Imagine your strongest opposing friend. What's the smartest thing they would say? Start there."*

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub can run this if they keep the clock and enforce the rules. The topics are pre-listed; the structure is timed. The sub's only real judgment call is intervening when a pair won't engage (use the coaching scripts above).

## Day 8 — Mini Pair & Defend (within Day 8's 45-min flow)

This is the **17-minute Pair & Defend** at 0:23–0:40 of Day 8. The lecture-companion covers the cohort-tools walk-through; this file covers the activity.

**Goal:** Every student does a real Pair & Defend on a *low-stakes* topic so the format is muscle memory before the first high-stakes one in Unit 1.

**Tool format:** Pair & Defend per [cohort-tools.md §B](../../../_shared/cohort-tools.md) — two-round simultaneous pair debate. The full protocol is 24 minutes; today we run a compressed 17-minute version because it's practice, not a graded exercise.

**Materials needed:**
- Today's topic on the board
- Scratch paper for brainstorm
- Timer

### Topic for today (suggested)

**On the board:**

> *"School uniforms make schools better."*
> *vs.*
> *"School uniforms don't make schools better."*

(Alternate if the room is loose enough: *"Pineapple belongs on pizza."* / *"Pineapple does not."* — same topic as Day 6, easier to scale up.)

### Pair up + assign sides [2 min]

Random pairing. Within each pair: the partner whose last name comes first alphabetically argues the FIRST proposition; the other argues the SECOND.

### Brainstorm both sides [3 min]

*"Each of you — on scratch paper — write down the 3 best arguments for each side. BOTH sides. The reason: in Round 2 you swap, and you'll need to make the OTHER side as well as your partner just made it."*

### Round 1 [5 min in pairs, simultaneous]

- Student A argues their assigned side (2 min)
- B asks one probing question (30 sec); A responds (30 sec)
- Student B argues their assigned side (2 min)
- A asks one probing question (30 sec); B responds (30 sec)

### Round 2 — sides swap [4 min in pairs, simultaneous]

- Student A now argues the OPPOSITE side (1.5 min) — pick up B's earlier argument and try to strengthen it
- Student B now argues the OPPOSITE side (1.5 min)
- 1 min of cross-questions in either direction

### Quick partner check-in [3 min]

*"In your pair: which argument from your partner did you find most compelling? Tell them."*

### Whole-class debrief [5 min — happens at 0:40–0:45]

See [lecture-companion.md §Day 8](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md).

### What to collect

Scratch papers (graded for completion only — evidence that both sides were brainstormed). This is *practice grading*: 100% if both sides have 3 arguments; 50% if only one side; 0% if blank.

### Trouble spots

- **"My partner refuses to swap sides in Round 2."** Stop the pair. *"The swap is the whole point. Without it, this is just a debate — and debates we already know how to do. The exercise is about understanding the side you disagree with. Swap or skip the round."*
- **"A pair is wildly out of pace with the timer."** Walk over and reset them. *"Move on to Round 2 — you're behind."*

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub can run this with the protocol above. The key sub coaching: ENFORCE the swap. Without the swap, this is not Pair & Defend.

## Day 10 — Test Administration + Bridge to Unit 1

This is the **full Day 10 facilitation** (also covered structurally in [lecture-companion.md §Day 10](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md)).

**Goal:** Collect Doug Letters. Administer the Day-10 blended test. Bridge to Unit 1 in the closing 3 minutes.

**Materials needed:**
- Printed [test-student.md](../assessments/unit-00-foundations-test-student.md) — one per student
- Attendance/Doug Letter tracking clipboard
- A pen for note-taking on Doug-Letter handoff

### Collect Doug Letters [0:00–0:05]

Call alphabetically. Mark off on clipboard. Accept partial letters with a note for late credit. No public commentary.

### Quick verbal review [0:05–0:10]

See [lecture-companion.md §Day 10](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md) for the warmup question sequence. Move fast. The point is calibration, not learning.

### Test administration [0:10–0:38]

Distribute the printed test. 28 minutes (publisher Ch 01 Test = 43 pts; locally-built = ~15 pts; estimated 22–28 min of work).

**Standard administration script:**

> *"This is a single-class-period test. You have 28 minutes. Answer every question. The True/False section requires you to alter the underlined word if the statement is false — don't just write 'F.' The essay at the end (Question 22 — the four tests) is worth 4 points; don't skip it.*
>
> *You may not use your reading packet, your outline, or your notes. You may not talk to a neighbor. Place your phone face-down on the corner of your desk.*
>
> *When you finish, turn your test over and sit quietly. If you have time at the end, re-check your answers. Begin."*

**Walk the room.** Do not answer content questions. If a student asks "what does this word mean" → *"You'll need to use your best judgment."* If a student is stuck on phrasing → you may clarify phrasing, not content.

### Bridge to Unit 1 [0:38–0:45]

See [lecture-companion.md §Day 10](../lectures/unit-00-foundations-lecture-companion.md) for the bridge script.

### What to collect

- **Doug Letters** (graded against the publisher's Ch 1 Assignment rubric — see [_source-text/_cd/UTT Unit 01 Files/01 Writing Assignment/](../_source-text/_cd/UTT%20Unit%2001%20Files/01%20Writing%20Assignment/) — typically 30 points)
- **Tests** (graded against [test-teacher.md](../assessments/unit-00-foundations-test-teacher.md))
- **Reflection journals from Day 5** and **response papers from Day 9** if not yet collected/graded

### Trouble spots

- **A student didn't finish the Doug Letter.** Take what they have, give partial credit, talk to them after class.
- **A student doesn't finish the test.** Standard policy. Score what's there.
- **A student tries to cheat.** Quietly remove the test, zero, talk after class, document.

### Sub-friendliness notes

A sub can absolutely administer this day. The Doug Letter rubric is the publisher's. The test answer key is in [test-teacher.md](../assessments/unit-00-foundations-test-teacher.md). The sub's only judgment call is the "what does this word mean" question — coach the sub to redirect rather than answer.
