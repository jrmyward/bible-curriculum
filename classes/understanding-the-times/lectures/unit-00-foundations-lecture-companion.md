## Day 1 — First Day of School / Framing the Course [45 min]

**Goal:** Students leave knowing (a) what this course is and what it isn't, (b) the cohort norms they'll be held to, and (c) that *everyone has a worldview* — including them. Not a content day; the worldview-concept material starts Day 2. Today is *culture-setting*.

**This is also the first day of school.** Pace accordingly. Adolescents will be nervous, distracted, sizing each other up, sizing you up. Read the room.

**On the board before students arrive:**

```text
Welcome to Understanding the Times
"What shapes how you see the world?"
```

### Opening: greet at the door [0:00–0:05]

Stand at the door. Greet each student by name as they enter. Hand them the syllabus and the [learning-outline-student.md](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-learning-outline-student.md) as they sit down. Brief on-screen agenda for the day (4 items): syllabus walk-through · opening question · what a worldview is (preview) · reading assignment.

### Syllabus walk-through [0:05–0:15]

Walk through the syllabus, but *narrate* rather than read aloud. Land four points:

1. **What this course IS:** *A survey of competing worldviews*. We're going to study nine of them on their own terms — Christianity first (because it's the baseline), then Judaism, Islam, Mormonism, Hinduism, Buddhism, New Spirituality, Secularism, and Revolutionary Lineage. By May you should be able to *sit across a coffee shop table from a thoughtful proponent of any of these and represent their view back to them in a way they'd affirm as fair.*

2. **What this course IS NOT:** Sunday school. Sunday school assumes you already believe. This class does not. You'll defend positions you disagree with. You'll meet Christians who think different things than you do. You'll be asked to articulate the Christian Gospel to someone who finds it absurd. The intellectual demand is real.

3. **What's expected of you:** Read the reading packets. Show up prepared. Engage charitably. Present a worldview fairly even when you disagree with it. Take the tests seriously — most of them are publisher-aligned, so what you learn here counts toward dual-credit later.

4. **How you'll be graded:** Worldview Briefs (1 per unit), Pair & Defend participation, unit tests, Doug Letters, Capstone presentation in Unit 10. Details on the syllabus.

### The opening question [0:15–0:30]

**WRITE ON BOARD (already there):** *"What shapes how you see the world?"*

This is the **structural opener of the course**. The question matters more than any single answer. Run it like this (full facilitation in [facilitation-guide.md §Day 1](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md)):

- 1 minute of silent thinking.
- 2 minutes in pairs (talk to the person next to you).
- 12 minutes of whole-class share — go around the room, catch every answer, write them on the board (one or two words per answer: *family · church · social media · friends · pain · pastor · books · YouTube · my dad · my mom · 9/11 · COVID · grandma · sports · music · video games · the internet*).

**Teacher rule for today:** Do not evaluate any answer. Catch them and name them. Your only job today is to get students talking and to make every student feel heard.

After the board fills: stand back, look at it, and say (without preamble):

> *"Look at this list. Every single thing on this board is something that's been shaping how you see the world — most of it without your permission, most of it without your awareness. That's what we mean by a* **worldview** *— the lens you've been looking through. Some of you knew you were looking through a lens. Most of you didn't. Both groups are about to find out a lot more about what's been on the lens."*

That's the unit's first teaching moment. Let it sit.

### What a worldview is — preview [0:30–0:40]

Brief lecture (no board work yet — that's tomorrow). Three points:

1. **A worldview is a pattern** — not a list of opinions but an *organized set of answers* that hang together. Pull one, the others move.
2. **Everyone has one** — the question is whether you've *examined* it. Most haven't. The point of this class is to make the lens visible so you can decide whether to keep it.
3. **You inherit your worldview the way you catch a cold.** That's the publisher's exact phrase — *people pick up their beliefs the way they catch colds, by being around other people.* If that bothers you a little, good. It should.

Don't try to define *worldview* tonight. Don't introduce the 10 disciplines yet. Don't preview the four tests. All that is Day 2 and after. Today's job is just to plant the question: *what have you been believing without knowing it?*

### Reading assignment + closing [0:40–0:45]

Hand out the [reading packet](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-reading-packet.md). Walk through the table of contents (15 seconds — they'll see it themselves). Assign:

- **Tonight:** Read Part 1 of the reading packet (*What Is a Worldview?*). 5 pages. Mark what you don't understand.
- **Tomorrow:** Bring the reading packet AND the [learning-outline-student.md](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-learning-outline-student.md) blank fill-in. We start filling it in.

Final 30 seconds: *"Welcome to senior Bible. I'm glad you're here. This is going to be a hard class. It's also going to be a good one. See you tomorrow."*

## Day 2 — The 10 Worldview Questions [45 min]

**Goal:** Students draft their *own* first-pass answers to each of the 10 worldview questions, *before* being told what any tradition says. This document becomes their starting point for the year; they will rewrite it in Unit 10 (May) and notice how their thinking has matured.

**Most of this period is student writing**, not lecture. The teacher's role is to frame, distribute, supervise, and debrief. Full facilitation structure in [facilitation-guide.md §Day 2](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md).

**On the board before students arrive:**

```text
Day 2 — Your First Worldview Brief
"Honest answers > impressive answers"
```

### Opening review [0:00–0:05]

Quick verbal check on last night's reading: *"In one sentence — what does the publisher mean by a 'worldview'?"* Take 2–3 answers; land on something close to *"a pattern of ideas, beliefs, convictions, and habits."*

Then: *"Today you draft your own answers to the 10 ultimate questions every worldview tries to answer. No textbook. No Bible. No phone. Just you and a pencil. The point is honesty, not theology."*

### Introduce the 10 questions [0:05–0:10]

Distribute the [10-worldview-questions handout](../lesson-plans-2026-27/week-01-aug-24/handouts/10-worldview-questions.md). Walk through the 10 questions — read each one aloud, in order:

1. **Theology:** *Is there a God? What is God like?*
2. **Philosophy:** *What is real? How do we know what we know?*
3. **Ethics:** *What is good? On what basis?*
4. **Biology:** *What is life, and where did it come from?*
5. **Psychology:** *What is a person? What makes someone whole or broken?*
6. **Sociology:** *How should people live together?*
7. **Law:** *What is justice? Where does authority come from?*
8. **Politics:** *What is the role of government?*
9. **Economics:** *What is wealth? How should it be used?*
10. **History:** *Where is history going? What is its meaning?*

**Frame the rules clearly:**

- Do not look anything up.
- Do not try to give a "right" answer. Give *your* answer.
- *"I don't know"* is a legitimate answer — write it if it's honest.
- 1–2 sentences per question. Don't over-write.

**The most important sentence to land:** *"The point is not that your answers change between now and May. The point is that you'll be able to say WHY they did or didn't."*

### Independent writing [0:10–0:30]

20 minutes. Students write silently. You walk the room. Don't read over shoulders; just be present. If a student looks paralyzed, gently: *"Pick any question — start there."*

### Closing reflection [0:30–0:40]

Two prompts, in order:

1. **In pairs (5 min):** Each student shares the question that was *hardest* to answer. *Why?* Was it because you don't know what you believe, or because you know but couldn't articulate it, or because the question itself didn't make sense?
2. **Whole class (5 min):** Take 3–4 voices. Don't resolve. Note patterns.

### Bridge to Day 3 [0:40–0:45]

*"Tonight: read Part 2 of the reading packet (Why Study Worldviews?). Then for Wednesday — Myers Chapter 1 in the textbook, pages 1–15. If textbooks aren't in your hands yet, I'll have printed excerpts."*

Collect the 10-questions handout (or tell students to keep it in their folder — it's their first Worldview Brief, and they'll rewrite it in May). **The handout does not get graded.** It's a baseline.

## Day 3 — Read Myers Ch 1 in Class [45 min]

**Goal:** Students engage Myers Ch 1 as a *text* — practicing close reading, asking questions, and starting to identify which of the publisher's claims they buy and which they want to push back on. This is the only day in Unit 0 where you read primary text together; use the time well.

**On the board before students arrive:**

```text
Day 3 — Myers Ch 1: Battle of Ideas
"Read with a pencil — mark what surprises you,
 mark what bothers you, mark what helps."
```

### Opening review [0:00–0:05]

Quick: *"From last night's Part 2 of the reading packet — give me one consequence of ideas. Doesn't have to be Nazism or communism. Could be smaller."* Take 3–4 answers.

Pivot: *"Today we read the actual textbook chapter that argues this position. Read along with me. Mark your text."*

### Guided reading [0:05–0:35]

Read Ch 1 together. Don't read every word aloud — that's 30 minutes of pure narration and students will check out. Instead, divide the chapter into ~5-page chunks and use this rhythm:

| Section | Approach | Time |
|---|---|---|
| §1–2 (tennis/chess + pattern recognition) | Read aloud; pause to ask *"What's the analogy?"* | 5 min |
| §3 (Noebel's origin story) | Summarize verbally; don't read | 2 min |
| §4–5 (ideas flow in patterns; world's patterns vs God's) | Silent read; one question on board: *"What do worldviews and GPS programs have in common?"* | 5 min |
| §6 (why care — Nazism + communism) | Read aloud; pause for impact | 5 min |
| §7 (a warning — Rorty quote, Sternberg case) | Read aloud; ask *"Has anything like this happened to you, or someone you know?"* | 5 min |
| §8 (inoculation theory) | Silent read; pull the six-defenses list on the board | 5 min |
| §9 (preview of six worldviews) | Skim — we'll come back to this Day 7 | 3 min |

### Synthesis [0:35–0:42]

Three questions. Take 1–2 voices on each. Don't resolve.

1. *"What's one thing in this chapter you agree with?"*
2. *"What's one thing you want to push back on?"*
3. *"What's one thing that confused you?"*

The third question is the most important. Write the confusions on the board. Promise to come back to them.

### Bridge to Day 4 [0:42–0:45]

*"Tomorrow: the four tests. We're going to learn the tool you'll use against every worldview this year — including Christianity. Reading: Part 5 of the reading packet (The Four Tests). Bring your fill-in outline; we add to Section 5."*

## Day 4 — The Four Tests + Practice on a Familiar Worldview [45 min]

**Goal:** Students learn the four tests (reason, outer world, inner world, real-world consequences) and apply at least two to a *familiar, non-religious worldview* — building the skill before we apply it to Christianity in Unit 1 and to every other worldview after.

The first half is teacher-led lecture; the second half is small-group practice. Full small-group structure in [facilitation-guide.md §Day 4](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md).

**On the board before students arrive:**

```text
Day 4 — The Four Tests
1. REASON          — Is it internally coherent?
2. OUTER WORLD     — Does it match what we observe?
3. INNER WORLD     — Does it match what we feel and know?
4. REAL WORLD      — What fruit does it bear at scale?
```

### Opening review [0:00–0:05]

*"In one sentence: why do worldviews matter?"* Take 2 voices. Land on *ideas have consequences.*

Pivot: *"Today you get the tool. The four tests are how you evaluate any worldview — including the one you brought through the door."*

### The Four Tests — teaching frame [0:05–0:25]

Walk each test in detail. ~5 min per test.

**Test 1 — Reason [5 min].** The question: *Is the worldview internally coherent? Does it contradict itself?* A worldview that fails this test asserts and denies the same thing in the same sense at the same time.

**Classic example (write on board):** *"There are no absolute truths."* Pause. *"That sentence is itself an absolute truth claim. The worldview that says there's no truth has just made a truth claim. That's internal contradiction. It fails the test of reason."*

**Test 2 — Outer World [5 min].** The question: *Does the worldview match what we observe in nature, history, and human experience?*

**Example to use:** Many ancient creation myths claim the universe is eternal. Modern cosmology — the Big Bang, the second law of thermodynamics — gives us strong evidence the universe had a beginning. A worldview that says "the universe is eternal" has to reckon with that evidence. It might still be defensible, but it has work to do.

**Test 3 — Inner World [5 min].** The question: *Does the worldview match our deepest moral, aesthetic, and relational intuitions?*

**Example to use:** Every human being you've ever met believes *cruelty to children is wrong*. Not "disliked" — *wrong.* A worldview that says "morality is just biology" — that our sense of right and wrong is an evolutionary trick — has to explain why we don't just *feel* cruelty is wrong but *know* it. That's the inner-world test.

**Test 4 — Real-World Consequences [5 min].** The question: *When the worldview is lived out at scale, what fruit does it bear?*

**Example to use:** The 20th century ran several worldviews at scale. Marxism in the USSR, China, Cambodia, Cuba, North Korea — over 100 million dead. National Socialism in Germany — ~21 million dead, not counting WWII combat. Secular liberal democracy — for all its flaws, hospitals, universities, religious freedom, the highest standard of living in human history. *By their fruits you shall know them* (Matt. 7:20). That's the test of real-world consequences.

### Frame the practice [0:25–0:30]

*"Now you try. In small groups, pick a* **familiar, non-religious worldview** *and apply two of the four tests. Examples I'll give you: a sitcom character's life philosophy. A song. A meme. A TikTok influencer's worldview. A friend's stated belief. Pick something* **you actually know.** *We test religions next week — not today."*

Why non-religious? Because the publisher's four-test framework works on any worldview. We're building the *skill* on something low-stakes before applying it to Christianity, Judaism, Islam, etc.

### Small-group work [0:30–0:42]

Hand off to facilitation-guide Day 4 — groups of 3–4, 12 min of work, the worksheet template is provided there.

### Whole-class synthesis [0:42–0:45]

Quick popcorn: *"Name one worldview your group tested + the test that exposed it most clearly."* Take 2–3 answers. *"Tomorrow is a sub day — video + reflection journal. We pick up Monday with steel-manning."*

## Day 6 — Steel-Manning [45 min]

*(Mon Aug 31 — first day back after the Aug 28 sub day.)*

**Goal:** Establish the standing rule of the course — *understand a position before you disagree with it* — and practice it on low-stakes topics. Steel-manning is the single highest-leverage skill of the year; if students don't internalize it by end of Unit 0, every later Pair & Defend will fall apart.

**On the board before students arrive:**

```text
Day 6 — The First Rule of This Classroom

STRAW-MAN  →  the weakest version of a view you disagree with
STEEL-MAN  →  the strongest version of a view you disagree with

"Understanding is not endorsement."
```

### Opening review [0:00–0:05]

*"Welcome back. From Friday's reflection journal — anyone want to share something that struck you about the video?"* Take 1–2 voices. Brief; this is not the focus today.

Pivot: *"Today is the first rule of this classroom. Without this rule, this class can't work."*

### Teach the two moves [0:05–0:20]

**Straw-manning — 5 min.** Define: *misrepresenting an opposing position to make it easier to knock down.*

Examples (read aloud, slowly — let each land):

- *"Atheists just want to sin without feeling guilty."*
- *"Christians believe the earth is flat because the Bible says so."*
- *"Muslims want to take over the world."*
- *"Progressives want to destroy the family."*

*"In every one of those, there are real people who hold the opposing view for reasons you haven't considered. By picking the worst version, you ensure you never actually engage the strong version. You 'win' the argument — against your own imagination. And here's the thing: nobody who actually holds that view ever changes their mind because of a straw-man attack. So even on its own terms, straw-manning is useless."*

**Steel-manning — 10 min.** Define: *representing an opposing position in its strongest, most charitable form — the version the person holding it would nod at and say "yes, that's what I mean."*

Walk through the table from the reading packet on the board (one row at a time):

| Straw | Steel |
|---|---|
| *"Atheists just want to sin."* | *"Many atheists believe the evidence for God's existence is inconclusive, and that living morally and meaningfully without religious belief is both possible and intellectually honest."* |
| *"Muslims want to take over the world."* | *"Many Muslims believe Islam provides a complete framework for both private devotion and public life, and that a society governed by Islamic principles would be more just than one governed by secular ones."* |
| *"Progressives want to destroy the family."* | *"Many progressives believe traditional family structures have historically excluded or harmed people, and that expanding our definition of 'family' protects more people from those harms."* |

**The teaching moment to land:** *"In each steel-manned version, we're not* **agreeing** *with the view. We're representing it the way its strongest proponent would. That's the difference between* understanding *and* endorsement. *This class will require you to understand many positions you don't endorse. That's not a betrayal of what you believe — it's the only way to honestly engage with what you don't."*

### Why this is the rule [0:20–0:30]

Four reasons (write on board, brief on each):

1. **It's the first ethical move.** People who hold views you disagree with have reasons. Taking those reasons seriously treats them as agents, not opponents.
2. **It makes real disagreement possible.** You cannot meaningfully disagree with a view you've never honestly represented.
3. **It's the skill the course is preparing you for.** This year ends with you sitting across from someone who holds one of these worldviews and trying to articulate the Gospel in a way that's meaningful to them. You cannot do that if you only know the caricature.
4. **It models intellectual humility.** Sometimes the strongest form of an opposing view is more defensible than the straw version you pictured. That's growth, not weakness.

### The Steel-Man Test [0:30–0:33]

**WRITE ON BOARD:**

> *Before you engage an opposing view, ask: "If a thoughtful proponent of this view were standing here right now, would they hear my summary as fair?"*

*"Yes → you're steel-manning. No → go back and try again."*

### Practice [0:33–0:43]

Hand off to [facilitation-guide.md §Day 6](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md). Pair students up, give them light topics (pineapple on pizza, cats vs. dogs, summer vs. fall — the practice topics from the existing steel-manning handout). Each student argues their assigned side for 2 minutes; the partner restates the argument back; swap; debrief as class.

### Closing [0:43–0:45]

The one rule that doesn't bend: *"You may not refuse your assigned side."* Lift this to a course-wide commitment. *"When we get to harder topics — Islam, atheism, postmodernism — you will be assigned sides you may strongly disagree with. That's the point. If you refuse to steel-man a view, you cannot meaningfully disagree with it."*

Bridge to Day 7: *"Tomorrow we look at the worldview grid — the 9 columns we're going to spend the year filling in. Bring your outline."*

## Day 7 — The Worldview Grid + Preview of the 9 Worldviews [45 min]

**Goal:** Students see the full scope of the year — 9 worldviews × 10 disciplines = 90 cells they'll learn to fill in. They walk out with a one-sentence orientation to each worldview and a sense of where Christianity sits among them.

**On the board before students arrive:** Tape up a wall-poster-sized version of the [worldview-grid-handout](../lesson-plans-2026-27/week-01-aug-24/handouts/worldview-grid-handout.md) if you have it printed. If not, draw the 9 column headers across the top of the board.

### Opening [0:00–0:05]

*"This is the map for the year. Today you see it. By May, you should be able to draw it from memory and use it in a real conversation."*

### Walk the columns [0:05–0:30]

3 min per column, one-sentence orientation to each worldview. Don't go deep — that's their respective units. Just establish the column.

**1. Christianity (Unit 1, starts Sep 9).** *"Monotheistic. God is one being eternally existing as three persons — Father, Son, Holy Spirit. The Bible is God's word. Jesus is God incarnate who died for our sins and rose bodily from the dead. This is the baseline. Every other worldview compares back to this."*

**2. Judaism (Unit 2, starts Sep 29).** *"Monotheistic but non-Trinitarian. The covenant tradition Christianity claims to fulfill. Modern Judaism isn't the Judaism of the Old Testament — after the destruction of the Temple in AD 70, rabbis rebuilt the religion around prayer, study, and acts of lovingkindness instead of sacrifice. Not in the publisher's textbook; we'll build it from primary sources."*

**3. Islam (Unit 3, starts Oct 13).** *"Monotheistic. Allah is one — radically one, not Trinitarian. Muhammad is the final prophet. The Quran is the direct word of Allah. 1.8 billion adherents. Honors Jesus as a prophet but not as God."*

**4. Mormonism (Unit 4, starts Nov 13).** *"Uses Christian vocabulary but means categorically different things by it. The Father once lived a mortal life and progressed to exaltation. Jesus is the firstborn spirit-son of God, not the eternal God of Christianity. Salvation involves grace + works + ordinances + temple covenants. Not in the publisher's textbook. You will have LDS conversations weekly here in eastern Idaho."*

**5. Hinduism (Unit 5, starts Dec 3).** *"3,000 years old. The self (Atman) is ultimately identical with the divine (Brahman). Reality as we experience it is largely illusion (Maya). The goal is liberation (Moksha) from the cycle of rebirth (Samsara). The publisher folds this into 'New Spirituality' — we don't."*

**6. Buddhism (Unit 6, starts Dec 16).** *"Departs from Hinduism on exactly the question of the self — Buddhism teaches there is no enduring self (anatta). Life is suffering (dukkha); the cause is craving; the path out is the Eightfold Path. Goal: nirvana — the extinguishing of craving. 500 million adherents."*

**7. New Spirituality (Unit 7, starts Feb 12).** *"What the publisher means by 'New Spirituality' — the Western syncretism of Tolle, Chopra, Byrne, the wellness/manifestation industry. Not Eastern religions; the Western blender'd version. The shortest unit (5 days) because the heavy lifting is done in Hinduism and Buddhism."*

**8. Secularism / New Atheism (Unit 8, starts Mar 1).** *"Humans are the center of reality. No God, no afterlife, no anything beyond what our senses can detect. The publisher classifies this as a religion — it makes claims about ultimate questions, even when those claims are denials. Hawking, Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris — the New Atheist movement is the rhetorical edge."*

**9. Revolutionary Lineage (Unit 9, starts Apr 8).** *"Three movements, one lineage. Marxism (class struggle ending in workers' paradise — 100M+ dead in the 20th century). American Progressivism (Marxist categories adapted to the American situation). Postmodernism (there are no metanarratives — itself a metanarrative). The deepest unit because it explains what's happening in your world right now."*

### The rows: 10 disciplines [0:30–0:40]

Quick walk across the row headers (already in the reading packet, Part 3). Just name them, in order, and put each on the board if not already there:

*Theology · Philosophy · Ethics · Biology · Psychology · Sociology · Law · Politics · Economics · History.*

*"Every cell in this grid is a worldview's one-sentence answer to that discipline's central question. 90 cells. We'll start filling them in next week, with Christianity. By May, you should be able to defend the Christianity column from memory and articulate any other column charitably enough that its strongest proponent would say 'yes, that's what I mean.'"*

### Bridge [0:40–0:45]

*"Tomorrow: cohort tools. You'll learn the formats — Discussion Brief, Pair & Defend, Case Study, Capstone — that we'll use over and over. And we'll do a mini Pair & Defend on a low-stakes topic so you've got the rhythm before Unit 1."*

## Day 8 — Cohort Tools Walk-Through + Mini Pair & Defend [45 min]

**Goal:** Students walk out knowing the four cohort tools by name, what each is for, and what good participation looks like in each. They also get one real run of Pair & Defend on a low-stakes topic — building muscle memory before Unit 1.

**On the board before students arrive:**

```text
Day 8 — The Tools You'll Use All Year
1. DISCUSSION BRIEF  →  1 page, written, before discussion
2. PAIR & DEFEND     →  both sides, both partners, 24 min
3. CASE STUDY        →  small groups, real scenario, vote at end
4. CAPSTONE          →  end-of-unit presentation
```

### Opening [0:00–0:03]

*"Today: the four tools. By the end of class you've heard each one and done one of them. Tomorrow's a sub day — you read Myers Ch 1 in full and write a response. Then Tuesday we synthesize and take the unit quiz."*

### Cohort tools walk-through [0:03–0:23]

5 minutes each. Don't go deep — refer to [_shared/cohort-tools.md](../../_shared/cohort-tools.md) for the canonical specifications. Land *what each is for* and *what good participation looks like.*

**1. Discussion Brief [5 min].** *Purpose:* get every student thinking *before* discussion starts. *Format:* 1 page, written, due at start of period. Either takes a position OR identifies the strongest objection OR writes the question the reading raised. *Grading:* completion + thoughtfulness, not "right" answers.

**2. Pair & Defend [5 min].** *Purpose:* every student articulates BOTH sides of a contested question — the side they hold AND the side they don't. *Format:* two-round simultaneous pair debate, 24 min total. *The key thing to land:* you will be ASSIGNED a side. You may not refuse it. Today (in 20 min) you'll practice this on a light topic so the format is familiar before harder ones.

**3. Case Study [5 min].** *Purpose:* move from abstract ideas to a concrete situation. *Format:* groups of 3–4, real scenario (a person facing a real choice), 10 min to discuss, 5 min per group to present, class votes on most compelling response. *What good looks like:* groups that name the *hard* part of the scenario, not just the easy answer.

**4. Capstone [5 min].** *Purpose:* the summative end-of-unit demonstration. *Format:* 5–7 min presentation per student, oral with a visual aid, must state a thesis + support + address a counterargument. *Final brief:* every student writes a 1-page reflection after capstones. *What good looks like:* clarity of thought, not polish.

### Mini Pair & Defend [0:23–0:40]

Light topic for today. The skill is the format; the topic is incidental. Hand off to [facilitation-guide.md §Day 8](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md). Suggested topic: *"School uniforms make schools better"* vs. *"School uniforms don't make schools better."* Or — if the room is loose — *"Pineapple belongs on pizza."*

5 min brainstorm both sides · 8 min Round 1 · 4 min Round 2 (swap sides).

### Whole-class debrief [0:40–0:45]

The debrief question (same one for every Pair & Defend the rest of the year):

> *"What was the strongest argument you heard from the side you started disagreeing with?"*

Take 3 voices. Don't resolve. Land: *"That's the move. That's what understanding is. You just did it on pizza. We'll do it on harder things all year. Bring your reading packet tomorrow — Friday is a sub day, then we wrap up Tuesday."*

## Day 10 — Synthesis + The Doug Letter + Unit 0 Blended Quiz [45 min]

*(Tue Sep 8 — Fri Sep 4 = no school, Mon Sep 7 = Labor Day.)*

**Goal:** Collect the Doug Letter. Administer the Day-10 blended test. Bridge to Unit 1 in the closing 3 minutes.

Most of this day is test administration, not lecture. Full administration logistics in [facilitation-guide.md §Day 10](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md).

**On the board before students arrive:**

```text
Day 10 — Unit 0 Wrap

1. Doug Letter due at start of class
2. Blended quiz (~30 min)
3. Bridge to Unit 1: Christianity, starts tomorrow
```

### Collect Doug Letters [0:00–0:05]

Call alphabetically. Mark off who handed in and who didn't. Late letters accepted, marked for late credit. No public shaming.

### Quick verbal review [0:05–0:10]

5 minutes of cumulative recall — fast popcorn:

- *"Define worldview."* (1 voice)
- *"Name three of the 10 disciplines."* (3 voices, each takes one)
- *"What's the difference between steel-manning and straw-manning?"* (1 voice)
- *"What does Romans 12:2 say?"* (1 voice — read from memory if able, or paraphrase)
- *"Why is indoctrination NOT a good defense against bad ideas?"* (1 voice)
- *"What's the publisher's six worldviews?"* (1 voice)
- *"What's this course's nine worldviews?"* (1 voice — different person)

This is the warmup. If students stumble on any of these, give them 60 seconds to flip to the answer in their notes. Then test.

### Test administration [0:10–0:38]

Distribute the [test-student.md](../assessments/unit-00-foundations-test-student.md). 28 minutes. Standard administration:

- Closed packet, closed notes, closed phone.
- Walk the room; don't answer content questions; clarify only phrasing.
- When done, students turn the test over and sit quietly.

### Bridge to Unit 1 [0:38–0:45]

After collecting tests:

*"Two weeks ago you walked in with a question on the board:* what shapes how you see the world? *Today you walk out with a working vocabulary, a framework, and your own first-draft worldview brief.*

*Tomorrow we start Unit 1. Christianity. The baseline. You'll spend 13 days learning what Christians actually believe — not the cultural caricature, not the assumed knowledge, but the actual claims. Then you'll learn to articulate it, to defend it, and to share it with someone who finds it strange.*

*Tomorrow's reading: Part 1 of the Unit 1 reading packet. I'll have it printed and ready as you walk in. See you then."*

## If You're a Substitute Teacher Reading This

Welcome. Unit 0 is dense (a lot of structural concepts to teach in 10 days) but the choreography is clear.

**Read first:** [learning-outline.md](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-learning-outline.md) (~10 min). That's the target for the unit.

**Then read:** the section of this file for the day you're teaching (~10–15 min). That's the choreography.

**Bring to class:** the printed reading packet (a copy for yourself), the [learning-outline-student.md](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-learning-outline-student.md) blank fill-in (so you can mirror what students are doing), and this file.

**The non-negotiables:** WRITE ON BOARD prompts go on the board. Time markers are real. Vocabulary in **bold** must be said out loud in the publisher's exact wording — that vocabulary appears on the Day-10 test.

**If you don't know an answer to a student question:** *"That's a question for Mr. Ward when he's back. Hold onto it and let's keep moving."* That is a complete answer. Don't improvise theology.

**Day 5 (Fri Aug 28) and Day 9 (Thu Sep 3) are pre-planned sub days.** See [facilitation-guide.md §Day 5](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md) and [§Day 9](../handouts/unit-00-foundations-facilitation-guide.md) for the full sub plans — they're self-contained.
