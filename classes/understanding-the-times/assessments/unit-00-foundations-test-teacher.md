## Short Answer [3 points each — 4 questions]

*Answer each question with at least one complete sentence.*

**20.** What is a worldview?

- *A worldview is the pattern of ideas, beliefs, convictions, and habits that help us make sense of God, the world, and our relationship to God and the world.* [1.1–1.2]

**21.** What are the six dominant Western worldviews covered in the publisher's textbook?

- *Christianity, Secularism, Marxism, Islam, New Spirituality, and Postmodernism.* [1.9]

**29.** Beyond the publisher's six, this course studies **nine** worldviews in dedicated units. Name all nine.

- *Christianity, Judaism, Islam, Mormonism, Hinduism, Buddhism, New Spirituality, Secularism / New Atheism, and Revolutionary Lineage (Marxism → Progressivism → Postmodernism).* [U0.4] Full credit for all nine in any order; deduct 0.3 per missing or wrong.

**30.** Define **steel-manning** and **straw-manning** in one sentence each. Then give one reason steel-manning is the standing rule of this classroom.

- *Steel-manning: representing an opposing position in its strongest, most charitable form. Straw-manning: misrepresenting an opposing position to make it easier to knock down. One acceptable reason for the rule: it makes real disagreement possible (you cannot meaningfully disagree with a view you've never honestly represented); OR it's the skill the course is preparing students for (articulating the Gospel to someone who holds a different worldview); OR it's the first ethical move (taking opposing reasons seriously treats people as agents, not opponents); OR it models intellectual humility.* [U0.6] Full credit (3 pts): both definitions + one reason. Partial credit (2 pts): definitions clear, reason missing or weak. (1 pt): one definition clear, the other confused.

## Essay [4–5 points — 2 questions]

*Answer in at least one well-developed paragraph.*

**22.** [Publisher item, 4 pts] What are the four tests for evaluating worldviews? Summarize each.

- **Test of reason:** *This test asks if a worldview can be logically stated and defended.*
- **Test of the outer world:** *This test asks if there is empirical evidence to support the worldview.*
- **Test of the inner world:** *This test asks if the worldview can adequately address our personal experiences.*
- **Test of the real world:** *This test asks if the worldview leads to any negative consequences when lived out at scale.* [1.12]

**Rubric:**
- 4 pts: All four tests named with the publisher's exact framing AND a one-sentence summary for each.
- 3 pts: All four named, summaries weak for 1–2.
- 2 pts: Three named, summaries partial.
- 1 pt: Tests named but not summarized.
- 0 pts: Off-topic or blank.

**23.** [Publisher item, 4 pts] Summarize the parable of the elephant.

- *The parable tells of an encounter between several blind men and a large pachyderm. As each of the men touches various parts of the animal, they each advocate a different interpretation for the nature of the animal. The man who touches the trunk believes the animal to be snakelike, while the man who encounters the tusk proclaims that it is like a spear, and so on.* [1.13]

**Rubric:**
- 4 pts: Captures the setup (multiple blind men, single elephant) + multiple specific contradictory descriptions.
- 3 pts: Setup captured, one or two specific descriptions named.
- 2 pts: Vague summary missing the contradictory-descriptions element.
- 1 pt: Vague reference to "the elephant parable" without detail.

**24.** [Publisher item, 4 pts] What is the moral of the elephant parable?

- *The moral of the parable is that every worldview is correct in its respective interpretation of reality, because no single worldview has the entire picture. The application we are to take away from this parable is essentially that competing worldviews should be careful not to make exclusive claims about the world in which we live. Since every worldview can only see part of the elephant, no worldview should make generalizations about the whole of reality.* [1.13]

**Rubric:**
- 4 pts: Captures BOTH (a) "everyone is partly right" AND (b) "no worldview should make exclusive claims."
- 3 pts: Captures one of the two clearly, the other weakly.
- 2 pts: Vague gesture at "all religions are equal" without naming the parable's logic.
- 1 pt: Off-target.

**25.** [Publisher item, 4 pts] What is the fundamental problem with the elephant parable?

- *The fundamental problem with the parable is that the application is a contradiction. The lesson of the parable is that everyone is limited to only a small portion of the elephant, yet the only way to understand this lesson is to see all of the parts of the elephant simultaneously. How can anyone know that we all have limited knowledge unless someone has knowledge of the entire picture?* [1.13]

**Rubric:**
- 4 pts: Names the self-contradiction explicitly — the parable secretly assumes the very view-of-the-whole it claims is impossible.
- 3 pts: Mentions the contradiction but doesn't fully articulate the "view-of-the-whole assumption" point.
- 2 pts: Critiques the parable on a different ground (e.g., "all religions can't be true because they contradict each other") — true, but not the *fundamental* problem the publisher asks about.
- 1 pt: Vague disagreement without explanation.

**31.** [Locally-built, 4 pts] Pick a familiar, **non-religious** worldview (a movie or sitcom character's life philosophy, a song lyric, a TikTok influencer's stated worldview, a common bumper-sticker philosophy, etc.). State it in one sentence. Then apply **two** of the four tests to it. For each test, identify (a) where the worldview *passes cleanly* and (b) where the worldview *runs into trouble.*

**Sample answer (4 pts):** *Students should pick a non-religious worldview (e.g., the YOLO philosophy — "you only live once, so do what feels good") and apply two of the four tests honestly. Strong response on YOLO might note: on REASON, the philosophy hangs together internally — there's nothing self-contradictory about prioritizing immediate experience — but its appeal depends on assuming there's no afterlife, which it doesn't argue for; on OUTER WORLD, observable evidence shows that people who consistently follow YOLO often experience negative consequences (debt, broken relationships, regret) — so the philosophy fails to predict what actually happens; on INNER WORLD, most people who live by YOLO eventually report feeling shallow or unsatisfied, suggesting the philosophy doesn't match deep human longings for meaning; on REAL-WORLD CONSEQUENCES, societies that elevate immediate gratification tend to produce poorer outcomes over generations.* [U0.5 + Day 4 practice]

**Rubric:**
- 4 pts: Non-religious worldview chosen + stated clearly + TWO tests named + BOTH passes and troubles identified for each test.
- 3 pts: Same shape but one test's analysis is thinner than the other.
- 2 pts: Only one test analyzed, OR a religious worldview chosen (we test those in later units; this question is the *practice* with a non-religious one).
- 1 pt: Names the tests but doesn't apply them concretely.
- 0 pts: Off-topic or blank.

## Notes on Adaptations from Publisher Original

This test stays much closer to the publisher's Ch 01 Test than Unit 1's blended test does to UTT Ch 02 — because Ch 01 is foundational content (definitions of worldview, the 10 disciplines, the four tests, the elephant parable) and doesn't have the cross-comparative items that Unit 1 had to adapt for the worldview-first ordering.

**Locally-built additions:**

- **Q26** (MC, 1 pt): steel-manning definition. The publisher's Ch 01 does not cover charitable representation; this course makes it the first curricular commitment. The MC tests basic recognition.
- **Q27** (T/F, 1 pt): the standing rule that students may not refuse their assigned side in Pair & Defend. This rule is course-wide and load-bearing; testing it on Day 10 reinforces that it isn't optional.
- **Q28** (Fill-in, 2 pts): production of the term "steel-manning" from its definition.
- **Q29** (Short answer, 3 pts): the course's nine worldviews. Required because the publisher's Q21 only asks for the publisher's six. Students need to be able to name both lists.
- **Q30** (Short answer, 3 pts): steel-manning + straw-manning + a reason for the rule. Tests integration, not just recall.
- **Q31** (Essay, 4 pts): application of the four tests to a non-religious worldview. Built directly from Day 4's small-group practice. The non-religious constraint preserves Christianity for Unit 1's Day 8 Four-Test application.
