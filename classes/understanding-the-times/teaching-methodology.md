# Worldview — Teaching Methodology

**Course:** Understanding the Times — A Survey of Competing Worldviews
**Grade:** 11–12
**School Year:** 2026–2027
**Companion docs:** [teaching-map.md](teaching-map.md) · [pacing-priority-guide.md](pacing-priority-guide.md) · [worldview-grid.md](worldview-grid.md)

---

## Purpose

This document records the deliberate choice of teaching style for the course. It is not a lesson-plan template — it is the answer to *"how should this material be delivered, given the design constraints we've already locked in?"*

The course commits to:

1. A **worldview-first** delivery (each worldview studied through all 10 disciplines before moving to the next), against the textbook's discipline-first ordering.
2. **Charitable representation** of every worldview as the top pedagogical commitment.
3. A **per-worldview Gospel conversation** as the capstone of every unit and the final exam.
4. **Dual-credit alignment** — students who choose the dual-credit option must succeed on a publisher-aligned final.

These four commitments together rule out any single teaching style. A pure-cohort course can't carry the content load. A pure-lecture course can't form Gospel conversationalists. A reading-driven course breaks on the textbook's structure. The course must be hybrid, and the hybrid must be intentional.

---

## The Structural Problem the Design Creates

Worldview-first delivery means each unit needs material from the textbook's worldview chapter (e.g., Ch 5 for Marxism) **plus** the Marxism slice of every discipline chapter (Ch 8 Theology, Ch 9 Philosophy, ... Ch 17 History). That is *one full chapter plus ten sub-sections from ten different chapters* per worldview unit.

The textbook is structured for sequential reading. The course is asking it to be a hypertext.

If that navigation is pushed to students as homework, the prepared third of the class will do it and the rest will arrive empty — and the cohort engine, which depends on prepared students, stalls. If it is pushed into class discussion, cohort time gets spent on textbook navigation that doesn't form anyone in anything.

The methodology has to solve this navigation problem before any other choices are made.

---

## What Each Style Is Good For

- **Direct instruction (lecture)** is uniquely good at *synthesis across sources*. The teacher already knows how the worldview chapter and the relevant slices of Ch 8–17 fit together; a lecture hands students the pre-integrated picture in 50 minutes. For a worldview-first course, this is irreplaceable.
- **Cohort / Socratic** is uniquely good at *formation*: charitable engagement, Gospel-conversation rehearsal, defending positions students don't personally hold, recognizing one's own unexamined assumptions. This is the half of the course the textbook can't reach.
- **Structured reading** is good for *primary-source encounter* — a Quran sura, a Marx excerpt, a Buddhist sutra, a Book of Mormon passage — where the voice of the source matters more than the synthesis. It is bad for cross-chapter textbook navigation.
- **Spaced retrieval (low-stakes quizzing)** is good for *dual-credit recall*. Short weekly quizzes on prior worldviews keep the grid alive in long-term memory and prepare students for the random-access recall the final demands.

## Phase-by-Phase Weighting Within Each Unit

The 9-day unit rhythm in [teaching-map.md](teaching-map.md) maps onto the four styles like this:

| Day | Phase | Primary mode | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Framing | Lecture (70%) + Q&A | The worldview's best face needs an integrated presentation, not student first-pass interpretation |
| 2–3 | Deep Dive (10 lenses) | Lecture (80%) + structured note-taking on the grid | This is the Ch 8–17 cross-chapter synthesis — the place that can't be handed off to students |
| 4 | Socratic Seminar | Cohort (100%) | Pure formation; lecture would defeat the point |
| 5 | Pair & Defend | Cohort (100%) | Two-round both-sides debate; every student argues both positions |
| 6 | Case Study | Cohort with worksheet structure | Group work with rails |
| 7 | Four-Test + vs. Christianity | Mixed — short lecture frame, then small-group application | The four-test framework needs teacher framing; the application is best done in pairs/quads |
| 8 | Gospel Conversation | Roleplay (100%) | The whole point of the course |
| 9 | Brief + Assessment | Independent | Individual demonstration |

**Aggregate weighting across a unit: ~40% direct instruction, ~50% cohort, ~10% independent.** Lecture weight is front-loaded so the cohort work has fuel; cohort weight peaks mid-unit when students have enough material to engage with charitably; the unit ends with individual demonstration.

## The Capital Investment That Makes This Work

The course rises or falls on **per-worldview reading packets**. One packet per worldview unit, built once over the summer before the course launches, reusable every year.

Each packet is ~10–20 stapled pages containing:

- The worldview's Myers/Noebel chapter, in full or excerpted
- The 10 relevant discipline-chapter excerpts from Ch 8–17, pulled into one continuous read in lens order (Theology → Philosophy → ... → History)
- 1–2 primary-source excerpts (the Marxist primary text, the sura, the Upanishad passage, the relevant Doctrine & Covenants section)
- The pre-printed slice of the [worldview-grid](worldview-grid.md) for this unit, blank, for students to fill in as the lectures unfold

The packet eliminates cross-chapter navigation entirely. Students never "find" anything in the textbook during a worldview unit — they read the packet. The textbook itself becomes a reference that lives in the backpack and gets reopened during the T1/T2/T3 synthesis review weeks (when the class is walking *across* the rows of the grid and the textbook's discipline-first structure becomes the right tool).

**Estimated prep cost:** 30–50 hours over the summer, front-loaded. This is real work, but it converts a chronic friction point into a one-time fixed cost. The packets pay back in instructional minutes, in homework completion rates, and in the absence of the "I couldn't find it in the book" excuse.

The four supplemental units (Judaism, Mormonism, Hinduism distinct, Buddhism distinct) need packets built from primary sources rather than from Myers/Noebel — a slightly heavier prep lift, but the same structure.

## Spaced Retrieval — A Standing Commitment

Starting Week 4 (the first week after Unit 1 ends), every Friday opens with a **5-minute retrieval quiz**: 5 cells from the worldview grid drawn from any worldview the class has already covered. Cumulative across the year.

By May the quizzes are testing the full 8 × 10 grid in random-access fashion, which is exactly what the dual-credit final will demand. The cost per week is 5 minutes of class time and 10 minutes of grading. The benefit is durable long-term recall — the part of mastery that worldview-first delivery would otherwise leave underdeveloped.

The quizzes are low-stakes (completion-graded plus a participation bump for accuracy), not high-stakes. They are practice, not assessment.

---

## Reading at Home

Reading is structured tightly:

- **Always:** "Read pages X–Y of the packet for tomorrow."
- **Never:** "Find and read the relevant material in the textbook."
- **Sometimes:** "Read this primary source we'll discuss tomorrow." (Always pre-distributed; never "go find it.")

The total reading load per night should sit at 20–40 pages on lecture-prep nights and lighter on cohort-prep nights. Heavier reading lands the night before a Deep Dive lecture, lighter reading the night before Pair & Defend or the Gospel Conversation.

---

## What This Methodology Refuses

- **Pure flipped classroom.** The students 11th–12th graders need lecture as a load-bearing element; expecting them to absorb 10 disciplines of synthesis from solo reading the night before would fail half the class.
- **Pure Socratic.** Without lecture-delivered synthesis, the discussions devolve into pooling ignorance. Cohort tools assume prepared students; the prep can't be optional.
- **Textbook-driven navigation.** The textbook is a reference, not a daily roadmap. Students don't navigate it during worldview units.
- **Project-based replacement of cohort engagement.** The Capstone is project-based. The rest of the year is not. Replacing daily cohort work with longer projects loses the per-unit Gospel-conversation rehearsal the course is built around.

---

## Net Recommendation

Hybrid, with intentional weighting per phase. Lecture for synthesis (the teacher does the work the textbook structure won't), cohort for formation (the part that actually makes Gospel conversationalists), structured reading via per-worldview packets (not via the textbook's table of contents), and short weekly retrieval quizzes for dual-credit recall.

The single biggest risk in this methodology is **underestimating packet prep.** Plan the summer work in. If those packets exist by August 24, the rest of the methodology works. If they don't, class time gets consumed doing what packets should have done — and the course quietly drifts toward the textbook's discipline-first structure because that's the path of least resistance.

Build the packets. The methodology depends on them.
