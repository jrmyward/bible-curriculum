Others insist our idea of happiness has little to do with living virtuously. Laws should

5. According to the natural law view, what kinds of rules lead to just laws? [14.3]
Professor Lon Fuller laid out eight rules for just laws based on a natural law that is shaped
by an internal morality possessed by all people:
• The rules must be spelled out so people will know what to do and what not to do.
• The rules must be publicized so people will know about them.
• The rules must not be used to judge actions taken before the rules were adopted.
• The rules must be clear and understandable.
• The rules must not contradict one another.
• It must be within the power of the people affected to follow the rules.
• The rules must not be changed frequently or the people who are to follow them
will be unable to decide what to do.
• Those in charge of enforcing the rules must actually do so.

What assumptions shape the Secularist understanding of law? [14.4]
The Secularist view of law is influenced by materialism, naturalism, and evolution.
Secularists reject the ideas of God, objective morality, and historical precedent. They
believe morality is constructed by humans and subject to change. There's no such thing

The Secularist view of law is far different from the Western tradition of common law.
Common law assumes that an action is legal unless it is specifically prohibited. Under
common law, the courts make decisions based on prior judicial precedent reached through
common sense and reason.
Common law assumes that what people did in the past should set a precedent for what
to do now. But from the Secularist viewpoint, humanity is evolving and we know more
now than people in the past did. Precedent isn't necessarily good; newer is better.

11. What assumptions shape the Postmodernist understanding of law? [14.6]
Postmodernists deny a connection between law and an objective standard of morality.
They see no essential nature or universal moral truth as the foundation for law. "There is
no objective stance but only a series of perspectives," says Harvard Law professor Martha
Minnow, "no neutrality, no escape from choice in judging.?"
Postmodernists view laws resulting from the power structures of Christianity and the
Enlightenment as unjust. Their understanding of justice in relation to the law is not to
enforce objective standards; it is to uncover and remedy various forms of oppression.
[Postmodernists attack] the concepts of reason and objective truth, condemning
them as components of white male domination. They prefer the more subjective
"ways of knowing" supposedly favored by women and minorities, such as story-
telling. As to the rule of law, it is an article of [Postmodern] faith that legal rules
are indeterminate and serve only to disguise the law's white male bias. (Daniel A.
Farber and Suzanna Sherry)
12. What is social constructionism and what role does it play in Postmodernist legal
thinking? [14.6]
Social constructionism is the belief that human beings ultimately construct a model
of social reality based upon their own subjective experiences and language. If we feel
socially oppressed, it's because others have constructed reality to secure their own power
(hegemony). Postmodernists assert that objective knowledge is, as described by Farber
and Sherry, "a power relation, one category of people benefiting at the expense of another
category of people"
One tool Postmodernists have used to unmask oppressive power structures is critical
legal studies, the practice of deconstructing laws in order to discover their subjec-
tive meaning and biased intent. Legal scholars use the "deconstruction" techniques of
Jacques Derrida to dissect laws to discover how they create or contribute to unequal
power relations.

"THE LAW" READING
In this 1850 excerpt from The Law, Frédéric Bastiat defines what law is: the collective or-
ganization of the individual right to lawful defense. What we have the right to do as indi-
viduals-defend our person, liberty, and property —a society has the collective right and
responsibility to do.
Bastiat explains that the role of government in enacting laws is to protect and preserve the
natural rights of its citizens, which include the right to enjoy the fruits of their labor and
protection from unjust attack. He then identifies two kinds of plunder: illegal and legal. II-
legal plunder (theft, swindling) is punishable by law and is why police and judicial systems
exist. Legal plunder is when the state takes from some individuals what belongs to them and
gives what it takes to other individuals to whom it does not belong.
Illegal plunder exists because of greed. This flaw has its origin in the sinful nature of man,
which impels him to satisfy his desires with the least possible effort. The law is often perverted
by the influence of greed. Those in power make and use laws to limit the personal freedom
of the citizens and to legally take property in ways that benefit those who make the laws.
Finally, Bastiat touches on the relationship between law and justice. To most people they
are synonymous. They erroneously hold that things are "just" because law makes them so.
When law and justice contradict each other, citizens will either lose their moral sense or lose
their respect for law. Examples of law and justice contradicting each other include slavery,
discrimination, and political or economic monopolies.
No society can exist for long unless its laws are respected to a certain degree. The safest way
to make laws respected is to make them just.

The law has been perverted by the influence of two entirely different causes: stupid

A Fatal Tendency of Mankind
Self-preservation and self-development are common aspirations among all people.
And if everyone enjoyed the unrestricted use of his faculties and the free disposi-

Property and Plunder

1. What is law and upon what is it based?
Law is the collective organization of the individual right to lawful defense. As the Declara-
tion of Independence puts it, humans are endowed by the Creator with certain inalienable
rights. Law is a collective effort to defend those rights.
Law is the substitution of a common force for individual force. And this common force is
to do only what the individual force has a natural right to do: to protect persons, liberties
and properties, and to cause justice to reign over all.
What we have the right to do as individuals—defend our person, liberty, and property—a
society has the collective right and responsibility to do. It enacts laws to protect these
rights and enforces these laws by punishing those who disobey them.
2. What is the role of government in making and enforcing laws?
The role of government in enacting laws is to protect and preserve the natural rights of
its citizens. These include the right to enjoy the fruits of their labor, including the ac-
cumulation of property, and protection from unjust attack.
A good government will not meddle in the logical order of things by passing laws that
take the property of those who have earned it and giving it wholesale to those who
haven't. Such state-mandated redistribution can lead to great displacements of capital,
labor, and population. Furthermore, these acts burden the government with increased
responsibilities requiring higher taxation, which must be enforced by law. This shifts the
role of law from protecting the individual to plundering the individual.
3. What's the difference between illegal plunder and legal plunder?
Illegal plunder is when one person takes the property of another, such as theft and swin-
dling. It is punishable by law that is enforced by the police and judicial system. Legal
plunder is when the state takes from some individuals what belongs to them and gives
what it takes to other individuals to whom it does not belong. Bastiat contends that
forms of legal plunder include tariffs, subsidies, progressive taxation, public schools,
guaranteed jobs, minimum wages, and free credit.
Legal plunder is when the law benefits one citizen at the expense of another by doing what
the citizen himself cannot do without committing a crime. For instance, if an individual
takes the property of another, it's a crime, but if the government takes the property of
an individual by law-for instance, taxation-it's not only legal but it's a crime not to
yield the property.

"THE PHILOSOPHY OF LAW" DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is the philosophy of law?
Philosophy is "the love of wisdom" and has to do with the first principles or fundamen-
tal beliefs in a discipline. Philosophers try to answer the big questions and uncover the
reasons behind what people think and how they behave.
The philosophy of law has to do with the conceptual and foundational issues dealing with
law on all levels. The two dominant approaches to law in our day are legal positivism,
which holds that whoever is in charge of the community determines the law, and natu-
ral law theory, which maintains that human law gets its justification from natural law.
2. What is the definition of law as put forth by Thomas Aquinas?
Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274) was a Catholic priest and one of the most important medi-
eval philosophers and theologians. He wrote many works on various subjects, including
the influential Summa Theologica. Aquinas put forth a definition of law that has been
used ever since by legal scholars:
"The law is an ordinance of reason for the common good made by him who has care of
the community and promulgated."
Ordinance of reason: Reasons are offered for its existence.
Common good: It benefits everyone without harming anyone; not for private gain.
Made by him who has care of the community: This refers to government on various levels:
local, state, and federal. It also refers to administrative agencies such as the IRS, EPA,
and HHS in the United States.
Promulgated: To promulgate means "to make known by open declaration; proclaim
formally or put into operation." Law has to be based on general principles that are well
known even if the details aren't.
3. What are some differences between legal positivism and natural law theory?
Legal positivism is the view that the law is no more and no less than what is posited by
those in authority. What the leaders in a region (community) say the law is, that's what
it is. Morality or higher principles are not necessary for the legitimacy of law. It is made
by the leaders currently in power and can be changed at any time. There is no appeal to
a higher authority outside the community.
Natural law theory holds that human law gets its justification from natural law. Many
would go on to say that natural law gets its authority from divine law and would site law
codes like the Ten Commandments as the foundation for a just legal system. Natural
law theory says that law is more than what is posited by those in authority. There are
principles behind it, such as fairness and equity, that aren't legislated; they just are.

4. Can you give some examples of laws that have been part of the European legal system

Post facto laws (also called ex post facto laws): These are laws passed after the fact, then
