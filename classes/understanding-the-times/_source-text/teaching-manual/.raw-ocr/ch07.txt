1. Can you summarize the postmodern worldview in a few words? [7.1]
Postmodernism is a reaction to the evils of modernism that sees truth claims as politi-
cal power plays rather than as statements of intrinsic reality. Truth is just a collection of
artifacts, a social construct; ultimate knowledge is inaccessible.
We ... [should] give up the correspondence theory of truth, and start treating moral
and scientific beliefs as tools for achieving greater human happiness, rather than as
representations of the intrinsic nature of reality. (Richard Rorty)

Modernism is based on empiricism —the idea that we can only know what our senses can

3. How does Postmodernism define truth? [7.2]
According to Postmodernism, truth is not objective, universal, or neutral. It is socially
"situated" or "embedded." Because of this "situatedness," we can't have unmediated,
direct, or immediate access to reality. We can't see any issue objectively because we are
limited by our experiences. Some Postmodernists deny that we ever have any direct
access to reality; we are only commenting on our own comments and thinking about
our own thinking.
To the Postmodernist, there's no such thing as capital "T" truth. Truth isn't something to
be found "out there." Truth is "socially constructed" through our interactions with one
another and filtered through our life experiences. Postmodernists claim that the idea
of objective truth is actually used to oppress others. All worldviews are biased in their
notions of reality and claims about truth.
4. What do Postmodernists have to say about the Abrahamic faiths? [7.2]
When it comes to the Abrahamic faiths (Judaism, Christianity, Islam), Postmodernists
reject their claims that humans can interact with reality and make truth claims from it.
Further, because Postmodernists don't believe truth can be known, the attempts by these
religions to impose truth is a means of oppression rather than fulfillment.

The history of Postmodernism can be traced through the lives and teachings of

Postmodernism's roots go back to the Greek school of the Sophists and the ideas of the

7. What did Friedrich Nietzsche contribute to Postmodernism? [7.3]
Friedrich Nietzsche has been called the father of Postmodernism. In the 1800s, this
German philosopher helped resurrect sophism, along with its insistence that objective
truth is unknowable. Nietzsche went from a childhood faith in Christ to becoming an
adamant atheist proclaiming, "God is dead."
Nietzsche wasn't saying God had once been alive and then died; he was saying that only
our belief in God kept him alive. Nietzsche thought that once we abandoned our belief
in God, all truth, meaning, and morality would die along with him. People needed to
accept that fact and come to grips with the implications of "killing God."

8.
What did Michel Foucault contribute to Postmodernism? [7.3]
Nietzsche influenced a generation of philosophers, among them Michel Foucault. His
youthful experiences with homosexuality, attempted suicide, and mental imbalance
shaped his intellectual development. He rejected absolute truth and sought to liberate
humanity from labeling, marginalization, and suppression.
Foucault believed modernism was as dead as Nietzsche's God. God was dead and mod-
ernism attempted to enthrone humanity in his place, but everything important was lost
along with God.
9. How does Postmodernism use language to discredit other worldviews? [7.4]
Postmodernism dismisses other worldviews by focusing on how they use language as a
tool of power and manipulation to create and control society. Postmodernist thinkers
like Ludwig Wittgenstein and J. L. Austin tried to show that there is no necessary rela-
tionship between a description and what's being described. In other words, a thought
about a Ferrari and the Ferrari itself are related only incidentally. Words are words and
things are things and they aren't intrinsically connected.
To the Postmodernist, language shapes the way we think and our approach to life. Some
philosophers in the early 1900s argued that we should build our philosophy around
language and relationships rather than around the nature of physical existence itself.
This movement was called structuralism.
10. What are structuralism and poststructuralism? What role do they play in
Postmodernism? [7.4]
Structuralism is an intellectual movement that believes human knowledge is not based
on an accurate understanding of reality but is the product of language developed over
time. Instead of focusing on scientific facts, structuralists look at how the way we use
language causes us to view facts differently based on our experiences. Language affects
our approach to life.

Many Postmodernists move beyond structuralism to poststructuralism. They think the

11. Why is deconstruction central to postmodern thinking? [74]
Deconstruction grows out of the Postmodernist belief that words can't accurately de-
scribe objects in the world; words and language have to be "deconstructed" to reveal their
underlying assumptions and ideologies. A deconstructionist believes that the reader's
interpretation is more important than the author's intent.

One type of language Postmodernists want to deconstruct is metanarratives, the unifying
stories about the world that supposedly explain everything. Metanarratives are paradigms
or worldviews that are so powerful that those who accept them can't see reality any other
way. Understanding metanarratives is a key to freeing ourselves from their grip.
To the philosopher Jean-Francis Lyotard, broad explanations are arrogant because our
experiences are so varied. People's experiences are what's important, not general state-
ments about the world. That's why he described Postmodernism as "incredulity toward
metanarratives."
12. What do Postmodernists mean by the term anti-realism? [7.4]
Anti-realism is a term used to describe the denial of the existence or accessibility of an
objective reality. Whatever is "real" cannot be known by us. Everyone has his or her own
interpretations of reality based on subjective experiences and cultural filters. We each
have our own small "t" truth, but can't share an objective truth that's true for all. That's
what's meant by the phrase, "That may be true for you, but not for me."
13. Is it accurate to call Postmodernism a worldview since it's more of a critique of other
worldviews than a positive pattern of ideas, beliefs, convictions, and habits of its
own? [7.5]
Postmodernism doesn't make truth claims of its own so much as analyze and deconstruct
the truth claims of other worldviews. Nevertheless, it does have a set of ideas, beliefs, con-
victions, and habits; so it is a worldview. It has discernable patterns of thought in each of
the ten disciplines were examining in this book that lead to certain beliefs and behaviors.
Postmodernists may deny having a worldview, but this is inconsistent because they do
have ideas and opinions about how the world should work. Whenever they speak out
on political or societal issues like government debt or traditional marriage, they're being
hypocritical in assuming that their particular viewpoint should influence the behavior
of others.
14. Does Postmodernism accept scientific explanations of reality? [7.6]
No. Postmodernism rejects all metanarratives, including those offered by science and
mathematics. Postmodernist thinkers see science as not so much the study and record-
ing of objective facts as the interpreting of so-called facts to fit the assumptions of the
scientists. Scientists can't tell us what's real, only what they think is real, which is why
scientific theories are constantly proven wrong and replaced by new theories.
Postmodernists don't think theories go wrong because they're flawed, but because true
theories are impossible. Reality is not accessible to us. When we try to access it, we're just
interpreting it. Our efforts to define truth are just interpretations of our interpretations.

18. What does Postmodernism say is wrong with us? (7.7]
The reason we even sense there is something wrong with us is because we believe in meta-
narratives such as Christianity. There are no universal truths that govern our lives, no God
to whom we are accountable, no absolute moral code to which we must adhere. Postmod-
ernists insist that to be fully human, we need to forget about God and eternity altogether.
Because no objective morality exists, there is no standard to hold up against our behavior
to decide whether it's right or not. If we consider something wrong, it's only because we
believe it to be in our minds.
19. What does Postmodernism say about how we should live? [7.7]
Leading Postmodernists advocate abandoning the search for objective truth in favor
of pragmatism: the idea that truth is not "out there" to be discerned, but has to be to
developed from own ideas about life based on our own experiences. Pragmatists don't
believe that statements of truth and metanarratives reflect reality.
Since there is no objective truth, Postmodernists like Richard Rorty suggests using
dialogue to reach some type of agreement on small "" truth in order to get along with
others: "[The] hope of agreement is never lost as long as the conversation lasts?"
20. How does Postmodernism view Christianity? [7.8]
Postmodernism views Christianity as a metanarrative that does more harm than good.
Christianity's insistence on absolute biblical truths like the existence of God, the sinfulness
of humankind, and the need for redemption in Christ, leads to violence and oppression.
Some Postmodernists believe the Christian metanarrative is responsible for most of the
evils in the world. The idea of God and eternity need to be removed from our thinking
so that we can move forward into the next evolution of humankind.
The very idea of deconstruction seems to suggest that the idea of God ought to be
eliminated from Western culture as a power play on the part of churches and others
with vested interests in its survival. (Alister McGrath)
Postmodernists and Christians would agree on the following points:
• Culture plays a role in shaping us.
• Experiences contribute to who we are.
• Language can be used to manipulate people.
But they would disagree on the following points:
• we can judge between competing ideas;
• we can perceive reality as it actually is;
• all language is manipulative.

There is a hybrid worldview of Christian Postmodernism, which believes that since
we are fallen and limited, we shouldn't make exclusive truth claims. This is called the

21. How does Postmodernism view Islam? [7.8]
Islam is another metanarrative that Postmodernists reject, although they're interested
in how Islamic ideas developed and propagated over time. Some Muslim philosophers
have developed postmodern-style assumptions that ultimate reality can't be reduced to

22. How does Postmodernism view New Spirituality? [7.8]
Postmodernists reject the New Spiritualists' claims that consciousness is the ground of
reality, and that all life is merging toward unity. But Postmodernists would agree with
some aspects of New Spirituality, like what Buddhists call "the Middle-way," which teaches
that things do not exist independently of our apprehending them. The Dalai Lama said,
"Emptiness is the ultimate truth."
23. How does Postmodernism view Secularism? [7.8]
Secularism dismisses Postmodernism as nihilistic in its claim that nothing can be known
or communicated. Postmodernism rejects the metanarrative of Secularism that says that
human reasoning and scientific knowledge can lead to the discovery of objective truth.
[Science offers] reasonably objective standards for judging its truth claims. Science
has become a universal language, speaking to all men and women no matter what
their cultural backgrounds. (Paul Kurtz)
24. How does Postmodernism view Marxism? [7.8]
Most of mainstream Postmodernists come from the Marxist atheistic tradition. But
the failure of Marxism to bring about utopia and its slaughter of millions has led many
Postmodernists to reject the Marxist metanarrative.
Postmodernists saw the power metanarratives like Marxism have over society. When
people believed a particular worldview was true, they acted on their beliefs and were
willing to do just about anything, including killing their fellow man. This is one reason
Postmodernism became a movement against metanarratives claiming to possess truth
about the world.

25. Can you briefly summarize some of the flaws in Postmodernism? [7.9]
Postmodernism takes some valid concerns too far. We can acknowledge differences
between people groups, but we can still communicate with those whose life experiences
and cultural backgrounds are different from ours. We are shaped by our cultures, but
this doesn't prevent us from seeing reality as it actually is.
Postmodernism ignores the power struggles created by language. If each community,
through its use of language, determines what is true, then who decides between rival
communities? Since no group can claim to know what is objectively "right," there's no
basis of truth to draw on in opposing a person like Hitler or a group like the Nazis.
Postmodernism is self-contradictory. The claim, "there is no absolute truth" is itself
claiming to be absolute truth. Even in their claims that truth does not exist, Postmodern-
sts are claiming that their ideas are truer than the ideas that say there is actually truth.
Postmodernism's view of personhood is disturbing. To say that human identity is
unstable, fragmented, or "in process" is to ignore all the evidence that we all share com-
mon needs and desires. The idea of the "self" as nothing more than a collage of social
constructions is at odds with what we know of others and ourselves.
Postmodernism leads to disastrous results. Misunderstanding the relationship between
words and meaning is dangerous. Confucius said,
"When words lose their meanings,
people lose their freedom." But the slaughter of millions in the twentieth century also
shows that when words lose their meaning, people lose their lives.

THE PARABLE OF THE MAD MAN
by Friedrich Nietzsche
Have you not heard of that madman who lit a lantern in the bright morning hours,
ran to the market place, and cried incessantly: "I seek God! I seek God!"-As many
of those who did not believe in God were standing around just then, he provoked
much laughter. Has he got lost? asked one. Did he lose his way like a child? asked
another. Or is he hiding? Is he afraid of us? Has he gone on a voyage? emigrated?—
Thus they yelled and laughed.
The madman jumped into their midst and pierced them with his eyes. "Whither
is God?" he cried; "I will tell you. We have killed him—you and I. All of us are his
murderers. But how did we do this? How could we drink up the sea? Who gave us the
sponge to wipe away the entire horizon? What were we doing when we unchained
this earth from its sun? Whither is it moving now? Whither are we moving? Away
from all suns? Are we not plunging continually? Backward, sideward, forward, in all
directions? Is there still any up or down? Are we not straying, as through an infinite
nothing? Do we not feel the breath of empty space? Has it not become colder? Is not
night continually closing in on us? Do we not need to light lanterns in the morning?
Do we hear nothing as yet of the noise of the gravediggers who are burying God? Do
we smell nothing as yet of the divine decomposition? Gods, too, decompose. God
is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him.
"How shall we comfort ourselves, the murderers of all murderers? What was holiest
and mightiest of all that the world has yet owned has bled to death under our knives:
who will wipe this blood off us? What water is there for us to clean ourselves? What
festivals of atonement, what sacred games shall we have to invent? Is not the greatness
of this deed too great for us? Must we ourselves not become gods simply to appear
worthy of it? There has never been a greater deed; and whoever is born after us-for
the sake of this deed he will belong to a higher history than all history hitherto."
Here the madman fell silent and looked again at his listeners; and they, too, were
silent and stared at him in astonishment. At last he threw his lantern on the ground,
and it broke into pieces and went out. "I have come too early," he said then; "my
time is not yet. This tremendous event is still on its way, still wandering; it has not
yet reached the ears of men.
Lightning and thunder require time; the light of the stars requires time; deeds, though
done, still require time to be seen and heard. This deed is still more distant from
them than most distant stars-and yet they have done it themselves.
It has been related further that on the same day the madman forced his way into
several churches and there struck up his requiem aeternam deo. Led out and called
to account, he is said always to have replied nothing but: "What after all are these
churches now if they are not the tombs and sepulchers of God?"

"THE PARABLE OF THE MADMAN" DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What do you know about Friedrich Nietzsche, the author of this parable? Do you
have a positive or negative opinion of him?
Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche was a German philosopher whose writings have had a
profound influence on the modern world. He held the Chair of Classical Philology at
the University of Basel in 1869 and resigned in 1879 at the age of twenty-four due to
health problems. He wrote many of his most influential works in the following decade,
including Thus Spoke Zarathustra (1883), Beyond Good and Evil (1886), and On the Ge-
nealogy of Morality (1887). At age forty-four, he suffered a complete loss of his mental
faculties and died in 1900.
While many academics respect Nietzsche for his thoughts and influence, others point
to how his thinking was used to support fascism, Nazism, and communism, to the great
harm of countless millions. Christians have rightly seen Nietzsche as an enemy of God,
the gospel, and the Christian worldview.
2. Why do you think Nietzsche chose a madman as the protagonist of his parable?
It's hard to know for sure, but here are a few possibilities:
• Nietzsche could have chosen a madman because the message he spoke would never
come from the lips of a sane person. It was too outlandish and heretical. And if he
wasn't thought of as a madman before, he soon would be.
• Nietzsche could have used a madman so there was nothing positive behind the
message. It stood on its own, not benefiting from the good character or reputation
of the messenger.
• The madman could represent what Nietzsche thought many people actually believed
in their hearts but were too afraid to speak. He was giving voice to their inner fears.
3. What charge did the madman level at the people? How did he prove their guilt?
The madman charged the people with killing God. As proof, he pointed out that they had
removed God from nature. They had rejected his laws and so lived without direction.
They had rejected his light and so lived in darkness.
The fact that the universe was now empty and cold meant God was no longer there. God
didn't leave on his own; he was murdered. And the madman couldn't believe that these
murderers didn't yet realize what they had done!

4. Why does the madman say his message wasn't received by the people?
Because he spoke too early and the truth of what he said was not yet apparent:
"I have come too early, he said then; "my time is not yet. This tremendous event
is still on its way, still wandering; it has not yet reached the ears of men. Lightning
and thunder require time; the light of the stars requires time; deeds, though done,
still require time to be seen and heard. This deed is still more distant from them
than most distant stars-and yet they have done it themselves.
Nietzsche was accusing society of having killed God but of not yet seeing the fruits of
his murder. Large shifts in thinking as represented by the theory of evolution and textual
criticism of the Bible were putting nails in God's coffin, and the average person didn't
realize it. Nor did people grasp the devastating impact this would have once it became
common knowledge.
And indeed, the atheistic postmodernism that blossomed in the twentieth century pro-
duced the most destructive societies of all time, resulting in untold suffering and the
deaths of millions.
What do you think the madman meant by his comment: "What after all are these
churches now if they are not the tombs and sepulchers of God?"
The madman saw the churches as tombs because there was no living God inside. God
must have been alive and active at some point for the churches to have been built in the
first place, but now they were empty. Nothing was going on in them that showed God's
presence and power in the face of philosophical and scientific attacks.
It may not have escaped Nietzsche that all Catholic churches and many Orthodox ones
have crucifixes inside portraying a dying Christ on the cross.

"THE POSTMODERN WORLDVIEW" DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What problem does Postmodernism see with the world?
Every worldview recognizes what it thinks is wrong with the world. The key problem
that postmodernists see is the search for truth. People believe they know the truth and
they proclaim it in a way the makes others uncomfortable.
Postmodernists believe we can't know anything about reality and anyone who says we
can know is part of the problem. The search for truth is the problem because we can't
know what's real. This skepticism goes back to Greek sophists like Gorgias, who taught
the following:
• Nothing exists.
• Even if something exists, nothing can be known about it.
• Even if something can be known about it, knowledge about it can't be communi-
cated to others.
• Even if it can be communicated, it cannot be understood.
2. How did we get from premodern to modern to postmodern?
The shift from premodern to modern took place at the Enlightenment. Pre-moderns
believed in the supernatural, trusted, biblical revelation and the authority of the church.
Moderns turned to skepticism, trusted in scientific investigation and technological
progress and the authority of human reason. Moderns believed progress was inevitable
and would solve all our problems.
But the twentieth century proved modernism to be a false hope. More people were killed
by governments than in all previous centuries combined. Postmodernists say this is where
religion and science took us. They want to get beyond religion and science.
3. What are the three key principles of postmodernism?
Poststructuralism: We cannot know the world directly. We can only know it as we
interpret it. Our talk about the objects in the world isn't really about those objects; it's
really about ourselves.
Deconstructionism: The origin of the text is not the important thing. By allowing the
reader to invent new meanings, the text is freed from the tyranny of the author's single
intended meaning.
Subjectivity: Broad explanations deserve suspicion because peoples experiences are so
varied. Our experiences, not general statements about the world, are what we should
pay attention to.

"THE POSTMODERN WORLDVIEW" DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
What are some appropriate responses to postmodernism?
Postmodernism highlights important concerns but takes them too far. We can recognize
that our experiences are a large part of who we are and yet make reasonable judgments
about competing viewpoints.
Postmodernism is blind to the true power struggle language creates. If each community,
through its use of language, determines what is true, then who decides between rival
communities?
Postmodernists contradict themselves. For example: "You aren't in the position to know
what is true for me." You might respond: "If that's the case, why are you imposing your
concept of truth on me?"
Postmodernism's view of personhood is unsettling. The idea of the "self" as nothing more
than a collage of social constructions is at odds with what we know of ourselves and others.
Postmodernism, when applied, can lead to grotesque results. For example: Supreme Court,
Roe v. Wade. Because the judges saw the constitution as an open document, they came up
with new interpretations of the original text, which has resulted in the deaths of millions.
5. What does Postmodernism think of the soul?
Nothing. The idea of a "soul" or "self" is nothing more than a collage of social construc-
tions. We don't have a self; we have multiple selves because that's what our experiences
develop.
But this is at odds with what we know of ourselves and others. Is it possible we act as if
human nature exists because it actually does?
