1. What is a worldview and what questions does it answer? [18.2]
A worldview is a pattern of ideas, beliefs, convictions, and habits that help us make
sense of God, the world, and our relationship to both. A worldview is like a mental map
that tells us how to navigate the world by recognizing the rules and patterns that exist.
A worldview helps us know where we need to go in life and how best to get there from
where we are.
A worldview answers the big questions such as:
• Who is God?
• Where did we come from?
• What does it mean to be human?
• How do we determine right from wrong?
• What happens when we die?
2. What are the six worldviews studied in this book? [18.2]
The six major worldviews covered in this book are
• Islam
• Secularism
• Marxism
• New Spirituality
• Postmodernism
• Christianity
3. What are some of the key distinctives of Islam? [18.3]
Nearly one-fourth of the world's population is Muslim. Founded by Muhammad, Islam
teaches that God, "Allah" in Arabic, created everything and set the universe in order.
Islam's distinctives include the following:
• Islam means "submission." A Muslim is "one who submits" to Allah and the prin-
ciples of Islam as embodied in the ummah, the worldwide community of Muslims.
• Muslims believe the Quran is the only authoritative scripture preserved without
error. It was dictated word for word to the prophet Muhammad through the angel
Jibril (Gabriel).
• Muslims believe every human is born a Muslim. Those not currently committed
to Islam are in rebellion to their nature, but in the end everyone will submit to
Allah. To become a Muslim does not require redemption but adherence to the
five pillars of Islam.
• Muhammad is the final and best prophet. His message supersedes the message of
Jesus, and therefore Islam supersedes Christianity.

4. What are the three main groups in modern Islam? [18.3]
The term Muslim is used to describe those who have grown up in Islam and try to follow
its practices in all areas of life.
Islamists refers to a separate group that embraces the full application of shariah law,

5. What are some of the key distinctives of Secularism? [18.4]
Somewhere around 15 percent of Americans are Secularists. Secularists are focused on
the here and now, with no thought of God or the world to come. Their key distinctives
include the following:
• Materialism: Only the physical world exists. If materialism is true, concerns about
non-material things like God, spirituality, or an afterlife are misguided and even
dangerous.
• Naturalism: Everything that exists can be explained through natural processes.
Our present state is best explained by evolution that began with the first self-
replicating molecule.
• Atheism: God does not exist, and belief in God keeps people unenlightened and
slows human progress. Belief in a world beyond this one is especially harmful.
Secularism believes that religion should not have an influence on education and govern-
ment. They have deep faith, but it's in the scientific method, not the divine. Their faith is
in human ability to solve the world's problem and create a perfect society.
What are some of the key distinctives of Marxism? [18.5]
As the followers of Karl Marx, Marists aim to revolt against capitalism and replace it
with a utopian society in which the state and economic classes no longer exist and all
property is publicly shared. One in five people in the world live under Marxist regimes,
which are anything but utopias. Marxism's key beliefs include the following:
Dialectical materialism: The belief that only the material world exists and that class
struggles are the mechanism behind social and economic progress. The way people view
the world and act on it (the thesis) spawns a reaction to change how things are done (the
antithesis). This dialectic process and materialistic philosophy will destroy capitalism
and usher in communism—an outcome that's historically inevitable.
Economic determinism: Economics ultimately determines the entire course of history.
History can be explained by the economic struggle between the bourgeoisie (property

7. What are some of the key distinctives of New Spirituality? [18.6]
New Spirituality, also known as, the New Age movement, neo-paganism, or new con-
sciousness, is committed to a radically un-Christian view of the world. Its influences
range from Hinduism and Buddhism to pop psychology. New Spirituality's key beliefs
include the following:
• Everything is consciousness: Consciousness is the divine interconnected essence of
reality. It is energy we can connect to and use. Everything is energy, even thoughts.
Good thoughts produce good energy and bad thoughts inhibit it.
• Every person is god: God is not a separate person with identifiable attributes but
rather an eternal, impersonal force of which we are a part. Every person is god.
The belief that everything is god is called pantheism.
• The purpose of life is overcoming "self": Success comes from being free from
the self, from any conception of ego. People gain temporary freedom from the
self through practices such as yoga and meditation. Final freedom comes through
reincarnation —a series of rebirths through which one achieves enlightenment.
Forgoing the self and tapping into higher consciousness leads to true goodness.
You do not become good by trying to be good, but by finding the goodness that is
already within you, and allowing that goodness to emerge. But it can only emerge
if something fundamental changes in your state of consciousness. (Eckhart Tolle)
8. What are some of the key distinctives of Postmodernism? [18.7]
Postmodernism is a reaction against modernism, (the period from the 1700s through
the 1900s). It believes any claims to objective understanding are arrogant and oppressive.
Since worldviews are held by people situated in particular cultures, none has a God's-eye
view of reality. Postmodernism's key beliefs include:
• Poststructuralism: Poststructuralism says communication is not about things
but about the motivation of those involved in the conversation. It focuses on how
language structures our relationships and interpretations of reality. Language is a
tool of power and manipulation. When we think we are describing reality, we are
only describing our particular way of seeing the world.
• Deconstruction: All texts need to be "deconstructed" to reveal their underlying
assumptions and ideologies. Readers should focus on their own thoughts rather
than on the meaning the author intended to communicate. The reader's interpreta-
tion is more important than the author's intent.

• Subjectivity: "Metanarratives" are grand stories of reality that try to explain every-

9. What are some of the key distinctives of Christianity? [18.8]
Almost one-third of the world's population embraces some form of Christianity. This
worldview posits the existence of God as a personal, relational, creative being who has
made himself known through nature and Scripture. God is not a force to be used but a
person to be known. Christianity's key beliefs include the following:
• God is the designer: Pure energy does not create; only persons create. Christian-
ity says a creator God, not a sequence of random accidents, is responsible for an
orderly, beautiful, meaningful cosmos.
• We are made in God's image and yet fallen: The Bible teaches we are persons
created with a soul composed of will, intellect, and emotions. Its description of
the fall into sin explains why there is hate in our hearts (Jer. 17:9), and why the
entire universe "groans" (Rom. 8:20-22).
• We may experience redemption: Jesus Christ, being fully God and fully man,
became flesh and offered humanity a way out of its sinful state of rebellion (Rom.
5:6-8). By acknowledging Jesus as Lord (Rom. 10:9), we can be born again as
children of God (1 Pet. 1:23).
• We have purpose: After spiritual rebirth, our understanding of God develops as
we love him with our hearts, souls, minds, and strength (Mark 12:30). The apostle
Paul told believers to "renew their minds" so they could understand God's will
(Rom. 12:1-2).
• Reality is knowable: God's revelation in nature and Scripture enables us to know
him as creator, redeemer, and lawgiver. We can discern from this revelation the
difference between right and wrong and live accordingly. This knowledge is in the
hearts of all (Rom. 2:15).

11. How is Christianity different from other worldviews? [18.9]
Christianity's doctrine of humans having inherent value as God's image-bearers is unique.
It has led to the development of science, education, modern medicine, and many other
good things. Christians elevated the status of women, built hospitals and schools, insti-
tuted ministries to the poor, and successfully opposed slavery.
The Christian doctrine of the fall, also unique among worldviews, identifies the source of

of humankind has led to so much good, while worldviews proclaiming the goodness of

Christianity is more than a set of commands and inspirational sayings. It's the story of a
personal God against whom his image-bearers rebelled. At great cost, God then bought
back the rebels to set things right. It's the one story that makes sense of all the others.
12. How do the different worldviews view theology? [18.10]
Theology is the study of God and seeks to answer the questions, "Is there a God?" and
"How does God relate to the world?"
Islam believes in one god who created the world, and who will one day hold humans
to account. Islam rejects the doctrine of the Trinity and the idea that Jesus Christ was
God in the flesh.
Secularism rejects all conceptions of the supernatural and the spiritual. It holds that God
is either nonexistent or irrelevant. Nothing is higher than humankind.
Marxism is aggressively atheistic. God does not, cannot, and must not exist. God is an
impediment to methods and aims of Marxism.
Postmodernism holds that true knowledge about God is impossible. Any knowledge
we claim to have about God is socially constructed.
New Spirituality believes god is the spiritual energy of which the universe is composed.
There is no difference in essence between the creator and the creation.

13. How does Christianity answer the two main questions of theology? [18.10]
Christianity proclaims the existence of a personal, triune God who desires to be in re-

14. How do the different worldviews view philosophy? [18.11]
Philosophy (love of wisdom) focuses on three questions:
• What is ultimately real? (metaphysics)
• What does it mean to know? (epistemology)
• With what do we contemplate these questions? (mind/body problem)
Islam affirms the natural and the supernatural, the material and the immaterial. It affirms
the universe was created by God, who sustains it and reveals himself in it. To Muslims,
the only way to know reality is to study the Quran and the example of Muhammad.
Secularism is materialistic and naturalistic. The material world is all that exists, and
all phenomena have a natural explanation. The only way to understand reality is the
scientific method.
Marxism is also materialistic and naturalistic. For the dialectical materialist, everything
comes from matter, even what we consider to be our minds. How do we know what we
know? Science.
New Spirituality believes ultimate reality is consciousness. Only spirit exists, and we
can know it by getting in touch with our "higher selves."
Postmodernism says we have no capacity to transcend our personal experiences to get
a God's-eye view of reality. Our words only describe our interpretation of reality.

16. How do the different worldviews view ethics? [18.12]
Ethics-the study of right and wrong-answers two important questions: "How should
we live?" and "How should we think through what life is about?" The answers to these
questions shine a light on other important questions.
Islam sees no division of ethics and law. Muslims derive their ethics from the Quran
and the Hadith, which describes the actions of Muhammad that Muslims consider to
be exemplary.
Secularism holds that "No god wrote the laws of good behavior into the cosmos. Nature
has no concern for good or bad, right or wrong" (Simon Blackburn).
Marxism sees everything moving toward the elimination of social and economic classes.
Anything promoting this advance is morally right; anything hindering it is morally wrong.
This is called proletariat morality.
New Spirituality sees ethics as happening inside a person. Internalized values allow us
to seek higher consciousness. And yet we are held accountable through the law of karma,
the belief that good is returned to those who do good and evil to those who do evil.
Postmodernism rejects the notion of universal moral norms based on reason or revela-
tion. "Universal moral principles must be eradicated and reverence for individual and
cultural uniqueness inculcated" (Adam Phillips).
17. How does Christianity answer the two main questions of ethics? [18.12]
(1) How should we live? (2) If everyone lived this way, would it be good for all?
Christianity says that what is morally true can be seen by those with an undistorted
ability to perceive. Ethics is simply an expansion of a generally revealed moral order. All
things good, true, and beautiful are based on God's character as revealed in nature and
Scripture. The task of Christian ethics is to determine what conforms to God's character
and what does not.
Because God is everywhere, an ethical system based on his character will reside in
every soul, across all cultures, at all times. Even among the diverse world cultures there
seems to be a common moral law. Christianity sees this as evidence of a universal law
and universal lawgiver.

18. How do the different worldviews view biology? [18.13]
Biology is the study of life. The most basic question of biology is "How did life come into
existence?" The question of first origin requires contemplation of God, even for scientists.
Islam believes in a divine creator. He is not nature; he is above and beyond his creation
(transcendent). Muslims believe that God's creation is perfect. The Quran describes the
universe as finely ordered.
Secularism believes "Man is the result of a purposeless and natural process that did not
have him in mind. He was not planned" (George Gaylord Simpson).
Marxism says only matter exists and Darwin's theory justified this view. If biological
life evolves toward greater complexity and hardier species, then sociological evolution
could provide a better state.
New Spirituality focuses on humanity's evolution toward higher consciousness, which
will result in collective consciousness—a state of pure energy and divine unity with the
cosmos.
Postmodernism says evolution is just a grand story. It did not have us in mind. Even
when we say "us," we're using words to interpret what we see, not describing an actual
essence such as humanness.
19. How does Christianity answer the main question of biology? [18.13]
Christianity believes God created humankind. Life only comes from pre-existing life.
Further, it is unlikely that random chance processes could produce anything recognizable
as intelligent. Language and information-rich systems always come from intelligence,
and life itself is built upon a language-based code in our DNA.
For Christian biologists, the world is bright and resonant in light of God's existence. As
a piece of art suggests an artist, the orderly and complex universe suggests a designer.
This insight makes true discovery possible and meaningful.

Psychology (literally "study of the soul") is the study of who and what we are and how we
develop as human beings. Psychology asks whether there is any reality beyond the mate-
rial world, and whether we are responsible to anything higher than our own sense of self.
Islam believes we do not find healing and peace by becoming more like our creator or
by pursuing our own good or by meditation, but by submission to Allah.
Secularism assumes the idea of a personal God is a myth. We are purely physical beings
without souls. Everything must be explained by material causes.

22. How do the different worldviews view sociology? [18.15]

23. How does Christianity understand sociology? [18.15]
Christianity believes God has ordained multiple institutions to play vital roles in the
collective caretaking of his creation. Christian sociology recognizes the value of persons,
as well as the balance between persons and society, and the valuable roles of the family,
government, and church. The family is the central institution of society, and society

24. How do the different worldviews view law? [18.16]
The study of law revolves around the question, "What constitutes orderly and just gov-
ernance?" There are two aspects to law. Order: nothing happens in our communities
without laws establishing order. Justice: society must have laws to protect rights and
punish wrongs.
Islam teaches that Allah reveals only his law, not himself. This law-shariah—is a timeless
manifestation of the will of God, subject neither to history nor circumstance.
Secularism rejects human "fallenness" and believes in "legal positivism," which holds
that laws are created by human authorities and that there is no connection between law
and morality.
Marxism sees laws as based on the will of the bourgeoisie. The proletariat must overthrow
such laws by any means necessary, including violence.
New Spirituality focuses on inner development. No law exists except that which is on
our hearts. Anything hindering our search for higher consciousness is bad.
Postmodernism rejects anything coming from traditional power structures like Chris-
tianity. Justice isn't based on objective standards; rather, it's the process of uncovering
various forms of oppression.

skeletal framework of natural law and tells us what God considers moral and lawful.
