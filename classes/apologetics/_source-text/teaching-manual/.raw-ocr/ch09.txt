OVERVIEW
Religious views influence relationships,
culture, and worldviews. A religion is a
system of beliefs, worship, or conduct that
prescribes certain responses to the divine,
sacred, or supernatural. A worldview
is a pattern of ideas. All human beings
worship something. The term cult refers
to a divergent group within a larger
religion, a new religious movement, or an
alternative religion. Major non-Christian
world religions include Judaism, Islam,
Hinduism, and Buddhism. Other religious
systems include Confucianism, Taoism,
Shinto, Sikhism, Jainism, the Baha'i faith,
folk religions, and pagan and neo-pagan
religions. Common religious cults include
the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day
Saints (LDS), Jehovah's Witnesses, Christian
Science, Scientology, Eastern religious
movements, the New Age movement, and
various others.
Religious pluralism involves many
different religions existing side by side in
society and comes in two forms, both of
which recognize the need for tolerance. One
approach emphasizes treating others with
respect despite differences, and the other
emphasizes accepting all religions as true.
Many claim that all religions have part of
the truth and either stem from the same
source or lead to the same end. In this, they
claim a God's-eye view of religious truth.
They also contradict Jesus' claim to be the
only way to know the Father.
Despite our disagreements with the
tenets of other religions, Christians can
engage in healthy interreligious dialogue by
talking to individual people, acknowledging
truth wherever it is found, and being
charitable. Some adherents of a faith may be
devout, and others only nominal. In either
case, healthy dialogue takes commitment,
careful listening, and patience. Ultimately,
healthy dialogue helps us fulfill our calling
to love people and share the gospel.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Describe two approaches to religious pluralism. [9.3]
The first approach does not consider all religions to be true, yet it
advocates tolerance by respecting everyone's right to choose their
religion and disagree civilly. The second approach views all religions
as subjectively true, claims all religions lead to God, and sees
criticism of others' views as intolerant.
2. How does the folktale of the elephant suggest that all religions
are equal? [9.3]
This folktale suggests that just as blind men can touch a small
portion of an elephant but cannot see the whole thing, humans
cannot see the entire truth about the divine. In this view, world
religions have a portion of the truth. Therefore, all religions are
partially true, but no religion can be true for everyone.
3. What are some problems with the elephant parable and John
Hick's notion of the Real? [9.4]
Possible answers: Both Hick and the elephant parable assume they
have a God's-eye view of the truth. If no religion can see the whole
truth, how is it that Hick and the elephant parable can? If the Real is
ultimately unknowable, as Hick says, he cannot know its attributes
or that it is the source of all the world's religions.
4. People often think it is humble and all-inclusive to say that no
religion has the whole truth. Explain whether this assessment is
correct. [9.4]
Possible answer: While it sounds humble and welcoming to say no
one religion has the whole truth, the person who says this is making
a religious claim that he or she believes is true for everyone. That's
an exclusive position. The person is really saying only he or she
understands the whole truth, and everyone else is merely grasping at
it—not really a humble position.
5. Why is it ultimately disrespectful to say that all religions are
the same? [9.4]
To claim that all religions are the same, you must ignore the many
differences and conflicting beliefs among religions. Instead of taking
time to understand the differences among religious people, you have
effectively dismissed all religious claims.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. List one similarity and one difference between Judaism and
Christianity. [9.5]
Jews and Christians believe in the same God who revealed himself in
the Old Testament. They both believe that justice, righteousness, and
compassion are important. Christians, however, believe that Jesus is
the Messiah, and it is only through him that we can be saved.
2. List one similarity and one difference between Islam and
Christianity. [9.5]
Both Muslims and Christians believe in one God who is utterly
holy. Both acknowledge several of the same prophets and emphasize
ethical conduct. Muslims believe that good deeds ultimately secure
salvation, whereas Christians believe salvation is by grace through
Christ alone. Muslims also reject the Christian belief that Jesus
is God.
3. According to Buddhism and Hinduism, how do karma and
reincarnation work, and what is the goal of these beliefs? [9.5]
According to these beliefs, people will experience positive effects
for every good deed accomplished and negative effects for every
bad deed, either in this life or the next. Those who do not achieve
enlightenment or pay off the negative effects of karma before
death will be reborn or reincarnated in a new form to continue the
journey toward enlightenment. The goal is to escape the cycle of
reincarnation and reconnect with the divine unity of the cosmos.
4. What are three unifying themes among most folk religions?
[9.6]
Folk religions usually do not have holy books. Knowledge and
tradition are passed down verbally from generation to generation.
They emphasize the ancestral homeland as the place of identity
formation. These religions see a deep connectedness between all
life—humans, the natural world, and the unknowable divine power.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How does Latter-Day Saints' theology differ from Christian
theology? 19.7]
Both Christians and Latter-Day Saints have great reverence for Jesus.
However, LDS do not believe that Jesus and God are one. They
teach Jesus and Satan are brothers, and all humans can achieve a
state of divinity like Jesus did. Also, unlike Christians, Latter-Day
Saints do not believe that we are saved by trusting in Jesus alone.
LDS must also be baptized by the LDS church, do good works, and
follow LDS teachings.
2. Why do you think there are so many religions today, with new
religious groups constantly arising? (9.7]
Possible answers: Increasing religious pluralism and the availability
of online religious information allow people to explore a wider
array of religious beliefs and practices than ever before. Some who
are dissatisfied with traditional structures yet still hunger for the
supernatural may seek to form their own personalized groups. They
may borrow aspects from various religious traditions, taking only
what they appreciate and leaving what they dislike.
3. Why is it important not to assume you already understand
a religious person's beliefs, even if you have studied his or her
religion? [9.8]
Every religion has adherents who differ widely in their commitment
level and opinions on doctrinal interpretation. Some people take
their faith very seriously and seek to conform to its ethical teachings.
Others may adhere to their religion as a cultural identity but do
not fully practice it. Some may blend their beliefs with local folk
religions or take an extremist interpretation of their religion. Asking
questions can help pinpoint an individual's perspective.
4. What does it mean to acknowledge truth wherever it is found?
[9.8]
To acknowledge truth wherever it is found means to recognize
points of agreement. God can make his truth known anywhere, and
his common grace is available to all. So, any person may understand
some elements of the truth, and we may find some aspects of truth
in many religions. This does not mean that all religions are true or
lead to God. Christianity holds that knowledge of Jesus Christ is
essential for salvation and a full understanding of the truth.
5. Why do you think it can be so difficult to understand what
other people believe? [9.8]
Possible answer: Everyone has a unique personality, story, and set of
experiences, all of which play a role in shaping their beliefs. Entering
another's experience or story requires intentionality and patience.
This is why sharing the gospel often begins with listening and asking
questions to understand the other person's beliefs and experiences.

RELIGIONS IN THE HOT SEAT
OBJECTIVE:
Students will identify world religions based on their
characteristics.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: student textbook
• Time required: 20-25 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Have one student sit facing the class in the "hot
seat." Write the name of one cult or religion from
Chapter 9.5-9.7 on the whiteboard behind the
student.
2. Explain that the student in the hot seat will
guess the name of the cult or religion based on
clues given by the rest of the class. If needed,
the student textbook may be referenced by
students giving clues. Direct students in the
class to raise their hands when ready to give a
clue. Have the student in the hot seat call on up
to three students for clues. After three clues, the
student in the hot seat will guess the name of
the religion. If the answer is correct, the student
in the hot seat and the three students who gave
the clues may all record one point on the score
tracker in their student manual.
3. Have a different student sit in the hot seat and
repeat the process. Continue until all religions
have been guessed. Have students add up their
points. Recognize the student with the most
points.

Activity
WORLD RELIGIONS JUMBLES
OBJECTIVE:
Students will identify and discuss true statements about
world religions.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: TR 9.A, timer, and pen
• Time required: 40-45 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Divide the class into groups of three or four students.
Distribute a blank piece of paper and a copy of TR 9.A
World Religions Jumbles to each team. Direct students to
place the blank piece of paper on top of TR 9.A until the
activity begins. Have each team choose a scribe to write
their answers on the blank paper during the activity.
2. Explain that teams will have four minutes to unscramble
a set of three sentences where the words are not in
correct sentence order. Each disorganized sentence
is called a word jumble. In each set, direct students to
analyze the words in the first sentence and reorganize
the words to form a meaningful sentence. Note the
order of words in some sentences may vary in student
answers which is acceptable if the words still form a
meaningful and accurate sentence. Inform students that
it may be necessary to insert a comma or commas in
some sentences. Students will repeat the process with
the second and third word jumbles in the set. Then, have
students discuss and confirm that each sentence is a true
statement about world religions.
3. Put four minutes on the timer. Direct groups to uncover
Set 1 on TR 9.A and begin unscrambling the word jumbles
in that set only. After four minutes, have students put
down their pens. Ask for volunteers from each group to
read out their answers. Confirm the correct answers from
the answer key and have students check their sentences
for accuracy. Scribes may record one point for each
correct sentence in that set in the Score Tracker in the
student manual.
4. Repeat the process for all eight sets.
5. Have groups calculate their final scores. Check the
answers of the group with the highest score to ensure
their unscrambled sentences are both meaningful and
grammatically correct. Announce the winner.
