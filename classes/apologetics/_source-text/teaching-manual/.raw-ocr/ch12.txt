OVERVIEW
Consider the idea that science and faith are
in conflict. Is this really true? Three main
views attempt to describe the relationship
between science and Christianity: conflict,
complementary, and collaborative views.
The conflict view sees Christianity as a
hindrance to science, the complementary
view sees it as valuable but entirely separate,
and collaborative view sees it as beneficial.
Through the ages, there have been
myths about Christianity being a hindrance
to science. In reality, Christianity has
played a significant role in spurring people
to scientific discovery. Modern science
Aourished in the 17th century. Intluential
scientists of this movement, such as Francis
Bacon, Robert Boyle, Johannes Kepler,
and Isaac Newton, were motivated in their
scientific endeavors by theistic assumptions.
At times, conflict developed between
Christianity and science. This was especially
evident in the Galileo incident with the
Catholic Church.
There are examples in which science
and Christianity have maintained a fruitful
partnership. Matters related to physics and
astronomy, such as quantum theory, the
anthropic principle and time offer insight
into the nature of the universe. However,
the science of origins has been debated
even within the Christian community.
From Darwin's theory of evolution to the
four Christian views of origins, there are a
variety of beliefs and evidence for each one.
Beyond origins, however, we also need to
consider what science and faith can tell us
about human life and death. When does
life begin? When does life end? What is the
Bible's approach to the use of medicine
to heal the body and mind? All these
topics are important as we seek answers to
life's big questions, but more importantly,
to maintain a collaborative partnership
between Christianity and science

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Before you read Chapter 12.1-12.2, what did you think about
science and faith conflicting? Explain.
Answers will vary.
2. How is biblical faith different from the definition of faith
sometimes offered by those who think science and faith conflict?
[12.1]
Biblical faith is trust in and allegiance to God. Some believe that this
kind of faith is simply belief in the absence of evidence. However,
biblical faith is not blind; instead, it is a confident trust in God
based on reason and evidence. Biblical faith calls us to engage our
minds in the pursuit of truth, using all the tools at our disposal,
including science.
3. What are some flaws in the conflict view of science and
Christianity? [12.2]
Conflict view advocates accept a faulty view of faith, holding
that faith is belief without evidence. They also frequently accept
scientism, the belief that science is the best or only way to obtain
reliable knowledge. However, scientism is deeply problematic as
this belief cannot be proven through science; it is a philosophical
claim. Therefore, scientism is self-refuting. Also, the conflict view
is flawed in assuming methodological naturalism, which limits the
scope of scientific explanations to purely natural causes and rules out
supernatural explanations before the evidence has been considered.
4. How might methodological naturalism hinder scientific
inquiry? [12.2]
Methodological naturalism could hinder scientific inquiry because
it does not consider all the available evidence. Even when there
are good scientific reasons for a supernatural explanation, the
methodological naturalist rejects such explanations and searches for
alternative explanations, which are sometimes speculative.
5. How is the complementary view different from the
collaborative view? [12.2]
Both views see science and Christianity as valuable truth-seeking
endeavors. However, the complementary (or two-domain) view
holds that science and Christianity cannot conflict or interact
meaningfully since their domains are entirely separate. However, it
is hard to keep these two domains apart. So, when there is overlap,
either science or Christianity will be prioritized, usually to the
detriment of the other. The collaborative view allows for meaningful
interaction between science and Christianity so that each can
beneficially inform the other.

make, and how are these rooted in the Christian worldview?
[12.3]
Scientists assume the universe behaves in orderly and predictable
ways. This assumption is rooted in Scripture, which speaks of God's
wisdom and skill in creation and the fixed order he established.
Scientists must also assume that our minds can be trusted to help us
gain true knowledge about the world. This assumption is justified
from a Christian worldview. Rational minds must come from a

about the world in ways that lead to the truth because God created

2. What does the proliferation of Christian and theistic scientists
in the seventeenth century tell us about the relationship between
Christianity and science in its earliest days? [12.3]
It shows that Christianity and science were not seen as enemies.
Instead, religious faith provided the foundation and motivation for
inquiry into the natural world.
3. What is the story of Galileo and the Catholic Church about,
and why is this important? [12.4]
The Galileo incident is primarily about the conflict between the
majority scientific view and the minority scientific view. The story
is important because it illustrates how difficult it is to overturn the
majority view without time and significant scientific evidence, even
when it is incorrect.
4. Why is it unreasonable to expect the Bible to completely align
with modern scientific views? [12.4]
The biblical writers were not attempting to write a science textbook.
They were not scientists, and they had neither the technical
vocabulary nor the scientific instruments available to scientists
today. They usually describe nature from the perspective of a human
observer.

things can we learn from the Galileo story? [12.4)
Possible answers: It is important not to overstate one's case. Mocking
others for their views is usually unproductive. We should be slow
to judge scientific theories before considering all available evidence.
Attempting to silence those who disagree is a poor strategy (in
addition to being morally wrong.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What does it mean to say that the material universe is
incomplete, and why is this important? [12.5]
It means that the material universe is neither self-contained nor self-
sustaining. The universe is dependent on outside, nonmaterial forces.
If this is true, then one cannot simply rule out God's existence or
relevance to the universe.
2. What is the anthropic principle, and how does it point to the
existence of God? [12.5]
The anthropic principle says that the universe contains all the
necessary properties that make the existence of intelligent life
inevitable. The universe seems especially designed for human beings
to explore. This principle draws our attention to the existence of an
intelligent being outside the universe who specifically designed the
universe for life. This is consistent with the biblical claim that God
created the universe and made human beings in his image.
3. What have physicists discovered about time that is consistent
with the Bible's grand story? [12.5]
Most physicists agree that we are moving forward through time from
a fixed past toward an unknown future. The Bible reveals a God
"from everlasting to everlasting," the Creator of time. Time began
at creation and will end when the final judgment occurs. What lies
beyond the time-bound universe is a glorious new creation.
4. If Darwin's theory of evolution was true, would it eliminate
the need for God? Explain. [12.6]
No, it would not. Even if true, Darwin's theory does not remove the
need for an intelligent agent since it does not attempt to explain how
the first life arose. Scientists have been unable to demonstrate how
life can rise from nonlife.
5. Is positing an intelligent designer simply an example of "God
in the gaps"? Explain. [12.6]
Suggesting that God is the explanation for information-rich systems
like DNA is not a lazy attempt to fill the gap in our current lack
of scientific knowledge. Instead, it is to follow the evidence where
it leads. Repeated experience shows that information-rich systems
come from intelligent minds. So, it is quite reasonable to argue an
intelligent designer must be behind DNA.

The molecules in our brain cells that lead to thoughts do not have

Scientifically, it has been demonstrated that life begins at conception.

are speculative enterprises, which, if feasible, would involve moral
complications and be potentially dehumanizing. The practice of
euthanasia is also riddled with moral, ethical and scientific problems
and could easily be exploited.

The Bible's solution to death is Jesus. On the cross, Jesus defeated
the great enemy on the cross, and in his resurrection, he proved
himself stronger than death. Those who trust in Jesus will, like him,
arise to new life when God's new creation arrives.

scientism.

5 Christianity and Science
Activity
PERSONAL VIEW ON
SCIENCE AND FAITH
OBJECTIVE:
Students will reevaluate their previously stated beliefs
about the relationship between science and faith.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: pen and student textbook
• Time required: 20-25 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Guide students to refer to the paragraph they wrote
in their student manual in Lesson 1 Activity: What's
your View? Have them review what they wrote
about their personal viewpoint on relationship
between science and Christianity.
2. Direct students to reevaluate and rewrite their
thoughts as they consider the information covered
in PS 12.7 Chapter Review.
3. Then, have students turn and talk with a partner
on the following:
•How have your ideas changed or developed on
this topic?
• What would you say now to confidently defend
your beliefs if challenged?
4. Call on volunteers to share their new ideas with
the class.
