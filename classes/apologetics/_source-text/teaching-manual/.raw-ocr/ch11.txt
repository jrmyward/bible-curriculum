Chapter 11
OVERVIEW
Throughout history, most humans have
believed in the supernatural. Scripture
repeatedly presents supernatural beings
and activities beginning with God creating
the universe.
Materialism and naturalism lead some
people to reject the supernatural. However,
there is compelling evidence for supernatural
beings and miraculous events. In particular,
three arguments consistent with Scripture
uphold the existence of God: the Kalam
cosmological argument, the design
argument, and the moral argument. This last
argument assumes natural law exists.
Some people believe in God but
discount miracles. They hold to deism, the
belief that God created the world but is not
active within it. But Christians believe God
still acts and does miracles today. A miracle
is an extraordinary supernatural sign that
transcends the laws of nature and points to
the present reality of God's kingdom.
Jesus' resurrection is a miracle essential
to the Christian faith. This chapter will
describe five facts that support the case for
the miracle of Christ's resurrection. Popular
theories that deny the resurrection do not
take these five facts into account. Therefore,
it is reasonable to believe Jesus rose from
the dead.
The Bible supports the existence of
supernatural beings such as angels and
demons. It teaches that God is sovereign
over all. Jesus triumphed over sin, evil, and
death. Though a spiritual battle continues,
Christians need not fear but can be
confident that God and his kingdom
will prevail.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Have you ever had an extraordinary experience that you
simply can't explain? What was that like? [11.1]
Answers will vary.
2. How does a person's worldview determine what he or she
believes about the supernatural? [11.1]
Some worldviews, like Christianity, allow for the existence of a
supernatural realm and make it possible to believe in supernatural
beings or miracles. Materialists do not believe anything outside the
material world exists, and naturalists believe everything must have a
natural explanation. Therefore, they do not believe in supernatural
beings or events.
3. What is the Kalam cosmological argument, and how is it an
argument for God's existence? [11.2]
The Kalam cosmological argument says that whatever begins to exist
has a cause. Since the universe began to exist, it must have a cause.
The argument supports God's existence because if the universe has
a cause, it must have been caused by something or someone who is
uncaused. According to Christian theology, God is such a being.
4. How would you defend the premise "Whatever begins to exist
has a cause"? [11.2]
Whatever begins to exist must have a cause because it is illogical
to think otherwise. We know that things do not simply pop into
existence out of thin air. Also, things cannot cause themselves to
exist (if they did, they would have to exist before they existed).
5. How would you defend the premise "The universe began to
exist"? [11.2]
The general theory of relativity says the universe is expanding,
so there had to be a point in time when it began expanding.
Hubble's law showed that galaxies are moving away from the Earth,
supporting the idea of an expanding universe. The second law of
thermodynamics says the universe is running out of usable energy.
If the universe was eternal, it would have run out of energy a long
time ago. Philosophically, it is impossible to countdown from
infinity, so if the universe were eternal, we would never have arrived
at the present moment.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Does design always require a designer? Explain. [11.3]
From experience, we know that design is usually the result of a
designer. While it is possible for something to give the appearance
of design without actually having been designed, such cases are the
exception and not the rule. When we see design, our first and most
natural inference is that there was a designer.
2. In addition to fine-tuning and DNA, what other evidence do
you see for design in the universe? [11.3]
Answers will vary, but may include irreducible complexity in
organisms, highly complex ecosystems, the human body, the ability
of humans to think and reason, and more.
3. What is the problem with the multiverse theory as an
explanation for why our planet appears to be designed? [11.3]
The main problem with this theory is that it is not based on
evidence. There is a much simpler answer, with more evidence: God.
Even if a multiverse existed, something would need to generate the
universes. That universe generator would require fine-tuning, which
again points to a designer.
4. What is natural law, and how does it lend support to the
existence of God? [11.4]
Natural law is a system of moral conduct derived from the natural
order of creation and known to all humans. It consists of the ethical
principles we all know without being told (for example, murder
and theft are wrong; honesty and generosity are good). Where did
natural law come from? Because humans need an objective standard
to tell right from wrong, it makes sense this standard would come
from outside humanity. Christians believe the objective standard of
morality comes from God.
5. Can evolution adequately explain the existence of morality?
Explain. [11.4]
Evolution could provide some explanations for human morality. For
example, some humans may choose to behave morally to promote
the species' survival. However, evolution cannot tell us how we
ought to act because it cannot provide an objective morality.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Is it reasonable to believe the Bible's account of God? Explain.
[11.5]
It is reasonable to believe the Bible's account of God. The Bible
has proven to be a historically accurate, truth-telling book that has
transformed the lives of millions for the good. If we can trust it to
tell us the truth about history and human beings, we are reasonably
justified in trusting it to tell us the truth about God.
2. How does Scripture's account of God align with the
cosmological, design, and moral arguments? [11.5]
Scripture supports the cosmological argument by teaching that the
universe began with God's creation. God exists apart from time
and space. He is immaterial, unimaginably powerful, and eternally
existing (and therefore, uncaused).
3. Scripture supports the design argument by showing God's
wisdom, power, and intentionality. Creation reflects God's
orderly design, and humans were created in his image.
Scripture supports the moral argument by making God the
originator of natural law. Right and wrong are derived from his
character. He is perfect, which makes him the objective reference
point for morality.
4. Why would a deist find it hard to believe in miracles? [11.6]
Deists find it hard to believe in miracles because they believe God
created the world but is not active within it. They hold a naturalistic
view of the universe and think anything said to be a miracle
probably has a natural explanation.
5. What is the purpose of a miracle from a biblical perspective?
[11.6]
Miracles transcend the laws of nature and point us to the present
reality of God's kingdom. They are signs of God's providential care
for his broken world, demonstrate God's goodness and power, and
foreshadow the restoration of all things.
6. How might you determine whether a miracle has occurred?
[11.6]
Miracles may happen at any time and place, but we cannot always
be sure when a miracle has actually occurred. To find out if a miracle
happened, we need to carefully examine the evidence for it. If what
happened is both highly improbable and independently special, it is
very likely that it was a miracle.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Why is Paul's conversion valuable evidence for the resurrection
of Jesus? [11.7]
Paul, a known persecutor of the early church, had no desire to see
Jesus raised from the dead. He knew the resurrection story but did
everything he could to stop it from being spread. Yet, something
happened to transform him into one of the leading figures in the
early Christian movement. According to Acts 9, Paul had a personal
encounter with Jesus that convinced him Jesus had, indeed, risen
from the dead.
2. Imagine that you were James, the brother of Jesus. What
would Jesus have had to do to convince you that he was God?
[11.7]
Answers will vary.
3. What makes the testimony of the women at Jesus' tomb so
significant? [11.8]
Women were not taken seriously as witnesses in the ancient world.
Their testimony was regarded as unreliable, yet all four Gospels
say women were the first witnesses of the empty tomb. Given the
prejudice against women's testimony, this detail would make the
resurrection reports sound unreliable to people of the day. There was
no reason for the gospel writers to include the women unless they
had been the first witnesses. This detail shows that the gospel authors
were telling the truth.
4. How would you respond to the statement, "The disciples
hallucinated": [11.8]
The idea that the disciples hallucinated doesn't account for Paul or
James' conversions. Also, if the disciples merely hallucinated, Jesus
would still be in the tomb, a fact easily verifiable by anyone alive
at the time of Jesus' death. Furthermore, Jesus appeared after his
resurrection to several groups of people, yet hallucinations cannot be
shared, so they could not all have hallucinated Jesus' resurrection.
5. How would you respond to the statement, "Jesus rose in a
spiritual sense"? [11.8]
This theory doesn't explain the apostles radical transformation, Paul
and James conversions, or the empty tomb. Also, an inner feeling
of peace is not an adequate basis on which to launch an entire
movement. Furthermore, this statement does not take the historical
context into account. If the resurrection was just an inner feeling, no
one at that time would have called it a resurrection.
6. How would you respond to the statement, "The disciples were superstitious
and likely to believe anything"? [11.8]
This statement suggests that people today are more intelligent than people in the
ancient world because we know that people don't rise from the dead. However,
except for some Jews who thought there would be a resurrection at the end of
history, no one in the ancient world believed that people could rise from the dead.
So, the statement attempts to dismiss the resurrection without considering the
evidence for it.

1. What's the difference between angels and demons? [11.9]
Angels serve God by doing his will and ministering to God and
humans. They may guard and protect humans, encourage and
strengthen them, or deliver commissions and other messages from
God. They also enact judgment and war against evil. Demons, in
contrast, are angels in rebellion against God who seek to derail his
plans and deceive humans. They tempt people to sin, encourage
false doctrine, and stand behind much of the evil in our world.
2. Do we need to be afraid of Satan and demons? [11.9]
Demonic forces are not to be trifled with, and we should take them
seriously. However, we do not need to fear them because Jesus is
more powerful. His life, death, and resurrection demonstrated his
power over the forces of evil. Christians are filled with the Holy
Spirit and fully equipped with the armor of God to stand against
deception and temptation. We are convinced that, in the end, God
will win the final victory over evil.
3. Explain the G. K. Chesterton quote from Chapter 11.10 in
your own words. [11.10]
Possible answer: Some people think it is unreasonable to believe
in miracles and that Christians only believe in miracles because
they are told to. However, Chesterton shows Christians can look
at all the evidence to determine if a miracle has occurred, while
disbelievers must reject miracles even if there is evidence for them,
because they hold to a doctrine that says supernatural events do not
happen.
4. How does the resurrection change the way you live? [11.10]
Answers will vary. If Jesus rose from the dead, his claims cannot be
dismissed. We must take his commands seriously and trust in him
for salvation. Because of the resurrection, we also have nothing to
fear. We can live confidently, knowing that sin and death do not
have the final word. God's truth and justice will ultimately prevail.
5. How can you become more aware of the supernatural in your
own life? [11.10]
Answers will vary. We can pray that God Would open our eyes to see
how he is working in our lives. We can look intentionally for signs
of the extraordinary or inexplicable. We can examine the evidence
for answered prayer in our and others' lives. We can rely on the
power of the Holy Spirit to live holy lives and resist evil.

FANTASTIC FIGURES
OBJECTIVE:
Students will illustrate supernatural beings based on
descriptions from the Bible.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: Bible, poster board, and art
supplies
• Time required: 25-30 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Divide the class into five groups. Explain that
each group is to illustrate a supernatural being as
described in Scripture. Add that sometimes the
Bible's descriptions differ from one another. Groups
must determine whether more than one illustration
is necessary to convey what their supernatural
being looks like. Make assignments as follows:
• Group 1: Cherubim (Exodus 25:18-20;
1 Kings 6:23-28; Ezekiel 1:5-14, 10:8-17;
Revelation 4:6-8)
•Group 2: Angels (Daniel 10:5-6; Matthew 28:2-3;
Luke 24:4; Acts 1:10; Revelation 10:1)
•Group 3: Demons (Leviticus 17:7;
Deuteronomy 32:16-17; 1 Corinthians 10:19-20;
Ephesians 6:12; Jude 1:6; Revelation 12:9)
• Group 4: Devil (2 Corinthians 11:14;
Ephesians 6:16; 1 Peter 5:8; Revelation 12:9, 20:2)
•Group 5: Jesus (Isaiah 52:14-53:5; Matthew 17:2;
Revelation 1:12-18, 19:11-16)
2. Provide each group with poster board and art
supplies. Allow time for groups to create their
poster.
3. Have groups take turns presenting their posters to
the class.
