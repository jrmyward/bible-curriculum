OVERVIEW
For many people, the doctrine of hell is
arguably one of the most uncomfortable
Christian beliefs to address. Some see it as
cruel, intolerant, or abusive. But Christians
speak of hell not out of spite but out of
love. The Bible describes hell as a place of
permanent separation from God after death.
Separation from God, not suffering, is the
greatest tragedy of hell. Eternal conscious
torment is the church's historical and most
widely held position on hell. This view also
has the most biblical evidence to support
it. Other proposed views on hell and the
afterlife include purgatory, annihilationism,
and universalism.
According to the Bible, hell was
prepared for Satan and other angels who
rebelled against God. Human beings who
ultimately say no to God share the same fate.
All human beings are guilty before God's
righteous judgment. God is just and will
not allow evil to go unchecked. Hell is the
ultimate expression of God's justice. Still, he
is patient and merciful, giving all people the
opportunity to repent. Those who trust in
God's gift of salvation through Jesus Christ
alone will be saved. Spending eternity in hell
is a choice many human beings make during
this life by continuing to rebel against God
and refusing to trust in Jesus.
Christians can take comfort in God's
justice and mercy, knowing that he is slow
to anger and will do what is right regarding
those who are too young to understand
and those who are cannot easily access
the gospel. In the meantime, Christians
participate in God's mission to bring the
gospel to those who have not yet heard.
Christians can do this graciously by leaving
judgment to God, taking evil seriously, and
amplifying the beauty of the gospel.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Do our feelings about hell affect the truth of this doctrine?
Explain. [14.1]
While the doctrine of hell may seem inhumane and offensive to
some, our feelings don't affect hell's existence. Either hell is real, or
it isn't. Our feelings don't change what is real. While emotions are
important, truth doesn't change to accommodate them.
2. How is believing in hell different from wishing it on someone?
[14.1]
Believing in God's justice does not require denying God's mercy.
God does not take pleasure in the destruction of the wicked.
Christians are not to take pleasure in it or wish hell on other people.
Instead, they are to desire other peoples redemption and restoration
to right relationship with God. Christianity teaches us to speak
of hell out of love, not unkindness or vindictiveness. While the
doctrine of hell has been abused at times, it's not inherently abusive.
3. How is Sheol different from hell? [14.2]
In the Old Testament, Sheol simply referred to the place of the dead.
Both the righteous and the wicked go down to Sheol, though their
fate isn't necessarily the same. In the New Testament, Jesus explained
the concept of hell as the place where God's future judgment of the
wicked is enacted. All who reject Christ spend eternity excluded
from God's presence.
4. What aspect of hell represents the greatest tragedy? [14.2]
While physical suffering or spiritual suffering from regret or loss
would be immense, the greatest tragedy is relational separation from
God. To be in hell is to be outside of God's kingdom, away from
God's love and presence.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How does knowing the early church fathers' beliefs about hell
help you understand this doctrine? [14.3]
Answers will vary but may include: It helps to know what the earliest
Christian leaders agreed on. They lived closer to the time the New
Testament was written and may have heard the disciples teach while
they were still alive. Knowing the earliest beliefs about hell might
help us discover the most biblical view.
2. What is eternal conscious torment? [14.3]
Eternal conscious torment is the traditional view of hell in which
the wicked will experience everlasting punishment. Those who have
rejected God and denied Jesus as Savior will experience separation
from God and will be tormented for all eternity. It's the most
commonly held view by Christians and the early church fathers
throughout history.
3. What are universalism and annihilationism? [14.3]
Universalism says that all people will eventually be reconciled to
God. Some universalists believe in hell, but they do not think it is
eternal. They believe everyone will ultimately be saved, a view that
has historically been considered to be heretical. Annihilationism is
the idea that the wicked will experience punishment in hell for a
limited, not eternal, period, after which they will cease to exist.
4. What is the purpose of hell? [14.4]
Hell was created to punish Satan and demons who have led people
astray. Hell also serves as a place where those who have rejected
Christ by refusing his payment for sin must pay the penalty for their
sin by being excluded from God's presence and kingdom.
5. Why can Christians remain hopeful even while believing in
hell? [14.4]
Christians can remain hopeful because human beings don't have to
inevitably end up in hell. Because God is merciful, he made a way
for people to be reconciled to him. Jesus died on the cross as our
substitute, paying the penalty for our moral transgressions.
6. What does it mean to say that the punishment of hell is
retributive? [14.4]
To say that the punishment of hell is retributive is to say it is
deserved. It is the just punishment for sin, the penalty for our
rebellion against God.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What are some arguments in favor of eternal conscious
torment? Do you find these arguments convincing? Explain.
[14.5]
Answers will vary but should include: Scripture often describes hell
as eternal. For example, Matthew 25:41-46 contrasts the eternal
bliss of the righteous with the eternal damnation of the wicked. If
the fate of the righteous is eternal, it makes sense that the fate of the
wicked is also eternal. Also, if God is ultimately good and worthy
of worship, sin against him would deserve the ultimate eternal
punishment (Romans 3:23, 6:23). This view has also been widely
held throughout church history.
2. What are some arguments in favor of annihilationism? Do you
find these arguments convincing? What are some weaknesses of
this view? [14.5]
Answers will vary but should include: Annihilationists hold that
because sins are finite, an infinite or eternal punishment for sin does
not fit the crime. They also believe words like eternal and everlasting
may not always be strictly literal. This position is not supported by
any clear biblical evidence. Furthermore, just punishments often
exceed the length of time taken to commit the crime, such as when
someone serves a lifetime sentence for murder.
3. What are some arguments in favor of universalism? What are
some problems with this position? [14.5]
Universalists argue that punishment in hell is restorative, not merely
retributive, and point to Bible verses that could suggest that all
people will eventually be reconciled to God. However, many other
Scriptures clearly state that only those who trust in Christ will be
saved and that the unrepentant will not inherit the kingdom of
God. Universalists would have to prove restoration of all things
cannot include final judgment on the wicked, and that verses about
restoration somehow cancel out verses about eternal judgment.
4. How does sin work on us like an addiction? [14.6]
All addictive substances hold out some false promise of satisfaction
or fulfillment. Those who use these substances find relief and
comfort at first, but the addictions never fully satisfy. They keep
users coming back for more. Eventually, the user becomes enslaved
and unable to break free. Sin works similarly. Sin promises us
satisfaction or fulfilment but quickly becomes addictive and enslaves
us in chains that only Jesus can break.
5. Explain what C. S. Lewis meant when he said, "There are only two kinds
of people in the end: those who say to God, "Thy will be done,' and those to
whom God says, in the end, "Thy will be done."" [14.6]
Lewis meant that hell is a place people choose. If we do not accept God's way
(receiving his forgiveness and salvation) and insist on having our way (living in
sin and rebellion against God), God will let us have our own way. He will not
force us to spend eternity with him. If the whole trajectory of our lives is away
from God, this will extend into eternity.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How are God's love and justice related? [14.7]
God's love and justice are not separate attributes. God's justice is an
expression of his love. Because he loves the world, he will not allow
evil to destroy it forever. He will bring judgment on sin. To allow
evil to go completely unchecked would not be loving. A God who is
not just cannot be a God of love. The relationship between these two
attributes can be stated like this: God is love, and because he is love,
he will be just.
2. How is hell an expression of God's justice? [14.7]
Many times, wicked people seem to get away with evil. Sometimes,
they evade the law or perpetrate violence on others without any
apparent consequences. Without hell, there would be no ultimate
justice for those who have suffered at the hands of evil. Thus, hell is
part of God's answer to the problem of evil. In hell, justice is truly
served on the wicked.
3. What happens to small children who die before trusting Jesus?
[14.8]
Scripture never directly addresses this question, though it does
contain some clues. Scripture indicates that God holds children
accountable once they understand the consequences of their actions
and can choose to accept or reject Christ. It seems possible that
children who die before understanding the difference between good
and evil will be saved. While we do not know what happens to
children who die before receiving Christ, we can trust God to do
what is right.
4. Can someone today be saved without hearing about Jesus?
Explain. [14.8]
Paul indicates that people need to hear the gospel message to have
faith, and Jesus commissioned his disciples to share this gospel
message worldwide. So, people need to hear about Jesus to be saved.
However, God may have other ways of communicating his message
to those who have never heard the gospel. Christians' role is to do
all we can to bring the gospel to those who have never heard it. We
trust God with the rest.
5. If God is capable of communicating his message to all people,
even to those we cannot reach, why bother to share the gospel
with anyone? [14.8]
While God may communicate his message without our help, God
wants to work through us to get his gospel message out. Sharing
the good news with another person is one of the most joyous and
fulfilling things we can do. God invites us to participate in his
mission, and we can do so with boldness, knowing that saving the
world is not ultimately up to us. Because we love Jesus, we obey his
call to share the gospel and participate in his mission.

It means to be bent or twisted.
4. Is anyone immune to sin? Explain.
None of us are free from sin. We inherited original sin from our

5. What does the doctrine of sin mean for equality?
All human beings are infected and enslaved by sin. There are no
good people and no bad people.
6. How is acknowledging the reality of sin "wildly optimistic"?
If sin isn't real, the world is already as good as it gets. By
acknowledging the reality of sin, we can also acknowledge God's
redemption and restoration.

FIVE COMPONENTS, ONE SKIT
