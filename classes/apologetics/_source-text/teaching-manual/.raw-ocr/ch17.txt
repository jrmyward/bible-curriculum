OVERVIEW
Human beings are storytelling creatures.
Stories illuminating the human condition
and mimicking the true story of reality in
the Bible resonate with us the most. Stories
suggest meaning and purpose, and they
help us make sense of our lives. The biblical
metanarrative connects our individual
stories to God's big story of Creation, the
Fall, Redemption Initiated, the Interlude,
Redemption Accomplished, Restoration
Initiated, and Restoration Accomplished.
This is the true global story, not Western
mythology.
Here is God's grand story summarized:
Human beings were created in God's image
to rule and be in a relationship with God,
self, others, and nature. The first humans
rebelled against God and broke his moral
laws. This action separated them and their
descendants from God and gave all people a
sin nature. Suffering, evil, chaos, and death
resulted from that rebellion. Without God's
intervention, that separation would last
into eternity. But God never gives up on
his creation, so he called Abraham and his
descendants to form a nation that would
point people to God and through which the
Savior would come. After much waiting and
anticipation in Israel, God became flesh as
Jesus Christ. He taught about the kingdom
of God and performed miracles proving who
he is. He lived the perfect life that humans
could not and died in our place to pay the
penalty for our rebellion. Jesus rose again,
defeating sin, death, and the powers of evil.
By trusting in him, we are restored to a right
relationship with God, ourselves, others, and
nature. Jesus established the church to bring
the gospel to the world and promised that
one day he will return to restore all things.
This is the enduring hope of the church.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is the difference between being winsome and being
weak in apologetics? [17.5]
Being winsome means presenting the good news in a gracious and
courageous way, speaking clearly and accurately with Scripture, and
demonstrating respect for your audience. Even if a person disagrees
with your arguments, they should not experience you as arrogant,
rude, or unfeeling. To be weak in apologetics is to compromise truth
in an effort not to offend anyone.
2. How does one become a person of character? [17.5]
Character is learned daily through making choices to follow God's
ways. Our choices build habits of obedience and lead to character
growth that prepares us to face larger challenges. Growing in
character begins with saying yes to the Spirit, seeking to imitate God,
and learning how to respond to each situation we face in God's way.
3. Have you ever faced opposition, criticism, or hostility for
being a Christian or sharing the gospel? If so, how did you
respond? [17.6]
Answers will vary.
4. Is it a waste of time when we share the gospel with someone
who rejects it? Explain. [17.6]
Sharing the gospel is never a waste of time. We do not know what
God may be doing in a person's heart or how the words we have
spoken may impact the person later. God always helps us grow
through every encounter, and he asks only that we be faithful to
share truth; the Holy Spirit takes responsibility for the results.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What should you do when you make a mistake during an
apologetic encounter? [17.7]
Answers will vary. There is no shame in admitting to the other person
that you have made an error in your arguments or failed to listen
attentively. When you make a mistake, you can acknowledge it and
try again. Or you may need to get back to the other person later on
a particular point. If you do this, research the topic in question or
point the person to someone with more expertise in that area.
2. Why do you think God's power is made perfect in weakness?
[17.7]
Answers will vary. When God shows himself to be strong in an area
where we are weak, we cannot chalk up our success to our talents
or expertise. Instead, God gets the glory—we are reminded of his
power, our dependence on him, and we grow in humility and trust.
3. What is a wounded healer? [17.7]
A wounded healer is someone who has experienced pain, discomfort,
or struggle in some area, offered that pain to God, and received
his comfort and healing. Because of this, the wounded healer can
uniquely help others experience God's comfort and healing.
4. Why is it important to reflect on apologetic encounters after
they occur? [17.8]
To become more effective apologists, we need more than experience.
Unless we reflect on our experiences, we cannot learn or grow.
Without reflection, it is difficult to identify areas of weakness and
prepare effectively for future encounters.

Can I Be an Apologist?
1. What does an apologist do, and how can we be better
apologists?
An apologist asks and answers questions. To be a better apologist,
ask better questions that lead to deeper study to find the answers.
2. What advice does the speaker give to apologists about
quickness?
While apologists offer reasons quickly, we are to be slow to speak
and quick to listen so we can determine which reason is best to offer.
3. What does the speaker say to do besides gathering information
by reading and listening?
Make room in your life for quiet in your day and your mind so that
you can grow in wisdom. We are encouraged to seek many mentors.
4. What advice did the speaker give that Daniel exemplified?
Like Daniel did, pay attention to the stories and art of the culture
around you. He learned the language and could quote Babylonian
poets better than those around him.
5. What final advice did the speaker give for becoming a better
apologist?
More than anything, apologists need humility. Sometimes, the words
"I don't know" are the best answer you can offer.
• Video 2:
Is Evangelism Important?
1. The speaker gives examples of four attitudes people have about
sharing their faith. Which one best describes you?
Answers will vary.
2. How do most people feel, and what are their concerns?
Most people are hesitant, concerned about saying the wrong thing,
looking foolish, or getting into conversations they cannot handle.
3. What does 2 Corinthians 5:20 mean?
Christians are to consider themselves as missionaries, ambassadors
for Christ. We have an opportunity to show off who Jesus is through
our actions and our words.
4. What tip does the speaker give regarding our self-image?
The speaker says most of us should see ourselves as gardeners rather than
harvesters. A nonbeliever may have hundreds of conversations before he or she
commits to Christ.
5. What goal does the speaker point people to?
The speaker quotes Greg Koukl's book Tactics, affirming that our goal should be
"to put a stone in someone's shoe," meaning to give them something to think
about.
6. What does the speaker say to pray for?
We are to pray for opportunities and courage to ask questions, and listen.
7. What does asking, "What do you mean by that?" accomplish?
It is a good starter question that doesn't require prior knowledge, and it gives the
other person the opportunity to think about what he or she believes and why.

How Do We Share the Gospel?
1. Sharing the gospel often intimidates people. How does the speaker
suggest we approach this task?
The speaker says the gospel is a story, and we tell stories all the time.
2. What is the first big theme we need to remember when sharing the gospel
story?
God exists. There's a personal being who is all-powerful, all-loving, all-good. He's
the same God who made us.
3. Describe the four parts of the gospel story identified by the speaker.
Creation, the fall, Jesus' rescuing us, and Jesus' return when all will be made new.
4. What is the main thing we should remember in sharing the gospel story?
God is the hero, and we are invited to participate in his work and share the
story with others. The gospel is news we would not have heard without someone
sharing it with us.
5. What is a common misconception people have about sharing the gospel?
People think they have to share the whole thing all at once. Sometimes, it is
better to start with a smaller part of the story, such as the part of the fall that the
person is experiencing.
6. What does starting with the fall allow you to do?
It enables you to sympathize with that person, to share about Jesus, and
hopefully create curiosity for a larger conversation about the gospel.

OBJECTIVE:
Students will analyze Scripture to deepen their
understanding of what it looks like to practice apologetics
and describe how they can apply these practices daily.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: paper and pen
• Time required: 10-15 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Direct students to read Ephesians 4:1-16 in their
student manual and make observations that will help
them write two personal applications.
2. Have students observe important words while reading
by circling action verbs, underlining descriptive
adjectives, and putting a box around nouns focusing on
terms that help them understand what it means to be
an apologist.
3. After reading the text, have students analyze the words
they identified and use them to form two sentences
that describe how they can practice apologetics in their
everyday lives at school, work, church, and more.
4. If time permits, invite students to turn to a partner and
share their responses.

Activity
FAITH CAROUSEL
OBJECTIVE:
Students will collaborate by creating visual
representations of the major ideas related to practicing
apologetics.
PREPARATION:
• Materials needed: student textbook, four poster
boards, markers, and a timer
• Time required: 45-50 minutes
PROCEDURE:
1. Divide the class into four groups and send each to
one of the four stations.
2. Explain to students they have eight minutes in each
station to create a visual summary of what they
have learned regarding the objectives indicated
on the back of their poster. Each group's goal is
to include creative representations of important
people, terms, ideas, and Bible passages on the
poster. Explain they should not expect to finish their
representations in the short time given. They should
leave room on the poster board for other groups'
contributions. Students may use their textbooks
or class notes. Give students a warning one minute
before their time is up.
3. Have groups rotate to the next station and have
another eight minutes to continue developing the
ideas the previous group started. As they work on a
new poster, students may add things the previous
group did not have time to include.
4. Continue rotating groups through the posters,
giving one-minute warnings before they rotate
until each group has contributed to each of the four
posters.
5. The goal of the fourth rotation should be to finish
the poster as best as possible.
6. If class time remains, have the students review
the posters together as a class and share the main
concepts represented and observations made
during this activity.
