OVERVIEW
The Bible is an ancient book that includes
some difficult passages. Christians often
face questions about the Bible when
they encourage others to experience its
life-changing power. It is vital that we
understand that the Bible is a story called
a metanarrative. It consists of 66 books in
various genres, arranged into two parts: the
Old Testament and the New Testament. The
Bible was written over 1,500 years ago by 40
authors who sought to reveal God and his
plan for creation.
Christians believe in inspiration, the
doctrine that God enabled people to write
the words of Scripture. We evaluate the
Bible's claims of truth by encountering God
in his Word and by assessing the external
evidence for the historical reliability of the
Old and New Testaments. Some contend
that the Bible is an ancient myth or that it is
full of factual and scientific errors; however,
we can test their claims and demonstrate the
authenticity of the Bible.
How was the Bible put together? What
is the difference between the Bible, the
Apocrypha, and other ancient books and
gospels that some say were left out of the
Bible? The early church used at least three
tests to conclude whether a book should be
part of the canon of Scripture.
The books in the New Testament are
nearly 2,000 years old, and those in the
Old Testament are much older. Is the text
we have today the same as the original? In
this chapter, we will look at the way the
Bible has been preserved and consider the
significance of textual variants.
We hope to approach the Bible
with an open mind. Being aware of our
presuppositions, we consider the Bible's
historical background and literary context,
engage with the biblical authors, and
experience the power of God's Word.

done, is doing, and will do. It also reveals the truth about us in
relationship with God so that we may live freely and fully in
God's world.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What does it mean to say the Bible is inspired by God? [2.3]
The word inspire means to breathe. When speaking of the Bible,
inspiration is the doctrine that God enabled people to write the
words of Scripture. The Bible claims that God communicated his
message to human writers and empowered them to communicate
this message using their own unique writing styles. God and
the human authors he inspired were intimately involved in the
composition of the Bible.
2. How would you respond to a person who claims the Bible is
just another book written by humans? [2.3]
Possible answers:
•Despite being written by dozens of authors over about 1,500 years,
the Bible tells one cohesive story.
• The Bible is historically reliable on dozens of accounts by
archaeology and ancient extra-biblical sources.
• Hundreds of biblical prophecies have been fulfilled.
• The Bible includes surprisingly honest and embarrassing details
about the sins and failures of God's people.
•The Bible continues to be widely read and has transformed millions
of lives for good.
3. What is the problem with saying that the Bible is true because
it claims to be true? Explain. [2.4]
• The argument is problematic because it is circular. The person
making this argument already assumes the truth of what he or she
is trying to prove. Few people are likely to be convinced a book is
true just because it claims to be true. The fact that the Bible claims
to be true demonstrates the biblical authors believed they were
communicating God's truth. The authenticity of the Bible can be
verified through investigation.
4. What are two ways we can evaluate the Bible's claim to be
true? [2.4]
• We can examine external information, such as historical evidence
that demonstrates the Bible's trustworthiness and reliability, and
seek trusted resources that explain any contradictions in the Bible.
We can note the Bible's impact on those who have read it.
• We can get to know the God who authored the Bible. This requires
an act of faith in trusting Jesus. Then, we can read Scripture, look
for indications of God's trustworthiness, and pray for wisdom and
understanding. As we get to know God, he reveals the truth of his
Word.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How would you respond to a person who claims the Bible is
full of myths? [2.5]
Possible answers:
•Ask the person what he or she means by the word myth since there
are many different understandings of this word.
• Ask for a specific example of a myth in the Bible. Such questions
ensure one is addressing the person's specific concern. They also
challenge him or her to investigate the evidence rather than simply
making assumptions.
•Acknowledge there are many similarities between biblical stories
and ancient pagan myths. However, this is unsurprising given
that biblical authors wrote from within a pagan culture. Yet, the
differences between the Bible and these myths are often more
numerous and surprising than the similarities.
2. How would you reply to someone who claims the Bible
contains scientific inaccuracies? [2.5]
Possible answers:
• Ask the person for a specific example of a scientific inaccuracy in
the Bible.
•Acknowledge the Bible is not a science textbook. It must be
evaluated in light of what it is trying to communicate about God
and his purposes for creation.
•Explain the Bible is not always meant to be taken literally. It
frequently uses metaphors and other literary devices to illustrate
aspects of God and his creation.
• Recognize it would not make sense for God to communicate to
ancient peoples in the language of modern science; they had neither
the technology nor the scientific vocabulary we have today. The
Bible often describes things simply, as they appear to the human eye.
3. How would you answer people who claim the exodus event is
not historical? [2.6]
Possible answers:
• Ask why they believe the exodus is not historical. Explain that
numerous discoveries have corroborated other stories from biblical
history. This being the case, giving the Bible the benefit of the
doubt is reasonable when there is no corroborating evidence.
Furthermore, the Bible is taken seriously as a historical document.
•Acknowledge that while there is no clear Egyptian record of the
exodus, discoveries are being made. It may be that the Egyptians
recorded the events, and the records are undiscovered. Or perhaps
they did not record it because it was an embarrassing defeat
(ancient Egyptians weren't in the habit of recording defeats).
• Recognize some discoveries do lend indirect support to the
historical reliability of the exodus story. Ask if this person will
explore some of that evidence with you.
APOLOGETICS G
4. How would you respond to a person who claims the Gospels are not
historically reliable because they have so many contradictions? [2.6]
Possible answers:
•Ask that person to give a specific example of a contradiction in the Gospels.
•Explain that the Gospels are similar in genre to Greco-Roman biographies, in
which narratives may be condensed or expanded, events may be reshuffled, and
speeches may be paraphrased. Rather than distorting the truth, such literary
moves help the author to make a specific point and highlight the truth.
•Explain that the Gospels are based on eyewitness testimony. We should expect
differences in the gospel writers accounts since each one, or the witnesses they
relied on, saw the event from a slightly different perspective.
• Recognize that many of the apparent differences are not so much contradictory
as complementary.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is the canon of Scripture, and why are the apocryphal
books not usually included in the Protestant Bible? [2.7]
The word canon comes from a Greek word that means rule or
standard for testing straightness. It refers to the collection of biblical
writings commonly accepted as genuine and authoritative. While most
Protestants see the apocryphal books as historically valuable, they are
usually not included in the Bible because the Jewish people did not
consider them canonical. Most Jews believed the prophetic voice had
ceased after the prophet Malachi.
2. Briefly summarize the three criteria for accepting a New
Testament book as canonical. [2.7]
•Apostolicity: Was the book written by an apostle or an associate of
an apostle?
•Orthodoxy: Did the book conform to the teachings of the apostles?
•Pedigree: Was the book accepted early on in the life of the church
and by the majority of churches across the region?
3. What might you explain to someone who claims important
books were excluded from the New Testament canon? [2.7]
Possible answers may include:
• Ask why he or she believes this, or which books were not included
in the canon that should have been.
• Explain that no church council officially created the New
Testament canon. The idea that powerful bishops at the Council of
Nicaea (AD 325) created the New Testament canon is historically
unfounded.
• Emphasize that numerous books, including the four Gospels,
Acts, and most of Paul's letters, were already circulating widely and
commonly accepted as authoritative by the mid-second century
long before Athanasius cataloged the 27 books we have in the New
Testament today.
• Explain why the so-called lost gospels fail all the tests of canonicity:
they were written between the second and fourth centuries, not
written by an apostle, taught gnosticism, and not widely accepted in
early Christian communities.
4. How would you respond to someone who claims the Old
Testament is not trustworthy because it has been corrupted over
time? [2.8]
Possible answers:
•Ask why they believe this to be the case.
• Explain while we no longer have the original autographs of the
Old Testament (just as we do not have the original autographs of
any ancient piece of writing), we do have over 17,000 manuscript
portions of the Old Testament. By comparing these manuscripts,
biblical scholars have recovered much of the content of the original
autographs with a high degree of accuracy.
APOLOGETICS G
• Recognize our knowledge of the scribes who produced the Masoretic Text (the
definitive copy of the Old Testament) reveals the extreme care they practiced in
copying Scripture.
•Explain that the copy of Isaiah found among the Dead Sea Scrolls, which was
1,000 years older than any copy of Isaiah known at the time, is remarkably
similar to later copies of Isaiah.
5. How might you answer the claim that the New Testament is not
trustworthy because the known manuscripts contain over 400,000 textual
variants? [2.8]
Acknowledge this is the case. Explain it is essential to distinguish between
different kinds of variants. Many variants are not of great concerndifferent
spellings, word orders, or words that cannot be easily translated. The most
important variants are those that affect the actual meaning of a text. Recognize
that a thorough study of these 400,000 variants reveals only about 4,000
have any effect on the meaning of a text, and none of them affect any major
doctrines.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. What is a presupposition? How do our presuppositions affect
the way we read the Bible? [2.9]
A presupposition is a belief you assume is true. We all have
presuppositions that we bring to the biblical text, and they can lead
us to read and interpret Scripture in radically different ways. For
example, if you presuppose the supernatural realm is non-existent,
you will find the Bible's claims about God and miracles to be foolish
or misguided at best. If, in contrast, you presuppose the existence
of a supernatural realm, you will find assertions about God and
miracles much more believable. No one can completely set aside
his or her presuppositions. We must allow our presuppositions to
be challenged by the biblical text if we are to understand what God
wants to communicate to us.
2. What is the difference between literary and historical context?
[2.9]
The historical context is a text's time, place, culture, and audience. It
helps us understand who a particular book was written to and how
that person or group of people would have interpreted what it says.
Literary context is a text's genre, structure, and grammar. It helps us
understand the meaning of individual words and phrases within a
larger whole.
3. Why is context important for Bible reading? [2.9]
The Bible was not written directly to us, though it was written for
us. We need to first understand what a passage meant to the original
audience, and what it means within the context of the whole Bible
before we can apply it to our own time and place. If we ignore
context, we might misconstrue the Bible's meaning.
4. Explain what it means to engage the biblical authors in a
dialogue. [2.9]
Engaging the biblical authors in a dialogue means giving them the
benefit of the doubt until we have understood what they are trying
to say. It requires imaginatively entering their world and looking
around, not bringing them into our world and judging them by our
modern standards and ways of thinking. It means asking thoughtful
questions of the biblical text and seeking answers through personal
study and conversation with other Christians.
5. How does the entire Bible point to Jesus? (2.10]
One of the Bible's main purposes is to reveal God to us. God is most
clearly revealed in Jesus John 1:18, Colossians 2:9). The whole
Bible is about Jesus. The Old Testament story looks ahead to him;
the Gospels speak directly about him; the epistles and other New
Testament writings look back to his life, death, and resurrection.

having the student who will answer stand up. Answers
must be given in a "who" or "what" question form. (For
example, "What is the Bible?" or "Who is Abraham?") If
the fastest team answers correctly within 10 seconds, the
points for that clue will be added to the team's score. If
the team answers incorrectly, the same number of points
will be lost. In that case, the second-fastest team may take
a guess for half of the point value. If correct, that team
receives the points. If they are incorrect, the third-fastest
team can make a guess for a point value that is again
reduced by half. The team that answers correctly chooses
the next clue.

• video 1:
What Makes the Bible Unique?
1. The speaker identifies six reasons that make the Bible unique
and uses a hand as a mnemonic (a memory aid). What is the first
reason, which he associates with the thumb?
The thumb reminds us that the Bible changes lives in a positive way.
2. What reason does the speaker associate with the pointer
finger?
The pointer finger reminds us that the Bible points to history.
The Bible is unique because it is supported by archaeological and
historical evidence.
3. What reason does the speaker associate with the big (the
longest) finger?
The big finger reminds us that the Bible answers the big questions of
life, and its answers are consistent with science and with our deepest
intuitions.
4. What reason does the speaker associate with the ring finger?
The ring finger reminds us that the Bible has supernatural unity.
Despite its being written over 1,500 years by different authors, it
tells a consistent and unified story from Genesis to Revelation-that
God created humanity, humanity rebelled, and God sent a Messiah
to rescue humanity.
5. What reason does the speaker associate with the pinky finger?
The pinky reminds us that the Bible includes prophecies. It predicts
future events, and it predicted the nature of the Messiah's life, death,
and crucifixion.
6. What reason does the speaker associate with a fist?
The fist reminds us that the Bible is a fighter. It supernaturally resists
corruption. The ancient text has been faithfully transmitted to us
today.
7. Which reason do you find most compelling? Why?
Answers will vary.

How Do We Know the Bible Is Reliable?
1. What good things do people who do not believe the Bible is God's
revelation say about the Bible?

The speaker said the Bible is accurate historically and archaeologically. External
evidence from secular history and archaeology confirms the details described in
the Bible.
4. What does the speaker say is internal confirmation for the Bible?
The speaker says the fulfillment of prophecies provides internal confirmation of

Apologetics drove the speaker back into Scripture because it asked the questions

What Is the Christian Metanarrative?
1. What does it mean to say that the Bible has an overarching story or
metanarrative?
It means the Bible tells a complete story about the way the world is, where we
came from (our origin), where we are going (our destination), and the way we

metanarrative?
Home, away, and home again.
4. How do the four words relate to our lives?
Every day, we get up, leave for work or school, and then return home. Beliefs
cause behavior. So, the four words and the rhythm of our lives are like a
microcosm of the story of the world, the metanarrative of Scripture.
