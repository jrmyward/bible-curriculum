OVERVIEW
Human beings are primarily defined
as created in God's image and loved
unconditionally by him. As Christians,
our identity is as God's children, saved and
redeemed by Jesus. Our identity informs our
calling to God's mission.
Because human beings have a sinful
nature, we can never earn God's favor
through good works. Apart from Christ,
we are unrighteous slaves to sin. We will
always be disappointed if we define ourselves
primarily by what we do. But by his grace
and through our faith in Jesus' death and
resurrection, God made a way for us to be
justified and sanctified. As Christians, our
mission is to live in love and relationship
with God and others, remember the biblical
story, and bring the gospel to those far from
Christ. We can live out our calling only by
abiding in Christ and cooperating with the
Holy Spirit.
Christian mission includes living as the
people of God in community, following the
commands of Christ, and telling the gospel
clearly to others. The Great Commission
implies that the telling aspect of our mission
includes evangelism, discipleship, and
missions. Some examples of these three
activities in action include the women at
Jesus' tomb, Mary (Marthas sister) sitting
at Jesus' feet, and Peter sharing the gospe
with Cornelius. Jesus disciples and the
apostle Paul modeled apologetics in action
as they defended the faith, commended it,
and translated it for listeners of different
backgrounds. Christians today can follow
their examples as we carry out God's mission
in our own lives.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Why do our actions as Christians matter? [4.1]
Our actions do not save us or define us. However, they show the
reality of our faith. Faith without works is dead. Christians are
not saved merely for themselves, but so they might become active
participants in God's mission. The gospel trains us in right living,
so how we live demonstrates love for God and others.
2. Why is it dangerous to define ourselves by what we do? [4.2]
Because we are limited and flawed beings, we can never perfectly
meet our own standards, let alone God's moral standards. So, if our
actions define us, it could be easy to become discouraged and think
God is disappointed in us. It could also be easy to start comparing
our works to the works of others and develop a self-righteous spirit.
3. How do we stay rooted in God's love? [4.2]
We stay rooted in God's love by recognizing that our identity is
grounded in God's love for us, not in what we do. It also helps to
remember what God has done for us through Christ's death—
providing justification— and is doing in us through sanctification.
4. What does it mean to be justified and sanctified? [4.2]
To be justified is to be declared righteous before God through
the work of Jesus Christ, who died on the cross to defeat evil and
pay the penalty for our sin so that he might declare us righteous
before God the Father. To be sanctified is to undergo the process of
becoming like Jesus. When we trust in Jesus, the Holy Spirit works
in us to transform us over time. Our role is to cooperate with him
by abiding in Christ and practicing spiritual disciplines.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Why aren't humans capable of fixing the world? [4.3]
Humans cannot fix the world because we are limited and marred
by sin. All our efforts to fix the world will be in some way tainted
or frustrated by sin. This does not mean we should do nothing.
We should anticipate the arrival of God's kingdom by doing what
is right and serving the people around us. However, we need to
recognize that Jesus is the only one who can save the world.
2. How do the Apostles' Creed and the Lord's Prayer help us
remember our true story? [4.3]
The Apostles' Creed and Lord's Prayer help us remember the key
elements of the true story. They remind us that we depend totally
on God, our Creator, Sustainer, Redeemer, Friend, and the primary
actor in the story. Christ has accomplished our salvation, and only
he can bring final restoration. We pray for God's kingdom to arrive
and remember our role is to share the gospel and live as ambassadors
of God's kingdom now.
3. What does it mean for Christians to be salt and light? [4.4]
Christians become like salt through sharing the gospel and doing
good works. They help preserve the world from decay, hold back sin,
and demonstrate a tasteful and attractive character for the world to
see. Christians are like light in that they hold out the hope of the
gospel, which brings light to a dark and troubled world.
4. What are practical ways Christians could act as salt and light
today? [4.4]
Answers will vary. Christians can be salt and light through sharing
the gospel, making the case for Christianity as the best model for
human flourishing, and caring for their communities by advocating
for the unborn, reaching across racial and political divides, caring
for the poor, stewarding creation, caring for orphans, giving shelter
to the homeless, and much more. Christians can point others to the
only hope for a lost and dying world, Jesus Christ.
5. In what ways are being, doing, and telling part of our
participation in God's mission? [4.4]
The being aspect involves how Christians live among each other.
We are called to love, serve, forgive, care for, and encourage one
another in obedience to God. The doing aspect deals with personal
interactions with non-Christians, including showing care and
concern for their spiritual and physical needs. The telling aspect
involves sharing the gospel through verbal or written words.
Without this telling aspect, the gospel cannot be fully heard.

Discipleship is necessary for all Christians at all times. Being a

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. How can apologetics serve as a precursor to evangelism? [4.7]
Apologetics can help us answer questions that cause non-Christians
to doubt the truth of Christianity. It can also remove roadblocks that
may prevent others from trusting Jesus.
2. What do you think it means to offer apologetic responses that
are reasonable, relevant, and desirable? [4.7]
An apologetic response is reasonable when clearly and accurately
stated in a true and logical way. It is relevant when it specifically
addresses a person's questions, problems, or needs. It is desirable
when it is presented attractively so others can see its value and wish
(at least in some way) for it to be true.
3. How can Christians commend the faith? [4.7]
Christians commend the faith by offering it to others in a way that
shows it to be good, true, and beautiful. This means simply holding
up Christianity for consideration and scrutiny without being pushy
or argumentative. This can include living according to the gospel
and showing how Christianity is reasonable and relevant to our lives
and the world.
4. What does it mean to translate the faith? What Christian
words might need to be translated? [4.7]
To translate the faith is to make it understandable to a particular
audience. This means speaking their language, explaining Christian
jargon, and using words and phrases they can connect with. Some
words that might need to be translated are: born again, saved, sin,
atonement, and gospel.
5. What can we learn from Jesus and Peter about defending the
faith? [4.8]
Evidence is an essential component of apologetics arguments. Both
Jesus and Peter pointed to specific evidence to support their message.
Jesus drew attention to his miracles and let John and his disciples
draw their own conclusions. Peter argued that his gospel message
was based on eyewitness testimony.
6. What can we learn from Philip and Tabitha about
commending the faith? (4.8)
Philip shows us that sometimes the best approach is simply to invite
people to experience or investigate Christianity for themselves
rather than trying to combat their cynicism abour Jesus. Tabitha
shows us the power of good works as an important witness to the
transformative power of the gospel, and her acts of charity in her
community were a powerful illustration of the beauty of the gospel.
APOLOGETICS G
7. What can we learn from Matthew and Jesus about translating the faith? [4.8]
We need to speak directly to the concerns of our audience and speak in a language
they understand. Matthew began his account with a detailed genealogy, which
showed that Jesus was in the line of David. This would have been impressive
and convincing to his Jewish readers. Jesus told stories about ordinary things to
illustrate much larger truths about the kingdom of God.
