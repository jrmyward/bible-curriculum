4. Deconstruction and Reconstruction
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to explain cognitive dissonance, faith
deconstruction, and faith reconstruction.
One of the most common causes of doubt is cognitive dissonance, an internal conflict
arising from incompatibility between different beliefs or between beliefs and actions.
Let's say you believe, as the Bible says, that God loves all people (John 3:16, Romans
5:8). But you notice some passages in the Old Testament seem to suggest that God hates
some people, like the Canaanites (Deuteronomy 20:16-18, Psalm 11:5). "If God loves all
people," you wonder, "why did he command the Israelites to wipe out the Canaanites!"
-a question we will address. What you believe about God and how God acts seems
contradictory. If this remains unresolved, you may experience cognitive dissonance.
When we see that we hold conflicting beliefs or that our behavior is out of line with
our beliefs, we may seek to investigate or make a change. In this way, cognitive dissonance
could help us grow. The Holy Spirit helps us to work through cognitive dissonance,
nudging us to study Scripture, pray, take our faith more seriously and to bring our
behavior into greater alignment with our beliefs.
Cognitive dissonance may also lead to a more difficult process called deconstruction.
That term was originally used to describe a form of postmodern literary analysis whereby a
reader seeks to dissect a text by exposing what is perceived to be its underlying ideologies.
At a more popular level, faith deconstruction refers to a process in which previously
accepted beliefs are dismantled.

experiences affect our perception of what is true. Earlier, we spoke of cognitive dissonance

of cognitive dissonance also arises when our beliefs do not match our experiences. This
situation frequently leads to doubt.

abstract question in search of a theological answer. The second is a question caused by a
personal experience of pain or suffering.

9. Attending to Doubt
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to demonstrate ways to deal with doubt.
We have now considered some of the major reasons why doubts arise and offered a few
ideas for how to work through them. Doubt is not a unique experience. While doubt
can be unsettling, it can also be "a healthy part of a Christ-centered, thoughtful life."6
According to pastor and apologist Tim Keller:
A faith without doubts is like a human body without any antibodies in it. People
who blithely go through life too busy or indifferent to ask hard questions about why
they believe as they do will find
themselves defenseless against either
the experience of tragedy or the
probing questions of a smart skeptic.
A person's faith can collapse almost
overnight if [he or] she has failed over
the years to listen patiently to [his or]
her own doubts, which should only be
discarded after long reflection.?
Hopefully, this brief look at
the complex topic of doubt has
demonstrated that while there is no one-size-fits-all method for dealing with doubt, it can
be approached thoughtfully. Each person who doubts requires a personalized approach
characterized by self-reflection, attentive listening, compassion, and patience. In addition
to these, here are a few general principles that can help you attend to your doubts as well
as the doubts of others.
When it comes to personal doubt:
• Remember that doubt is not a bad thing. It can lead to great spiritual growth.
• Examine your doubt to see where it is coming from—is it an intellectual,
emotional, experiential, or practical doubt?
• Pray. Tell God about the doubts you are experiencing and ask for guidance.
• Take action. Depending on the nature of your doubt, you may need to seek
intellectual answers through books and competent authorities, engage your
emotions in the pursuit of truth, talk to someone about your experiences, or turn
away from sin.
• Read Scripture. Rather than simply relying on what others tell you about the Bible,
read it for yourself. If your doubt is related to something in Scripture, investigate the
appropriate passages and seek help from commentaries and other resources.
• Doubt your doubts. Your doubts do not always tell you the truth about reality, so
learn how to question them.
• Don't doubt alone. Find wise, loving, compassionate, and biblically grounded
friends or mentors to accompany you through your doubts or deconstruction. Seek
God's truth in relationship with other people.
What about interacting with others who doubt? An excellent place to start is Jude 1:22.
We need to first "have mercy on those who doubr." We also need to avoid judgmentalism
and seek to understand where their doubt is coming from. Only after listening carefully
and asking clarifying questions should we attempt a response. Try to avoid two errors.
First, don't hit doubters with dogma. Dogma is a belief that a group holds as absolute
and authoritative. Dogma has an important place in Christian theology, bur
dogmatic statements (such as, "The Bible says Jesus rose from the dead.
Since you're a Christian, you should just believe it.") rarely help those
who doubt. Second, beware of relying too much on your knowledge or
cleverness. The following poem reflects on this danger:
The Apologist's Evening Prayer
From all my lame defeats and oh! Much more
From all the victories that I seemed to score;
From cleverness shot forth on Thy behalf
At which, while angels weep, the audience laugh;
From all my proofs of Thy divinity,
Thou, who wouldst give no sign, deliver me.
Thoughts are but coins. Let me not trust, instead
Of Thee, their thin worn image of Thy head.
From all my thoughts, even from my thoughts of Thee,
O thou fair Silence, fall, and set me free.
Lord of the narrow gate and the needles eye,
Take from me all my trumpery lest I die.
-C. S. Lewis!®
Lewis knew the perpetual temptation of self-reliance and overconfidence in apologetics.
He warned that apologetics itself could be a cause of doubt. "No doctrine of that Faith
seems to me so spectral, so unreal as one that I have just successfully defended in a public
debate." Lewis' point was that if we are to do apologetics well, we should not rely too
heavily on our own reason, forgetting the reality we are defending. Instead, we seek to
fall back "continually ... into the Reality... Christ Himself" This means regularly
attending to our personal relationship with Jesus.
We are called to demonstrate the reality of Christ to the world in the power of the
Spirit. And only the reality of Christ can bring people to faith despite their doubts. The
best thing we can do for those doubring or deconstructing is to be with them, listen to
them, walk with them, pray with them, share their pain, and sometimes just be silent.
This is how we demonstrate the loving and compassionate reality of Christ.

10. Conclusion
In this life, it is unlikely that our doubts will be entirely vanquished, all our questions will
be thoroughly answered, or we will always be able to make sense of our experiences. There
is much that we will only understand when the new creation arrives. In the meantime, we
aim to grow in holiness, understanding, and love.
We can seek answers to our doubts, whether they be intellectual, emotional,
experiential, or practical. Yet the goal of the Christian life is not to remove all doubt.
And that's good news because, technically speaking, that would be impossible. Christian
theologian Alister McGrath sums it up well,
As human beings, we have no choice but to realize we must live in the absence of any
clear, unambiguous, absolute, and purely rational truths. We must indeed articulate
and defend criteria by which our beliefs may be justified; yet we must also realize that
those beliefs may lie beyond proof.2°
Part of what it means to be a human is to recognize that we depend on God. We were
made with a seemingly boundless capacity for learning and growth but not the ability to
know all God knows. In fact, there are some things humans may not
know (Deuteronomy 29:29). Furthermore, sin has affected our reason
and emotions, making the quest for truth challenging. God is with us
in doubt, but he does not always tell us what we want to know when
we want to know it, nor does he always answer our doubts the way we
would like (see Job 38-41). God invites us into a loving relationship
where our understanding of him and our trust in him can grow. In
this relationship, our search for answers begins and ends with God.
Doubt, then, is something we can learn to live with. John Calvin
put it this way:
Surely, while we teach that faith ought to be certain and assured,
we cannot imagine any certainty that is not tinged with doubt, or
any assurance that is not assailed by some anxiety. We [also] say that
believers are in perpetual conflict with their own unbelief. [We cannot say our consciences
[will be] undisturbed. [Yet] we deny that ... [we can] fall away and depart from the
certain assurance received from God's mercy.?!
You may never be able to satisfy all your intellectual doubts. Specific emotional
difficulties may cause you to doubt (again) something you used to believe confidently. You
may have an experience that throws your entire belief system into question. Or, you may
be plagued by doubt as you struggle with Christianity's practical relevance. But no matter
what, God's mercy and grace are available to you. God is near to you in your times of
doubt. You may not always be confident, but you are always safe with God.
One way that God shows us his grace is by forming and calling us into the church. As
messy as it sometimes is, all Christians need to find a community of fellow Christians who
can walk alongside them in their doubts. In the company of those who are committed to
Jesus, honest about their doubts, and willing to explore, we can all grow together as we
find answers to our doubts and learn to live in faith even when we don't have answers. The
church can provide a safe space where we can explore our doubts in the pursuit of truth.
But what is truth? Is it possible to know truth? We'll explore this in the next chapter.
