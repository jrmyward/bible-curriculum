4. Free Will and the Origin of Evil
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to validate God's design and purpose of free will.
The Christian approach to the problem of evil begins with Creation. God created the

Seven times in Genesis 1, God calls his creation good. Good is that which embodies or

Evil had no place in God's good creation. So, if God did not create it, where did evil

Though God did not create evil, he did create

actual. Why would God do this? Couldn't he have created a world in which it was
impossible for people to choose evil? And even if he didn't do that, couldn't he stop evil or
at least erase the consequences of sin?
There are no easy answers to these questions, but that does not mean there are no
answers at all. One possible explanation is the free-will defense, articulated by philosopher
Alvin Plantinga. Here's how it goes:
A world containing creatures who are significantly free (and freely perform more good
than evil actions) is more valuable, all else being equal, than a world containing no free
creatures at all. Now God can create free creatures, but He can't cause or determine
them to do only what is right. For if He does so, then they aren't significantly free
after all; and they do not do what is right freely. To create creatures capable of moral
good, therefore, He must create creatures capable of moral evil; and He can't give these
creatures the freedom to perform evil and at the same time prevent them from doing
so. As it turned out, sadly enough, some of the free creatures God created went wrong
in the exercise of their freedom; this is the source of moral evil. The fact that free
creatures sometimes go wrong, however, counts neither against God's omnipotence nor
against His goodness; for He could have forestalled the occurrence of moral evil only
by removing the possibility of moral good!
Making creatures free to choose good or evil but preventing them from choosing
evil is not freedom. God could, of course, have made us robors, incapable of acting on
our own. Yet, we cannot truly love unless we are free to act and choose between hate
and love. If God prevented free creatures from choosing evil, he would destroy the
greatest possible good, the ability to love!" Without freedom, our humanity would be
severely diminished. To desire this kind of world is essentially "wishing ourselves out of
existence."2o The free-will defense provides one possible explanation for moral evil.
But what of natural evil? Many Christians believe that natural evil, like moral evil,
can be traced back to the fall of humanity into sin (Genesis 3). Romans 8:20-21 supports
this idea by saying that the creation was "subjected to futility" and will one day be "set
free from its bondage to corruption." Some philosophers suggest that God made a world
structured to maximize good and minimize evil-something referred to as the best
possible world. In this view, fires, earthquakes, and tornadoes are challenging realities, but
adherents to this view consider they may be a part of the world's proper functioning and
the maximization of good.? They may make types and incidents of good possible that
would not exist otherwise. Some suggest that these natural events existed even before the
Fall, but God may have protected humans from their destructive consequences. Still
others suggest that natural evil could be the result of demonic activity. In summary, a
good God can have good reasons for allowing what we call natural evil.

9. The Church's Response to Evil
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to describe four ways in which the church can
respond to evil.
We live in an in-between time. God's kingdom has arrived but has not yet been fully
realized. What are we to do in the meantime? This is where the church comes in. The
church is God's instrument to proclaim the good news of God's kingdom. As a body of
believers, we announce Jesus victory over sin and death and live our lives in anticipation
of his return and the earth's ultimate renewal.
N. T. Wright summarized it this way: "The cross is not just an example to be followed;
It is an achievement to be worked out, put into practice."* How does the church do this?
Let's consider four ways.

First, the church laments. A lament is an expression of grief, regret, sorrow, or sadness.
When we lament, we do not minimize pain, pretend it doesn't exist, or act as though
everything is really okay. We passionately and urgently cry out to God over our suffering
and on behalf of others in distress. As Christians, we are called to "weep with those
who weep" (Romans 12:15). Ecclesiastes reminds us that there is a time for everything,
including mourning (Ecclesiastes 3:4). In lament, we express our anguish without
attempting to offer explanations. We do not use cheap slogans or speculate about what
God's reasons might be for allowing suffering. Instead, we sit with those who suffer,
sometimes in silence—for as long as they need. In so doing, we become a tangible
expression of Christ's loving presence, a sign that God has not abandoned them in their
suffering.
The second way the church responds to evil is by recognizing that suffering is
essential to our calling as Christians. Jesus promises that there will be trials in this life
(John 16:33), and he calls us to deny ourselves, pick up our cross, and follow him (Luke
9:23). We are not to seek out suffering, but neither are we to avoid it at all costs. It is no
mark of good character to suffer for doing evil, but if we suffer for doing good, we will
be blessed (1 Peter 3:13-17). When we suffer for the gospel's sake, we suffer for a good
cause. Jesus promises to be with those who suffer, ultimately giving them the victory
and eternal life (1 Peter 5:10, Revelation 7:9-17).
Third, the church fights against evil and anticipates Christ's final restoration by
working for good here and now. This means fighting against corruption and injustice,
protecting the unborn, standing up for human rights, and mobilizing people to help the

world lost in sin and suffering.
10. Conclusion
In this chapter, we've seen an all loving God who knows that evil exists and cares enough
to do something about it. But evil only makes sense in a world in which God exists and is
the standard of good. God may have reasons for allowing evil, which we, in our finitude,
simply cannot understand. Yet, even if we do not have a full answer, we have the assurance
of God's presence and his ultimate victory over evil. Let's conclude this chapter with a few
thoughts on the book of Job, which brings us face-to-face with the mystery of evil.
While Job was mourning his unjust suffering, three friends came around to comfort
him. When they saw his condition, they "raised their voices and wept." For the first
seven days, they sat with Job in total silence (Job 2:11-13). When Job finally opened up,
pouring out his anguish and misery, each of his friends replied with words of advice,
trying to explain why all these bad things had
happened. Undoubtedly, they only wanted to help
Job, but their comfort was better when they were
silent. Latching onto the wisdom of their day, they
affirmed God's justice and declared that he could
do no wrong; if Job was suffering, there must be
an explanation—namely, Job had sinned and was
refusing to repent.
Perhaps it would have been easier for Job to
simply admit to some sin, yet he knew he had
done nothing to deserve his suffering. Job longed
for an audience with God:
Oh, that I knew where I might find him,
that I might come even to his seat!
I would lay my case before him
and fill my mouth with arguments. —Job 23:3-4
After 35 chapters of discussion between Job and his friends, God does, indeed, show
up. Finally, we will have an explanation; Job's suffering will all make sense. God can tell
Job that it was all a test, that it was Satan's doing. Yet, nothing of the kind comes out of
God's mouth. Instead of giving Job answers, God asks him many questions. At the end of
God's questioning, Job declares that God can do all things and his purposes can never be
thwarted. He acknowledges his limited understanding and repents. His concluding words
to God are striking: "I had heard of you by the hearing of the ear, but now my eye sees
you" (Job 42:5). In seeing God, Job finds himself once again able to trust him.
That invitation to trust is the same one God offers us today. Thousands of people
throughout history have called out to God in their suffering. They complain, doubt, and
curse, but in their suffering, they still find hope in God and hold onto faith. How this
happens is a mystery. Perhaps they, like Job, have had a personal encounter with God.
