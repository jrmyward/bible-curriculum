1. Developing a Personal Apologetic Approach
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to construct a personal approach to incorporating
apologetics into your daily life and relationships.
Throughout this book, we have looked at 10 major apologetic issues—the trustworthiness
of Scripture, the nature of truth, religious pluralism, atheism and skepticism, the existence
of God and miracles, the relationship between science and Christianity, the problem of
evil, hell, slavery and violence in Scripture, and lastly, sex and gender in Scripture. (These
were covered in Chapters 3 and 8-16.) In this chapter, we'll move away from addressing
apologetic questions to discussing what it looks like to share this information with others.

Your approach to apologetics will be shaped by how God has gifted
you. To do apologetics well, be yourself. This would be easy if it were
not for sin, which complicates everything, so "be yourself" sometimes
turns into "do whatever feels good to you." That's not what we mean.
To be yourself in the Christian sense is to live into who God has
created you to be. In Christ, we are a new creation, no longer bound
by slavery to sin, and called to live holy lives (Romans 6:17-18,
2 Corinthians 5:17, 1 Thessalonians 4:1-12). Living as a new creation
means dying daily to sin and submitting all our thoughts and actions
to Christ (Romans 6:11, Galatians 5:24, James 4:7).
We cannot understand or become who we are meant to be apart from God. That is
why spiritual disciplines are so important. We draw deeper into our relationship with God
through practices like Scripture reading, meditation, and prayer. In so doing, we come to
know God personally, experiencing his deep love for us and learning about his desire for
our good and compassion for the world. We learn to worship him as Lord and imitate him
in every area of our lives.
We also cannot become who God created us to be in isolation from others. Good
spiritual companions help us live into our strengths and acknowledge our weaknesses. In
church community, we also learn that we do not have to be experts in everything and can
give and receive encouragement, correction, and support. God has equipped each person
with different gifts so that, together, we can grow into the fullness of who God made us to
be (Ephesians 4:1-16).
When it comes to apologetics, being yourself means that you do not attempt to be
someone you are not. Some people excel in public speaking, others are skilled in writing,
and others commend the gospel primarily through everyday actions and conversations.
Few of us will be called to be full-time apologists, taking up university posts or traveling
around the country giving lectures. Most of us will do apologetics in quieter ways—in
coffee shop conversations, in our backyards, at school or work, through acts of service,
compassionate listening, or generous giving.

New believers also need to know that the grace they have received is not a license to
do whatever they please but a catalyst for holy living in obedience to Jesus (Romans G).
You can explain that when we trust Christ, we make him Lord of our life. We commit to
following his ways (Luke 9:23, John 14:21). Growing to be more like Christ is a lifelong
process, but it begins by getting to know Jesus in the Gospels, following his commands,
and submitting ourselves to the transforming work of the Holy Spirit. We can help
new believers internalize these truths by reading Scripture with them, demonstrating
unconditional love and forgiveness, and showing Jesus' love in our actions of service.
Relationship with others. Suppose you grew up on a farm in rural lowa, and then you
pick up and move to New York City one day. How would that feel? You would have to
learn a new culture, way of communicating, and way of living. Taking the subway to work
would be a huge adjustment from driving your pickup truck across town. The city's culture
would also be far different—there would be new people to meet, new words to learn, and

background. The church is "a people

from other friends or family. That is
why no one is meant to do Christianity alone. Every new believer needs a wider body of

along the faith journey. So, one of the most important things we can do for a new believer
is to encourage him or her to connect with the family of God. Finding community in a
biblically sound, loving church is vital for all believers.
Doctrine and apologetics. Another crucial area of discipleship is learning sound
doctrine. Our spiritual lives are grounded in core biblical truths, and unless we grasp
these, our foundation will be shaky. New believers need instruction in the nature of God
as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit; the grand story of Scripture and their place within it; and
essential doctrines about salvation, sanctification, judgment, and eternity. New believers
also need to continue learning about apologetics, as their faith will eventually come under
fire. Connecting them with a local pastor or Christian mentor is an excellent place to start.
Consider also the role God might want you to play in a new believer's life. Ask your pastor
what resources you could use to help instruct a new believer in the basics.

10. Conclusion
We share the good news with others through evangelism, apologetics, and discipleship
because we follow Jesus, and this is the work he has called us to do. Sharing the good news
is no mere duty but the most incredible privilege and joyous work imaginable. When we
follow Jesus this way, we receive a fantastic reward—we experience the joy of freely giving
of ourselves as we participate in God's mission to the world.
This chapter has been about sharing apologetics with others. But perhaps you are
thinking, I am still determining if Christianity is true. I'm not even sure if I'm a Christian
or if I buy all the arguments in this book. What should I do? If this is where you are, that
is okay. God wants to know you wherever you are on the spiritual journey. Hopefully,
this book has given you awareness of the reasonableness of the Christian faith. There are,
undoubtedly, many more questions to explore.
But now, it's time for some questions to consider: Are you curious enough to ask the
hard questions? Are you willing to do the work of investigating Christianity? Are you open
to the possibility that Jesus wants a personal relationship with you? Are you willing to give
this a chance? If you answered yes to any of those
questions, this book is only the beginning. Earlier
