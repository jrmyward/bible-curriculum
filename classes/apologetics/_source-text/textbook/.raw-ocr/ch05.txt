2. Empowered by the Holy Spirit
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to describe the effect
of the Holy Spirit's power on the disciples as they
preached the gospel.
When Jesus commanded his disciples to "go therefore and
make disciples of all nations," he bracketed his command with
two essential truths: "All authority in heaven and on earth has
been given to me" and "Behold, I am with you always, to the end of
the age" (Matthew 28:18-20). These truths empower us to participate
in God's mission confidently. First, we know that Jesus has ultimate authority.
Nothing can overcome the gospel, and no person is outside of Jesus' reach. Second, we
can have confidence that when we share the gospel, Jesus is by our side. God calls us and
equips us to form attitudes and habits that reflect Christ, and this includes releasing our
fears of what being a follower of Christ may require of us.*
Of course, Jesus is not with us physically when we share the gospel. So, what did
he mean by saying,
"I am with you always"? Before his death, Jesus promised to send
someone in his place to be with the disciples. He told them it was actually to their
advantage that he was going away so that "the Helper" would come to them (John 16:7).
This Helper is the Holy Spirit, the third person of the Trinity. A person receives the
Holy Spirit when one puts their trust in Christ. He dwells in the heart of each Christian
(Galatians 4:6), teaching, bringing to mind Jesus' words, and guiding each of us in the
way of truth (John 14:26).
Fulfilling the prophet's words in Joel 2:28-29, the arrival of the Holy Spirit was a
dramatic event. The story is told in Acts 2. Jesus had ascended to heaven, and the
disciples were waiting in Jerusalem. Fifty days had passed since Jesus left, and as they
were gathered together:
And suddenly there came from heaven a sound like a mighty rushing wind, and
it filled the entire house where they were sitting. And divided tongues as of fire
appeared to them and rested on each one of them. And they were all filled with the
Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit gave them utterance.
-Acts 2:2-4
The disciples, filled with God's Spirit, went out into the streets and began to speak in
other languages. All the people who heard them were amazed; some even thought they
were drunk (Acts 2:15). Undaunted, the apostle Peter stood up and preached a sermon
about impending judgment and the rescue God had accomplished through Jesus. Cut to
the heart by Peter's sermon, they cried out, "Brothers, what shall we do?" Peter's response
was immediate: "Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for
the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit" (Acts 2:37-38).
Approximately 3,000 people became Christians that day, now known as Pentecost.
The Spirit's power remains evident throughout the rest of the book of Acts. Though
packed full of stories about missionaries taking the gospel all over the Roman Empire, the
Holy Spirit is the main character in Acts. This shows us that the power to speak boldly
in Jesus' name comes not from our own savvy or intellectual
brilliance but from the Holy Spirit. Jesus impressed this on his
disciples. He said they should not be worried when they face
trials and persecutions because the Holy Spirit would give them
the words to say (Matthew 10:19-20). Throughout Acts, we see
this happening as Peter and John stood before the Sanhedrin
(Acts 4:1-22), as Stephen preached before his accusers (Acts 7),
as Paul defended himself before Agrippa (Acts 26), and in many
other examples. Jesus' disciples, who were afraid and confused,
were now empowered by the Holy Spirit, boldly declaring Jesus
as Lord and "living the gospel of Jesus through the power of the
Holy Spirit."
So we can be confident when sharing the gospel, knowing
that the power to share this message is not something we must
generate within ourselves. If we rely on our own strengths and
abilities, we will almost certainly fail. The power for mission
comes from the Holy Spirit, who guides us daily and gives us the
courage to speak about Jesus.

• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to apply five techniques to prepare emotionally
for evangelistic or apologetic encounters.
As we prepare for participation in God's mission, we also need to examine the role of
emotions, the mental states arising from temperament, mood, circumstances, relationships,
and more. Emotions are the natural ways we
respond to life's many joys and challenges.
All aspects of our emotional lives are relevant
in evangelistic or apologetic encounters. Let's
consider a few ways we can prepare for these

Assess your emotions. When you think about
sharing the gospel, how do you feel? Do you
feel excited and confident, grateful for the
opportunity to share? Or do you feel pressure
and shame? Perhaps you feel indifference or
apathy, as in, "it's not my problem," or find
yourself prideful, focused only on winning

an emotional dimension. God feels love, joy, compassion, anger, grief, and pleasure.
Zephaniah 3:17 exemplifies how God expresses his great joy and love for us. In Mark 3:5,
Jesus feels anger and is deeply distressed over people with stubborn hearts. You can tell
God how you are feeling and know he understands; then, pray for a clear mind, courage,
the right words, and a willing heart to love others.
Beware of pressure and shame. Many Christians feel intense pressure to share the gospel
with every person they meet, and when they fail to do so, they experience shame. To avoid
this trap, we must remember that the Holy Spirit leads and guides, inviting us into greater
experiences of God's grace and nudging us to share that grace with others (John 14:16-26).
The Holy Spirit will not pressure, threaten, or induce shame. In contrast, Satan seeks to
coerce and manipulate us. He pushes us along, causing us to feel pressure, threatening
shame, or telling us that we are a disappointment to God. By listening to the Spirit and
rejecting Satan's lies, we can participate in God's mission with joy.!
Be authentic. When you are sharing the gospel, be yourself. Lean into the gifts and
talents God has given you. Don't try to be like some famous preacher or use someone else's
prepackaged words. Be authentic and share your heart with the person. If you fear that
what you say might offend that person, tell him or her that and ask for understanding. If
you are unsure of how to answer a question, admit it. If something they say upsets you, ask
clarifying questions for greater understanding. The best way to prepare to respond in these
Ways is to practice being authentic daily. Don't try to be someone you're not. Remember
that you are God's beloved, and live confidently within that place of security.

8.A Loving Encounter
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to describe principles for evangelism and
apologetics from Jesus' conversation with the Samaritan woman.
If we desire to demonstrate Jesus' love in our evangelistic and apologetic encounters,
we can do no better than to learn from him. Jesus came to seek and save the lost (Luke
19:10). One of his most remarkable encounters, the one with the woman at the well, is
recorded in John 4:1-42. John tells us that Jesus was on his way from Judea to Galilee
and that he had to pass through Samaria (John 4:4). In Jesus' day, Jews frequently went
out of their way to avoid Samaria or at least tried to get through it as quickly as possible.
Samaritans were despised by the Jews, so much so that Jews were instructed to avoid even
crossing shadows with a Samaritan. Given this animosity, it seems there was a deeper
reason why Jesus felt compelled to go there.
Coming upon an old well outside the city of Sychar, Jesus sat down and sent his
disciples into town to get provisions. As he rested, a woman approached to draw water
from the well. Then Jesus smashed all social conventions.
And so when Jesus begins to speak to her, he is deliberately reaching across almost
every significant barrier that people can put up between themselves... a racial barrier,
a cultural barrier, a gender barrier, and a moral barrier.... He reaches right across all
the human divides in order to connect to her. She is amazed, and we should be, too.?
Jesus humbly revealed his vulnerability by asking the Samaritan woman for a drink.23
She was shocked because "Jews have no dealings with Samaritans" (John 4:9). According
to Jewish tradition, Samaritans were unclean, so drinking from a vessel a Samaritan had
touched would lead to defilement. Jesus, it seems, didn't care about that.
Jesus also broke convention by engaging this woman in a theological discussion.
Women in both Jewish and Samaritan societies had low social status. Rabbis would not
take them as disciples. They were not allowed to testify in court since they were considered
untrustworthy» This woman might have been pitied or despised by her neighbors since she
had been divorced five times and was currently living with a man who wasn't her husband.
She was alone when she came to the well.
Jesus first spoke to her of living water, awakening her desire to never be thirsty again.
Though he knew she was living with a man who was not her husband, Jesus didn't condemn
her for it. Then, when the Samaritan woman attempted to engage Jesus in a debate between
Jews and Samaritans about the proper place of worship, Jesus refused to get entangled. He
didn't say, "You Samaritans have it all wrong! You need to get back to Jerusalem, the one
true place of worship!" Instead, he invited her to see worship in a new way. The time is
coming, he said, "when the true worshippers will worship the Father in spirit and truth"
(John 4:23). Finally, after she raised the issue, Jesus revealed that he was the Messiah. She
responded with joy and amazement, telling her whole town about him.
There is much to learn from this encounter. First, it clarifies that the gospel is for
everyone, not just a particular ethnicity, gender, or class. Second, notice that Jesus did not
talk down to the Samaritan woman. He took her seriously and treated her with gentleness
and respect. Third, even though he confronted
her sin, he did not condemn her for it. Instead, he
offered her an opportunity for honest self-reflection.
Fourth, his message was addressed to her whole
person —both desire for "deep soul satisfaction"
(living water) and for purpose—everyone worships
something and "bases their lives on something that
requires faith."26 Fifth, Jesus didn't argue about who
was right and who was wrong; he drew attention to
what really mattered. Finally, Jesus didn't pressure
her to trust him. He simply claimed to be the
Messiah and left it up to her to decide.

Similarly, in 1 Corinthians 3:6-8, Paul spoke of the importance of both planting and
watering in God's mission. Let's consider what planting, watering, and reaping might
look like in practice.
Planting. Planting a seed is offering someone something to think about that he or she
might not have considered before. For example, Jamar hears the gospel for the first
time when Deion, a Christian student on his college campus, approaches him. Jamar is
curious and not yet ready to trust Jesus, yet a seed has been planted. Or perhaps Emma,
a committed atheist, finds herself reevaluating God's existence after she hears a speaker
make a compelling case for intelligent design. A seed has been planted. Apologist Greg
Koukl refers to this as putting a stone in
someone's shoe. "All I want to do is give
him something worth thinking about.
I want him to hobble away on a nugget
of truth that annoys him in a good
way, something he can't simply ignore
because it continues to poke at him."28
Watering. The person who waters helps
the seed to grow. For example, Jamar
finds the gospel appealing, but he is put
off by the few Christians he knows who
don't seem to live much like Jesus. Yet,
as he gets to know Deion, he notices
that Deion is humble, generous, and hospitable with his time and resources. Whether he
knows it or not, Deion is watering. In Emma's case, the seed that the speaker planted is
watered when she meets Suzanne, a thoughtful Christian who helps Emma work through
her many questions about Christianity. Eventually, Suzanne and Emma agree to study the
Bible together. Suzanne is engaged in the task of watering.
Reaping. The reaping stage is when someone puts his or her faith and trust in Jesus. Jamar
has graduated from college. Though he is not yet a Christian, he walks into a church one
Sunday where he hears a message about Jesus' death and resurrection. After the service,
Jamar speaks with the pastor and puts his faith in Jesus. The pastor is reaping the harvest
from the seed Deion planted and watered. Another harvest: after a year or so of talking
with Suzanne, Emma's final objection to Christianity collapses, and she sees her need for
Jesus. Suzanne stands by supportively as Emma acknowledges Jesus as Lord.
Who, then, is responsible for the salvation of Jamar and Emma? The Holy Spirit-who
has been drawing, convicting, and reaching out to them from day one. The credit belongs
to God alone. Yet in these conversion stories, Deion, the speaker on intelligent design,
Suzanne, and the pastor all participated in what the Holy Spirit was already doing in
Jamar and Emma's lives. As these illustrations show, those who plant or water don't always
reap, but their witness is just as essential.
10. Conclusion
Jesus' message to the Samaritan woman resulted in great fruit; the Samaritan woman and
the town shared great enthusiasm over Jesus (John 4:27-30, 39-42). Many Samaritans
came to believe that Jesus was the Messiah. His strategy was clearly effective. Yet, much
to our frustration, no one-size-fits-all method for evangelism or apologetics will guarantee
results. Sometimes, even with the best intentions and the most loving approach, we
may not see people accept the gospel or our arguments for Christianity. But judging our
participation in God's mission merely by the results we see is to misunderstand our role.
Remember, the power for mission comes from the Holy Spirit. Just as the Holy Spirit
spoke powerfully through the disciples on the day of Pentecost, he wants to speak through
us today. And we are motivated for our mission by God's love. As we grow deeper in love
with Jesus, we can't help but share that love with others.
We also discovered preparation's essential role in our participation in God's mission.
We must be willing to lay down our need to be right and successful while at the same
time preparing ourselves spiritually, intellectually, and emotionally. Doing so allows us to
adopt a personalized and loving approach that imitates the generous love of Jesus.
In our encounters, sometimes we will plant a seed, sometimes we will water, and
sometimes we will reap the harvest. No matter our role or roles in a person's life, we join
in what God has already done. Knowing that we participate in God's mission, rather than
the people responsible for accomplishing it, relieves us of the burden of "getting results."
Our job is simply to go as prepared as
possible, following the Holy Spirit's leading
to speak to whomever he wants us to. The
results are never up to us.
In fact, "winning people to Christ" is not
actually something we can do. We can only
invite people to the foot of the cross so that
they might encounter Jesus for themselves.
If we forget this, evangelism, apologetics,
and any other participation in God's
mission becomes a pride-filled contest or a
weighty burden we cannot bear.
So then, what empowered the disciples? What gave the early Christians confidence?
What helped them overcome fear and other obstacles to sharing the good news about
Jesus? The answer is this: they knew that God's Spirit was at work within and among
them, that nothing could separate them from God's love, and that they weren't responsible
for the results, only for faithfully preparing and sharing the message. These same truths
can inspire us today.
That said, trusting God to speak through us and leaving the results to him does
not negate the need for preparation. In the next chapter, we'll consider the intellect—
specifically, using logic and reason.
