2. The Concept of Hell in Scripture
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to describe the concept of eternal separation from
God in death and hell.
The Old Testament has surprisingly little to say about the fate of human beings after
death. When the Old Testament writers speak about death, they often use the word
Sheol. Sometimes translated as pit or grave, the word has various shades of meaning and
interpretations. A synonym for death, Sheol was also described as a "place of darkness."
Both the righteous and the wicked go to Sheol (Genesis 42:38, Numbers 16:30), though
this does not necessarily mean their fate is the same. Thus, Sheol may simply refer to the
place of the dead.
When we turn to the New Testament, the concept of hell as we know it begins to
emerge. It is found most frequently on the lips of Jesus. Eleven times in the Gospels, Jesus
uses the Greek word gehenna (translated hell).? This word is a translation of the phrase
valley of Hinnom from the book of Jeremiah, where it is used in the context of "God's
future judgment of the wicked" (Jeremiah 7:29-34, 19:6-9, 32:35). Jesus knew that some
people were in real danger of going to hell, and his frequent warnings about it are usually
presented in the context of judgment. Hell is for those who have rejected the true God
and have refused to turn away from evil. Their fate is regularly contrasted with that of
the righteous. For example, when speaking of the final judgment, Jesus declared, "these
[the wicked] will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life"
(Matthew 25:46). The wicked will find themselves excluded from the eternal life of God.
Hell may thus be defined as a place where all who reject Christ spend eternity excluded from
Gods presence.

God does indeed bring justice, but he is incredibly patient and merciful, continually

send prophets who urged Israel to repent. Repeatedly, he delayed judgment as he waited

us the greatest possible gift-total forgiveness and complete reconciliation. Because of his
mercy, all people who repent, turning to Jesus Christ in faith, will receive that gift.

unable to hear the truth clearly. It is important
to remember that God judges people for their sins, not for what they know; since all
have sinned (Romans 3:23), God would be justified to judge even the ones who have not
heard the gospel. Still, his heart is for their salvation. Romans 10:20 cites God speaking
through Isaiah to Israel as saying,
"I have been found by those who did not seek me; I
have shown myself to those who did not ask for me." Salvation is a gift, and God desires
that all people might receive it through repentance (2 Peter 3:9). God often works through
human beings to share the gospel message. Still, we also know that God has revealed the
truth about himself through general revelation (Romans 1:19-20), and he "rewards those
who seek him" (Hebrews 11:6). Jesus also promised that the final judgment will not come
until the gospel has been preached "to all nations" (Matthew 24:14). There are many
stories of people who have found Christ through unusual means and in extraordinary
circumstances. God calls us to participate in his mission, so we share the gospel, confident
that God can get his message to those we can't reach.32
Ultimately, we do not know what God has in store for children or those who haven't
heard, but we can trust God to do what is right. When God was about to bring judgment
on the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah, Abraham asked God if he would spare Sodom if
righteous people could be found there. He appealed to God's justice:
Will you indeed sweep away the righteous with the wicked?... Far be it from you
to do such a thing, to put the righteous to death with the wicked... Shall not the
Judge of all the earth do what is just? —Genesis 18:23-25
Through relationship, Abraham learned the truth about God. He knew that God was
just and merciful, so he boldly appealed to God's character. We can do the same, trusting
that God is far more just and compassionate than we could ever be.

9. Talking about Hell
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to articulate three principles for communicating
about hell compassionately and persuasively.
Speaking about hell is never easy-some people even believe that it is cruel-but if what
we have said about hell in this chapter is true, we cannot remain silent. If you saw a
child about to place a hand on a hot stove and you said nothing to warn him or her, that
would demonstrate indifference or hatred, not love. Similarly, ignoring or minimizing the
doctrine of hell is not loving. As apologist Douglas Groothuis observes, "Christianity is
the highest stakes proposition imaginable. There is eternal life to gain or lose, depending
on our answer to God."33 Indeed, hell is a matter of life and death.
We can be confident that God will give everyone ample opportunity to respond to him.
However, God also calls us to share the whole gospel, snatching people from the grasp of
hell (Jude 1:22-23). How can we communicate this terrible reality compassionately and
persuasively? Let's consider three important things to keep in mind.
Leave judgment to God. When it comes to hell, some people feel confident that they
can identify who is in and who is out. Yet, by its very nature, God's kingdom is always a
surprise.34 In Jesus' day, most people would have thought the Pharisees would definitely
enter God's kingdom. But Jesus, who was able to see into the human heart (John 2:25),
knew that the Pharisees were "whitewashed tombs" and in danger of judgment (Matthew
23:13-39). At the final judgment, some who think they've got it all together will be sent
away from God's presence, while the most unlikely will find solace in God's kingdom
(Matthew 25:31-46). We warn others about
hell, but deciding who goes there is not
our place. While we can be confident that
salvation comes through knowing Jesus,
only God, in his infinite wisdom, justice,
and mercy, can make the final judgment
concerning a person's eternal destination.
Take evil seriously. Part of speaking
truthfully about hell is taking the reality of
evil seriously. And that begins with dealing
with evil in our own lives. The power of sin
has been broken through Christ, yet we must
die to it daily (Romans G:11, 1 Peter 2:24). We must say no to sin daily. And when we fail,
we need to confess, repent, and receive God's gracious forgiveness. Taking evil seriously
also includes confronting it when we see it in the world. Sometimes, that will mean having
a difficult conversation with a friend about sin in his or her life. At other times, it will
mean calling out evil in public places. Calling out evil is not the same as condemning
people to hell. It warns them that our actions have consequences now and in eternity.
To do this, we need courage and discernment. Those gifts come as we develop our own
relationship with God.

Amplify the beauty of the gospel. Ultimately, those who do not yet follow Jesus need
much more than a warning about hell. They need to know about God's love and the
glorious future that he wants to share with them. The main reason to trust Christ is not
to escape hell but to be reconciled to God in personal, loving relationship. In entering this

10. Conclusion
What have we learned about hell? We began by noting that no one likes the idea of
hell, yet our feelings do not determine whether it is true. We then saw how Scripture
repeatedly warns about judgment in the Old and the New Testaments (though the
doctrine of hell comes mainly from the New Testament). Hell is a place where all who
reject Christ spend eternity excluded from God's presence.
Hell is described using images of darkness and fire. It is
associated with torment, anguish, and regret. The ultimate
tragedy of hell is separation from God.
Throughout church history, Christians have understood
the doctrine of hell in several different ways. Most Christians,
past and present, believe that hell involves some kind of eternal
conscious torment. Others believe the punishments of hell last
for only a finite period, after which the person is annihilated.
Some believe that hell is a kind of purgatory in which people
are purified for heaven. Still, others believe that God will
eventually bring everyone to himself.
The primary purpose of hell is to punish Satan and demons,
though some humans will share this same fate-specifically,
those who have rejected Christ and persisted in evil. At the
final judgment, the reality of our faith will be evaluated by our actions. Ultimately, those
whose lives are characterized by turning away from God will find themselves outside of
his kingdom. Most believe that either the punishment or the consequences of hell will be
eternal.
Surprisingly, hell is something that people choose. All humans have been corrupted
and enslaved by sin. Jesus offers to set us free, but he will not force us to spend eternity
with him if we do not want to be in his presence. We may continue turning away from
God forever. But to do so is not freedom. Separation from God is to be locked in a prison
of one's own making. Finally, hell is the ultimate expression of God's justice. Because God
loves the world, he will not allow evil to pollute it forever. He will bring justice to those
who do evil. We do not know the fate of children or those who haven't heard, but we can
be confident that God will do what is right in his infnite wisdom, justice, and mercy.
As we reflect on the terrible reality of hell, we need to ask ourselves some hard
questions. What is the trajectory of my life? Am I the kind of person for whom away from
God is the only place I would want to be? Am I living a life consumed by self-centeredness
and evil? If so, what would it look like to turn from that toward God?
We have said that hell can begin even now if we persist in self-centered, wicked
behavior. Yet, no one needs to face the terrible reality of hell. As C. S. Lewis noted, "The
doors of hell are locked on the inside."3 If we want him to, Christ will set us free. We
need not live in a prison of our own making. There is a way out. And the way out leads
to freedom, joy, and ultimate satisfaction. Knowing the God of perfect love and justice is
eternal life, more than the fullest life we could imagine.
In the next chapter, we'll dive deeper into the justice of God as we explore depictions
of violence in the Bible.
