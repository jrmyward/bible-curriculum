2. What the Bible Is and Isn't
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to explain what the Bible is and how it is
What is the Bible? In the scenario, Charlie assumes the beatitudes in Matthew 5 are rules,
and we can become better people by following them. This is how some people view the
Bible as a whole—as a set of rules or instructions to help us live better lives and become
nicer people. Though there are rules in the Bible, calling it a rule book isn't accurate.
For starters, if the Bible were a rule book, it might have been much shorter. Why
include stories, poems, history, and prophecy if the main idea is to communicate rules?
Furthermore, in the scenario, Jasmine rightly points out that we don't necessarily need
the Bible to tell us how to be good people. We might learn how to be relatively good
people through experience, from others around us, or even from reading about Abraham
Lincoln's life or the Buddha's teachings.
So, if the Bible is not a rule book, what is it? We need to look at the Bible's content to
answer this question. The Bible comprises 66 books and letters written by over 40 authors
over at least 1,500 years. Remarkably, these 66 books come together to tell one cohesive
story. And that, first and foremost, is what the Bible is—a story. The Bible claims to be the
true story of the whole world. This story is told in two primary sections.
The Old Testament tells the story of the creation, the fall of humanity, and the history
of Israel as they struggled to live out their calling to be a kingdom of priests for all nations.
The Old Testament is known to Jews as Tanakh, and it consists of three parts: the Law (the
Torah) includes Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy; the Prophets
(Nevi im) includes Joshua, Judges, 1 and 2 Samuel, 1 and 2 Kings, the three major prophets
(Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel), and the 12 minor prophets; the Writings (Ketuvim) include
the Psalms, Job, Proverbs, and everything else.
The first four books of the New Testament, the Gospels-Matthew, Mark, Luke, and
John— describe Jesus life, death, resurrection, and ascension, events that Christians see
as the focal point of history. The book of Acts is about the spread of the gospel and the
formation of the church. The epistles are letters Paul and others wrote to the churches to
help Christians live their new lives in Jesus. The final New Testament book, Revelation,
encourages churches facing persecution and reveals God's plan to restore all things.
Within the Bible's grand story are smaller stories, poems, prophecies, building
instructions, laws, letters, parables, and more. All of the different genres in the Bible help
us to see the triune God, his purposes for creation, and how he works in the world-past,
present, and future. The Bible also shows us who we are in relation to God, revealing how
we can live faithfully and fruitfully in the world as his image-bearers. In this way, the Bible
is like a compass that guides us in wise living. The Bible points us to God so we can come to
know him and discern how to live freely and fully in the world he created.
Yet, these essential truths are not things we can figure out on our own. In order to
know God truly and enter into relationship with him, he must reveal himself to us. That
is why the Bible is often called God's revelation. The word revelation simply means making
known or unveiling. The Bible reveals God as he truly, is as well as his plans and purposes
for the world.

4. Is the Bible True?
› OBJECTIVE: You will be able to distinguish between two ways of evaluating the
Bible's claims to be true.
The authors of Numbers, Titus, and Hebrews all declare that God cannot lie (Titus 1:2,
Numbers 23:19, Hebrews 6:18). It follows that if the Bible is inspired by God, then it is
also true in what it affirms.
"The word of the Lord proves true." —2 Samuel 22:31
"The words of the Lord are pure words, like silver refined in a furnace
on the ground, purified seven times?" —Psalm 12:6
"Every word of God proves true." — Proverbs 30:5
"Your word is truth." —John 17:17
These are bold words, but do they prove anything? After all, these statements about
the Bible come from the Bible itself. To say the Bible is true because it says it is true is not
sufficient evidence. To argue in this fashion is to argue in a circle. It is a logical fallacy, also
known as begging the question (we'll discuss logical fallacies in detail in Chapter 6).
Person 1: "How do we know the Bible is true?"
Person 2: "Because the writers of the Bible said it was true."
Person 1: "But how do we know what they say is true?"
Person 2: "Because the Bible is true."
In this scenario, Person 2 has already assumed what he or
she is trying to prove-namely, that the Bible is true-and
is merely restating that assumption rather than making a
convincing argument. But is there any way to evaluate the
Bible's claims to be true? There are at least two ways, both of
which work together. First, we can investigate the internal
and external evidence. We might ask questions like "Is there
evidence supporting the Bible's historical claims?" "Are there
obvious contradictions or errors in the Bible?" "Does the
Bible ring true to people's experience and help them make
sense of the world?" There is much evidence to support the
Bible's claims. External evidence can establish that the Bible is
historically reliable, but not whether its overall message is true
with absolute certainty.
Second, we can get to know the God who authored the
Bible. This means entering into relationship with God and
dialoguing with him in prayer, both requiring an act of
trust. We must trust, at least for the moment, that God exists and has revealed himself
in Scripture. Then, we can ask questions like "How has God shown himself to be
trustworthy?" "Based on what the Bible says of God, is he the kind of person who would
lie?" "Would I be able to trust such a God?"

5. Are There Myths and Scientific
Inaccuracies in the Bible?
• OBJECTIVE: You will be able to critique the assumptions
that the Bible is an ancient myth full of factual and scientific
errors.
To say the Bible is true in all that it affirms is a bold claim that some
people find problematic. Some suggest the Bible is merely another
ancient myth, while others, like Lily and lain, argue the Bible is full
of factual and scientific errors. Let's consider each of these objections
in turn.
Myths. In popular usage, the term myth usually refers to a made-up
story or a popular belief that has been proven false. However, when speaking of the stories
from ancient cultures, a myth is better described as a story that influences a particular
people's way of understanding and approaching the world. In the ancient Near Eastern
world, where the Bible was written, numerous myths circulated about the gods and their
interactions with the world. Recent studies have revealed a number of similarities between
the Bible and these ancient myths, leading some scholars to suggest that biblical authors
merely copied from these myths to create their own.
A full-scale exploration of this claim is beyond the scope of this book. However,
we may note that a close investigation of these myths has revealed that the similarities
between the Bible and ancient myths are superficial, and the differences are significant.
The biblical authors' conception of God and the universe is, in many ways, at odds with
the dominant views of the time. However, the biblical authors sometimes used familiar
"thought forms and stories" of the ancient world and invested them with new meanings,
thus highlighting the uniqueness of Israelite belief and practice? The distinctions between
the two are meaningful to understanding who God is.
Consider one example. One ancient text predating some of the Old Testament
identifies Baal, a Canaanite god, as the rider of the clouds. The Old Testament frequently
refers to Yahweh as a rider of clouds (Deuteronomy 33:26, Psalm 68:32-33, Psalm 104:1-4,
Isaiah 19:1). Did the biblical authors merely copy
from the Canaanite myth? According to biblical
scholar John Currid, "It is more likely that the
biblical authors [are] making an implicit criticism.
Baal does not ride on clouds; Yahweh does!"
Scientific inaccuracies. Some readers have noticed
the Bible includes explanations of the physical
universe that appear to be at odds with modern
scientific knowledge. For example, Psalm 104:5
says God "set the earth on its foundations, so that
it should never be moved." Psalm 19:1-6 describes
the sunrise "like a strong man, runs its course with
joy," while Job 38:13 talks about the dawn taking the
earth by its skirts. We know such descriptions do not describe the world scientifically, so
how can we understand these phrases?
First, we should note the Bible is not a science textbook. It is a book about God, his
plan for the world, and his interactions with human beings. So, when the biblical authors
say the earth is set on its foundations and never moves, they are not trying to give a
scientifically accurate statement about the earth; instead, they are making a claim about
God's creative power and sustaining influence. We use expressions like this all the time.
For example, we might describe something as "unmoving" when we know the Earth and
everything on it is moving quickly through space. Furthermore, not everything in the
Bible is meant to be taken literally. The Bible employs many figures of speech and other
literary devices. Psalm 19: 5 compares the sunrise to an athlete running with enthusiasm,
and in the context of Job 38, the idea of the dawn taking the earth by its skirts is clearly
Second, the Bible is the revelation of God to human beings in particular times and
places. If God is going to communicate with humans, he must speak in a language they
can understand. It would not make sense for him to talk to the original intended audience
in modern scientific terms. Ancient peoples had neither the technical equipment nor the
scientific vocabulary we have today. Because of this, the biblical writers often describe
things simply "as they appear to the human eye unaided by modern instruments."

church and that taught the material world was inherently evil. Finally, these books were
not widely used across early Christian communities (pedigree).
8. How We Got the Bible Part II
› OBJECTIVE: You will be able to discuss how the Bible was preserved and
explain why textual variants do not affect the reliability of biblical manuscripts.
The books of the New Testament are nearly 2,000 years old, and the Old Testament books
are much older. Over the centuries, these books have been copied, recopied, translated,
and circulated worldwide. Can we be confident that the Bible has been accurately
preserved in all that time?
This question is complicated by the fact that we do not have access to the original
autographs—that is, the original Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek manuscripts of Scripture.
We do not have access to the original autograph of any ancient writing. All we have
today are copies of the original manuscripts (and copies of those copies). However, this
isn't as big a problem as it sounds. The question is: "Do we have enough manuscript
evidence to reconstruct the original autographs with a high degree of accuracy?"
The answer is yes. Let's begin with the Old Testament. One source estimates that as
many as 17,000 biblical manuscripts dated prior to the eighteenth century exist today. (For
comparison, Homers Iliad has approximately 1,900 manuscripts.)2 By comparing all of
these manuscripts, biblical scholars have been able to recover the content of the original
autographs with a high degree of accuracy.

Scrolls. One scroll in this collection contained a copy of the book of Isaiah that was at least
1,000 years older than any other copy of Isaiah
known at the time. Remarkably, this scroll
was almost identical to later copies, with most
differences being minor spelling variations.?

with a high degree of accuracy. Because the Bible has been reliably and meticulously
transmitted, we can trust Scripture to reveal to us the same God it revealed to its original
hearers.

10. Conclusion
We began this chapter with Ryan's story. We saw his excitement in sharing the Bible with
his friends, followed by his bewilderment and uncertainty about how to answer their
questions. Hopefully, this chapter could help Ryan and others like him better understand
the purpose of Scripture and thoughtfully discuss some of the questions people have about
it. Yet, there is so much more to learn and explore! Before we wrap up, let's summarize
what we've encountered in this chapter.
First, we saw that the Bible is not a rule book but a library composed of 66 books of
various genres, which all come together to tell one grand story about God-what he has
done, is doing, and will do for the world. We learned how God actively inspired human
authors to communicate his divine message. Next, we examined the Bible's claim to be
true and determined to discover its truth by exploring the external evidence to get to know
the God of Scripture.
In our discussion about the trustworthiness of Scripture, we argued that the Bible
essentially differs from ancient myths and that there are important reasons why the
Bible does not use modern scientific language. While there is not always clear historical
evidence to support every event in the Bible, there are good reasons to believe the Bible's
historical claims. Furthermore, we saw that many things in Scripture have been historically
affirmed. We also observed how historical writing during Bible times differs from current
documentation methods.
Next, we explored how we got the Bible. We learned that the church did not create the
canon of Scripture, but rather developed reliable criteria for recognizing which books God
had already inspired. We also discovered that the Bible has been accurately transmitted,
giving us confidence that the Bible we have today is as trustworthy as the original
autographs.
Together, these various pieces of evidence make a strong case for the Bible's
trustworthiness. Yet, such evidence will do us little good if we do not read the Bible
ourselves. That's why we surveyed three important tips for reading the Bible.
There is still one lingering question, though: "Why read the Bible to begin with?" In a
word, the answer is Jesus. The whole biblical story is concerned with God, and Jesus reveals
to us what God is really like. Jesus completes the story of Israel and fulfills God's promises
to his people. Jesus suffers and gives his life to defeat evil and grant salvation. And it is
through Jesus that we are given the opportunity to become the people he created us to
be. If you read Scripture with an open heart and mind, you can see how the whole book
points to Jesus. As one biblical scholar summarizes, "The whole of Scripture either points
ahead to Jesus as its fulfillment (Old Testament) or looks back to Jesus as the author and
perfecter of our faith (New Testament)." 40
Presenting apologetics for the Bible can greatly help encourage someone to seriously
consider its claims. As we seek to defend or commend the Bible to others, we must always
remember that encountering Jesus is what will truly change their hearts and minds. That,
ultimately, is why we read the Bible—to encounter the living God revealed to us in Jesus,
to revel in his great love for us, and to experience his transforming grace. In doing so, we
enter God's grand story. In the next chapter, we'll explore what that story looks like.
