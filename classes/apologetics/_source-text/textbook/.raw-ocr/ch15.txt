of Canaan as a genocide is far too simplistic. Instead, we do know it was enacted as God's
judgment on wickedness, defeated false gods, and gave Israel a home from which to know
God and make him known.

How can we reconcile these violent outcries with other passages of Scripture that warn
against harsh, rash, and corrupting talk (Proverbs 12:18, 15:1; Ephesians 4:29)? Let's
consider three contextual clues.
Honest outcries of the oppressed. The psalms are filled with the honest felings of God's
people, including their expressions of rage. Consider Psalm 137-9 (quoted). The context
is the fall of Jerusalem to the Babylonians in 586 Bc, during which Israelite captives
would have experienced the full brutality of ancient siege warfare_" wives were raped,
children were trampled, families were ruined and lives were torn apart." The psalmist is
understandably angry and imagining the worst possible thing that could happen to his
enemies: that God violently removes their descendants. Disturbing as it is, this isn't hate
speech; instead, it is the honest outcry of someone who has suffered terrible injustice and
wants God to mete out justice equal to the crime. For those of us who haven't experienced
such things, such psalms are hard to understand. But for those who have suffered great
harm, these Scriptures may help them express their true feelings to God and we should be
careful about how we pray them. It is always appropriate for God's people to cry out for
justice while also being open to his redemption of our enemies (see Jonah 4:1-11,
Matthew 5:43-47, Revelation 6:10)
Poetic language. The psalms' language is poetic, using metaphors and rich symbolism.
When the psalmist asks God to "break the arm of the wicked," he isn't asking God to
actually break bones. He is asking God to break the strength and power of the enemy.
Similarly, breaking teeth could be a metaphor for
ending deceptive, slanderous, or otherwise evil
talk. These verses are no less disturbing for being
metaphorical, but poetry is supposed to stun us
by using bold images to draw our attention to the
depths of our experience.

As God himself, Jesus could have enabled his

He could have summoned an angel army to take

(Luke 10:25-37), love our enemies and pray for

is, indeed, the all-powerful King, he didn't usher

own life, suffering violence at the hands of his own creatures, and forgiving them even
as they killed him (Luke 23:34). Jesus conquers evil through his self-giving love.
But some have wondered if the cross is really the loving act of God that Christians
have proclaimed it to be. To some, it looks as though God is an angry father, hell-bent
on taking out his wrath on somebody. We, as sinners, are his natural target. For our
sake, Jesus gets in the way and takes a beating so we don't get fried by God's maniacal
wrath. This view says Jesus' death on the cross is essentially cosmic child abuse, a terrible
distortion of John 3:16. Instead of "God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son,"
we end up with, "God so hated the world, that he killed his only Son."30
Is this actually what Scripture teaches? Hardly. While it is true that our sin brought
us under God's wrath (Romans 1:18, 1:29-32, 2:4), it is also true that Jesus took our
place, suffering the punishment we deserve (1 John 4:10, 1 Peter 2:24). He laid down his
life willingly (John 10:11, 17-18; Philippians 2:5-11). The Bible teaches us that God so
loves the world that he will not—indeed, cannot—allow sin to go forever unchecked. He
must bring judgment on evil. God's wrath is not arbitrary. But God is not quick to anger
(Psalm 103:8). He is patient and merciful, holding back judgment and giving people
ample time to turn from their evil ways.
And here is the remarkable thing about the cross. Instead of God pouring his wrath on
somebody else, God pours it on himself. For Jesus is God in the flesh — the same God who
created the world, called the people of Israel, brought judgment on evildoers, and abounds
in steadfast love. He is one God in three Persons united in their purpose to redeem
humanity. The Father didn't bully the Son while the Spirit looked on. To say that the cross
is child abuse is to obscure the fact that the God who is angry at sin is the same God who
gave up his own life to pay for that sin. Jesus isn't the nice god who gets in the way of the
angry god's wrath. He is the God who is angry at sin, and he is the one who suffers for it.
In so doing, he sets us free from the power of sin and death so that we might be reconciled
to God and become fully human, fully alive.
10. Conclusion
We began this chapter by highlighting important tools for reading troubling passages of
Scripture. We need to distinguish between descriptive and prescriptive and also look for
how God works for redemption through imperfect circumstances. We need to discover the
spirit of the text, which we can often do by examining the literary and historical context.
These tools helped us to see how the Bible significantly improved the lot of captive women
and slaves in ancient Israel. In the Greco-Roman
world of the first century, biblical authors put
slaves on equal footing with their freeborn
counterparts, providing a rationale that would
eventually lead to greater freedom.
When it comes to violence in the Bible, we
learned that God's violence is neither arbitrary
nor uncontrolled. It is his just and measured
response to persistent evil. We saw that the
conquest of Canaan was not ethnically motivated
nor a genocide; instead, it was an expression of
God's justice and an effort to rid the land of
idolatrous and destructive influence. We also
learned that we can express our pain, distress, anger, and violent feelings to God while
leaving vengeance to him. Finally, Jesus makes clear God's ultimate ethic of self-giving
love and forgiveness. Rather than perpetuating violence, he suffered it for our sake.
In the end, there is no Scriptural basis for justifying slavery or racism today, as
all people bear God's image, Jesus loves and died for all, and all are equal in him.
Furthermore, initiating or joining in acts of violence against others as a church body is
totally out of bounds. The conquest of Canaan was a one-time event, done in a specific
time and place by a specific people. Such passages do not give the church permission to
engage in violence."| (Whether individual Christians may serve in their nation's military
is too complex an issue to deal with in this book. Christians are to share in Christs
Victory over evil, not through taking up clubs and swords, but in laying down their lives
(Revelation 12:11) 3- We follow Jesus' example, being willing to suffer for the sake of the
gospel (Philippians 2:5-11, 1 Peter 3:8-22).
