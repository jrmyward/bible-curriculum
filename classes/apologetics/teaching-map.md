## Design Philosophy

**Compress the publisher's pacing; keep its spine intact.** Summit designs Apologetics at
**8 class lessons per chapter × 18 chapters = 144 lessons** (each chapter has 10 subsections
grouped into ~8 class sessions, ending with a Chapter Test). Our usable 2026–27 calendar is
**145 instructional days**, so the fit is nearly one-to-one: each chapter runs **~8 days**
(a few 9-day and 7-day blocks to land tests on clean dates and trimester seams). The
publisher's sequence, activities, videos, Study Guide, and chapter tests (`TR n.8`) are kept
and reused as-is; we generate only pacing, lesson-sequencing plans, and application handouts.

**Publisher delivery, with surgical debate days.** This is the publisher model (like
Foundations), not a discussion-first cohort rebuild — the publisher's own material already
builds in discussion questions, activities, group work, and case-study dialogues. The one
thing worth adding is *defense-first sequencing* (students commit to / defend a position
**before** the answer is taught) on the handful of back-half chapters where the content is
genuinely adversarial. Those are marked **⚔️ signature debate day** below; each replaces the
chapter's seminar/application day with a real Pair & Defend or Case Study (see
[`_shared/cohort-tools.md`](../../_shared/cohort-tools.md)). Everything else stays publisher
content-first.

**Trimester seams fall on the book's own seams.** Each trimester closes on a chapter test,
and each break lands between chapters rather than inside one:

- **T1 ends after Logic and Love** (Ch 6 test, Nov 10) — closes the "foundations + reasoning" block.
- **T2 ends after Christianity and Science** (Ch 12 test, on the T2 end date, Feb 25) — closes the
  "truth, religions, and the case for God" block.
- **T3 ends after the Conclusion** (Ch 18 test, May 14), leaving the final light week for course wrap.

## Class Periods

Periods run **~60 minutes most days**, **~45 minutes on Wednesdays** (a shorter schedule).
Lesson plans time each day accordingly — Wednesdays get a tighter plan (good for tests,
review, or a single focused activity rather than content + activity).

## Per-Chapter Rhythm (8-day block)

| Block day | Activity |
|---|---|
| 1–5 | **Content + activity** — the chapter's subsections (paired as the publisher's Chapter Syllabus groups them), each with its built-in activity, group work, or Bible study |
| 6 | **Seminar / application** — teacher-manual discussion questions (provided with answers) + the chapter's video lesson & video questions · **or ⚔️ signature debate day** on marked chapters |
| 7 | **Review** — key terms & key verses; student-manual Study Guide (`TR n.SG`) |
| 8 | **Chapter Test** — publisher `TR n.8` |

Longer blocks (9 days) add one content day; shorter blocks (7 days) merge two thinner
subsections. `**SUB:**` days are teacher absences (school in session) — run a self-contained
activity a substitute can manage (chapter test, Study Guide work, video + worksheet, in-class
writing). Detailed sub plans come from `/generate-substitute-plan`.

## Signature Debate Days (inventory)

Defense-first Pair & Defend / Case Study replacing the chapter's seminar day, on the chapters
where adversarial content makes it worthwhile:

- **Ch 8 — Truth and Tolerance:** objective vs. subjective/relative truth.
- **Ch 9 — Religion Roundup:** the publisher's built-in **9.9 "A Dialogue with a Muslim"** as a
  structured interreligious-dialogue role-play.
- **Ch 11 — The Supernatural and Truth:** defend an argument for God (Kalam / design / moral) or
  confront an alternative theory of the resurrection.
- **Ch 13 — Evil and Suffering:** the problem of evil (state the atheist's case first, then respond).
- **Ch 14 — Hell:** "is hell just?" — four perspectives, defended.

These are **optional and adjustable** — run them if/when the class is ready for structured
dialogue; otherwise the day reverts to the standard publisher seminar/application. Front-third
chapters (Bible, metanarrative, Logic & Love skills) stay content-first.

  in-class discussion, activities, and application work (and the signature-debate rubrics on the
  chapters that use them).
