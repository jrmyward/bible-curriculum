## 1 · Unit Overview

Chapter 1 introduces apologetics, the work of sharing, defending, and commending the Christian
faith. Students begin with the honest, awkward moments that come up when Christians talk about
what they believe, and they learn that apologetics complements evangelism by helping believers
answer hard questions and, along the way, strengthen their own faith. The chapter grounds the
Christian's mission in the Bible's one story, summarized in six acts and an interlude, and in the
Great Commission. It distinguishes evangelism from apologetics, contrasts forceful or dishonest
methods with the way of Jesus and Paul, and frames the Christian's real struggle as a spiritual
battle whose chief weapon is prayer. It treats reason and doubt honestly: Christianity can be
shown to be reasonable, though not proven beyond all possible doubt, and doubt itself can drive
growth. Because it comes first, the chapter previews the whole year and asks students to see
themselves as participants in God's mission rather than spectators.

---

## 2 · Essential Questions

- Why should Christians be prepared to explain and defend their faith?
- What is apologetics, and how is it different from evangelism?
- How is the Christian's mission grounded in the Bible's story and the Great Commission?
- What is the place of reason, and of doubt, in following Jesus?
- How can we share and defend the faith in a way that reflects the character of Christ?

---

## 3 · Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Explain why it matters to be prepared to explain the Christian faith, and describe the purpose
  of apologetics.
- Define a worldview and explain why finding the worldview that fits reality matters.
- Analyze how the mission of God runs through the six acts of the biblical story, and explain
  three aspects of that mission given in the Great Commission (Matthew 28:18–20).
- Differentiate evangelism (a clear presentation, an active demonstration, and a generous
  invitation) from apologetics (defending, commending, and translating the faith).
- Contrast ineffective, forceful approaches with the model of Jesus and Paul for reaching people.
- Describe the weapons of the Christian's spiritual battle, chiefly prayer, and explain the roles
  of reason and doubt in apologetics.
- Examine the nine questions apologetics commonly answers, and explain how God empowers believers
  for his mission.

---

## 4 · Faith Learning Integration

This unit sets apologetics inside the Christian worldview and the mission of God, rather than
treating it as debate technique. The teaching aims to form students, not only inform them: they
name their own readiness and doubts honestly, practice representing Christ with knowledge,
wisdom, and character, and learn to listen before they answer. The chapter holds grace and truth
together, defending the faith without adopting the worldly methods of manipulation or force. The
goal is for students to understand what they believe and why, and to see themselves as called
participants in God's mission who can act with confidence because of the presence of the Holy
Spirit.

---

## 5 · Biblical Integration

The Bible is the main text of this unit.

- Isaiah 1:18 (key verse). God says, "Come now, let us reason together." Reason has a place in
  faith, and God himself invites it.
- Matthew 28:18–20 (key verse). The Great Commission grounds the Christian's mission: all
  authority belongs to Jesus, we go and make disciples of all nations, and Jesus is with us
  always.
- John 20:21 (key verse). "As the Father has sent me, even so I am sending you." Every believer
  is sent, not only the professionals.
- Ephesians 6:12 (key verse). The real struggle is not against flesh and blood but against the
  spiritual powers of evil; the Christian's chief weapon is prayer.
- The six-act story. Students trace God's mission across the whole canon, from Creation to New
  Creation, as the setting for evangelism and apologetics.
- Servanthood in 1 Timothy and Titus. Paul's letters to Timothy and Titus, from the age of the
  church, describe the character of a good servant of God, which shapes how believers share and
  defend the faith.
