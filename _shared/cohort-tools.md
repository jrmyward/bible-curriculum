# Cohort Teaching Tools

These four tools form the backbone of the cohort-style, discussion-first teaching model. Each tool is designed to move students from passive reading into active engagement with ideas. Use them in sequence across a unit, or mix and match as the material requires.

---

## A. The Apologetics/Discussion Brief

**Purpose:** Get every student thinking before the discussion starts. The brief is a short written response that forces students to commit to a position or question before class dialogue begins. It also gives you a written artifact to assess participation and track growth over the semester.

**Format:**
- 1 page maximum (handwritten or typed, teacher's preference)
- Prompt is tied to the current chapter or reading assignment
- Students answer one of the following:
  - Take a position and defend it in 3–5 sentences
  - Identify the strongest objection to the chapter's argument and respond to it
  - Write one question the reading raised and explain why it matters

**Collection:** Briefs are collected at the start of the discussion period. They are graded on completion and thoughtfulness, not on having the "right" answer. The goal is honest engagement, not performance.

**Tips:**
- Rotate prompt styles so students build different muscles (defending, questioning, summarizing).
- Occasionally use briefs as discussion starters — read a strong one aloud (with permission) to kick off the conversation.

---

## B. Pair & Defend

**Purpose:** Force students to articulate and defend a position they may or may not personally hold. This builds empathy for opposing views and sharpens rhetorical skill. Students learn that understanding an argument is not the same as agreeing with it.

**Assignment:**
- Pair students randomly (or strategically, depending on the class dynamic).
- Assign each partner a position related to the chapter's core question. One defends the chapter's thesis; the other argues against it.
- Positions are assigned, not chosen — students must argue the side they're given.

**Timing:**
- 3 minutes: each partner presents their argument (6 minutes total)
- 2 minutes: each partner asks one follow-up question (4 minutes total)
- 5 minutes: open debrief with the full class

**Debrief Question:** After the exercise, ask the full class: *"What was the strongest argument you heard from the side you disagreed with?"* This redirects the conversation from winning to listening.

**Tips:**
- Remind students before every round: the goal is to understand, not to win.
- If a student struggles with their assigned side, that's a teaching moment — help them see why someone might hold that view.

## C. Case Study

**Purpose:** Move from abstract ideas to concrete application. Case studies ground the chapter's concepts in real-world scenarios students might actually encounter. They also build collaborative problem-solving skills.

**Scenario Types:**
- **Ethical dilemma:** A situation with no clean answer — two competing goods or an unavoidable trade-off.
- **Cultural encounter:** A conversation or conflict between people with different worldviews.
- **Historical parallel:** A past event that mirrors the chapter's themes, presented without the resolution so students must reason through it.

**Group Structure:**
- Groups of 3–4 students
- Each group receives the same scenario (or different scenarios from the same chapter, depending on class size)
- Each group must produce: (1) a summary of the dilemma, (2) their recommended response, and (3) the strongest counterargument to their own recommendation

**Timing:**
- 10 minutes: read and discuss in groups
- 5 minutes: each group presents their recommendation (scale to group count)
- 5 minutes: class vote on the most compelling response, followed by teacher debrief

**Voting:** After presentations, the class votes on which group's recommendation they found most compelling. This isn't about popularity — frame it as *"Which response would you trust in a real situation?"* The vote gives you a natural transition into your own debrief and any points the groups missed.

**Tips:**
- Write scenarios that don't have an obvious Sunday School answer. The value is in the tension.
- Rotate group composition regularly so students don't settle into comfortable roles.

## D. Capstone Project

**Purpose:** The capstone is the summative assessment for each unit or major section. It asks students to synthesize everything they've learned — reading, discussion, case studies — into a single coherent presentation. This is where they prove they can think, not just recall.

**Presentation:**
- 5–7 minutes per student (or per pair, if you allow collaborative capstones)
- Format: oral presentation with a visual aid (slides, poster, handout — student's choice)
- The presentation must: (1) state a thesis related to the unit's central question, (2) support it with evidence from the text and class discussions, and (3) address at least one counterargument

**Q&A:**
- After each presentation, 2–3 minutes of questions from classmates
- Encourage questions that challenge the thesis, not just clarifications
- You may ask one question yourself if the class is quiet

**Final Brief:**
- After all presentations are complete, every student writes a final brief (same format as the discussion brief)
- Prompt: *"How has your thinking changed since the beginning of this unit? What's the strongest idea you encountered, and why?"*
- This is collected and graded as part of the capstone assessment

**Closing Prompt:** End the unit with a single question to the class, asked aloud but not answered in writing: *"What question from this unit are you still thinking about?"* Let it sit. Don't resolve it. The goal is to leave students with something worth carrying forward.

**Tips:**
- Set clear expectations for the visual aid — it supports the argument, it doesn't replace it.
- Grade on clarity of thought and quality of evidence, not polish or presentation style.
- The final brief is often the most revealing piece of student writing you'll collect. Read them carefully.
