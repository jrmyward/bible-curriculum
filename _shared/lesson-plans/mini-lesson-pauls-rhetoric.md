# Mini-Lesson: Paul's Rhetoric in Romans

**Duration:** 25–30 minutes  
**Format:** Close reading + discussion  
**Materials:** Bibles (Romans 6:1–14), Rhetoric 101 handout, projection for text

---

## Objective

Students will identify rhetorical techniques Paul uses in Romans and explain how they strengthen his argument for the Gospel.

---

## Opening (3 min)

**Hook:**  
"When you think of the Apostle Paul, what comes to mind?"  
*(Answers: missionary, writer, persecutor-turned-preacher, etc.)*

"Here's one you might not think of: **master debater.** Paul was trained in Greek rhetoric — the same techniques used by philosophers, lawyers, and politicians in the Roman Empire. When he writes letters, he's not just sharing thoughts — he's *building a case.*

Today we're reading Romans like a debate transcript."

---

## Instruction (5 min)

### Background: Rhetoric in the Ancient World

- **Rhetoric** = the art of persuasive argument.
- Greek and Roman students studied it the way we study essay structure.
- Paul was educated in both Jewish Scripture *and* Greek rhetoric (Acts 22:3, educated under Gamaliel; likely exposed to Hellenistic culture in Tarsus).

### Paul's Strategy in Romans

Romans is structured like a **legal argument**:
1. **State the problem** (Romans 1–3: humanity is guilty before God).
2. **Present the solution** (Romans 3–5: justification by faith in Christ).
3. **Answer objections** (Romans 6–8: grace doesn't lead to sin; the law isn't evil).
4. **Address loose ends** (Romans 9–11: what about Israel?).
5. **Apply the verdict** (Romans 12–16: live accordingly).

**Key rhetorical tools Paul uses:**
- **Diatribe** — imagined objections from an opponent.
- **Rhetorical questions** — questions meant to guide thinking, not to get an answer.
- **Scripture quotation** — appeals to shared authority.
- **Logical progression** — if A, then B; B is true, therefore A.

---

## Close Reading: Romans 6:1–14 (12 min)

### Step 1: Read Aloud (Student or Teacher)

Read Romans 6:1–14 together.

### Step 2: Identify the Rhetorical Moves

**Project or write on the board:**

| Verse(s) | What Paul Does | Rhetorical Technique |
|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 6:1 | "What shall we say then? Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound?" | **Diatribe** — imagined objection |
| 6:2 | "By no means! How can we who died to sin still live in it?" | **Rhetorical question** (answer is obvious) |
| 6:3–4 | "Do you not know that all of us who have been baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death?" | **Appeal to shared knowledge** ("you already know this") |
| 6:5–10 | Explains the logic: died with Christ → raised with Him → sin no longer master | **Logical progression** (if this, then that) |
| 6:11 | "So you also must consider yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus." | **Imperative conclusion** (application of logic) |
| 6:12–14 | Commands based on the argument | **Practical application** (now live accordingly) |

**Discussion prompts:**

1. **Why does Paul start with an objection he hasn't even received yet?**  
   *(Anticipates what opponents will say; shows he's thought it through; diffuses it before it becomes a real challenge.)*

2. **What's the effect of the rhetorical question in v. 2?**  
   *(Makes the answer feel obvious; invites the reader to agree rather than resist.)*

3. **How does Paul use baptism as evidence in vv. 3–4?**  
   *(Appeals to shared experience — something Roman Christians already practiced and understood; uses it to illustrate a theological point.)*

4. **Where's the logos, pathos, and ethos in this passage?**  
   - **Logos:** The logical progression (died to sin → can't live in it).  
   - **Pathos:** Imagery of death, burial, resurrection — vivid and emotionally resonant.  
   - **Ethos:** Paul's authority as an apostle + shared Scripture (vv. 10–11 echo OT themes).

---

## Practice (7 min)

### Activity: Find Paul's Diatribe

**Instructions:**  
"Paul uses diatribe *all over* Romans. Open your Bible and skim Romans 3, 6, 7, and 9. Find one more example of Paul raising an objection and answering it."

**Examples they should find:**
- Romans 3:1 — "Then what advantage has the Jew?"
- Romans 3:5 — "But if our unrighteousness serves to show the righteousness of God, what shall we say?"
- Romans 3:31 — "Do we then overthrow the law by this faith?"
- Romans 7:7 — "What then shall we say? That the law is sin?"
- Romans 9:14 — "What shall we say then? Is there injustice on God's part?"

**Debrief:**  
"Why does Paul do this so often?"  
*(Keeps readers engaged; shows he's wrestling with real questions; builds trust by not dodging hard issues.)*

---

## Closing (3 min)

**Synthesis:**  
"Paul didn't just *say* the Gospel — he **argued** for it. He knew people would push back, so he anticipated their questions and built his case like a lawyer.

When you defend your faith, you're doing the same thing. You're not manipulating people into belief — you're showing them that Christianity *makes sense*, that it answers objections, that it stands up under scrutiny.

That's what we're practicing in this class."

**Optional Extension:**  
"Next time you read a New Testament letter, ask: *What argument is being made? What objections are being answered?* You'll see rhetoric everywhere."

---

## Notes for Teacher

- **Tie this to current work.** If students are preparing oral defenses or written arguments, reference Paul's structure: "Do what Paul does — raise the objection yourself, then answer it."
- **Don't over-theologize.** Stay focused on *how* Paul argues, not deep dives into justification or sanctification (unless that's the day's topic).
- **Cross-reference with Rhetoric 101 handout.** This is applied rhetoric — students should see the connection.
- **Use in apologetics context.** When students face "If God is good, why is there evil?" they can diatribe it: "Someone might ask... here's the answer."

---

## Follow-Up Application

In the next discussion or debate, require students to:
1. **Raise one objection to their own position** (diatribe).
2. **Use at least one rhetorical question** to guide their audience.
3. **Build a logical progression** (if A, then B).

This turns abstract learning into concrete skill.
