# Mini-Lesson: Identifying Logical Fallacies

**Duration:** 20–25 minutes  
**Format:** Direct instruction + practice  
**Materials:** Rhetoric 101 handout, projection/board for examples

---

## Objective

Students will be able to identify common logical fallacies in arguments and explain why they weaken reasoning.

---

## Opening (3 min)

**Hook:**  
"Raise your hand if you've ever won an argument online."  
*(Pause for laughs.)*  
"Now keep your hand up if you *changed the other person's mind.*"  
*(Hands go down.)*

"Most arguments fail not because people disagree, but because they use **bad reasoning**. Today we're learning to spot the flaws — both in other people's arguments *and your own*."

**Distribute:** Rhetoric 101 handout (fallacies section).

---

## Instruction (8 min)

### What is a Fallacy?

A **fallacy** is a flaw in reasoning that makes an argument weak or invalid. It *sounds* persuasive but doesn't actually prove anything.

### Why This Matters

- You'll encounter fallacies in media, politics, and everyday conversation.
- You'll be tempted to use them yourself when you don't have strong evidence.
- **In this class**, I'll call them out — and you should too.

### The Big Three (Most Common)

Walk through these three with examples on the board:

#### 1. **Ad Hominem** (Attack the Person)
- "You can't trust Dr. Smith's research on climate — he works for an oil company."
- **Ask:** "Does who he works for make his *data* false? Or do we need to check the data itself?"

#### 2. **Straw Man** (Misrepresent the Argument)
- "Christians want to ban all science and go back to the Dark Ages."
- **Ask:** "Is that what Christians actually argue, or is it easier to attack this made-up version?"

#### 3. **False Dilemma** (Either/Or When More Options Exist)
- "Either you support my policy or you don't care about people."
- **Ask:** "Could someone care about people *and* disagree with your solution?"

### Quick Reference: Other Common Fallacies

Briefly name (don't deep-dive):
- **Appeal to Popularity** — "Everyone believes it, so it's true."
- **Appeal to Emotion** — Using feelings instead of logic.
- **Begging the Question** — Circular reasoning (assuming what you're trying to prove).
- **Slippery Slope** — "If we allow X, Y will inevitably happen" (without proof).

*"All of these are on the handout with definitions and examples. Keep it. We'll use it all semester."*

---

## Practice (10 min)

### Activity: Fallacy Detective

**Instructions:**  
"I'm going to show you 6 statements. For each one, work with a partner to:  
1. Identify the fallacy (if any).  
2. Explain *why* it's a fallacy.  
3. Suggest how to make the argument stronger."

**Examples (project or read aloud):**

1. "You say we should forgive others, but you're still mad at your brother — so you're wrong."  
   *(Tu Quoque / Hypocrisy — his inconsistency doesn't make the claim false)*

2. "Most teenagers think absolute truth is outdated, so moral relativism must be correct."  
   *(Appeal to Popularity — truth isn't decided by poll)*

3. "If we start questioning one Bible passage, soon we'll reject the whole thing and lose our faith."  
   *(Slippery Slope — why is that progression inevitable?)*

4. "You're either 100% committed to this ministry or you don't really love God."  
   *(False Dilemma — other levels of commitment exist)*

5. "How can you say premarital sex is wrong? What about people who really love each other?"  
   *(Appeal to Emotion — feelings don't settle the moral question)*

6. "The Bible is God's Word because it says so in 2 Timothy 3:16."  
   *(Begging the Question — circular; need external evidence for inspiration)*

**Debrief as a class:** Go through answers, affirm correct identifications, clarify confusion.

---

## Closing (3 min)

**Synthesis:**  
"Learning fallacies isn't about 'winning' arguments by catching people in mistakes. It's about **thinking clearly** and **arguing honestly**. If your position is true, you don't *need* fallacies to defend it.

Over the next few weeks, I'll pause discussions and say things like, 'Hold on — that sounded like a straw man. Can you restate their actual position?' You'll do the same for each other. That's how we grow."

**Assignment (if desired):**  
"This week, find one example of a fallacy in the wild — news article, social media, conversation — and bring it to class. Write down the quote and name the fallacy."

---

## Notes for Teacher

- **Don't let this become pedantic.** The goal is sharper thinking, not "gotcha" moments.
- **Model it yourself.** If you catch yourself using a fallacy, name it: "Wait, I just used an appeal to authority there. Let me give you the actual evidence."
- **Tie to upcoming lessons.** When you hit worldview debates (e.g., naturalism vs. theism), students will use this lens to evaluate arguments on both sides.
