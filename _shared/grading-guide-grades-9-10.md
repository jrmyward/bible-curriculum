# Grading Guide — Grades 9–10

**For:** Old Testament Survey & New Testament Survey Classes  
**Teaching Model:** Cohort-style, discussion-first Bible curriculum  
**School:** Watersprings School  
**Grade Level:** 9–10

---

## Grading Philosophy (Grades 9–10)

Grades 9–10 students are **building foundational knowledge** and **learning to think critically** about Scripture. The cohort teaching model at this level emphasizes:

- **Curiosity** — Are they asking questions and exploring the text?
- **Participation** — Are they engaging in discussions and activities?
- **Understanding** — Do they grasp the story, structure, and themes of Scripture?
- **Application** — Can they connect Scripture to their own lives?

**NOT graded on:**
- Advanced theological analysis (that's for grades 11–12)
- Perfect recall of dates, names, or details
- Defending complex apologetic positions

### Key Differences from Grades 11–12

| Grades 9–10 (Survey Courses) | Grades 11–12 (Apologetics/Worldview) |
|------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Goal:** Build biblical literacy and engagement | **Goal:** Defend and articulate the faith |
| **Focus:** Story, structure, themes | **Focus:** Theology, philosophy, competing worldviews |
| **Assessment:** Comprehension + application | **Assessment:** Synthesis + defense |
| **Capstones:** Creative projects or presentations | **Capstones:** Argumentative presentations with counterarguments |
| **Tone:** Exploratory, curious | **Tone:** Analytical, apologetic |

---

## Assessment Types & Grading Methods (Grades 9–10)

| Assessment | Weight | Grading Method | Key Focus |
|------------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| **Discussion Briefs** | 3–5% each | 3-point scale (100% / 67% / 33%) | Curiosity and engagement with the text |
| **Pair & Defend** | Participation | Pass/Fail (100% / 50% / 0%) | Defending a position (simplified format) |
| **Case Studies** | Participation | Pass/Fail (100% / 50% / 0%) | Group work, creative problem-solving |
| **Creative Projects** | 10–15% each | 100-point rubric | Understanding + creativity + application |
| **Scripture Memory** | 5–10% | Points-based | Memorization and recitation |
| **Final Exam** | 15–20% | Traditional exam | Comprehension, connections, application |

## 1. Discussion Briefs (3–5% each)

### What You're Grading (Grades 9–10)
- **Engagement with the text** — Did they read it and think about it?
- **Curiosity** — Are they asking questions?
- **Personal connection** — Can they connect Scripture to their own life?

### Grading Scale (Same as 11–12, but adjusted expectations)

| Grade | Criteria | What It Looks Like |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| **3 pts (100%)** | Complete, thoughtful, personal | Student engages with the text, asks a question, or makes a personal connection |
| **2 pts (67%)** | Complete but surface-level | Student summarizes the passage but doesn't go deeper |
| **1 pt (33%)** | Incomplete or off-topic | Student didn't read the text or didn't answer the prompt |
| **0 pts (0%)** | Not submitted | — |

### Example: Discussion Brief #1 (Old Testament Survey — Genesis 1–3)

**Prompt:** *"After reading Genesis 1–3, what question do you have about creation, the fall, or human nature?"*

---

**Student A (3/3 — Full Credit):**
> "Why did God put the tree of the knowledge of good and evil in the garden if He knew Adam and Eve would eat from it? It seems like a setup. But maybe the point is that real love requires the freedom to choose. If God had made it impossible to sin, we'd be robots, not people. I'm still thinking about this."

**Why Full Credit:**
- Asks a genuine question (the tree's purpose)
- Shows wrestling with the text (not just accepting it at face value)
- Makes a tentative connection (freedom = love)
- Honest about still thinking ("I'm still thinking about this")

---

**Student B (2/3 — Partial Credit):**
> "Genesis 1–3 is about creation and the fall. God made the world, Adam and Eve sinned, and they got kicked out of the garden."

**Why Partial Credit:**
- Accurate summary, but no deeper engagement
- No question, no personal connection
- Feels like they read it but didn't think about it

---

**Student C (1/3 — Minimal Credit):**
> "I don't really get Genesis. It's confusing."

**Why Minimal Credit:**
- Doesn't engage with the text (no specifics)
- No question (just says "confusing" without asking what's confusing)
- Too short to show real effort

---

### Grading Tips (Grades 9–10)

- **Celebrate questions.** A brief that's mostly questions ("Why did...? How does...?") deserves full credit. You want them curious.
- **Look for personal connections.** "This reminds me of..." or "I've always wondered..." shows engagement.
- **Don't penalize simple language.** A 9th grader won't write like a 12th grader. Grade on thought, not polish.
- **Use briefs to guide discussion.** Read a question-heavy brief aloud to kick off class: "Student X asked a great question..."

## 2. Pair & Defend (Participation-Based — Simplified for Grades 9–10)

### What You're Grading
- **Did they participate?**
- **Did they defend their assigned position?**
- **Did they listen respectfully?**

### Format Adjustment for Grades 9–10

Pair & Defend at this level is **simpler and shorter**:
- **2 minutes per side** (instead of 3 minutes for grades 11–12)
- **Prompts are less abstract** (e.g., "Was David right to fight Goliath?" vs. "Does the Bible's treatment of slavery reveal timeless principles?")
- **Debrief is more guided** (you help them see both sides, rather than expecting them to do it independently)

### Grading Scale (Same as 11–12)

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **100%** | Participated fully. Defended their assigned position for 2 min. Listened respectfully. |
| **50%** | Participated but unprepared. Didn't use the full 2 min, or struggled to articulate the position. |
| **0%** | Absent or refused to participate. |

### Example: Pair & Defend #1 (Old Testament Survey — Week 10)

**Positions:** *"Abraham was right to lie about Sarah being his sister / Abraham was wrong to lie about Sarah."* (Genesis 12)

---

**Student A (100% — Full Credit):**
- Defended "Abraham was right to lie" for 2 minutes
- Argued: "Abraham was protecting his family. If he told the truth, Pharaoh might have killed him to take Sarah. Sometimes you have to lie to survive."
- Listened to the opposing argument respectfully
- In debrief, acknowledged: "I see why lying is wrong, but I still think Abraham was in a tough spot."

---

**Student B (50% — Partial Credit):**
- Defended "Abraham was wrong to lie," but only for 1 minute
- Struggled to articulate: "Lying is bad. Um... yeah, he shouldn't have done it."
- Didn't listen during the opposing argument (distracted)
- **Why Partial Credit:** Showed up, but wasn't prepared

---

**Student C (0% — No Credit):**
- Refused to defend the assigned position: "I think Abraham was right, so I'm not going to argue he was wrong."
- **Why No Credit:** The point is to argue a position you don't hold. Refusal = 0.

---

### Grading Tips (Grades 9–10)

- **Keep prompts concrete.** "Was David right to fight Goliath?" is easier than "Does David's fight against Goliath demonstrate faith or presumption?"
- **Model the exercise first.** In the first Pair & Defend, you and a student (or two students) model it so the class sees how it works.
- **Give grace for nervousness.** Many 9th graders have never done this before. If they try but struggle, give 100%. Save 50% for students who clearly didn't prepare.

## 3. Case Studies (Participation-Based — Simplified for Grades 9–10)

### What You're Grading
- **Did they participate in the group work?**
- **Did their group complete the task?**
- **Did they contribute to the presentation?**

### Format Adjustment for Grades 9–10

Case Studies at this level are **more creative and less analytical**:
- **Scenarios are simpler** (e.g., "You're Joseph's brothers. How do you explain what you did to your dad?" vs. "Analyze the ethics of Joseph's brothers selling him into slavery through a biblical worldview lens.")
- **Deliverables are lighter** (summary + recommendation, no counterargument required)
- **Presentations are shorter** (3 min instead of 5 min)

### Grading Scale (Same as 11–12)

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **100%** | Participated in group work. Group completed the task. Contributed to presentation. |
| **50%** | Participated but group deliverables were incomplete or rushed. |
| **0%** | Absent, refused to participate, or clearly did not contribute to the group. |

### Example: Case Study #1 (Old Testament Survey — Week 8)

**Scenario:** *"You are Joseph's brothers, years after you sold him into slavery. Joseph is now the second most powerful man in Egypt, and you need food because of the famine. What do you say to him?"*

---

**Group 1 (100% — Full Credit):**
- **Summary:** "We're Joseph's brothers. We sold him into slavery when we were younger because we were jealous. Now we need his help, but we're terrified he'll punish us."
- **Recommendation:** "We should confess what we did, apologize, and ask for mercy. Lying or pretending we didn't do it will only make it worse."
- **Presentation:** Clear, honest, showed understanding of the story and the moral dilemma

---

**Group 2 (50% — Partial Credit):**
- **Summary:** "We're Joseph's brothers and we need food."
- **Recommendation:** "We should ask him for food."
- **Presentation:** Rushed, didn't engage with the guilt/confession aspect of the scenario
- **Why Partial Credit:** They did the work, but missed the point of the case study

---

**Student X in Group 3 (0% — No Credit):**
- Sat with group but didn't contribute
- Peer feedback confirms they were on their phone the whole time
- **Why No Credit:** Participation grades require participation

---

### Grading Tips (Grades 9–10)

- **Make scenarios fun.** "You're one of the spies Moses sent into Canaan. What do you report back?" is more engaging than "Analyze the spies' report in Numbers 13."
- **Allow creativity.** If a group acts out their scenario instead of just presenting, that's great. Give full credit.
- **Use peer feedback.** Ask: "Did everyone in your group contribute?" You'll know who coasted.

## 4. Creative Projects (10–15% each — Replaces Capstones for Grades 9–10)

### Why Creative Projects Instead of Capstones?

Grades 9–10 are **building biblical literacy**, not yet defending complex theological positions. Creative projects allow students to:
- **Demonstrate understanding** of the text
- **Apply Scripture** to their own lives
- **Engage creatively** (art, writing, video, drama, etc.)

### Project Examples (Old Testament Survey)

| Unit | Project Options | What You're Testing |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Creation & Fall** | Create a visual timeline of Genesis 1–11 OR Write a psalm about creation | Understanding of structure and themes |
| **Patriarchs** | Write a "where are they now?" profile of Abraham, Isaac, or Jacob OR Create a family tree with brief bios | Understanding of character development and God's covenant |
| **Exodus & Law** | Design a mini "Tabernacle tour guide" OR Write a modern parable illustrating one of the Ten Commandments | Understanding of worship and law |
| **Judges & Kings** | Create a "Top 5 Judges/Kings" ranking with justifications OR Write a diary entry from the perspective of a prophet | Understanding of leadership and faithfulness |
| **Prophets & Exile** | Create a comic strip of a prophet's message OR Write a letter from an exile to someone back home | Understanding of prophetic message and exile experience |

### Grading Rubric (100 Points Total)

| Category | Points | What You're Looking For |
|----------|--------|-------------------------|
| **Understanding of Text** | 40 | Does the project demonstrate accurate understanding of the biblical text? |
| **Creativity & Effort** | 30 | Did the student put thought and effort into the project? Is it creative or just the bare minimum? |
| **Application** | 20 | Does the project connect Scripture to the student's own life or the modern world? |
| **Presentation Quality** | 10 | Is the project clear, organized, and visually appealing? (Don't overweight this—effort matters more than polish.) |

---

### Example: Creative Project #1 (Old Testament Survey — Patriarchs Unit)

**Prompt:** *"Choose one of the patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, or Joseph). Create a 'Character Profile' that includes: (1) Key life events, (2) How God's covenant with Abraham is passed down or fulfilled, (3) One lesson from their life that applies to you today."*

---

#### Student A — 92/100 (A)

**Project:** Visual poster with illustrated timeline of Jacob's life

**Understanding of Text (38/40):**
- Key events: birth (grabbing Esau's heel), stealing the blessing, ladder dream, wrestling with God, reconciliation with Esau
- Covenant connection: God renews the covenant at Bethel (Genesis 28:13-15), names him "Israel"
- Lost 2 points: didn't mention Jacob's 12 sons (the 12 tribes)

**Creativity & Effort (28/30):**
- Hand-drawn illustrations for each event
- Timeline is clear and visually engaging
- Lost 2 points: some illustrations are rushed (last-minute effort?)

**Application (18/20):**
- "Jacob was a deceiver, but God didn't give up on him. That reminds me that God can work in my life even when I mess up."
- Lost 2 points: could go deeper (how does this change how you live today?)

**Presentation Quality (8/10):**
- Clear, organized, easy to follow
- Lost 2 points: some text is hard to read (small handwriting)

**Total: 92/100 (A)**

---

#### Student B — 76/100 (C)

**Project:** Typed one-page profile of Abraham

**Understanding of Text (30/40):**
- Key events: called by God, promised a son, Isaac born, tested with Isaac's sacrifice
- Covenant connection: mentioned but not explained (just says "God made a covenant with Abraham")
- Lost 10 points: missing key events (Hagar/Ishmael, Sodom/Gomorrah), no detail on covenant promises

**Creativity & Effort (18/30):**
- Typed document, no visuals
- Bare minimum (1 page, double-spaced, large font)
- Lost 12 points: low effort, no creativity

**Application (15/20):**
- "Abraham trusted God even when it was hard. I should trust God too."
- Lost 5 points: very generic, doesn't explain how this applies to their specific life

**Presentation Quality (8/10):**
- Clear and organized (typed)
- Lost 2 points: no visuals, just text

**Total: 71/100 (C-)**  
**Why This Grade:** Student understood the basics but didn't put much effort in. The application is shallow ("I should trust God too" — how? when? why?). Passing grade, but not impressive.

---

#### Student C — 55/100 (F)

**Project:** Hand-written half-page on Joseph

**Understanding of Text (20/40):**
- Key events: coat of many colors, sold by brothers, became ruler in Egypt
- Covenant connection: not mentioned at all
- Lost 20 points: major gaps (no mention of dreams, prison, forgiveness of brothers)

**Creativity & Effort (10/30):**
- Half a page, handwritten, no visuals
- Feels rushed (written the night before or in class)
- Lost 20 points: minimal effort

**Application (10/20):**
- "Joseph's story is interesting."
- Lost 10 points: not an application (just an observation)

**Presentation Quality (5/10):**
- Hard to read (messy handwriting)
- Lost 5 points: poor presentation

**Total: 45/100 (F)**  
**Why This Grade:** Student didn't meet the basic requirements. No covenant connection, no real application, minimal effort. Failing grade.

---

### Grading Tips (Grades 9–10)

- **Celebrate creativity.** A student who writes a rap about the Exodus deserves full creativity points, even if it's not "polished."
- **Grade on understanding first.** A project can be beautiful but wrong. Accuracy matters more than aesthetics.
- **Don't penalize lack of artistic skill.** A stick-figure comic that demonstrates understanding is better than a beautiful poster that's biblically inaccurate.
- **Require application.** The "So what?" question is critical. "What does this mean for your life?" should be answered in every project.

## 5. Scripture Memory (5–10% — Optional but Recommended)

### Why Scripture Memory?

Grades 9–10 are a **formative time** for hiding God's Word in students' hearts (Psalm 119:11). Scripture memory:
- Builds biblical literacy
- Provides spiritual resources for life's challenges
- Trains the mind to think in biblical categories

### How to Grade

**Points-based system** (simple and objective):
- Students memorize assigned passages (weekly or biweekly)
- Recite to you, a TA, or a classmate during class
- Earn points for accuracy and completion

| Accuracy | Points |
|----------|--------|
| **100% accurate** | 10/10 |
| **95–99% accurate** (1–2 small errors) | 9/10 |
| **90–94% accurate** (3–4 small errors) | 8/10 |
| **Below 90% accurate** | Retry next class period (no points yet) |
| **Not attempted** | 0/10 |

### Suggested Passages (Old Testament Survey)

| Unit | Passage | Why This Passage? |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| **Creation & Fall** | Genesis 1:1, 27–28 | Foundation of biblical worldview (creation, image of God) |
| **Patriarchs** | Genesis 12:1–3 | Abrahamic covenant (blessing to all nations) |
| **Exodus & Law** | Exodus 20:1–17 | Ten Commandments (abbreviated version) |
| **Judges & Kings** | 1 Samuel 16:7 | God looks at the heart (David's anointing) |
| **Prophets & Exile** | Jeremiah 29:11–13 | Hope in exile (often misapplied, but important) |
| **Wisdom Literature** | Proverbs 3:5–6 | Trust in the Lord (classic) |

### Grading Tips

- **Make it low-stakes.** Scripture memory should be a gift, not a burden. 5–10% of the grade is enough to motivate without stressing students out.
- **Allow retries.** If a student doesn't get it the first time, let them try again next class. The goal is memorization, not punishment.
- **Celebrate progress.** A student who goes from 70% accuracy to 95% accuracy is growing. Celebrate that.
- **Tie it to the unit.** Memorize passages that connect to what you're studying. Context helps retention.

## 6. Final Exam (15–20% — Cumulative)

### Exam Structure (Suggested for Grades 9–10)

| Section | Points | Format | What You're Testing |
|---------|--------|--------|---------------------|
| **Part 1: Recall** | 40 | Multiple choice / Fill-in-the-blank | Key people, events, and themes from OT or NT |
| **Part 2: Connections** | 30 | Short answer (2–3 sentences each) | "How does this passage connect to the larger story of Scripture?" |
| **Part 3: Application** | 30 | Essay (1 page) | "Choose one person/event from the OT/NT. What does their story teach you about God or the Christian life?" |

---

### Example Questions (Old Testament Survey)

**Part 1: Recall (40 points)**

1. **Who was the first king of Israel?** (2 points)
   - **Answer:** Saul

2. **What did God promise Abraham in the covenant?** (2 points)
   - **Answer:** Land, descendants (a great nation), and blessing to all nations

3. **Which prophet confronted King Ahab about Naboth's vineyard?** (2 points)
   - **Answer:** Elijah

4. **Fill in the blank: "The Lord does not look at the things people look at. People look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the __________."** (1 Samuel 16:7)
   - **Answer:** heart

**Grading:** 2 points per correct answer. Partial credit (1 point) for partially correct answers.

---

**Part 2: Connections (30 points — 3 questions, 10 points each)**

**Question:** *"How does the story of the Exodus connect to the larger story of God's redemption?"*

---

**Student A (10/10 — Full Credit):**
> "The Exodus is a picture of God rescuing His people from slavery. Just like Israel was enslaved in Egypt and God saved them through the Passover lamb and the Red Sea, all people are enslaved to sin and God saves us through Jesus (the ultimate Passover lamb). The Exodus shows that God is a rescuer."

**Why Full Credit:** Makes the connection (Exodus → Jesus), explains it clearly, ties it to the bigger story of redemption.

---

**Student B (6/10 — Partial Credit):**
> "The Exodus is when God saved Israel from Egypt. It's an important story."

**Why Partial Credit:** Accurate but doesn't answer the question. Lost 4 points for not explaining *how* it connects to the larger story.

---

**Part 3: Application Essay (30 points)**

**Prompt:** *"Choose one person or event from the Old Testament. What does their story teach you about God or the Christian life?"*

---

**Student A (28/30 — A)**

> **David and Goliath: God Uses the Weak**
>
> When I read the story of David and Goliath (1 Samuel 17), I used to think it was about being brave. But now I see it's really about God using weak people to show His strength. David was the youngest brother, a shepherd, and nobody expected him to fight Goliath. But David trusted that God would help him, and God did.
>
> This teaches me that I don't have to be the strongest or the smartest for God to use me. When I feel weak or afraid, I can trust God like David did. It also reminds me that God doesn't need my strength—He just needs my faith.

**Grading Breakdown:**
- **Understanding of text (10/10):** Accurate summary, grasps the main point (God uses the weak)
- **Theological insight (10/10):** Connects to a biblical theme (God's strength in weakness)
- **Application (8/10):** Clear personal application ("when I feel weak or afraid..."). Lost 2 points for not being more specific (when? how will you respond differently?).

**Total: 28/30 (A)**

---

**Student B (20/30 — C-)**

> **Joseph**
>
> Joseph was sold by his brothers and became a ruler in Egypt. His story teaches me that God has a plan. Even when bad things happen, God is in control. I should trust God like Joseph did.

**Grading Breakdown:**
- **Understanding of text (7/10):** Accurate but surface-level. Lost 3 points for missing key details (dreams, forgiveness, "you meant it for evil, but God meant it for good").
- **Theological insight (7/10):** "God has a plan" is true but vague. Lost 3 points for not explaining *how* Joseph's story reveals God's character or plan.
- **Application (6/10):** Generic ("I should trust God"). Lost 4 points for not explaining how Joseph's story changes the way they live.

**Total: 20/30 (C-)**

---

### Grading Tips (Grades 9–10)

- **Part 1 (Recall):** Grade quickly. 2 points per correct answer. Don't overthink it.
- **Part 2 (Connections):** Look for students making links between OT and NT, or between individual stories and the larger narrative. "This connects to Jesus" or "This shows God's faithfulness" = full credit.
- **Part 3 (Application):** Grade on understanding + application. A student who understands the text but can't apply it gets partial credit. A student who applies it personally (not just "I should...") gets full credit.

## Pro Tips for Grading (Grades 9–10)

1. **Grade for curiosity, not perfection.** A 9th grader who asks good questions is more valuable than one who memorizes the right answers.

2. **Celebrate creativity.** If a student submits a comic strip, a song, or a video, that's great. Grade on understanding and effort, not production value.

3. **Use Scripture memory as a gift.** Frame it as "hiding God's Word in your heart," not "one more thing to memorize for a grade."

4. **Don't over-correct Discussion Briefs.** A brief that's full of questions ("Why did God...? How does this...?") deserves full credit. You want them curious, not confident.

5. **Make Pair & Defend fun.** Keep it light. "You're defending Jonah's decision to run away from God. Go!" It's a game, not a theology exam.

6. **Grade Creative Projects on understanding first, creativity second.** A stick-figure comic that demonstrates understanding is better than a beautiful poster that's biblically inaccurate.

7. **Use peer feedback for Case Studies.** Ask: "Did everyone contribute?" You'll know who coasted.

8. **Give retakes on Scripture memory.** The goal is memorization, not punishment. Let them try again.

9. **Don't make the final exam intimidating.** Frame it as "show me what you've learned this year." It's a celebration, not a stress test.

10. **Read the application essays carefully.** They'll tell you what landed and what didn't. Use them to adjust your teaching for next year.
