# Grading Guide — Cohort Teaching Model

**For:** Understanding the Faith & Worldview Classes  
**Teaching Model:** Cohort-style, discussion-first Bible curriculum  
**School:** Watersprings School  
**Grade Level:** 11–12

---

## Grading Philosophy

The cohort teaching model values **depth over breadth** and **engagement over performance**. Students are graded on:

- **Thoughtfulness** — Are they wrestling with ideas?
- **Honesty** — Are they being real, or telling you what they think you want to hear?
- **Growth** — Are they changing how they think, not just what they know?

**NOT graded on:**
- Having the "right" answer on Discussion Briefs
- Winning debates in Pair & Defend
- Polish or presentation style in Capstones

---

## Assessment Types & Grading Methods

| Assessment | Weight | Grading Method | Key Focus |
|------------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| **Discussion Briefs** | 3–5% each | 3-point scale (100% / 67% / 33%) | Thoughtfulness, not correctness |
| **Pair & Defend** | Participation | Pass/Fail (100% / 50% / 0%) | Defending assigned position, respectful engagement |
| **Case Studies** | Participation | Pass/Fail (100% / 50% / 0%) | Group work, deliverables, presentation |
| **Capstones** | 12–15% each | 100-point rubric | Thesis clarity, evidence quality, counterargument |
| **Final Exam** | 20% | Traditional exam | Recall, compare/contrast, application |
| **Reflection Essay** | 5% | 3-point scale (100% / 75% / 50%) | Depth of reflection, honesty |

## 1. Discussion Briefs (3–5% each)

### What You're Grading
- **Completion** — Did they write it?
- **Thoughtfulness** — Did they engage with the prompt?
- **Honesty** — Are they wrestling with the question, or performing?

### Grading Scale (3-Point System)

| Grade | Criteria | What It Looks Like |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| **3 pts (100%)** | Complete, thoughtful, honest engagement | Student answers the prompt directly, shows evidence of thinking, raises a genuine question or takes a defensible position |
| **2 pts (67%)** | Complete but superficial | Student answers the prompt but doesn't go deeper than surface-level observations. Feels like they're checking a box. |
| **1 pt (33%)** | Incomplete or off-topic | Student didn't answer the prompt, or the brief is too short to show real engagement (1–2 sentences instead of a paragraph) |
| **0 pts (0%)** | Not submitted | — |

### Example: Discussion Brief #1 (Understanding the Faith, Week 1)

**Prompt:** *"Why would someone need to 'practice' defending their faith? What makes this different from just believing?"*

---

**Student A (3/3 — Full Credit):**
> "Practicing apologetics is different from just believing because belief is internal, but apologetics is external—it requires you to articulate *why* you believe. I've always known Christianity is true, but when my friend asked me last year why I trust the Bible, I couldn't give a good answer. I just said 'because I have faith,' which didn't help him at all. Practicing means being ready to give a reason for what you believe, not just feeling it. It's like the difference between being able to play piano and being able to explain *how* you play piano."

**Why Full Credit:**
- Directly answers the prompt (practice vs. belief)
- Shows personal reflection (the friend conversation)
- Makes a clear distinction (internal vs. external, feeling vs. articulating)
- Uses an analogy to clarify thinking

---

**Student B (2/3 — Partial Credit):**
> "You need to practice defending your faith because people will ask you questions. It's different from just believing because practicing means you can explain it better."

**Why Partial Credit:**
- Answers the prompt, but doesn't go deeper
- No personal reflection or examples
- Surface-level thinking ("explain it better" — but why does that matter?)

---

**Student C (1/3 — Minimal Credit):**
> "I think it's important to practice apologetics. Faith is good."

**Why Minimal Credit:**
- Doesn't actually answer the prompt (why practice? how is it different from belief?)
- Too short to show engagement
- Feels like they didn't read the question carefully

---

### Grading Tips

- **Grade quickly.** Use the 3-point scale and spend no more than 1–2 minutes per brief.
- **Don't grade on "correct" answers.** A student who writes, "I don't understand why prophecy matters—it feels like cherry-picking verses" deserves full credit if they're wrestling honestly.
- **Look for honesty.** The best briefs often start with "I'm confused about..." or "I've always thought... but now I'm wondering..."
- **Use briefs to guide discussion.** Read a strong brief aloud (with permission) to kick off class discussion.

## 2. Pair & Defend (Participation-Based)

### What You're Grading
- **Did they show up prepared?**
- **Did they defend their assigned position (even if they disagreed with it)?**
- **Did they engage respectfully during Q&A?**

### Grading Scale (Pass/Fail)

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **100%** | Participated fully. Defended their assigned position for 3 min. Asked/answered follow-up questions. Engaged respectfully. |
| **50%** | Participated but unprepared. Didn't use the full 3 min, or struggled to articulate the assigned position. |
| **0%** | Absent or refused to participate. |

### Example: Pair & Defend #2 (Understanding the Faith, Week 4)

**Positions:** *"The Bible's slavery texts are culturally bound / The Bible's slavery texts reveal timeless principles."*

---

**Student A (100% — Full Credit):**
- Defended "culturally bound" position for 3 minutes
- Argued: "The Bible regulated slavery in its cultural context, but the trajectory of Scripture is toward abolition. Paul tells Philemon to receive Onesimus 'no longer as a slave, but... as a dear brother' (Philemon 1:16). The principle is human dignity; the cultural expression evolved."
- Asked follow-up question: "If the slavery texts are timeless, does that mean Christians today should support slavery?"
- Engaged respectfully even though they personally disagree with the position they defended

---

**Student B (50% — Partial Credit):**
- Defended "timeless principles" position, but only for 90 seconds
- Struggled to articulate the position: "Um... I guess slavery was different back then? I don't really know."
- Didn't ask a follow-up question
- **Why Partial Credit:** They showed up and tried, but weren't prepared

---

**Student C (0% — No Credit):**
- Refused to defend their assigned position: "I don't agree with this, so I'm not going to argue for it."
- **Why No Credit:** The whole point of Pair & Defend is to argue a position you don't hold. Refusal to participate = 0.

---

### Grading Tips

- **Fail students who refuse to participate.** The pedagogical value is in defending a position you don't hold. If they won't do it, they miss the point.
- **Give partial credit for effort.** If a student tries but struggles, give them 50%. The goal is to encourage participation, not punish weak arguments.
- **Watch for disrespectful engagement.** If a student argues their position by mocking the opposing view, dock points or give a warning.

---

## 3. Case Studies (Participation-Based)

### What You're Grading
- **Did they participate in the group work?**
- **Did their group produce the required deliverables?** (summary, recommendation, counterargument)
- **Did they present their group's findings?**

### Grading Scale (Pass/Fail)

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **100%** | Participated in group work. Group produced all 3 deliverables. Contributed to presentation. |
| **50%** | Participated but group deliverables were incomplete or rushed. |
| **0%** | Absent, refused to participate, or clearly did not contribute to the group. |

### Example: Case Study #2 (Understanding the Faith, Week 6)

**Scenario:** *A skeptic claims the Bible is full of contradictions. Your group must respond.*

---

**Group 1 (100% — Full Credit):**
- **Summary of dilemma:** "The skeptic points to differences between Gospel accounts (e.g., how many angels at the tomb?). Are these contradictions or complementary perspectives?"
- **Recommendation:** "These are complementary, not contradictory. Different eyewitnesses emphasize different details. This is actually evidence of authenticity—if all four Gospels were identical, we'd suspect collusion."
- **Counterargument to our recommendation:** "But if the Bible is inspired by God, shouldn't every detail match perfectly? Why would God allow 'differences' in the text?"
- **Presentation:** Clear, organized, addressed the counterargument thoughtfully

---

**Group 2 (50% — Partial Credit):**
- **Summary of dilemma:** "The skeptic says the Bible has contradictions."
- **Recommendation:** "We think the Bible doesn't have contradictions."
- **Counterargument:** (None provided)
- **Presentation:** Rushed, didn't engage with the scenario in depth
- **Why Partial Credit:** They did the work, but it was superficial. Missing the counterargument.

---

**Student X in Group 3 (0% — No Credit):**
- Sat with group but spent the entire time on their phone
- Did not contribute to discussion or presentation
- **Why No Credit:** Participation grades require participation. Peer feedback confirms they didn't engage.

---

### Grading Tips

- **Use peer feedback.** At the end of group work, ask: "Did everyone in your group contribute equally? If not, who didn't pull their weight?" You'll know who to give 0s to.
- **Don't grade on the "right" answer.** Grade on whether they engaged with the scenario thoughtfully. A weak recommendation with a good counterargument is better than a strong recommendation with no counterargument.
- **Rotate groups regularly.** Don't let students settle into comfortable roles (one person does all the work, others coast).

## 4. Capstones (12–15% each — Major Assessment)

### Grading Rubric (100 Points Total)

| Category | Points | What You're Looking For |
|----------|--------|-------------------------|
| **Thesis Clarity** | 25 | Is the thesis clear, specific, and defensible? Does it answer the prompt? |
| **Evidence Quality** | 30 | Does the student use evidence from the text, class discussions, and primary sources? Is the evidence relevant and well-explained? |
| **Counterargument** | 25 | Does the student address a *real* objection (not a straw man)? Is the response thoughtful and substantive? |
| **Presentation Skill** | 10 | Is the presentation clear and organized? (Don't overweight polish—this is about ideas, not performance.) |
| **Final Brief** | 10 | Does the Final Brief show depth of reflection and evidence of listening to peers? |

---

### Example: Capstone #1 (Understanding the Faith — Biblical Reliability)

**Prompt:** *"Make the case for biblical reliability. Your thesis must address one specific objection and defend it using evidence from Ch 4–6."*

---

#### Student A — 95/100 (A)

**Thesis (25/25):**
> "The New Testament is the most reliable ancient text in existence, and skeptics who reject it while accepting other ancient works apply an inconsistent standard."

- **Why Full Points:** Clear, specific, defensible. Directly addresses the objection (skeptics apply inconsistent standards).

**Evidence (28/30):**
- Manuscript count: 5,800+ Greek NT manuscripts vs. ~10 for Caesar's *Gallic Wars*
- Time gap: NT manuscripts within 50–100 years vs. 1,000 years for most classics
- Textual consistency: 99.5% agreement across manuscripts
- Cited Ch 4 and Ch 6 specifically, plus external sources (Josephus, Tacitus)

- **Why 28/30:** Strong evidence, well-explained. Lost 2 points for not addressing *why* manuscript count matters (could have explained how more manuscripts = better cross-checking).

**Counterargument (23/25):**
- **Objection:** "But the NT was written by Christians, so it's biased."
- **Response:** "All ancient history was written by people with a stake in the events. We don't reject Josephus because he was Jewish, or Tacitus because he was Roman. The question is: does the evidence support the text's claims? For the NT, archaeology, external sources, and internal consistency say yes."

- **Why 23/25:** Good counterargument, thoughtful response. Lost 2 points because the objection could be stronger (e.g., "bias leads to fabrication, not just selective reporting"). The response is good, but doesn't fully engage with the *strongest* version of the objection.

**Presentation Skill (9/10):**
- Clear, organized, good pacing
- Visual aid (slide with manuscript comparison chart) supported the argument
- Lost 1 point for going slightly over time (7:30 instead of 7:00)

**Final Brief (10/10):**
> "I came into this unit thinking manuscript evidence was boring, but now I see why it matters. The strongest idea I encountered was [Student X]'s point about applying the same standard to all ancient texts. If we reject the NT, we have to reject almost everything we know about the ancient world. That's the argument I want to remember."

- **Why Full Points:** Shows genuine reflection, evidence of listening to peers, clear takeaway.

**Total: 95/100 (A)**

---

#### Student B — 78/100 (C+)

**Thesis (18/25):**
> "The Bible is reliable because it has lots of manuscripts and archaeology proves it's true."

- **Why 18/25:** Too broad ("The Bible" — OT and NT? All claims? All genres?). "Archaeology proves it's true" is overstated (archaeology supports the text, but doesn't "prove" theological claims). Lost 7 points for lack of specificity.

**Evidence (20/30):**
- Mentioned manuscript count (but didn't give specific numbers)
- Mentioned Dead Sea Scrolls (from Ch 6) but didn't explain their significance
- Didn't cite any specific chapters or external sources

- **Why 20/30:** Evidence is present but thin. Lost 10 points for lack of depth and specificity.

**Counterargument (15/25):**
- **Objection:** "Some people say the Bible has errors."
- **Response:** "But the manuscripts agree, so it doesn't have errors."

- **Why 15/25:** Straw man objection ("some people say" — who? what errors?). Response doesn't actually engage with the objection (manuscript agreement proves textual reliability, not factual accuracy). Lost 10 points.

**Presentation Skill (8/10):**
- Clear delivery, but disorganized (jumped between topics)
- Visual aid (poster with "Bible is true!" written in large letters) didn't support the argument
- Lost 2 points for lack of organization

**Final Brief (7/10):**
> "I learned that the Bible has a lot of manuscripts. I think that's important."

- **Why 7/10:** Shows some engagement, but doesn't reflect on *why* it's important or what they learned from peers. Lost 3 points for superficiality.

**Total: 78/100 (C+)**

---

#### Student C — 60/100 (D-)

**Thesis (10/25):**
> "I believe the Bible is true."

- **Why 10/25:** Not a defensible thesis. "I believe" is a statement of personal conviction, not an argument. Doesn't address a specific objection. Lost 15 points.

**Evidence (12/30):**
- Mentioned "faith" as the reason to trust the Bible
- Didn't cite the textbook or use any specific evidence from Ch 4–6

- **Why 12/30:** No textual evidence, no external sources. Lost 18 points.

**Counterargument (8/25):**
- **Objection:** "People don't believe in the Bible."
- **Response:** "But they should, because it's God's Word."

- **Why 8/25:** Weak objection (not specific). Response is circular reasoning (the Bible is true because it's God's Word, but the question is *how do we know* it's God's Word?). Lost 17 points.

**Presentation Skill (7/10):**
- Clear delivery, but only 3 minutes (instead of 5–7)
- No visual aid
- Lost 3 points for short length and lack of depth

**Final Brief (8/10):**
> "I learned that other students have different ideas about the Bible. I still think faith is most important."

- **Why 8/10:** Shows some reflection, but doesn't engage with the evidence or arguments from the unit. Lost 2 points.

**Total: 60/100 (D-)**

**Why This Grade:** Student didn't engage with the assignment. The prompt asked for a case using *evidence from Ch 4–6*, but they relied on personal conviction instead. Passing grade (barely) because they showed up and tried, but didn't meet the basic requirements.

---

### Grading Tips

- **Use the rubric.** Print it out, mark scores in each category, write 1–2 sentences of feedback per category. Students will know exactly where they lost points.
- **Grade the Final Brief separately.** A student might bomb their presentation but write a thoughtful Final Brief showing they learned from peers—that's worth something.
- **Don't overweight presentation skill.** A student who struggles to speak publicly but has a razor-sharp thesis should score well. This is about ideas, not performance.
- **The Final Capstone should be weighted more heavily.** It's cumulative, longer, and requires synthesis. In Worldview, it's 15% (vs. 12% for earlier capstones).

## 5. Final Exam (20% in Worldview — Cumulative)

### Exam Structure (Suggested)

| Section | Points | Format | What You're Testing |
|---------|--------|--------|---------------------|
| **Part 1: Recall** | 30 | Multiple choice / Fill-in-the-blank | Key concepts from all 8 worldviews (theology, philosophy, ethics, etc.) |
| **Part 2: Compare/Contrast** | 40 | Short answer (3–5 sentences each) | "How does Christianity answer X differently than Islam/Secularism/Postmodernism?" |
| **Part 3: Application** | 30 | Essay (1–2 pages) | "Apply a biblical worldview to a contemporary issue. Engage with at least one competing worldview." |

---

### Example Questions

**Part 1: Recall (30 points)**

1. **What is the Islamic view of God (Allah)?** (2 points)
   - **Answer:** Strict monotheism. Allah is one, indivisible, has no partners or equals. Rejects the Trinity.

2. **What is the Marxist view of history?** (2 points)
   - **Answer:** History is driven by class struggle. Society progresses through dialectical materialism toward a classless utopia (communism).

3. **What is the Buddhist diagnosis of the human condition?** (2 points)
   - **Answer:** Life is suffering (dukkha). The cause of suffering is desire/attachment. Liberation (nirvana) comes through eliminating desire via the Eightfold Path.

4. **Fill in the blank: Postmodernism rejects ________ narratives and claims that truth is ________.**
   - **Answer:** grand; socially constructed / relative

**Grading:** 2 points per correct answer. Partial credit (1 point) for partially correct answers.

---

**Part 2: Compare/Contrast (40 points — 4 questions, 10 points each)**

**Question:** *"How does Christianity's view of ethics differ from Secularism's view of ethics?"*

---

**Student A (10/10 — Full Credit):**
> "Christianity grounds ethics in God's character—objective, unchanging, transcendent. What is good is what reflects God's nature. Secularism, lacking a transcendent foundation, grounds ethics in human reason (Kant), utility (Mill), or social contract (Hobbes). Without God, secular ethics struggle to ground *objective* morality—why is human flourishing 'good' if there's no ultimate purpose? Christianity answers: because humans are made in God's image."

**Why Full Credit:** Clear contrast, accurate representation of both worldviews, engages with the underlying issue (objective vs. subjective morality).

---

**Student B (6/10 — Partial Credit):**
> "Christianity says God decides what's right and wrong. Secularism says humans decide."

**Why Partial Credit:** Accurate but superficial. Doesn't explain *why* this difference matters or how each worldview justifies its ethics. Lost 4 points for lack of depth.

---

**Part 3: Application Essay (30 points)**

**Prompt:** *"Choose one contemporary issue (bioethics, social justice, technology, or religious freedom). Defend a biblical worldview response, engaging with at least one competing worldview."*

---

**Student A (28/30 — A)**

> **Abortion: A Biblical Worldview vs. Secularism**
>
> The biblical worldview grounds the sanctity of human life in the imago Dei—humans are made in God's image (Genesis 1:27), and therefore possess intrinsic dignity from conception. Psalm 139:13-16 describes God knitting David together in the womb, suggesting personhood begins before birth. From this foundation, abortion is the unjust taking of innocent life, a violation of the sixth commandment ("You shall not murder").
>
> Secularism, lacking a transcendent foundation for human dignity, struggles to ground the personhood of the unborn. Peter Singer argues that personhood requires self-awareness, rationality, and autonomy—criteria that exclude not only fetuses but also newborns and the severely disabled. If human value is grounded in functional capacity rather than intrinsic worth, then abortion (and infanticide) become morally permissible.
>
> The biblical worldview provides a stronger foundation: humans have value not because of what they can *do*, but because of *whose* image they bear. This grounds the protection of the vulnerable (unborn, disabled, elderly) in a way secular ethics cannot.

**Grading Breakdown:**
- **Thesis clarity (10/10):** Clear position, directly addresses the prompt
- **Biblical worldview defense (10/10):** Cites Scripture, explains the theological foundation (imago Dei)
- **Engagement with competing worldview (8/10):** Accurately represents secularism (Singer), identifies the key weakness (functional personhood). Lost 2 points for not addressing a *stronger* secular argument (e.g., bodily autonomy).

**Total: 28/30 (A)**

---

**Student B (18/30 — C-)**

> **Social Justice: A Biblical Worldview**
>
> The Bible talks a lot about justice. Christians should care about the poor because Jesus said to love your neighbor. Marxism also cares about the poor, but Marxism is atheistic, so it's wrong. The biblical worldview is better because it has God.

**Grading Breakdown:**
- **Thesis clarity (5/10):** Vague ("Christians should care about the poor" — but how? based on what principles?). Lost 5 points.
- **Biblical worldview defense (5/10):** Mentions Jesus, but doesn't explain the *biblical foundation* for justice (God's character, image of God, covenant community). Lost 5 points.
- **Engagement with competing worldview (3/10):** Mentions Marxism but doesn't explain Marx's view of justice (class-based, redistributive). Response is shallow ("Marxism is atheistic, so it's wrong" — doesn't engage with the actual argument). Lost 7 points.
- **Writing quality (-5):** Too short (1 paragraph instead of 1–2 pages), lacks depth

**Total: 18/30 (C-)**

**Why This Grade:** Student didn't engage with the material in depth. The essay shows surface-level thinking and doesn't meet the prompt's requirement to "defend" the biblical worldview (no real defense, just assertion).

---

### Grading Tips

- **Part 1 (Recall):** Grade quickly. 2 points per correct answer, 1 point for partially correct. Don't overthink it.
- **Part 2 (Compare/Contrast):** Look for accurate representation of both worldviews + engagement with the underlying issue. A student who says "Christianity is better" without explaining *why* gets partial credit at best.
- **Part 3 (Application Essay):** Grade on thesis clarity (10 pts), biblical defense (10 pts), engagement with competing worldview (10 pts). Dock points for superficiality, not for disagreeing with you.

## 6. Reflection Essay (5% in Worldview)

### What You're Grading
- **Depth of reflection** — Has the course shaped their thinking?
- **Honesty** — Are they being real, or telling you what they think you want to hear?
- **Engagement with course content** — Do they reference specific worldviews, discussions, or ideas?

### Grading Scale (3-Point System)

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **100%** | Deep reflection, honest engagement, references specific course content |
| **75%** | Some reflection, but generic or surface-level |
| **50%** | Minimal effort, didn't engage with the prompt |
| **0%** | Not submitted |

---

### Example: Reflection Essay (Worldview, Week 35)

**Prompt:** *"How has this course shaped your worldview? What question are you still thinking about?"*

---

**Student A (100% — Full Credit):**
> "Coming into this course, I thought all religions were basically the same—different paths to the same God. Studying Islam, Buddhism, and Hinduism showed me that's not true. Islam's Allah is radically different from the Christian God (no Trinity, no incarnation, no Father-Son relationship). Buddhism doesn't even have a personal God—ultimate reality is void (nirvana). These aren't 'different paths'—they're contradictory truth claims.
>
> The question I'm still thinking about: If Christianity is true, what does that mean for my Buddhist neighbor? I believe Jesus is the only way to the Father, but I also believe God is just and loving. How do I hold both of those truths together? I don't have an answer yet, but this course gave me the tools to think about it more carefully."

**Why Full Credit:** Deep reflection, honest wrestling with a hard question, references specific worldviews (Islam, Buddhism), shows genuine change in thinking.

---

**Student B (75% — Partial Credit):**
> "This course taught me about different worldviews. I learned about Islam, Buddhism, and Secularism. I think Christianity is the best worldview. I'm still thinking about how to share my faith with people who believe differently."

**Why Partial Credit:** Some engagement, but generic. Doesn't show *how* their thinking changed or *what* specific ideas challenged them. Lost 25 points for superficiality.

---

### Grading Tips

- **Look for honesty.** The best reflection essays often start with "I used to think... but now I realize..." or "The question I can't shake is..."
- **Don't penalize doubt.** A student who writes, "I'm not sure how to reconcile God's justice with eternal punishment" is being honest. That's worth full credit.
- **Grade quickly.** This is a low-stakes assessment. Use the 3-point scale and move on.

---

## Pro Tips for Grading

1. **Grade Discussion Briefs quickly.** Use the 3-point scale and spend no more than 1–2 minutes per brief. Students need to know you're reading them, but they don't need extensive feedback every time.

2. **Use the rubric for Capstones.** Print it out, mark scores in each category, write 1–2 sentences of feedback per category. Students will know exactly where they lost points.

3. **Grade the Final Brief separately from the presentation.** A student might bomb their presentation but write a thoughtful Final Brief showing they learned from peers—that's worth something.

4. **Don't grade on "correct" answers for Discussion Briefs.** You want honest engagement, not performance. A student who writes, "I don't understand why prophecy matters—it feels like cherry-picking verses" deserves full credit if they're wrestling honestly.

5. **Fail students who refuse to participate in Pair & Defend.** The whole point is to argue a position you don't hold. If they won't do it, they're missing the pedagogical value.

6. **Use peer feedback for Case Studies.** At the end of group work, ask each student: "Did everyone in your group contribute equally? If not, who didn't pull their weight?" You'll know who to give 0s to.

7. **The Final Capstone should be weighted more heavily.** It's cumulative, longer, and requires synthesis. In Worldview, it's 15% (vs. 12% for earlier capstones). Make sure the rubric reflects that.

8. **Grade for growth, not perfection.** A student who goes from shallow thinking in Week 1 to deep wrestling in Week 35 deserves to be celebrated, even if their arguments aren't airtight.

9. **Return graded work quickly.** Capstones should be graded within 1 week. Discussion Briefs within 2–3 days. Students forget what they wrote if you wait too long.

10. **Read the Final Briefs carefully.** They're often the most honest window into student thinking you'll get all year. Use them to adjust your teaching for the next unit.
