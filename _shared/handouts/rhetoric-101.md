# Rhetoric 101: Argument, Persuasion, and Logic

## What is Rhetoric?

**Rhetoric** is the art of effective communication and persuasion. It's not manipulation — it's the skill of making truth clear, compelling, and defensible. Every time you defend an idea, write an essay, or explain why you believe something, you're using rhetoric.

## The Three Appeals (Aristotle)

When you make an argument, you can appeal to three different aspects of your audience:

### Logos (Logic)
- Appeals to **reason** and **evidence**
- Uses facts, data, logical structure, cause-and-effect
- *Example:* "The Bible contains prophecies written centuries before Jesus that He fulfilled — therefore, it has predictive accuracy beyond human ability."

### Pathos (Emotion)
- Appeals to **feelings** and **values**
- Uses stories, vivid language, shared experiences
- *Example:* "Imagine a world where truth is whatever the powerful say it is — where your voice, your conscience, your very self can be erased by someone else's definition."

### Ethos (Character/Credibility)
- Appeals to **trustworthiness** and **authority**
- Establishes why *you* (or your source) should be believed
- *Example:* "C.S. Lewis was an atheist professor at Oxford before converting — he understands the objections from the inside."

**Good arguments use all three.** Logos without pathos is cold. Pathos without logos is manipulative. Ethos without either is empty.

## How to Structure an Argument

1. **Claim** — What are you arguing? Be specific.
   - Weak: "Christianity is good."
   - Strong: "Christianity provides the only coherent basis for human rights."

2. **Evidence** — Why should I believe you?
   - Scripture, history, logic, experience, expert testimony

3. **Warrant** — How does your evidence prove your claim?
   - This is the bridge. Don't assume it's obvious.

4. **Counterargument** — What would someone on the other side say?
   - Acknowledge it, then refute it.

5. **Conclusion** — Restate your claim with the weight of your argument behind it.

## Common Logical Fallacies

A **fallacy** is a flaw in reasoning that makes an argument weak or invalid. Learn to spot these — in your own arguments and others'.

### 1. **Ad Hominem** (Attack the Person)
- Attacking the *person* instead of their *argument*.
- *Example:* "You can't trust his view on ethics — he's a Republican."
- *Why it fails:* The truth of a claim doesn't depend on who says it.

### 2. **Straw Man**
- Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack.
- *Example:* "Christians think you should never question anything and just blindly obey."
- *Why it fails:* You're arguing against something they never said.

### 3. **Appeal to Authority**
- Citing an authority *outside their area of expertise* or *assuming authority = truth*.
- *Example:* "Einstein believed in God, so theism must be true."
- *Why it fails:* Authorities can be wrong. Their expertise must be relevant.

### 4. **Appeal to Popularity** (Bandwagon)
- Assuming something is true because many people believe it.
- *Example:* "Most people today don't believe in absolute truth, so it must not exist."
- *Why it fails:* Truth isn't decided by majority vote.

### 5. **Appeal to Emotion**
- Using emotion *instead of* logic to win an argument.
- *Example:* "How can you say abortion is wrong? Don't you care about women in crisis?"
- *Why it fails:* Compassion and truth both matter — but feelings don't settle the question.

### 6. **False Dilemma** (Either/Or)
- Presenting only two options when more exist.
- *Example:* "Either you believe in evolution or you're anti-science."
- *Why it fails:* There are other positions (e.g., theistic evolution, Intelligent Design).

### 7. **Begging the Question** (Circular Reasoning)
- Assuming the conclusion in your premise.
- *Example:* "The Bible is true because God wrote it, and I know God wrote it because the Bible says so."
- *Why it fails:* You haven't given independent reasons to believe.

### 8. **Red Herring**
- Introducing an irrelevant point to distract from the real issue.
- *Example:* "Why focus on abortion? What about poverty and war?"
- *Why it fails:* Other issues being important doesn't make *this* issue less important.

### 9. **Slippery Slope**
- Claiming that one step will inevitably lead to extreme consequences — without proving the connection.
- *Example:* "If we allow people to question one doctrine, soon no one will believe anything."
- *Why it fails:* You must show *why* the progression is inevitable, not just assert it.

### 10. **Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc** (False Cause)
- Assuming that because B followed A, A caused B.
- *Example:* "I prayed for a parking spot and found one — so God answers trivial prayers."
- *Why it fails:* Correlation ≠ causation. Other explanations may exist.

### 11. **Tu Quoque** (You Too / Hypocrisy)
- Dismissing an argument because the person doesn't live up to it.
- *Example:* "You say lying is wrong, but you lied last week — so your argument is invalid."
- *Why it fails:* Hypocrisy doesn't make the *claim* false, only the person inconsistent.

### 12. **Hasty Generalization**
- Drawing a broad conclusion from insufficient evidence.
- *Example:* "I knew one Christian who was judgmental, so all Christians are hypocrites."
- *Why it fails:* One example doesn't establish a pattern.

## How to Identify Fallacies

1. **Ask: Is the reasoning valid?** Does the conclusion actually follow from the premises?
2. **Ask: Is the evidence relevant?** Or is it a distraction?
3. **Ask: Are there hidden assumptions?** What does the argument take for granted?
4. **Ask: Could the opposite be true with the same reasoning?** If yes, the argument is weak.

## Rhetoric in Scripture: Paul's Strategy

The Apostle Paul was a master rhetorician. He used classical Greek rhetorical techniques to defend the Gospel — especially in **Romans**, where he structures his argument like a legal case:

- **Romans 1–3:** Establishes the *problem* (all have sinned).
- **Romans 3–5:** Presents the *solution* (justification by faith).
- **Romans 6–8:** Answers *objections* (grace leading to license, the role of the law).
- **Romans 9–11:** Addresses *Israel's place* in God's plan.
- **Romans 12–16:** Moves to *application* (how to live in light of the Gospel).

Paul uses:
- **Diatribe** (imagined objections: "What shall we say then?" — Rom 6:1)
- **Rhetorical questions** ("Is God unjust?" — Rom 9:14)
- **Quotations from authority** (OT Scripture)
- **Logical progression** (if A, then B; B is true, therefore A)

**Key insight:** Paul doesn't just *assert* the Gospel — he *argues* for it, anticipating objections and building a case. That's what you're learning to do.
