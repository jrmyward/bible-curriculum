# The Four Ds: A Framework for Doctrinal Disagreement

## Why This Matters

Not all disagreements are equal. Some doctrines are worth dying for. Others are worth discussing but not dividing over. Knowing the difference keeps us from two errors:

1. **Doctrinal minimalism** — treating everything as optional ("just love Jesus, doctrine doesn't matter")
2. **Doctrinal maximalism** — treating every disagreement as gospel-level ("if you don't agree on [minor issue], you're not a real Christian")

This framework helps us fight for what matters, discuss what's important, and give grace where Scripture gives freedom.

---

## The Four Categories

### 1. **DIE FOR** — Essential Gospel Truths

**Definition:** Core Christian beliefs that define the faith itself. Denying these puts you outside historic, orthodox Christianity.

**Biblical basis:** "Matters of first importance" (1 Cor 15:3–5), doctrines that define the Gospel itself (Gal 1:6–9)

**Examples:**
- The Trinity (God is one being in three persons)
- The deity and humanity of Christ (fully God, fully man)
- Salvation by grace alone through faith alone in Christ alone
- The bodily resurrection of Jesus
- The authority and inspiration of Scripture
- The future return of Christ and final judgment

**How to engage:**
- **Defend boldly.** These are non-negotiable.
- **Teach clearly.** Don't assume people know why these matter.
- **Break fellowship if necessary.** Paul says if someone preaches a different gospel, "let him be accursed" (Gal 1:8–9).

**Classroom application:**
- When we discuss these, we're building a foundation — not debating whether it's true.
- Disagreement here means one party has moved outside Christian orthodoxy.

---

### 2. **DIVIDE FOR** — Denominational Distinctives

**Definition:** Important secondary doctrines that sincere, Bible-believing Christians disagree on. These often determine which church you join but don't affect your salvation.

**Biblical basis:** Christians can disagree on these and still be "in Christ" — unity in essentials, liberty in non-essentials

**Examples:**
- **Baptism:** Mode (immersion vs. sprinkling) and subjects (believer's baptism vs. infant baptism)
- **Church government:** Elder-led, congregational, episcopal
- **Spiritual gifts:** Cessationist vs. continuationist (are sign gifts still active?)
- **Soteriology:** Calvinism vs. Arminianism (predestination, free will, perseverance of the saints)
- **Women in ministry:** Complementarian vs. egalitarian

**How to engage:**
- **Study deeply.** These aren't trivial — they shape how we read Scripture and practice faith.
- **Argue respectfully.** You can disagree strongly without questioning someone's Christianity.
- **Acknowledge godly people on both sides.** If Charles Spurgeon and John Wesley both loved Jesus but disagreed on election, humility is warranted.

**Classroom application:**
- When we debate these, we're learning *how* to interpret Scripture faithfully.
- You may land in different denominations because of these — and that's okay.
- Don't treat your position as "Die For" unless Scripture is crystal clear (and even then, check history — has the church universally agreed?).

---

### 3. **DEBATE FOR** — Important but Not Divisive

**Definition:** Issues worth discussing within the same church/classroom, but not worth breaking fellowship over. Faithful Christians can disagree and stay in community.

**Biblical basis:** "Disputable matters" (Rom 14:1) — issues where Scripture gives principles but not exhaustive prescriptions

**Examples:**
- **Eschatology details:** Premil, amil, postmil; pre-trib, mid-trib, post-trib rapture
- **Creation and science:** Young earth, old earth, framework view, evolutionary creationism
- **Worship style:** Traditional, contemporary, blended; liturgical vs. spontaneous
- **Church involvement in social/political issues:** How much? Which ones?
- **Bible translation philosophy:** Formal equivalence vs. dynamic equivalence

**How to engage:**
- **Discuss freely.** These are interesting, important, and worth thinking through.
- **Hold positions humbly.** If the early church fathers disagreed, maybe you don't have the final answer.
- **Focus on principles, not dogma.** E.g., "God created" is Die For; *how* He created is Debate For.

**Classroom application:**
- This is where we practice *how to disagree well.*
- Learn to argue for your view while respecting others.
- Don't let these debates create factions ("I follow Paul," "I follow Apollos" — 1 Cor 1:12).

---

### 4. **DECIDE FOR YOURSELF** — Personal Conscience Matters

**Definition:** Issues where Scripture gives freedom and individuals must follow their own conscience before God. Romans 14–15 is the key text.

**Biblical basis:** "One person esteems one day as better than another, while another esteems all days alike. Each one should be fully convinced in his own mind" (Rom 14:5)

**Examples:**
- **Food and drink:** Alcohol, diet (vegetarian, kosher, fasting)
- **Sabbath observance:** Sunday rest, Saturday Sabbath, or no special day
- **Entertainment:** Movies, music, gaming (what's permissible vs. wise)
- **Education choices:** Public, private, homeschool, Christian school
- **Bible translation preference:** ESV, NIV, NASB, KJV — pick one and read it
- **Cultural practices:** Clothing styles, holiday observance (Christmas, Halloween)

**How to engage:**
- **Don't judge others** (Rom 14:3–4, 10).
- **Don't flaunt your freedom** if it causes a weaker believer to stumble (Rom 14:13–21).
- **Respect conscience.** If someone says, "I can't do that in good faith," don't push them.
- **Teach principles, not rules.** "Is it helpful? Does it build up? Does it glorify God?" (1 Cor 10:23, 31)

**Classroom application:**
- We won't debate these in class — they're personal between you and God.
- If someone asks, "Is [X] a sin?", the answer is often, "What does your conscience say? What does Scripture say? Are you trying to justify something or honor God?"

| **Die For** | Essential | The Gospel itself | Defend boldly, teach clearly, break fellowship if denied | Deity of Christ |
| **Divide For** | Very Important | Church membership, denominational identity | Study deeply, argue respectfully, acknowledge godly disagreement | Baptism mode |
| **Debate For** | Important | Understanding, practice, wisdom | Discuss freely, hold humbly, don't create factions | Eschatology details |
| **Decide For** | Personal | Individual conscience before God | Don't judge, don't flaunt, respect freedom | Alcohol, entertainment |

## How to Use This in Class

Before discussing any doctrine or ethical issue, we'll ask:

**"Which D is this?"**

Then place a sticky note or magnet on the classroom poster under the correct category. This helps us:

1. **Set emotional temperature.** Die For = passionate defense. Decide For = give freedom.
2. **Choose appropriate tone.** Debate For = "Here's what I think and why..." Divide For = "Here's what Scripture seems to teach, but I respect disagreement."
3. **Avoid false equivalence.** Don't treat eschatology like the resurrection.

---

## Watch Out For

### **Category Creep**
- Don't upgrade **Debate For** to **Divide For** just because you feel strongly.
- Don't downgrade **Die For** to **Debate For** to avoid conflict.

### **Historical Myopia**
- Just because *you* were taught it as essential doesn't mean the church universal agrees.
- Check: Have Christians across time and cultures held this? Or is it recent/regional?

### **Weaponizing Categories**
- Don't say, "That's just Decide For, so you can't criticize me."
- If someone's "personal conviction" contradicts Scripture, it's not a Romans 14 issue — it's a discipleship issue.

## Discussion Protocols by Category

### **Die For**
- **Goal:** Understand and defend
- **Tone:** Confident, clear
- **Disagreement:** Clarify misunderstanding or recognize one party is outside orthodoxy
- **Example question:** "Why is the Trinity essential to Christianity? What's lost if we deny it?"

### **Divide For**
- **Goal:** Understand both sides, form a conviction
- **Tone:** Respectful, rigorous
- **Disagreement:** Expected and healthy — argue for your view, respect others'
- **Example question:** "What's the best biblical case for infant baptism? For believer's baptism?"

### **Debate For**
- **Goal:** Explore ideas, learn nuance
- **Tone:** Curious, humble
- **Disagreement:** Celebrated — "iron sharpens iron"
- **Example question:** "What would change in our practice if we held a different eschatological view?"

### **Decide For**
- **Goal:** Apply biblical principles to personal life
- **Tone:** Pastoral, gracious
- **Disagreement:** Not really a debate — "Here's what I do and why; you decide before God"
- **Example question:** "How do you decide what media is edifying for you personally?"

---

## Conclusion

The Four Ds framework isn't about dodging hard questions or pretending doctrine doesn't matter. It's about **fighting the right fights in the right way.**

- **Die For:** Defend the faith once delivered to the saints.
- **Divide For:** Hold convictions firmly, disagree graciously.
- **Debate For:** Think deeply, argue well, stay unified.
- **Decide For:** Follow your conscience, give others freedom.

As Paul writes:

> "If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all" (Rom 12:18).

We pursue truth *and* unity — not one at the expense of the other.
